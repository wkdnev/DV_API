### AU-1 — Policy and Procedures (Control)

This requirement is about making sure the organisation has a clear, written plan for how it will:

- check what people and systems are doing,
- show that these checks are carried out properly, and
- remain responsible and answerable for the results.

Without an up-to-date policy and practical procedures, important evidence could be missed, responsibilities could be unclear, and the organisation may not meet legal and regulatory expectations after security incidents, failed audits, major changes, or new rules.

The organisation must create an organisation-wide audit and accountability policy that sets out its purpose, scope, roles, responsibilities, leadership commitment, how different parts of the business will work together, and how compliance will be ensured. This policy must align with relevant laws, directives, regulations, and internal standards.

The Chief Information Security Officer, working with the Audit and Accountability Policy Working Group, must manage producing, documenting, sharing, and maintaining these documents.

The policy must be reviewed at least every year, and also after security incidents, audit findings, major organisational changes, or changes to relevant laws and directives. The procedures must be reviewed at least every quarter, and after the same triggers. Both the policy and procedures must be shared with the named leadership, owners, the compliance team, and internal audit staff.

##### CISO-owned audit & accountability policy and quarterly procedures

- Category: Manual
- Priority: Critical

###### WHAT
Create and maintain an organisation-level **audit and accountability policy** and supporting **audit and accountability procedures** that cover the AU family controls used in the organisation. This should include clear roles, responsibilities, coordination, and alignment with compliance requirements.

###### WHY (control requirement)
AU-1 requires that audit and accountability are governed by **documented policy and procedures** (not just repeating the AU controls). The guidance also requires that:
- the policy and procedures are **consistent with applicable laws, executive instructions, regulations, organisational policies, standards, and guidance**;
- the **security and privacy programmes** work together when developing them;
- the **Chief Information Security Officer (CISO)** is the named person responsible for managing the policy and procedures;
- the policy is reviewed and updated **at least once every year** and after defined events (for example, incidents, audit findings, major organisational changes, and changes to laws or executive instructions);
- the procedures are reviewed and updated **at least once every quarter** and after the same types of events;
- the documents are shared with the defined people and roles.

###### HOW (specific steps/approach)
1. **Create one official document set** (policy plus procedures) in a controlled repository. Include version history, effective dates, and a clear mapping to the AU family controls used for the enterprise application environment.
2. **Set governance ownership and a clear workflow**: appoint the **CISO** as the official owner and create an **Audit and Accountability Policy Working Group** (including the compliance office, internal audit, security operations centre (SOC) lead, and a privacy representative). Use an approval process with recorded sign-off.
3. **Write the policy** to include: purpose, scope, roles and responsibilities, management commitment, coordination between security and privacy programmes, and alignment to applicable laws, executive instructions, regulations, and standards.
4. **Write role-based procedures** that explain how the policy is carried out in practice for: system owners, SOC analysts, and internal audit. Include specific outputs and evidence records (for example, review records, update triggers, and acknowledgement of sharing) so the procedures can be audited.
5. **Set review and update timing and event triggers** exactly as required: policy **annually** and after incidents/audit findings/major organisational changes/legal or executive instruction changes; procedures **quarterly** and after the same triggers. Record the trigger type and the reason for each update.
6. **Share the documents with evidence**: publish the policy to the defined leadership/system/compliance audience, and share the procedures with system owners, SOC analysts, and internal audit. Keep evidence of sharing (for example, the publication record and acknowledgements where possible).

###### WHO
- **Chief Information Security Officer (CISO)**: owner and manager of the audit and accountability policy and procedures; chairs the governance workflow.
- **Audit and Accountability Policy Working Group**: drafts, reviews, and coordinates alignment between security and privacy.
- **Compliance office and internal audit**: check that the documents are auditable and that evidence expectations are clear.
- **Security operations centre (SOC) lead**: ensures the procedures reflect day-to-day operational monitoring and accountability processes.
- **Privacy representative**: ensures the privacy programme collaborates and remains aligned.

###### Acceptance criteria
- An organisation-level **audit and accountability policy** exists, is approved by the CISO/working group, and clearly aligns to applicable laws, executive instructions, regulations, and standards.
- An organisation-level **audit and accountability procedures** document set exists, is role-based (system owners, SOC analysts, internal audit), and explains how it is implemented and what evidence is produced.
- The document repository includes **version history**, effective dates, and traceable approvals.
- Review timing is in place: the policy has a documented **annual review** date, and the procedures have a documented **quarterly review** date.
- Event-driven update triggers are documented and linked to change-control records (incidents, audit findings, major organisational changes, and legal or executive instruction changes).
- Sharing records exist showing the policy was shared with **CISO, senior leadership, system owners, and the compliance office**, and that the procedures were shared with **system owners, SOC analysts, and internal audit**.

###### Actions Required for Compliance

- [ ] Create an organisation-wide audit and accountability policy document set in a controlled repository, with version control and an approval workflow.
- [ ] Define the Chief Information Security Officer (CISO) as the official responsible for managing the security policy and procedures, and for recording the governance responsibilities in the working group charter.
- [ ] Draft the policy content to include: its purpose, what it covers (scope), who is responsible for what (roles and responsibilities), senior management commitment, how security and privacy teams coordinate, and how it aligns with relevant compliance requirements.
- [ ] Create role-based audit and accountability procedures for system owners, security operations centre (SOC) analysts, and internal audit, including the required evidence records.
- [ ] Set a review and update schedule: review the policy every year and review the procedures every quarter. Keep a written list of specific events that trigger updates.
- [ ] Share the policy and procedures with the specified people or roles, and keep proof that they were shared (for example, the publication record and any acknowledgements where applicable).

### AU-2 — Event Logging (Control)

This expectation is about making sure your systems keep a clear, reliable record of important security events, so you can review them later and understand what happened. Without this, suspicious activity—such as repeated failed logins, attempts to gain extra permissions, misuse of administrator accounts, use of smart card credentials, unauthorised changes to security settings, access to sensitive data, attempts to export data, or signs that someone is trying to interfere with the audit record—could go unnoticed or be impossible to piece together after an incident.

To meet this, the organisation must first identify which types of events the system can record. It must then agree with the relevant internal teams which event types are needed for investigations, including successful and failed logins, privilege escalation, use of administrator accounts, use of smart card credentials, security-relevant configuration changes, access to classified or controlled data, data export or download, suspected audit log tampering, and use of authentication tokens.

Every selected event must be recorded every time it occurs. There must also be extra logging for high-volume events during 24/7 incident response, and at least monthly for other times. The organisation must document why these choices are sufficient for investigations after an incident, and review and update the selected event types at least quarterly.

##### Configure application audit event types and SIEM ingestion

- Category: Software
- Priority: Critical

###### WHAT
Define the exact set of security-relevant event types the application must record (and when/how often), then configure the application and supporting components so those events are recorded when they happen and reliably sent into the organisation’s security information and event management (SIEM) system and audit storage.

###### WHY (control requirement)
AU-2 requires security and privacy event logging. This includes choosing a subset of event types “at a given point in time”, recording each selected event type “on occurrence”, and reviewing and updating the selected event types periodically. The organisation’s AU-2 requirements define which event types to use and the required logging frequency/situation.

###### HOW (specific steps/approach)
1. **Create or confirm the application event logging requirements** using the organisation-defined event types and subset:
   - Event types (subset): successful and failed logons; privilege escalation; use of administrative accounts; use of personal identity verification (PIV) credentials; security-relevant configuration changes; access to classified or controlled data; data export/download; audit log tampering; authentication token use.
2. **Implement logging in the application** so each selected event type is generated by the relevant parts of the code and integration points:
   - Authentication events: record on successful and failed login attempts (including outcomes from the identity provider).
   - Privilege/admin events: record on privilege escalation and administrative account use (including role changes and administrative actions).
   - Credential usage: record on PIV credential use (where applicable through identity integration) and on authentication token use (for example, token issuance, refresh, and validation events where supported).
   - Security-relevant configuration changes: record when security settings change (for example, authentication policy, access control model changes, audit settings changes).
   - Data events: record when classified or controlled data is accessed and when data is exported/downloaded.
   - Audit integrity: record events showing audit log tampering attempts (where the platform provides detection signals). If detection is not supported, record “audit pipeline disabled/unavailable” and “log integrity check failed” signals.
3. **Set the logging frequency/situation** to match the organisation-defined rule:
   - Record each selected event type every time it occurs.
   - For high-volume events, ensure the system can switch to “incident-response mode” to record every occurrence during 24/7 incident response, then return to the monthly baseline otherwise.
4. **Configure the log pipeline for completeness and usefulness**:
   - Ensure logs include the minimum forensic details: timestamp with time zone, user/actor identity, source internet protocol (IP) address and host, session identifier, event type, outcome, and relevant object/data classification tags.
   - Ensure SIEM ingestion is set up for the application’s event format (normalisation/parsing) and that events can be searched by event type.
5. **Set up quarterly review evidence**:
   - Keep a version-controlled record of the enabled event-type subset and the reason for any disabled or high-volume categories.
   - Provide evidence that the current production configuration matches the approved subset (for example, configuration exports/screenshots and SIEM ingestion validation results).

###### WHO (role responsible)
- **Application Security/Engineering Lead**: owns the mapping between event types and the application code paths, and the logging implementation.
- **Platform/DevOps Engineer**: owns log pipeline configuration and incident-response mode controls.
- **Security operations centre (SOC)/SIEM Administrator**: checks that events are parsed and ingested correctly, and provides feedback on how useful the events are.
- **Information Security/Privacy Officer (if applicable)**: reviews the privacy impact of the logged fields and approves the final event-type subset.

###### Acceptance criteria
- For every event type in the organisation-defined subset, the application produces an audit event **on occurrence** in production (or the relevant live environment), and the SIEM shows the event type with correct parsing.
- High-volume event handling follows the organisation-defined frequency/situation: every occurrence during 24/7 incident response and monthly otherwise (shown through configuration and test/validation evidence).
- Audit log tampering and audit pipeline integrity signals are present (either explicit tampering detection events, or integrity/pipeline failure events) and are ingested into the SIEM.
- A quarterly review record exists showing the enabled event-type subset and evidence that the production configuration matches the approved list.

###### Actions Required for Compliance

- [ ] Confirm that the application’s enabled “AU-2” event-type subset matches “au-02_odp.02”, and record the reason for any exclusions.
- [ ] Add audit logging so that each required event type is recorded from the relevant parts of the application, including: logons, privilege escalation, use of administrative functions, personal identity verification (PIV) and credential use, security configuration changes, access to controlled data, export or download activity, signals that audit logs have not been tampered with, and token use.
- [ ] Set the logging frequency so that every selected type of event is recorded when it happens. For high-volume events, enable an incident-response mode that runs 24 hours a day, 7 days a week; for all other events, use the standard monthly baseline.
- [ ] Set up the logging pipeline (agent, appender, and collector) to standardise log events and send them to the security information and event management (SIEM) system with the required fields: timestamp, actor, outcome, session, source, and object/classification tags.
- [ ] Check that the security information and event management (SIEM) system can correctly read and bring in data by creating test events for every required event type and confirming that those events can be found using the SIEM search function.
- [ ] Create a quarterly review record that links the approved set of event types to the current production evidence (configuration export files and verification that security information and event management (SIEM) ingestion is working).

### AU-3 — Content of Audit Records (Control)

This requirement is about making sure the organisation’s records of important system and user activity are complete enough to explain what happened, when it happened, where it happened, who was involved, and what the outcome was. Without these records, it is much harder to spot wrongdoing, investigate mistakes, or understand why something failed. That can leave sensitive information exposed, disrupt services, or harm privacy, with no clear way to prove what occurred.  

To meet this expectation, the organisation must ensure that for every event it decides to record, each saved audit record clearly shows: the type of event; the date and time it happened; the location involved (for example, the system or service); what triggered it (for example, the user, device, or process); the outcome (including whether it succeeded or failed, and any relevant details); and the identities of any people, systems, or other entities connected to the event, including any affected items such as files or target addresses where applicable.

##### Enforce AU-3 audit record fields via SIEM ingestion schema

- Category: Software
- Priority: Critical

###### WHAT
Set up an organisation-approved format for audit records and make sure it is enforced when logs are collected, so that every kept audit record includes: (a) a description of the event, (b) dependable time stamps, (c) the source and destination addresses/locations where relevant, (d) the user or system/process involved, (e) whether it succeeded or failed, and (f) any extra user/system/process details needed for that type of event. Also include the filename(s) and the affected file or other target resource when the event involves files or resources.

###### WHY (control requirement)
Control AU-3 requires audit records to include enough information to describe what happened, when it happened, where it came from and where it went (where relevant), who or what performed the action, whether it succeeded or failed, and which filenames were involved. This is needed to support auditing/investigation and accountability.

###### HOW (specific steps/approach)
1. **Define a standard audit event format** for the application’s log sources (web/application programming interface (API), admin actions, authentication/authorisation, data access, and system/service events), with the required fields mapped to the AU-3 requirements:
   - `event_description` (event type / what happened)
   - `event_time` (time stamp)
   - `event_location` (system/service/application context)
   - `source_address` and `destination_address` (where relevant)
   - `actor_user_id` / `actor_process_id` (user/system/process identifiers)
   - `outcome` + `success`/`failure` (success or fail indication)
   - `result_details` (event-specific outcome)
   - `file_name` / `target_resource` (filenames/resources when relevant)
2. **Check at the point of log collection** in the security information and event management (SIEM) / log pipeline (for example, a collector/forwarder plus a normalisation service) to ensure required fields are present and in the correct format. For events that do not meet the requirements:
   - send them to a **quarantine area** (or dead-letter queue) and create a remediation ticket for the responsible team,
   - do not silently discard them or accept them if they are missing required AU-3 fields.
3. **Standardise and enhance identity details** so the “who did it” information is consistent:
   - map application session/user identifiers and service account identifiers to the organisation’s official identity records (identity provider (IdP) / identity governance and administration (IGA)),
   - capture process identifiers when the application provides them (for example, job IDs, workflow IDs, API client identifiers).
4. **Ensure time accuracy** for audit usefulness:
   - require network time protocol (NTP)-synchronised hosts/containers for the application and log collectors,
   - add checks during log collection for clock differences, and flag/reject events outside an agreed acceptable time-drift window.
5. **Handle audit information in a privacy-protecting way**:
   - identify fields that may contain personal data (for example, free-text parameters, usernames, filenames that include personal information),
   - apply tokenisation/redaction rules that keep investigatory value (for example, stable hashes for linking related events) while preventing unnecessary disclosure in the audit record.

###### WHO (role responsible)
- **Security Engineering / Security Operations Centre (SOC) Engineering**: owns the standard audit record format, the log-collection validation rules, and the quarantine/remediation process.
- **Application Engineering Lead**: ensures the application provides the required event fields (or that adapters extract them) for web/API, admin, and data-access events.
- **Identity and Access Management (IAM) team**: provides the official identity mapping/enrichment.
- **Privacy Officer / Data Protection Lead** (with Security): approves the redaction/tokenisation rules for audit records.

###### Acceptance criteria
- For each configured audit event type kept by the application/SIEM pipeline, the stored audit record contains at least the AU-3 required elements (event description, time stamp, source/destination/locations where relevant, user/system/process identifiers, success/failure, and filenames/target resources where relevant).
- Log-collection validation clearly rejects or quarantines events missing required AU-3 fields, with measurable counts and remediation tickets.
- Time stamps are consistent within the agreed drift window, and clock-difference issues are flagged.
- Privacy controls are applied to known fields that may contain personal data, with evidence that redaction/tokenisation is active and does not break event linking (for example, stable identifiers remain consistent).

###### Actions Required for Compliance

- [ ] Create a standard (canonical) AU-3 audit event schema that maps application and security information and event management (SIEM) fields to: event description, date and time, location, source and destination, who carried out the action (actor identifiers), the result (outcome), and any file names or other target resources.
- [ ] Set up checks during data intake into the security information and event management (SIEM) and log system to ensure each event includes the required AU-3 fields, and that each field follows the correct format and appears the correct number of times for its event type.
- [ ] Set up a quarantine location for audit events that do not meet requirements, and create a fix (remediation) ticket process that is sent to the application or log-source owner.
- [ ] Enable identity enrichment so that actor user and process identifiers are matched to the organisation’s official identities, and service accounts are represented consistently.
- [ ] Ensure system time is accurate by synchronising clocks using the Network Time Protocol (NTP) or Precision Time Protocol (PTP), and add checks during data intake for clock differences (clock skew) with alerts.
- [ ] Use privacy-protecting redaction and tokenisation for audit fields that contain personal data, while keeping the ability to link related records (for example, using stable hashes), and document the rules.

#### AU-3.1 — Additional Audit Information (Enhancement)

This requirement ensures the system’s event records do more than confirm that something happened. They must also include the key information needed to understand who did it and which decision rules the system applied. Without this, investigations after an incident can be delayed because you cannot reliably trace what was allowed or blocked, how the system handled the request, or which real person used a shared group account. This increases the risk that wrongdoing goes unnoticed and that innocent people are wrongly blamed.

To meet this expectation, the organisation must configure the system so that, for each relevant event, every audit record includes:
- the identifiers of the access decision rules used,
- the identifiers of the handling rules used, and
- the individual identity of the person acting through any group account.

The organisation must document these requirements in its audit and accountability policy and supporting procedures. It must also ensure the system design and configuration are set up to produce these fields, maintain a list of which events are covered, and ensure authorised staff can review the records to confirm the required information is present, with privacy considerations addressed in the privacy plan.

##### Audit enrichment with access/flow rule IDs and group-user identity

- Category: Software
- Priority: Critical

###### WHAT must be done
Set up the application and its enforcement components so that the generated audit records include the organisation-defined **additional information**: **(1) the access control rule identifiers used, (2) the flow control rule identifiers used, and (3) the individual identity of users acting through group accounts**.

###### WHY (control requirement)
NIST AU-3(1) (Enhancement) requires audit records to include the additional information defined by **param: au-03.01_odp**. The guidance also requires limiting added audit details to only what is explicitly needed, to avoid misleading information, make relevant information harder to find, and reduce privacy risk.

###### HOW (specific steps/approach)
1. **Define a standard audit format (schema)** for the three required fields (for example, `access_rule_id`, `flow_rule_id`, `group_user_identity`) and make sure they are filled in for every relevant event.
2. **Record rule identifiers at the decision point**: update and standardise the access-control and flow-control enforcement layers (for example, application programming interface (API) gateway, web application firewall (WAF), reverse proxy, zero trust network access (ZTNA), application authorisation middleware, network policy controller) so that when a decision is made, the audit event includes the **exact rule identifier(s) used** (not only whether the request was allowed or blocked).
3. **Ensure group-account actions are tied to an individual**: connect to the organisation’s identity provider so the audit event records the **individual principal** acting through any group account (for example, a session-linked identity, a workload identity mapping, or a step-up identity). Do not record only the group identifier.
4. **Apply a privacy and need-to-know filter**: add only the explicitly required fields, plus the minimum non-sensitive context already needed for audit usefulness (for example, timestamp, event type, and target resource identifiers already present). Do not add free-text, sensitive content, or unnecessary attributes.
5. **Verify end to end**: create automated tests (or scripted integration tests) that trigger representative allow/deny and flow-control decisions, including cases where group accounts are used, and confirm the resulting audit records contain the three required fields with the correct values.
6. **Make evidence operational**: ensure the logging configuration is version-controlled, and that security information and event management (SIEM) or log collector mappings keep these fields without renaming, truncating, or suppressing them.

###### WHO (role responsible)
- **Application Security Engineer / Platform Engineer**: implement audit record enrichment and decision logging in the enforcement layers.
- **Identity and Access Management (IAM) Engineer**: ensure actions using group accounts are linked to individual identities.
- **Security Operations / SIEM Engineer**: confirm log ingestion and field preservation into the SIEM.
- **Privacy Officer (or delegated privacy lead)**: approve the privacy/need-to-know filter for audit enrichment.

###### Acceptance criteria
- For all relevant events, audit records include non-empty values for:
  - `access_rule_id` (identifier of the access control rule used)
  - `flow_rule_id` (identifier of the flow control rule used)
  - `group_user_identity` (individual identity of the person acting through a group account)
- Audit enrichment is limited to the explicitly required additional information (plus minimal existing audit context) and does not include sensitive free-text payloads.
- Automated tests show correct population of fields for both allow/deny and flow-control decision paths, including group-account usage.
- SIEM/log collector pipelines preserve the fields end to end (no truncation or renaming that would reduce audit usefulness).

###### Actions Required for Compliance

- [ ] Create an audit event format (schema) that includes `access_rule_id`, `flow_rule_id`, and `group_user_identity`, and map it to au-03.01_odp.
- [ ] Update the access-control enforcement components so that, at the moment a decision is made, they record the identifier of the access rule that was used in the audit event.
- [ ] Update the flow-control enforcement components so that, at the moment a decision is made, they record the identifier of the flow rule that was used in the audit event.
- [ ] Integrate audit enrichment with enterprise identity and access management (IAM) so that actions involving groups and accounts are recorded under the individual user’s identity (not only the group).
- [ ] Set up a privacy and “need to know” filter so enrichment is limited to only the specific fields that are explicitly required, plus the minimum existing audit information.
- [ ] Set up automated integration tests that create allow/deny and traffic-control events (including group account scenarios) and check that the three required fields are present and correct.
- [ ] Check that security information and event management (SIEM) and log collector ingestion keeps all three fields unchanged from start to finish (no truncation, suppression, or renaming).

#### AU-3.3 — Limit Personally Identifiable Information Elements (Enhancement)

This requirement is about making sure audit logs do not accidentally store too much personal information about people. Audit records are used to show what happened in a system. However, if they include unnecessary personal details, that extra information could be misused, exposed, or cause privacy harm—even if the original purpose was only to record events.

To meet this expectation, the organisation must ensure that audit records include only the specific items identified in its privacy risk assessment: a pseudonymised user identifier (so it cannot directly identify the person), the source Internet Protocol (IP) address, the event date and time, the system identifier, and the access result.

The organisation should base what goes into audit records on the results of the privacy risk assessment, set clear procedures for what is allowed, and be able to demonstrate that no other personal information is recorded in audit logs.

##### Enforce audit log PII allowlist with pseudonymised user identifier

- Category: Software
- Priority: Critical

###### WHAT
Set up an auditable enforcement mechanism that limits personally identifiable information (PII) in audit records to only the elements agreed in the privacy risk assessment: **user identifier (pseudonymised), source Internet Protocol (IP) address, event date and time, system identifier, access outcome**.

###### WHY (control requirement)
NIST SP 800-53 **AU-3(3)** (enhancement) requires that audit records include only the PII elements identified in the privacy risk assessment (parameter **au-03.03_odp: elements**). Reducing unnecessary PII in audit records lowers privacy risk.

###### HOW (specific steps/approach)
1. **Create an “Audit Record PII Allowlist”** based directly on the privacy risk assessment, and map each allowed element to the exact log field names produced by each component (application, operating system, middleware, network/security devices, and any operational technology (OT) gateway/historian if applicable).
2. **Pseudonymise the user identifier** in the log creation process (for example, one-way tokenisation or hashing using a secret stored in a secrets manager). Make sure the pseudonymised value is consistent enough to link related events, but cannot be used to identify the person directly.
3. **Limit log content to the allowlist** at the source where possible (turn off or omit fields such as raw usernames, email addresses, free-text messages that may include personal data, and user-agent strings if they can contain identifiers). If you cannot control the source, use a **controlled transformation/normalisation step** that removes any disallowed fields before events are written to the audit store/security information and event management (SIEM).
4. **Apply the configuration consistently using policy-as-code** (for example, infrastructure-as-code templates and logging policy modules) so that production, testing, and disaster recovery environments cannot add extra PII fields without review.
5. **Add automated checks** to confirm that every audit event matches the allowlist (only the five permitted PII elements are present; no other fields that contain PII). Stop builds/deployments if the checks find disallowed fields.
6. **Produce audit-ready evidence** by periodically sampling audit records and generating a report that shows field-by-field compliance with the allowlist, including confirmation that the user identifier is pseudonymised.

###### WHO (role responsible)
- **Application Logging Owner / Engineering Lead**: updates the logging structure and implements pseudonymisation in the application/logging process.
- **Security Engineering / Privacy Officer (or delegated privacy role)**: approves the allowlist derived from the privacy risk assessment and authorises changes.
- **Platform/Infrastructure Team**: implements policy-as-code enforcement and the transformation step where needed.
- **SIEM/Monitoring Administrator**: ensures the audit store ingestion process removes/normalises disallowed fields and supports compliance reporting.

###### Acceptance criteria
- Audit records contain **only** the five permitted PII elements: **pseudonymised user identifier, source IP address, event date and time, system identifier, access outcome**.
- The user identifier in audit records is **pseudonymised** (not directly identifying) and generated using the approved method.
- Automated conformance checks show that **no additional PII elements** appear in sampled audit events across representative systems and environments.
- Evidence is available (reports and sample extracts) demonstrating allowlist compliance and that the enforcement is working effectively.

###### Actions Required for Compliance

- [ ] Identify and document the exact personal data (PII) items that are allowed for audit purposes, based on the privacy risk assessment (au-03.03_odp: elements). Then map each allowed item to the specific log field names used in each system.
- [ ] Set up pseudonymisation for the user identifier in the audit logging process by replacing it with a one-way token (hash) generated using a securely managed secret.
- [ ] Set up logging for the application, operating system, and middleware to record only the approved (allowlisted) information. Turn off or omit common fields that may contain personal information and any free-text entries that could include personal data.
- [ ] Set up a controlled process to transform and standardise logs (or a security information and event management (SIEM) intake rule) so that, before events are saved, any fields not on the approved allowlist are removed.
- [ ] Set up automated policy checks (policy-as-code) to enforce approved logging fields (allowlisting) across all environments, and stop deployments that add any fields that are not allowed.
- [ ] Run automated checks on the audit records your system produces, and regularly review a sample of stored audit records to provide evidence that you are complying with requirements.

### AU-4 — Audit Log Storage Capacity (Control)

This requirement is about making sure your organisation has enough storage space to keep a complete record of important system activity for long enough. If audit logs run out of space, records may be deleted early, or logging may be reduced or stopped. This makes it harder to investigate incidents, confirm what happened, and meet legal or contractual obligations—especially when some records are more sensitive.

The organisation must plan and document how audit log storage will be managed, so that all audit records are kept for at least 365 days, including audit logs that contain Confidential and Secret information. It must also configure each relevant system so its audit records are stored for the full 365 days in practice, not just on paper, and keep clear evidence of the storage and retention settings used.

Finally, it must have practical procedures to monitor storage capacity and take timely action if capacity is at risk, so audit logging capability is never lost due to storage limits.

##### Provision audit log storage for 365 days (incl. Confidential/Secret)

- Category: Manual
- Priority: Critical

###### WHAT
Set aside and prepare enough storage for audit logs so they are kept for **at least 365 days** for **all types of audit logs**, including audit logs that contain **Confidential** and **Secret** information.

###### WHY (control requirement)
AU-4 requires the organisation to **set aside enough audit log storage capacity** to meet the organisation’s **audit log retention requirements** (parameter **au-04_odp**). The guidance explains that having enough capacity lowers the risk of reaching storage limits, which can otherwise cause **audit logging capability to be lost or reduced**.

###### HOW (specific approach)
1. **Set the capacity target**: set the audit log storage capacity/retention target to **365 days minimum** for all audit logs, explicitly including **Confidential and Secret** audit events (as per **au-04_odp: 365 days for all audit logs, including Confidential and Secret data**).
2. **Size the storage using real usage**: work out the required storage based on recent audit event volume (events per day), average event size, indexing/overhead, compression, and any replication/retention overhead (if applicable), plus expected growth. Add operational headroom (for example, a growth buffer) so storage limits are not exceeded.
3. **Apply retention rules in the logging process**: configure the **security information and event management (SIEM)** / logging platform and any log routing or forwarding components so logs cannot be cut off before 365 days. Make sure **Confidential/Secret** audit events are sent to the same retention tier or a longer-retention tier.
4. **Include every system that produces audit logs**: confirm that every system/component that creates audit records (including applications, identity systems, network activity, admin actions, and any operational technology (OT) or edge gateways if present) forwards/stores audit logs for 365 days—either centrally or using local buffering/retention when forwarding is not practical.

- The configured logging/SIEM retention settings and routing rules show **no truncation or deletion before 365 days** for any audit log tier.

- Storage capacity monitoring is in place with alerts and runbooks, and evidence shows alerts trigger before capacity runs out.

#### AU-4.1 — Transfer to Alternate Storage (Enhancement)

This requirement is about making sure records of important system activity are not kept only on the same computer that created them. If audit logs are not moved promptly to a separate location, you risk losing evidence if storage becomes full, if a system fails, or if someone tries to alter the records. This can make investigations and compliance checks much harder.

To meet this expectation, the organisation must move each set of audit logs to a different system, part of a system, or storage media within 24 hours of the logs being created, using a destination that is clearly separate from the source. The organisation must also be able to prove that the move took place by keeping records of the transfer, and must ensure the transferred logs are actually available in the new storage location.

##### Off-load audit logs to alternate storage within 24 hours

- Category: Software
- Priority: Critical

###### WHAT
Set up an automated process to move audit logs from the system or component that creates them to a different system, component, or storage location, **within 24 hours of the audit log being created**.

###### WHY (control requirement)
NIST SP 800-53 **AU-4(1)** requires that **audit logs are sent to a different system, system component, or storage location** than the one that generated them, at the organisation-defined **frequency** (here: **within 24 hours of audit log generation**).

###### HOW (specific steps/approach)
1. **Define the transfer boundary and destination**: list every audit log source (application/service/host) and set up a **separate** destination for off-loading (for example, a dedicated log server, a separate security information and event management (SIEM) intake endpoint, or a separate object storage bucket with different access controls). Make sure the destination is **not** the same system/component/storage location as the source.
2. **Use store-and-forward with measured transfer time**: send audit events to an off-load service that saves them immediately into a **durable queue/buffer**, then writes them to the alternate destination **later**. Configure retry and back-off behaviour and queue size so that, even when things run slower than normal, the **total time from creation to arrival** stays within **24 hours**.
3. **Create transfer records (audit evidence)**: for each moved batch/segment, record transfer details (source identifier, destination identifier, batch ID, first and last event times, transfer start and end times, status, and any error information). Store these records so they can be reviewed during audits.
4. **Confirm the logs arrived in the alternate storage**: after writing, run automated checks (for example, confirm the destination acknowledged the write, and periodically reconcile that batch IDs exist and can be retrieved from the alternate destination).
5. **Secure the transfer path**: require mutual Transport Layer Security (TLS), use least-privilege service identities, and restrict network access so only the off-load service can write to the alternate destination.
6. **Alert if the 24-hour target is at risk**: implement monitoring that calculates the “age of the oldest audit event not yet transferred” for each source and raises alerts when it approaches the **24-hour** limit or when transfers fail.

###### WHO
- **Application/Platform Engineering Lead**: responsible for building the off-load process and connecting it to the destination.
- **Security Engineering (SIEM/Logging)**: defines the destination, integrity and retention expectations, and monitoring/alerting.
- **Operations/DevOps**: ensures queue sizing, runbooks, and operational monitoring are in place.

###### Acceptance criteria
- For each audit log source, the organisation can show that **all audit events are transferred to the alternate destination within 24 hours** of being created (confirmed using transfer records and reconciliation).
- The alternate destination is clearly **different** from the logging source (different system/component/storage location).
- Transfer records exist for transferred batches, including success/failure and timestamps.
- Automated checks confirm transferred logs are present and can be retrieved from the alternate destination.
- Monitoring/alerting triggers when transfer delays approach or exceed 24 hours, and transfer failures are visible for investigation.

###### Actions Required for Compliance

- [ ] Identify every source of audit logs, and for each one set a single backup destination on a different system, component, or storage media.
- [ ] Set up a store-and-forward off-load service that uses reliable queued messages and automatic retry, so transfers happen at least every 24 hours.
- [ ] Set up audit log forwarding so that the time taken to transfer each event can be measured from when it is created through to when it is written to the alternate storage.
- [ ] For each batch or segment, create and store transfer records that include the source and destination identifiers, the time of the transfer, the current status, and any errors.
- [ ] Add automated checks to confirm that each transferred batch has arrived at the alternate destination and can be retrieved there.
- [ ] Set up mutual Transport Layer Security (mutual TLS), use service identities with the minimum permissions needed (least privilege), and restrict network access for the data transfer route.
- [ ] Set up monitoring and alerts when the “age of the oldest event that has not yet been transferred” is approaching or has reached 24 hours, and when event transfers fail.

### AU-5 — Response to Audit Logging Process Failures (Control)

This requirement ensures that if the system that records important activity stops working properly, the right people are informed right away and the organisation can still understand what happened. Without this, you could lose or overwrite the very records needed to investigate issues, confirm what occurred, and respond quickly to wrongdoing or mistakes—especially if the storage for the records becomes full or the recording process fails.

Within 15 minutes of noticing an audit logging process failure, the organisation must alert the Security Operations Centre on-call lead, the system owner, and the incident response lead. It must then notify the Security Operations Centre and the incident response lead, preserve relevant evidence so it cannot be lost, and assess what impact the failure may have had.

The organisation should only stop or slow down audit logging when it is necessary to prevent further loss of audit records.

##### AU-5 automated audit logging failure escalation with evidence capture

- Category: Software
- Priority: Critical

###### WHAT
Set up automated detection and response for **audit logging process failures** (for example, problems capturing or sending audit logs, audit system health issues, and audit log storage getting close to or over capacity). If a failure is detected, the system must **alert the designated roles within 15 minutes**, and then take **additional actions**. These actions include **preserving evidence**, **checking the likely impact**, and **pausing or slowing audit logging only when necessary to prevent further loss of audit records**.

###### WHY (control requirement)
AU-5 requires the organisation to respond to audit logging process failures by taking actions defined by the organisation, including:
- **Alerting the right people/roles** (as defined by the organisation) **within 15 minutes** of detecting the failure.
- Taking **additional actions** (as defined by the organisation), such as notifying the security operations centre (SOC) or incident response team, **preserving evidence**, and **checking the likely impact**.
- Taking **pause or slowdown actions only when necessary** to prevent further loss of audit records.

###### HOW (specific steps/approach)
###### 1) Define what counts as a failure and how to map it to the right area
- Create a mapping between each **audit log repository** (a distinct component where audit logs are stored) and the data sources that show:
  - audit log capture or forwarding failures (collector or agent errors, ingestion errors, pipeline error codes)
  - audit system health failures (audit service down or not healthy)
  - storage capacity risk (approaching, at, or over a set threshold)
- Make sure the response can apply actions at the correct level: **repository**, **system**, and/or the **total organisation capacity**, based on how your organisation defines the scope.

###### 2) Implement an automated detection-to-escalation process (time to first notification)
- Set up monitoring and alert rules so they trigger immediately for any AU-5 failure signal.
- Use an automated response workflow (security orchestration, automation and response (SOAR)-style) that:
  - creates an incident or ticket
  - sends the **first notifications** to the roles defined by the organisation:
    - **SOC on-call lead**
    - **information system owner**
    - **incident response lead**
  - meets the **15-minute** requirement by escalating to an alternative contact method (for example, phone/text/voice escalation) if acknowledgement is not received quickly.

###### 3) Automatically preserve evidence (restricted and reviewable)
- When triggered, automatically collect and store in a restricted evidence location:
  - failure indicators (error logs, error codes, health check results)
  - relevant pipeline measures (ingestion delay, queue or backlog depth, number of dropped items)
  - snapshots of relevant settings (audit logging configuration, collector or forwarder configuration, security information and event management (SIEM) or log storage routing configuration)
  - the state of the system/audit service at the time of detection
- Ensure the evidence is time-stamped and linked to the incident record.

###### 4) Prompt for impact assessment and require it before closing the incident
- Require structured information in the incident workflow for:
  - affected repository or system
  - start time and estimated duration
  - completeness risk (for example, gaps, delays, or drop rate)
  - affected event types (if they can be determined)
- Send the incident to the incident response lead to complete the assessment.

###### 5) Pause or slow down only when appropriate, with clear reasons and safeguards
- Put safeguards in place so the workflow can only **pause or slow audit logging** when storage-risk conditions are met (for example, repository at or over capacity, or an imminent risk of losing audit records).
- Require a clear written justification, and record the decision rationale, scope, and time window.
- Default behaviour: notify, preserve evidence, and assess impact; do not pause or slow down unless the safeguard conditions are met.

###### WHO
- **SOC on-call lead**: receives the initial alert within 15 minutes and coordinates initial triage.
- **Information system owner**: receives the initial alert and confirms the system and audit system context.
- **Incident response lead**: receives the initial alert and is responsible for the impact assessment and response decisions.
- **Platform or observability engineering (implementation owner)**: implements the detection rules, the automated response workflow, evidence capture, and the safeguards for pause or slowdown actions.

###### Acceptance criteria
- For each detected audit logging process failure, the workflow sends the **first notification** to **SOC on-call lead, information system owner, and incident response lead** within **15 minutes**.
- Evidence collected includes failure indicators, relevant measures, and configuration or system state, stored in a restricted location and linked to the incident.
- The incident workflow requires completion of an **impact assessment** by the incident response lead.
- The workflow performs **pause or slowdown actions only** when the storage-risk safeguards are met, and records the justification, scope, and timestamps.
- For storage-related failures, the workflow applies actions to the correct **audit log repository/system/total capacity scope** as defined by the organisation.

###### Actions Required for Compliance

- [ ] Define the signs that audit logging has failed, and link each sign to the correct audit log repository or system and to the relevant total capacity scope.
- [ ] Set up monitoring alerts that trigger immediately if audit records fail to be captured or forwarded, or if storage capacity reaches defined limits.
- [ ] Set up an alert-handling workflow that sends the first alerts to the security operations centre (SOC) on-call lead, the information system owner, and the incident response lead within 15 minutes.
- [ ] Automate the collection of restricted evidence (such as failure signs, performance measures, configuration snapshots, and the audit and system status) and link it to the incident.
- [ ] Add an impact assessment form with required fields, and route the completed form to the incident response lead.
- [ ] Set policy “guardrails” so that stop or throttling actions happen only when the agreed storage-risk conditions are met, and record the reason, the scope, and the date and time.

#### AU-5.1 — Storage Capacity Warning (Enhancement)

This requirement is about making sure the people responsible for storing audit logs are warned early when the space for keeping them is running out. If the storage fills up without warning, important records of system activity may stop being saved or may be lost. This makes it harder to investigate problems, identify misuse, or prove what happened when something goes wrong.

To meet this expectation, the organisation must set up monitoring for every audit log storage location, even if they are managed in different parts of the business. It must trigger an alert when each location reaches 80% of its maximum allocated storage.

When a location reaches 80%, the organisation must send a warning within 1 hour to both:
- the audit log repository administrator, and
- the information system owner for that repository.

This must be done for the correct repository and must not be sent to only one person.

The organisation should also be able to demonstrate the warning process, the named recipients, and evidence that warnings were sent on time.

##### Per-repository 80% audit log capacity warnings to named owners

- Category: Software
- Priority: Critical

###### WHAT
Set up an automated warning for each audit log storage repository. The warning should trigger when that repository’s used audit log storage reaches **80%** of its **maximum** allowed audit log storage capacity, and it should be sent to **the audit log repository administrator** and **the information system owner**.

###### WHY (control requirement)
AU-5(1) enhancement requires: “Provide a warning to {{personnel/roles/locations}} within {{time period}} when allocated audit log storage volume reaches {{percentage}} of repository maximum audit log storage capacity.”

This solution directly satisfies:
- **Recipients**: audit log repository administrator and information system owner (AU-5(1) param au-05.01_odp.01)
- **Timing**: warning delivered **within 1 hour** (au-05.01_odp.02)
- **Threshold**: trigger at **80%** (au-05.01_odp.03)
- **Multiple repositories**: each repository is monitored on its own (control guidance)

###### HOW (specific steps/approach)
1. **Create and keep up to date an official repository list** that maps each audit log storage repository to:
   - repository identifier
   - maximum allocated capacity
   - the two recipients: audit log repository administrator and information system owner (from the organisation’s responsibility and ownership register)
2. **Make sure the audit log storage platform provides the correct capacity information for each repository**, such as:
   - “allocated audit log storage used” (current usage)
   - “maximum allocated audit log storage capacity” (maximum)
   Ensure these figures are labelled with the repository identifier so alerts cannot be sent to the wrong repository.
3. **Set up alert rules for each repository** so they trigger when usage is **>= 80%** (au-05.01_odp.03). Use a “first time only” approach (de-duplication) so the same warning is not repeatedly sent, while still ensuring the initial warning is sent.
4. **Set up an automated notification process** so that when an alert triggers, it sends the warning to **both** recipients (AU-5(1) param au-05.01_odp.01) **within 60 minutes** of the threshold being reached. The message must include:
   - repository identifier
   - usage percentage, maximum capacity, and current date/time
   - threshold value (80%)
   - a link/reference to the repository monitoring view
5. **Demonstrate timing and traceability** by keeping records for every warning event:
   - alert trigger date/time
   - notification sent date/time
   - list of recipients (both parties)
   - repository identifier
   Store this evidence in an auditable system (for example, a ticket/IT service management record, or an audit log system history with tamper-resistant retention).
6. **Test using a controlled simulation** in a non-production environment (or a test repository) to confirm:
   - the repository-to-recipient mapping is correct
   - the 80% threshold behaviour is correct
   - notifications are sent to both recipients
   - the worst-case end-to-end delay is **under 60 minutes**

###### WHO
- **Security/Compliance engineer**: maps the control, defines what evidence is needed, and agrees acceptance criteria.
- **Security information and event management (SIEM)/logging platform engineer (or site reliability engineering (SRE)/platform team)**: provides the capacity metrics, sets up alert rules, and builds the notification workflow.
- **IT service management (ITSM)/Operations team**: ensures the notification method and evidence retention meet audit requirements.

###### Acceptance criteria
- For every configured audit log storage repository, an alert triggers at **80%** allocated usage.
- When a repository reaches the threshold, warnings are sent to **both** the audit log repository administrator and the information system owner.
- For tested scenarios, the time from threshold breach to notification being sent is **<= 60 minutes**.
- For each warning event, auditable evidence exists showing the repository identifier, recipients, and timestamps.
- Alerts are specific to each repository (no incorrect cross-repository sending).

###### Actions Required for Compliance

- [ ] Create an official list of every audit log storage repository, including each repository’s maximum storage capacity, and name the two required recipients: the repository administrator and the information system owner.
- [ ] Set up the audit log storage system to report, for each repository, how much storage it has been allocated to use and the maximum storage capacity it is allowed to use.
- [ ] Set alert rules for each repository so they trigger when used capacity reaches 80% or more of the maximum, and use de-duplication to avoid repeated alerts that could overwhelm people.
- [ ] Set up an automated process that sends the warning to both recipients and includes the repository ID, the usage percentage, the threshold, and the relevant dates and times.
- [ ] Configure alert checking and message sending so that alerts are delivered end-to-end within 60 minutes of reaching an 80% breach level.
- [ ] For every warning event, record unchangeable evidence (alert time, send time, recipients, and repository ID) and keep it for audit purposes.
- [ ] Run a controlled test to confirm that the repository is mapped correctly, that the alert threshold triggers as expected, that messages are sent to both recipients, and that delivery happens within 60 minutes or less.

#### AU-5.2 — Real-time Alerts (Enhancement)

This requirement is about making sure you are warned immediately if your system’s record-keeping stops working properly, so problems are spotted and dealt with quickly. If alerts are delayed or do not arrive when audit records cannot be created, when the storage location for those records runs out of space, or when the records cannot be sent to the correct destination, you risk losing evidence of what happened. This can delay investigations and may also mean you do not meet legal or regulatory obligations.

To meet this requirement, the organisation must automatically send a real-time alert within 5 seconds of detecting any of these three types of audit logging failure to both the Security Operations Centre (SOC) on-call incident responder and the system owner. The alert must always reach the correct people, clearly say which type of failure occurred, and still be sent promptly even if multiple failures happen close together.

##### Real-time alerts for audit logging failures (≤5s) to SOC + owner

- Category: Software
- Priority: Critical

###### WHAT
Set up an automated, event-based alert system that sends a real-time alert **within 5 seconds** to **(1) the Security Operations Centre (SOC) on-call incident responder** and **(2) the system owner** whenever any of these audit failures happen:
- audit log generation failures
- audit log storage capacity exhaustion
- audit log transmission failures

###### WHY (control requirement)
The National Institute of Standards and Technology (NIST) AU-5(2) enhancement requires: “Provide an alert within {{ param, au-05.02_odp.01 }} to {{ param, au-05.02_odp.02 }} when the following audit failure events occur: {{ param, au-05.02_odp.03 }}.” The guidance also requires real-time delivery (within seconds or less).

###### HOW (specific steps/approach)
###### 1) Define and classify the three failure types at the point of detection
Update the audit logging process and/or the audit agent/forwarder so it detects and reports a structured “audit failure event” for each category:
- **Generation failure**: the system cannot create or write audit records (for example, audit service errors, audit record write failures).
- **Storage capacity exhaustion**: the audit buffer/queue/disk/retention area is almost full, or cannot accept more audit records.
- **Transmission failure**: the system cannot send audit logs to the configured destination (for example, forwarder errors, receiver not reachable, Transport Layer Security (TLS)/session failures).

Each reported event must include:
- failure type (generation/storage/transmission)
- the affected component/system identifier
- the time the problem was detected (monotonic/UTC) and a correlation ID
- severity (mapped to the alerting process)

###### 2) Trigger immediate alerting with a hard time limit (≤5 seconds)
Create an alert workflow that starts directly from the reported audit failure event (no grouping or batching) and guarantees an end-to-end time of **≤5 seconds** from detection to alert delivery.
- Configure the alerting integration in “immediate” mode for these specific failure types.
- Do not suppress these events. Use **idempotency based on the correlation ID** so that repeated failures still create separate alerts when they are different events.

###### 3) Send the alert to both required recipients using dependable delivery methods
For each failure event, send the alert to:
- **Security Operations Centre (SOC) on-call incident responder** (for example, paging/incident escalation channel)
- **System owner** (for example, authenticated messaging plus ticket/Short Message Service (SMS)/voice fallback if your organisation uses it)

The alert message must clearly state the failure type and include the correlation ID and detection time so responders can triage quickly.

###### 4) Keep evidence of timing and delivery
For each alert instance, record the following in an audit trail separate from the failing audit pipeline:
- correlation ID
- detection timestamp
- alert creation timestamp
- delivery timestamps for each recipient channel
- delivery result (success/failure) and any retry attempts

###### 5) Validate with fault-injection tests
In a staging environment that matches production settings, simulate each failure type and verify:
- the alert is received by **both** recipients
- the time from trigger to receipt is **≤5 seconds**
- multiple failures occurring close together still produce separate alerts

###### WHO
- **Security Operations Centre (SOC) on-call incident responder**: receives and acknowledges alerts; helps validate the alert content.
- **System owner**: receives alerts and ensures responsibility for remediation is taken.
- **Application/Platform engineering (audit pipeline owners)**: implements detection, event reporting, and the alert workflow.
- **Security engineering / Security Information and Event Management (SIEM) and Security Orchestration, Automation and Response (SOAR) administrators**: sets up routing, delivery behaviour, and evidence logging.

###### Acceptance criteria
1. For each of the three failure types (generation, storage exhaustion, transmission), an alert is delivered to **both** the Security Operations Centre (SOC) on-call incident responder and the system owner **within 5 seconds** of detection.
2. Alert content includes: failure type, affected system/component, correlation ID, and detection timestamp.
3. Rapid successive failures result in separate alerts (no suppression for these failure types), while idempotency prevents duplicate alerts for the same correlation ID.
4. Evidence is available showing detection-to-delivery timing for each recipient channel for every test case.
5. Fault-injection tests in a staging environment using production-like routing and recipient configuration demonstrate compliance.

###### Actions Required for Compliance

- [ ] Set up the audit logging process to detect and record structured events when there are failures in generating logs, running out of storage space, or sending the logs.
- [ ] Set up an immediate alert process (no batching) that triggers every time an audit failure event is recorded, and ensures the alert is delivered within an end-to-end time budget of 5 seconds.
- [ ] Set up alert routing so notifications are sent to both the security operations centre (SOC) on-call incident responder and the system owner, using dependable paging and messaging methods.

- [ ] Run fault-injection tests in the staging environment for all three failure types, and confirm that the time from trigger to receipt is 5 seconds or less for both recipients.

#### AU-5.3 — Configurable Traffic Volume Thresholds (Enhancement)

This requirement is about preventing your system from being overloaded by too much network activity when that would cause your audit records (records of activity) to run out of storage. If the system keeps trying to save audit records while the log storage is full, you could lose the ability to prove what happened, investigate issues, or meet legal and internal responsibilities.

To meet this expectation, the organisation must first work out the maximum amount of audit-record storage it can safely use. It must then set adjustable limits on network traffic volume based on that capacity, and clearly document how those limits were calculated. During processing, the system must monitor network traffic volume. If it exceeds the agreed limits, it must follow the agreed response behaviour. In this case, the organisation’s chosen option is to delay processing, so processing must be delayed to prevent audit logging from exhausting storage.

The organisation must also record the threshold settings and the chosen delay or reject option, ensure this approach is included in its plans and design documents, provide procedures for audit processing failures, and keep audit records that show when traffic exceeded the limits and when the system delayed processing.

##### Configurable network traffic thresholds with delay on audit-log pressure

- Category: Software
- Priority: Critical

###### WHAT
Set up a control that continuously measures network communication traffic volume and compares it to **configurable thresholds** based on the amount of space available for storing audit logs. If the traffic volume goes above the configured threshold, the system must apply the organisation’s chosen response behaviour for this control: **delay**.

###### WHY (control requirement)
NIST AU-5(3) (enhancement) requires the organisation to **enforce configurable network communication traffic volume thresholds** that reflect limits on **audit log storage capacity**. It also requires the organisation to **reject or delay processing** of network communication traffic when audit logging information would exceed the storage capacity. The delay/reject action must be triggered by the established thresholds and must be adjustable as audit log storage capacity changes.

###### HOW (specific steps/approach)
1. **Convert audit-log capacity into a traffic “audit-pressure” budget**: work out the maximum sustainable audit logging throughput (for example, bytes per second and/or events per second) based on audit log storage capacity, how long logs are kept, compression, and the largest expected audit event size.
2. **Convert the budget into traffic volume thresholds**: translate measurable network traffic indicators at the point traffic enters the system (for example, new connections per second, requests per second, bytes per second, or session rate from IPFIX/NetFlow) into the expected audit log generation rate using monitoring data and load testing. Create at least two thresholds: a main threshold that triggers the exceed response, and optionally a lower warning threshold for operational visibility.
3. **Enforce the thresholds at network entry**: configure an edge enforcement component (for example, an application programming interface (API) gateway, web application firewall (WAF)/next-generation firewall (NGFW), traffic manager, or service mesh entry point) to:
   - calculate current traffic volume over a defined measurement window,
   - compare it to the **active** thresholds,
   - if the threshold is exceeded, apply the configured **delay** action (for example, queue/hold with a bounded wait time, or slow down request acceptance) rather than allowing unlimited processing.
4. **Allow threshold updates without weakening enforcement**: store the threshold values and the exceed action in a central configuration service (or a version-controlled policy) and ensure the enforcement layer can reload/update thresholds when changes are made.
5. **Keep evidence of when thresholds are exceeded and what action was taken**: ensure the enforcement component produces security-relevant logs/events showing (a) the threshold value, (b) the measured traffic volume, (c) the time the threshold was exceeded, and (d) that **delay** was applied. Ensure these events are sent to the organisation’s audit logging pipeline.
6. **Update procedures for audit-processing problems**: update operational runbooks so that if audit logging is degraded or unavailable while delay is active, staff know how to respond (for example, fail-safe behaviour, escalation, and confirmation that audit evidence is still being captured as required).

###### WHO
- **Security Architect / Compliance Lead**: approves the method for converting capacity to thresholds and the acceptance criteria.
- **Network/Security Engineering (Edge Platform Owner)**: implements and operates the network entry enforcement configuration.
- **Application Owner / Site Reliability Engineering (SRE)**: validates the telemetry-to-threshold mapping, measurement windows, and runbook updates.
- **Security Operations Centre (SOC)/security information and event management (SIEM) Operations**: ensures exceedance events are correctly collected and retained.

###### Acceptance criteria
- The system enforces **configurable** network traffic volume thresholds derived from audit log storage capacity.
- When measured traffic exceeds the configured threshold, the system applies the organisation-defined response **delay** (not rejection) in a bounded, predictable way.
- Thresholds can be updated when audit log storage capacity changes, and the enforcement uses the **currently active** configuration.
- Audit evidence exists showing when the threshold was exceeded and that **delay** was applied (including timestamps and measured values).
- Operational procedures/runbooks cover how to handle audit-processing failures related to this enforcement mechanism.

###### Actions Required for Compliance

- [ ] Calculate the maximum sustainable rate at which audit logs can be written, based on audit-log storage capacity, how long logs are kept (retention), how much they are compressed, and the largest expected audit event size in the worst case.
- [ ] Define measurable indicators for incoming network traffic and set traffic-volume thresholds that match the audit log processing capacity budget.
- [ ] Set up the entry-traffic control to check the current traffic volume against the active limits, and when the limit is exceeded, apply the configured action: **delay**.
- [ ] Set up dynamic configuration so that threshold values and the “exceed” action can be updated whenever the audit log storage capacity changes.
- [ ] Set up and check security logs for when traffic goes above a set limit, recording the measured traffic, the limit value, and the time the delay was activated.
- [ ] Update the audit-processing failure procedures to explain how to operate and check that the system is behaving correctly when audit logging is degraded and a delay is triggered.

#### AU-5.4 — Shutdown on Failure (Enhancement)

This requirement is about what the system should do if it cannot record and send important activity information for long enough to matter. If audit logs cannot be created or sent to the organisation’s central record for more than 15 minutes, it becomes harder to spot wrongdoing, investigate incidents, or confirm what happened. This increases the risk of fraud, misuse of data, and weak accountability.

To meet this expectation, the organisation must decide in advance what the system will do in this situation. It must either:
- automatically carry out a partial shutdown, or
- continue running in a reduced, degraded mode with limited mission or business functions (where appropriate).

When the problem lasts for 15 minutes, the system must automatically apply the chosen response, unless there is another logging method that is available and can be used. If such an alternative exists, the system should continue operating normally.

The organisation must document the trigger (the 15-minute logging failure) and the response in its procedures, system design, security plan, and privacy plan where relevant. It must also keep evidence that the system detected the failure and took the required action.

##### Auto partial shutdown/degraded mode on audit log loss >15 mins

- Category: Software
- Priority: Critical

###### WHAT
Set up an automated response that, when **audit logging failures** occur—meaning **audit logs stop being created or cannot be sent to the organisation’s central audit repository for more than 15 minutes**—the system **automatically carries out** the organisation-approved response: **partial system shutdown** or **degraded operating mode with reduced capability**, **unless** an **alternative audit logging capability** exists and can be used.

###### WHY (control requirement)
The NIST Special Publication 800-53 **AU-5(4)** enhancement requires the organisation to **carry out a defined response** when **audit logging failures** occur (as defined by the organisation), and to **avoid the shutdown/degraded action** when an **alternative audit logging capability** is available.

###### HOW (specific steps/approach)
1. **Define the trigger and timing in the system**: implement a health check that continuously confirms whether audit events are (a) being created and (b) successfully sent to the **central audit repository**. Start a timer when either condition fails, and trigger only if the failure continues for **more than 15 minutes**.
2. **Check whether an alternative audit logging capability is usable**: before applying shutdown/degraded actions, confirm that an alternative route is **usable** (for example, a secondary collector/repository can be reached, or local write-ahead buffering is in place with guaranteed later forwarding, and this is confirmed as healthy). The system must not enter the response mode if the alternative capability is confirmed usable.
3. **Automate the response**: when the trigger activates and no alternative capability is usable, automatically carry out the pre-approved response:
   - **Partial system shutdown**: stop or restrict **non-essential** services/components, while keeping the minimum needed to maintain safety and continuity.
   - **Degraded mode**: switch to a reduced set of features, limiting operation to **mission/business-essential** activities only.
4. **Record evidence of detection and action**: create an internal, tamper-evident record (for example, a local event log with integrity protection) that shows: the trigger condition, start and end times, whether alternative logging was usable, and which response was used.
5. **Operationalise with runbooks and configuration**: document the trigger definition, how the response is mapped (what “partial shutdown” and “degraded mode” mean for this application), and the criteria for deciding whether the alternative capability is usable in the system’s operational procedures and security documentation.

###### WHO
- **Application Owner / System Owner**: chooses and approves how “partial shutdown” versus “degraded mode” maps to the application’s services.
- **Security Engineering (Security Information and Event Management (SIEM)/Logging) Team**: implements the audit pipeline health checks, verifies whether alternative logging is usable, and produces the evidence record.
- **Platform/Infrastructure Team**: implements the automated service changes (shutdown/degraded) in the hosting environment (for example, orchestration controls, feature flags, and service scaling/disablement).

###### Acceptance criteria
- If audit log creation or sending to the central repository is unavailable for **more than 15 minutes**, the system automatically enters the configured **partial shutdown** or **degraded mode**.
- If an **alternative audit logging capability** exists and is verified as **usable**, the system **does not** enter shutdown/degraded mode.
- The system produces audit-ready evidence showing: detection of the failure, the **more than 15 minutes** duration, the decision on alternative capability usability, and the response taken.
- The response behaviour is consistent across production and disaster recovery failover scenarios (where applicable) and is validated through controlled fault injection testing.

###### Actions Required for Compliance

- [ ] Set up an audit pipeline health check to detect when audit logs stop being created or fail to reach the central repository, and to measure how long this takes.
- [ ] Set up a timer that waits for more than 15 minutes of continuous failure before triggering an alert, and only starts counting once the failure threshold has been exceeded.
- [ ] Add checks to confirm that the alternative audit logging option works before taking any shutdown or degraded-service action.
- [ ] Automate the selected response (partial shutdown or reduced-function mode) by turning off or limiting non-essential services or features.
- [ ] Create tamper-evident records showing when the trigger started and ended, which alternate capability decision was made, and which response was used.
- [ ] Document, in operational procedures and security documentation, what triggers each action, how the response is chosen, and the criteria for using an alternative capability.

#### AU-5.5 — Alternate Audit Logging Capability (Enhancement)

This requirement is about ensuring your organisation can still keep trustworthy records of important user activity even if its main logging system stops working. If the main system fails, there is a risk that key events—such as people logging in, changes to what they are allowed to do, or access to sensitive secret information—could be missed or lost. This would make it harder to investigate issues, confirm what happened, or detect misuse.

To meet this expectation, the organisation must set up an alternative logging route that automatically takes over when the primary logging failure is detected, without staff having to start it manually. While the failure continues, the system must capture the relevant events and store them for 24 hours in an on-premises, tamper-resistant, write-once record store. This must enforce full 24-hour retention even if the primary logging system starts working again sooner.

The organisation must document: what triggers the switch, what events are covered (which may be only the affected subset), the procedures to follow, the system design, how privacy is handled, and the configuration. It must also assign clear responsibilities so staff can demonstrate the behaviour and provide evidence that it worked.

##### Automatic alternate audit logging to on-prem immutable store (24h)

- Category: Software
- Priority: Critical

###### WHAT
Set up an additional (backup) audit logging capability that automatically turns on if the main audit logging stops working. It will record a defined subset of security-relevant audit events (per **au-05.05_odp**) and store them in an **on-premises immutable** system for **24 hours**.

###### WHY (control requirement)
NIST AU-5(5) requires that, if the main audit logging capability fails, the organisation provides an alternative audit logging capability that supports **au-05.05_odp**. The guidance allows the backup capability to record only a **subset** of the main capability’s audit events that are affected by the failure.

###### HOW (specific approach)
1. **Define the takeover trigger (clear and testable):**
   - Treat the main audit logging as “failed” when one or more conditions occur, such as:
     - no main audit events are received for a defined time period from a source (for example, per application/host/identity provider), or
     - the main log destination/collector shows ongoing errors or timeouts, or
     - the main processing pipeline’s queue grows beyond a set limit and event losses are detected.
   - Implement this as an automated health-check/telemetry rule.

2. **Set up an alternate ingestion route (separate pipeline):**
   - Deploy a second collector/forwarder layer (using separate hosts and, where possible, a separate network route) that can keep receiving the same security events.
   - Configure this alternate route in **standby**, so it can automatically switch to **active capture** when the trigger occurs.

3. **Record only the required event subset (au-05.05_odp):**
   - During takeover, capture and keep security-relevant audit events including (at minimum):
     - **logins**,
     - **privilege changes**, and
     - **access to Secret data**.
   - If the application reports these using different event types or fields, convert them into a standard (common) format and filter to only the documented subset.

4. **Store in an on-premises immutable system with 24-hour retention:**
   - Write events to an on-premises immutable/append-only mechanism (for example, WORM-like object storage, an append-only log service, or tamper-evident ledger-style storage).
   - Set retention so that events captured during the failure period are kept for **exactly 24 hours** (and not reduced if the main system recovers).

5. **Ensure automatic activation and stopping behaviour:**
   - When the trigger condition is met, automatically enable alternate capture and keep it running until the trigger condition clears.
   - Apply retention based on when the events were captured, so main recovery does not reduce the 24-hour requirement.

6. **Produce audit-ready evidence:**
   - Write an immutable “alternate logging activated/deactivated” marker event (including timestamp and reason code) to the same immutable store.
   - Provide operational dashboards/alerts showing trigger checks, activation time, and event counts for the in-scope subset.

###### WHO
- **Security Engineering / SOC Engineering Lead:** defines how events map to the required subset, defines the takeover trigger logic, and designs the immutable storage approach.
- **Platform/Infrastructure Engineer:** implements the alternate collectors, network routing, and immutable storage configuration.
- **Application Owner / IAM Integration Owner:** confirms that login/privilege/Secret-access events are available for alternate capture and provides field mappings.
- **GRC/Compliance Owner:** checks that the documented subset matches **au-05.05_odp** and that the evidence is suitable for audit.

###### Acceptance criteria
- If main audit logging is intentionally disrupted (for example, the main collector is stopped or the main destination is unreachable), the alternate capability must:
  - activate automatically without operator involvement,
  - capture the in-scope events (logins, privilege changes, Secret data access) for the failure period,
  - write records to the on-premises immutable store,
  - retain captured events for **24 hours** regardless of when the main system recovers,
  - record an immutable activation marker that includes the trigger reason and timestamps.
- When main logging is restored and the trigger condition clears, alternate capture must stop according to the defined trigger logic, while previously captured events remain available for the full 24-hour retention period.

###### Actions Required for Compliance

- [ ] Define and document the specific conditions that will trigger an “objective takeover” when primary audit logging fails, including the failure thresholds and how often they are checked.
- [ ] Set up an independent backup event intake pipeline (using separate collectors and hosts, and—where possible—separate routing) that is automatically activated.
- [ ] Identify and select the relevant application and identity and access management (IAM) audit events so the alternate processing pipeline captures only the au-05.05_odp subset (logins, changes to privileges, and access to secret data).
- [ ] Set up an on-premises audit log that cannot be changed (an “append-only” record) and keep it for at least 24 hours based on when each event was recorded.
- [ ] Add permanent (unchangeable) activation and deactivation marker events to the same record store, including the time and a reason code.
- [ ] Run a controlled test that intentionally causes a failure to confirm that the automatic switch-over works, that the correct set of events is recorded, and that event records are kept for 24 hours even after the main system has recovered.

### AU-6 — Audit Record Review, Analysis, and Reporting (Control)

This requirement is about regularly checking the organisation’s activity records to spot suspicious behaviour early, understand what it could mean, and respond appropriately. The organisation must review and analyse audit logs for signs of someone gaining extra privileges, unusual or suspicious logins, evidence that data may be leaving the organisation, and any actions that break security rules or system settings. For high-risk systems, this must be done every day; for all other systems, it must be done every week. The organisation must then assess the potential harm if the activity is real.  

The results must be reported to the incident response team and the information security office. The organisation must also be prepared to increase or reduce how often, how widely, and how deeply these checks are carried out when the organisation’s risk level changes, using information from law enforcement, intelligence sources, or other credible information.  

To make this work consistently, the organisation must document the process, assign responsibilities, include it in relevant plans and policies, and keep records of findings and actions taken.

##### SIEM/SOAR audit log review with daily/weekly cadence and impact reporting

- Category: Software
- Priority: Critical

###### WHAT
Set up a central system to review and analyse audit records using a **security information and event management (SIEM)** and **security orchestration, automation and response (SOAR)** platform. This system will:  
1) review and analyse audit logs on a schedule set by the organisation,  
2) spot signs of privilege escalation, unusual sign-ins, possible data theft, and breaches of security policy or configuration rules,  
3) estimate the potential impact if the activity is real,  
4) send results to the incident response team and the information security office, and  
5) increase or reduce how often and how deeply it reviews when credible new risk information is received.

###### WHY (control requirement)
**Audit requirement AU-6** says the organisation must review and analyse audit records for inappropriate or unusual activity, assess potential impact, report results to the relevant parts of the organisation (including incident response and security/privacy teams), and adjust how often and how deeply reviews are done based on new information. The organisation’s defined parameters are:
- **frequency**: daily for high-risk systems and weekly for others (au-06_odp.01)
- **inappropriate or unusual activity**: privilege escalation, unusual sign-ins, possible data theft indicators, and breaches of policy/configuration (au-06_odp.02)
- **personnel/roles**: incident response team and information security office (au-06_odp.03)

###### HOW (specific steps/approach)
1. **Define “high-risk systems” and link them to the right log sources** using the organisation’s official list of assets and their data classification/risk level. Mark each system/asset in the SIEM so the workflow can enforce **daily vs weekly** review.
2. **Collect and standardise audit records** from the application and related components (for example: operating system and sign-in logs, logs of privileged actions, identity provider/single sign-on logs, remote access/virtual private network logs, configuration change logs, and application admin/audit events). Make sure times match across sources and protect log integrity (for example, using write-once/immutable storage where possible).
3. **Set up detection checks (detection-as-code)** based on the defined categories of inappropriate or unusual activity:
   - privilege escalation indicators (for example: changes to roles/groups, granting of administrative privileges, changes to credentials/tokens, changes to audit-related settings)
   - unusual sign-in indicators (for example: impossible travel, new device, unusual location/time, downgrades of multi-factor authentication)
   - data theft indicators (for example: unusually large data leaving the system, new export actions, suspicious application programming interface patterns, moving data from staging to storage)
   - policy/configuration violations (for example: disabling security controls, risky configuration changes, changes to firewall/audit settings)
4. **Automate initial review and impact assessment** in SOAR by adding context to alerts, such as how critical the asset is, who the user is, and what changes have recently occurred. Produce a consistent “potential impact” summary for each finding (for example: likely impact on confidentiality/integrity, which business process may be affected, and whether it matches known change periods).
5. **Send results to the required recipients**: configure SOAR playbooks to send findings to the **incident response team** and the **information security office** (au-06_odp.03), including links to evidence (log queries), the activity category, and the impact assessment.
6. **Run reviews on the required schedule and adjust when risk changes**:
   - set up automated review workflows: **daily** for assets marked high-risk and **weekly** for others (au-06_odp.01)
   - when credible risk-change information is received (for example from law enforcement/intelligence/other credible sources), update the detection checks and expand how broadly and how deeply the review is done (for example: enable additional detections, widen the time window of logs reviewed, and increase how detailed analyst review is). Record why the change was made.

###### WHO (roles responsible)
- **Security Operations Centre (SOC) / Security Operations (SecOps)**: owns SIEM/SOAR configuration, detection checks, triage workflows, and ensures scheduled reviews are carried out.
- **Incident Response Team**: receives findings, checks whether the activity is suspicious, and starts response actions.
- **Information Security Office**: receives findings, ensures alignment with governance requirements, and oversees reporting and closure.
- **Application/Infrastructure owners (as needed)**: provide system context, change windows, and inputs on asset criticality.

###### Acceptance criteria (audit-ready)
- The organisation can show that **high-risk systems are reviewed daily** and **other systems weekly** (evidence: SIEM/SOAR job schedules and review run logs) (au-06_odp.01).
- Detection checks cover the defined categories: **privilege escalation, unusual sign-ins, data theft indicators, and policy/configuration violations** (au-06_odp.02).
- For each finding, the workflow produces an **impact assessment** describing the potential harm if the activity is real.
- Findings are **reported to the incident response team and the information security office** with consistent evidence and categorisation (au-06_odp.03).
- When credible risk-change information is received, the organisation updates review **frequency/scope/depth** and keeps the reason for the change and the supporting evidence (for example, a ticket/change record linked to updates to the detection checks).
- Review outputs (findings, triage decisions, and routing actions) are kept for audit purposes.

###### Actions Required for Compliance

- [ ] Label each asset or system in the security information and event management (SIEM) as “high risk” or “other” according to the organisation’s risk tiering model.
- [ ] Collect and standardise audit records from the application and the integrated identity, configuration, and remote access components, and send them to the security information and event management (SIEM) system.
- [ ] Set up alerts to detect attempts to gain higher privileges, unusual sign-in activity, signs that data may be leaving the organisation, and breaches of security policies or configuration rules.
- [ ] Set up security orchestration, automation and response (SOAR) triage playbooks to add more information to alerts and produce a documented assessment of the potential impact
- [ ] Set up automatic routing of findings to the incident response team and the information security office, including links to the supporting evidence.
- [ ] Set up and keep records of daily review processes for high-risk systems, and weekly review processes for all other systems.
- [ ] Create a documented process to update rule packs and to widen the scope and depth of reviews when credible information shows that the risk level has changed.

#### AU-6.1 — Automated Process Integration (Enhancement)

This requirement is about making sure your organisation’s checks of system activity are not done only by hand, but are brought together automatically. This helps you spot suspicious behaviour quickly and ensures the results are reported properly. Without this, important audit information could be missed, reviewed too late, or put together inconsistently. As a result, real incidents might not be investigated promptly, and oversight reporting (including Inspector General audits) could be incomplete or difficult to prove with evidence.

#### AU-6.3 — Correlate Audit Record Repositories (Enhancement)

This requirement is about bringing together evidence from different sources so the organisation can see the full picture of what is happening, rather than relying on separate, isolated snapshots. If audit records are not linked across multiple systems, important clues can be missed, patterns may go unnoticed, and the organisation may not understand how issues affect overall risk, day-to-day business activities, or specific systems.

To meet this expectation, the organisation must set out why and how it will link audit records, decide which systems to include, collect the relevant records so they can be compared, and analyse them together to produce combined views—such as a single linked timeline of events. The results must be used in the organisation’s audit review, analysis and reporting. Named people must be responsible for this work, and information security and privacy roles must be involved.

The organisation must also ensure its systems are set up to produce consistent, usable records that can be matched together, keep the records available, and document the approach and the methods used. The organisation must ensure the outputs support awareness at the organisational, business-process and system levels, including sharing context with other organisations where required.

AU-6(4) (Enhancement) requires the organisation to **provide and implement the capability to centrally review and analyse audit records from multiple components**. The guidance also states that **automated mechanisms** for central review and analysis can include **SIEM products**.

###### HOW (specific steps/approach)
1. **Define the central review scope**: create an onboarding register that lists the components in scope (for example: identity provider (IdP), virtual private network (VPN)/remote access, web/application programming interface (API) front ends, application services, databases, privileged access tools, and administration consoles) and the types of audit records to review (for example: authentication events, changes to privileges, administrative actions, access to sensitive data, and policy/authorisation denials).
2. **Centralise collection and standardise**: set up secure log sending from each component to the central SIEM (secure transport layer (TLS); mutual authentication where supported). Make sure each event includes consistent information such as: time of event, source component, host/application/service, user/service account, action, result, severity, and linking details (such as session ID/transaction/request ID where available).
3. **Set up automated checking and analysis**: create scheduled searches, dashboards, and alerting/correlation rules for review topics that span multiple components (for example: a chain of privileged actions across IdP → application → database within a defined time window; repeated failed logins followed by successful privileged access).
4. **Produce review outputs that can be audited**: configure report templates that export review evidence (for example: PDF/CSV) showing the review time window, the identifiers for the query/correlation rule used, and proof that the analysis was run (including the reviewer identity/role where the workflow supports this). Store outputs in an access-controlled repository that matches the organisation’s retention requirements.
5. **Operate with controlled change**: manage SIEM settings for parsing, correlation rules, and report content using change control (a versioned rules/content repository; promotion through development/test/production; approvals). Ensure access to SIEM review functions is role-based and limited to authorised staff.
6. **Run and keep evidence**: carry out the defined review process on a scheduled basis, keep evidence that reviews and reports were produced, and periodically check that required sources are still connected and generating the expected audit records.

###### WHO (role responsible)
- **Security Operations Lead / security operations centre (SOC) Analyst Manager**: owns the review use-cases, dashboards, and scheduled analyses.

- **Data Protection/Privacy Officer (where applicable)**: reviews any privacy-related impact of central log retention and access.

###### Acceptance criteria
- The SIEM receives audit records from **multiple defined components**, and events can be searched in a **single central view**.
- Automated scheduled reviews and correlation analyses are in place for the defined in-scope review topics.
- Cross-component correlation works for at least one representative scenario (for example: a privileged action chain across at least two components) and produces an exportable review output.
- Review outputs (reports/exports) are stored with traceability (time window and rule/query identifiers) and access is restricted to authorised roles.
- Evidence shows that SIEM review content and correlation rules are managed through controlled change (versioning and approvals) and that review runs are completed according to the documented procedure.

- [ ] Develop and test rules that match related events from different sources for at least one privileged-access scenario and one authentication scenario.
- [ ] Set up reusable report templates that include the review time window and the correlation and query identifiers.
- [ ] Put security information and event management (SIEM) parsing, correlation, and reporting content under change control, with versioning and promotion between environments.

#### AU-6.5 — Integrated Analysis of Audit Records (Enhancement)

This expectation is about joining the dots. When you review records of what happened in your systems, you should also look at other evidence—such as vulnerability scan results, operational monitoring alerts, and performance information—to identify behaviour that looks suspicious or should not be happening. If you do not use this combined view, you may miss real attacks, mistake harmless activity for a problem, or fail to notice unusual activity occurring at the same time as warning signs in other data.

To meet this expectation, the organisation must:
- define which additional sources will be used (including, where available, threat information and known “indicators of compromise”)
- document how the combined analysis will work
- ensure audit records from different parts of the organisation can be brought together and linked using time, system, user, or indicator

The review must be carried out by named people, with appropriate input from those responsible for privacy and security. The organisation must also use repeatable methods and standard analysis approaches where possible, and adjust them to fit its systems.

##### SIEM-integrated correlation of audit, scan, monitoring and performance

- Category: Software
- Priority: Critical

###### WHAT
Set up an integrated analysis capability in the security information and event management (SIEM) system (and optional security orchestration, automation and response (SOAR)) that brings together **audit record information** with **vulnerability scanning information**, **system monitoring information**, and **performance data**—and, where available, **threat intelligence feeds and indicator-of-compromise (IoC) data**—to spot **inappropriate or unusual activity**.

###### WHY (control requirement)
Control AU-6(5) requires that analysis of audit records is combined with analysis of the organisation’s defined sources (parameter **au-06.05_odp.01**: vulnerability scanning information, system monitoring information, performance data) to improve the ability to identify inappropriate or unusual activity. The guidance explains that “integrated analysis” means comparing and linking information collected from scanning and monitoring (and other data sources) with audit record analysis. It also notes that linking with vulnerability scanning helps confirm whether scan results are reliable, and helps match attack detection events to scanning results.

###### HOW (specific steps/approach)
1. **Define the inputs and matching fields for integrated analysis**: document which log or audit sources count as “audit record information” and which other sources will be used (at minimum vulnerability scan results, monitoring alerts, and performance data; optionally threat intelligence feeds and IoCs from **au-06.05_odp.02**). Define the fields used to link related items (for example: asset identifier, user/account, internet protocol (IP)/media access control (MAC) address, service, and time windows).
2. **Bring data into the SIEM and put it into a consistent format**: make sure vulnerability scan results are loaded with asset details, timestamps, finding identifiers (for example, common vulnerabilities and exposures (CVE)/common weakness enumeration (CWE) where available), and supporting evidence. Make sure monitoring alerts and performance metrics are loaded and formatted using the same asset/user identifiers and timestamps.
3. **Create rules that link related events across sources**: build correlation logic that connects audit events to (a) relevant scan findings for the same asset and time window, (b) monitoring detections for the same user/host and time window, and (c) performance anomalies that match the same sessions or transactions where possible.
4. **Generate one combined investigation outcome (single conclusion)**: configure the SIEM/SOAR workflow so that when an audit-based case is created, it automatically adds the linked scan, monitoring, and performance evidence and produces one integrated assessment (not separate reviews).
5. **Use standardised analysis scripts where needed**: where data needs to be interpreted or enriched (for example, extracting user/asset/session details from application audit logs), use standard scripts maintained by the organisation, with local adjustments for the application’s log formats.
6. **Allow for missing data without losing the conclusion**: ensure the workflow can still produce an integrated conclusion if one type of evidence is not available (for example, scan data is missing for a time window). Clearly record which evidence types were available.
7. **Set clear named responsibility for review and accountability**: configure case ownership and approval steps so named analysts (including security and privacy involvement where required by the organisation) review the integrated outputs and record the outcomes.

###### WHO
- **Security Operations Lead / security operations centre (SOC) Analyst (named role)**: responsible for tuning the correlation rules and reviewing cases.
- **SIEM Engineer**: implements data loading, consistent formatting, correlation rules, and enrichment scripts.
- **Application Owner / Logging Engineer**: ensures the audit record fields needed for linking (asset/user/time/session) are present and correctly formatted.
- **Information Security and Privacy representatives (as applicable)**: review the integrated analysis workflow to meet privacy and accountability requirements.

###### Acceptance criteria
- The SIEM can link an audit event with at least **vulnerability scan information**, **system monitoring alerts**, and **performance data** for the same asset/user/time window, producing one integrated case summary.
- Correlation rules clearly link audit events to scan findings and monitoring detections (with evidence attached) and include performance anomaly context where available.
- Data loading stores vulnerability scan findings, monitoring alerts, and performance metrics using consistent identifiers and timestamps that support correlation.
- Standardised enrichment and parsing scripts are used for audit record normalisation (with documented local adjustments).
- If one evidence source is unavailable, the workflow still produces an integrated conclusion and records which evidence types were available.
- Case ownership and review steps are set up for named reviewers, and the integrated outputs are auditable (evidence is retained for the investigation).

###### Actions Required for Compliance

- [ ] Define the combined analysis data sources and matching fields (asset, user, Internet Protocol address, and time windows) used to produce audit, scanning, monitoring, and performance evidence.
- [ ] Set up security information and event management (SIEM) to standardise vulnerability scan results, monitoring alerts, and performance measurements, using the same identifiers and timestamps for all data.
- [ ] Set up security information and event management (SIEM) rules that connect audit records to related results from scanning, alerts from monitoring, and matching unusual performance patterns.
- [ ] Create security orchestration, automation and response (SOAR) or security information and event management (SIEM) workflow playbooks to produce one joined investigation story, including supporting evidence from all related sources.
- [ ] Create and deploy standard audit parsing and enrichment scripts to extract the required information for correlation, and make local adjustments to match the format of each application log.
- [ ] Set up who owns each case and the named review steps, so analysts produce and record integrated conclusions, with the availability of supporting evidence noted.

#### AU-6.6 — Correlation with Physical Monitoring (Enhancement)

This requirement is about making sure the organisation can connect what people do on its computer systems with whether those same people were physically on site at the relevant time. Without this, suspicious or harmful activity can be harder to detect, because computer records may look normal on their own even if the person was not actually present, or when unusual patterns need stronger context for investigation.

To meet this expectation, the organisation must set up a method to combine information from its computer activity records with its physical access records, using a shared way to identify the person. This linking must support investigations. The organisation must also:

- document why and how the linking works  
- keep clear procedures for reviewing, analysing, and reporting the combined information  
- document how physical access is monitored  
- record evidence that the linking has been carried out  
- include this approach in its system security plan and, where applicable, its privacy plan  

The organisation must assign named responsibilities to the people who review the records, manage physical monitoring, and oversee information security and privacy.

##### SIEM correlation of logical audit events with physical access logs

- Category: Software
- Priority: Critical

###### WHAT

The NIST AU-6(6) enhancement requires combining **information from computer audit records** with **information from physical access monitoring** to improve the ability to identify suspicious, inappropriate, unusual, or harmful activity. The guidance also highlights the value of linking a person’s computer access identity to their presence at the site at the time of the computer access.

###### HOW (specific steps/approach)
1. **Define the correlation design**: document the purpose of the correlation, the **time matching method** (for example, matching events by time using a configurable time window such as ±15 minutes), and how data from both sources will flow into the correlation engine.

Keep this mapping under change control and version it.
3. **Collect and standardise the logs** in the organisation’s security information and event management (SIEM) / log analytics platform:

Create a correlated investigation record when suspicious conditions are found (for example, unusual use of privileges, unexpected sign-in patterns, or access outside the person’s expected role).
5. **Produce investigation outputs**: ensure correlated alerts/cases include both:
   - the physical access evidence (door/location, time, credential/person ID), and
   - the computer audit evidence (event time, action, system/application, person identifier).
6. **Keep correlation evidence for audit purposes**: retain the correlated output details, the correlation rule identifiers/versions, and the version of the identity mapping used at the time of correlation.

###### Acceptance criteria

- The identity mapping between the computer access person identifier and the physical credential/person ID is consistent, versioned, and controlled through change management.
- Correlation rules are implemented in an auditable way (rule IDs/versions recorded), and correlated outputs retain enough evidence to support investigation.
- The system security plan (and privacy plan where applicable) is updated to describe the correlation capability, data sources, and handling considerations.

#### AU-6.7 — Permitted Actions (Enhancement)

This requirement is about ensuring that only the right people and automated tasks are allowed to do the right actions, and that audit records are protected from any other changes. If permissions are not clearly defined and enforced, someone could accidentally or deliberately change, delete, or incorrectly add information to audit records. This could weaken investigations, compliance checks, and accountability.

The organisation must clearly define and document which actions are allowed for each relevant system process, role, and individual user involved in reviewing, analysing, and reporting audit records. It must use the minimum access needed—for example, allowing only reading, or allowing reading plus writing or adding entries—while restricting actions such as running (execution) and deleting where they are not required.

The organisation must keep these permissions up to date in its official policies and procedures, ensure its account and access management processes apply and enforce the permissions in practice, and confirm that the system blocks any actions that are not explicitly permitted.

##### Enforce least-privilege permitted actions on audit records via RBAC

- Category: Software
- Priority: Critical

###### WHAT
For each **{{ insert: param, au-06.07_odp }} (role, user)** involved in reviewing, analysing, and reporting audit record information, define and enforce the **actions the system is allowed to take** on audit record information (at minimum: **read, write, execute, append, delete**). The system must **block any action that is not explicitly permitted**.

###### WHY (control requirement)
AU-6(7) requires the organisation to **specify permitted actions** for each **{{ insert: param, au-06.07_odp }}** linked to the review, analysis, and reporting of audit record information, applying least privilege. Permitted actions must be **enforced by the system** and include **read, write, execute, append, and delete**.

###### HOW (specific steps/approach)
1. **Create an audit-record permission matrix** that lists each relevant **role/user** (for example: reviewer, analyst, report author, approver, privileged administrator, service account) and the allowed action types for each audit-record object/workspace (for example: log store, case workspace, report draft repository, evidence attachments). The matrix must clearly state which of **read/write/execute/append/delete** are permitted (and which are not) for each role/user.
2. **Build enforcement into the application and supporting services** using the organisation’s identity and access control approach (for example, role-based access control (RBAC) and attribute-based access control (ABAC)) so that each action type is technically controlled:
   - **Read**: allow viewing audit record information.
   - **Append**: allow adding analysis notes or case annotations without enabling wider changes.
   - **Write**: allow updating analysis fields only where needed.
   - **Execute**: allow only the workflow actions required for review/analysis/reporting (for example, generating a report, approving a step), not arbitrary code execution.
   - **Delete**: allow only zero or explicitly justified “break-glass” roles; otherwise deny.
   The system must return an access failure for any action that is not permitted.
3. **Manage permissions through account management workflows** (group-based entitlements managed through the central identity provider) so that permitted actions are assigned to the correct **role/user** and removed when responsibilities change.
4. **Keep the permission matrix auditable and under change control** by treating it as the source of truth and applying updates through controlled configuration management (for example, infrastructure and application configuration managed via version control, with peer review and approvals).
5. **Test enforcement and prevent unauthorised changes (“drift”)** by running automated checks that compare the effective permissions in the live environment against the documented matrix, and by carrying out periodic access reviews/attestations for each role/user.

###### WHO
- **Application Owner / Security Architect**: define the permission matrix and least-privilege mapping.
- **Identity and Access Management (IAM) Engineer / Platform Engineer**: implement RBAC and access-control enforcement, and manage identity provider group entitlements.
- **Security Operations Centre (SOC)/Compliance Lead**: confirm that permitted actions match the review/analysis/reporting responsibilities.
- **System Administrators (restricted)**: maintain configuration only through approved change management.

###### Acceptance criteria
- For every **{{ insert: param, au-06.07_odp }} (role/user)** in the audit review/analysis/reporting workflow, the system has an explicit, documented mapping of permitted actions covering **read/write/execute/append/delete**.
- The application and any integrated services **enforce** these permitted actions (attempts to perform non-permitted actions are denied by the system).
- **Delete** is denied for all roles/users where it is not explicitly required by the matrix.
- Effective permissions in production match the documented matrix (verified through drift checks and/or access review evidence).
- Permission changes are traceable to approved change records and configuration versions.

###### Actions Required for Compliance

- [ ] Create an audit-record permission matrix that maps each **au-06.07_odp** role or user to the actions they are allowed to perform on each audit-record object (read, write, execute, append, or delete).
- [ ] Implement role-based access control (RBAC) and authorisation checks in the application and the integrated audit-record services, so any actions a user is not permitted to take are blocked.
- [ ] Set up identity provider (IdP) group entitlements so each role or user gets only the permissions listed in the permissions matrix.
- [ ] Set up a controlled process for changes to the permission matrix and authorisation settings, with version tracking, formal approvals, and a complete audit trail.

- [ ] Carry out regular access reviews (attestations) for each role and user to confirm that the permissions allowed for their job still follow the principle of least privilege.

#### AU-6.8 — Full Text Analysis of Privileged Commands (Enhancement)

This requirement is about checking the exact details of what powerful users type when they perform high-privilege actions—not just confirming that they acted. The risk it addresses is that harmful or unauthorised activity could be hidden within the command details, such as a specific account, file location, or option, and be missed if you only look at the command’s name.

To meet this expectation, the organisation must review the full text of every recorded high-privilege command, including all parts and settings (parameters). This review must be done in a separate, physically isolated analysis environment, or on a dedicated separate system used only for this checking, so the checking process cannot affect, or reveal information about, the environment where elevated access is available.

The organisation should use both:
- searches for known suspicious patterns, and
- sensible checks based on likely misuse,

to detect less obvious wrongdoing. The organisation must also document what is checked, how it is checked, and how the results are reviewed and reported. Named individuals must be responsible for reviewing, analysing, reporting, and overseeing privacy and security.

##### Physically isolated privileged command full-text analysis pipeline

- Category: Software
- Priority: Critical

###### WHAT
Set up a dedicated, physically separate analysis component/subsystem that performs **full text analysis** of **logged privileged commands, including the command parameters** (not just the command names) using **pattern matching and judgement-based checks (heuristics)**.

###### WHY (control requirement)
AU-6(8) enhancement requires the organisation to **“perform a full text analysis of logged privileged commands”** using a **physically separate component/subsystem** (or another dedicated system) that is used **only for that analysis**. This ensures the analysis cannot weaken, affect, or interfere with the privileged command/audit source environment. The full text analysis must consider the **full command text (commands and parameters)** and must include **pattern matching and heuristics**.

###### HOW (specific steps/approach)
1. **Create a dedicated analysis zone**: use a separate appliance or virtual machine host in a physically separate environment, or use an **air-gapped** design (with one-way transfer). This zone must have **no administrative connection** back to the privileged execution environment.
2. **Send privileged command audit records** to the analysis zone using a controlled method (for example, a one-way data transfer approach, or a scheduled batch export with integrity checks). Make sure the exported record includes the **full command string exactly as it was logged**, including any quoting/escaping and parameters.
3. **Keep the full command text unchanged** on the analysis system: store an immutable copy for traceability. Ensure analysis is performed on the **complete command line**, not on a derived “command name” field.
4. **Build detection logic on the analysis system**:
   - **Pattern matching** (regular expressions/keyword patterns) across the full command text for known risky elements (for example: privilege escalation flags, suspicious file paths, indicators of credentials, destructive actions, and unusual combinations of options).
   - **Heuristic scoring** for unusual or risky combinations of parameters (for example: rare flag sets, path traversal indicators, attempts to access sensitive locations, or use of interpreters in elevated contexts).
5. **Produce findings that can be audited** and that point back to the original audit event (timestamp, user, session, host, and the specific full command/parameter parts that triggered the rules/heuristics). Send these findings into the organisation’s case/alert workflow **without giving the analysis system any ability to run privileged commands**.
6. **Set up review and reporting procedures**: define who reviews findings, how often, escalation thresholds, and how privacy and security considerations are handled (for example, reducing or redacting data in reports while keeping full evidence in the isolated analysis store).
7. **Document the analysis scope and configuration**: list which privileged command sources are included, which command types are analysed, the rule/heuristic versions, tool versions, and the transfer/export method, plus the operational runbook for analysis and review.

###### WHO
- **Security Operations Centre (SOC) / Detection Engineering**: implement and maintain the detection rules/heuristics and the analysis runbook.
- **Security Information and Event Management (SIEM) / Logging Engineering**: implement the export process to ensure full command text is transferred.
- **Information Security / Privacy Officer (where applicable)**: approve the privacy/data handling approach for command parameters in reports.
- **System Owner / Privileged Access Governance Lead**: ensure the scope is covered and that review/escalation procedures are followed.

###### Acceptance criteria
- Privileged command audit events are exported to a **physically separate** analysis system with **no administrative route** back to the privileged environment.
- The analysis engine checks the **full command text, including parameters**, for every eligible privileged command event.
- Detection includes both **pattern matching** and **heuristic** checks across the full command line.
- Findings include **evidence** (the full command and the specific triggering parameter parts) and can be traced back to the originating audit event.
- The organisation can provide audit-ready evidence of: scope, rule/heuristic versions, export/transfer method, and the named review/escalation procedures.

###### Actions Required for Compliance

- [ ] Define which privileged command sources are eligible, and confirm that the audit records include the full command line (the command and its parameters) exactly as it was executed.
- [ ] Set up a separate, physically isolated “privileged command analysis” appliance or zone, with no administrative network connection back to the privileged execution environment.
- [ ] Set up a controlled export process (either a one-way transfer or a batch export that checks data integrity) to send the complete command text to the analysis area.
- [ ] Set up the analysis tool to check the full command text using both pattern matching (regular expressions and keywords) and a heuristic scoring method.
- [ ] Store analysed command evidence in write-once (immutable) storage, and produce findings that point back to the original audit event and the specific triggering parameter text fragments.
- [ ] Add the findings to the organisation’s alert and ticket workflow using the minimum necessary permissions, and do not allow the analysis system to run privileged commands.
- [ ] Create and practise an audit-ready runbook that explains how often reviews happen, when issues are escalated, and how personal data and privacy are handled for command parameters.

#### AU-6.9 — Correlation with Information from Nontechnical Sources (Enhancement)

This requirement is about connecting sensitive information about incidents with the organisation’s official activity records. The aim is to spot patterns that could indicate wrongdoing by an individual. Without this careful linking, important clues about breaches of harassment-related policies or improper use of company information could be missed. It could also lead the organisation to waste time investigating the wrong people, while increasing the risk of accidentally exposing private information.

The organisation must clearly set out which types of non-public records are allowed to be linked, and explain why this is being done to improve organisation-wide awareness of potential security and insider-risk situations. The organisation must only carry out the linking when there is a reasonable suspicion that someone is involved in an incident, using documented review, analysis, and reporting steps. Named staff must be responsible for the work, and it must be overseen by the teams responsible for information security and privacy.

Access to the sensitive non-public records must be restricted. The organisation must keep evidence of what was linked and what was found. Before any more targeted, directed analysis starts, it must obtain legal advice.

##### Case-gated correlation of sensitive nontechnical records with audit logs

- Category: Software
- Priority: Critical

###### WHAT
Set up a controlled, case-by-case process that links **authorised non-technical incident-related records** (for example, harassment or policy-violation records, and records of improper use of information assets) with **audit record information**. This will help the organisation build a shared view of what is happening, to spot possible malicious insider activity.

###### WHY (control requirement)
AU-6(9) (Enhancement) requires the organisation to **link information from non-technical sources with audit record information** to improve organisation-wide situational awareness. Guidance says this linking should generally happen **only when someone is suspected of being involved in an incident**. It also requires that the organisation **limits access** to sensitive non-technical sources, and that **legal advice is obtained before starting** any such activity.

###### HOW (specific steps/approach)
1. **Define which non-technical records are allowed**: create a documented list of the non-technical record types that can be linked (at minimum: harassment/policy-violation incident records, and improper use of information-asset incident records). Include the required information needed for linking, such as: the person involved, the incident date/time range, the affected asset or system identifiers, and the incident reference.
2. **Use case approval gates with a legal basis**: require a case intake ticket/workflow that records (a) the documented basis for **reasonable suspicion**, (b) a reference to **legal advice approval** (or the legal counsel ticket/approval ID), and (c) the agreed **linking scope** (time window, person(s), and systems/asset identifiers). Linking must be stopped if any of these items are missing.
3. **Limit who can access non-technical sources**: use role-based and attribute-based access controls so only the authorised case team (for example, Insider Risk Lead, Information Security, Privacy, and the Legal liaison as applicable) can view non-technical records. Apply least-privilege access and time-limited access (for example, “just-in-time” access) with strong authentication.
4. **Do the linking using the SIEM/forensics workflow**: match the case person(s) to the relevant audit identities (for example, user IDs, service accounts, and device IDs). Then run linking searches against audit sources (for example, authentication events, administrative actions, and data access events) within the approved scope.
5. **Keep the results as evidence**: save the linking results as case evidence, including: the non-technical record reference(s), the audit record identifiers/timestamps used, the linking search method (or search IDs), the analyst’s identity, and access logs showing who viewed or ran the linking and when.
6. **Require documented review and reporting**: ensure the linking results are reviewed by the accountable roles (Information Security and Privacy oversight as applicable). Produce reporting for the insider-risk process, with retention aligned to organisational policy.

###### WHO
- **Insider Risk Lead / SOC Lead**: owns the case workflow and ensures linking is case-approved and within scope.
- **Information Security (or Security Operations)**: owns running the audit log linking and ensures the evidence is complete and reliable.
- **Privacy Officer (where applicable)**: provides oversight for privacy impact and access restrictions.
- **Legal Counsel**: provides and/or approves legal advice before any directed analysis begins.
- **System/Platform Owner (SIEM/Insider Risk tooling)**: implements the technical controls (access enforcement, running the linking, and storing evidence).

###### Acceptance criteria
- Linking non-technical records with audit logs cannot be carried out unless the case intake includes (1) documented reasonable suspicion and (2) a legal advice approval reference.
- Only authorised case-team roles can access non-technical records; access is logged and time-limited where supported.
- Linking results are stored with complete evidence: non-technical record references, audit record identifiers/timestamps, linking search identifiers/method, analyst identity, and access logs.
- Linking is carried out strictly within the approved scope (time window, person(s), and systems/asset identifiers) and produces a report that can be reviewed for the insider-risk process.

###### Actions Required for Compliance

### AU-7 — Audit Record Reduction and Report Generation (Control)

This requirement ensures that authorised staff can gather and summarise existing records of system activity. This helps them quickly review what happened, identify unusual patterns, and produce clear reports if something goes wrong later. Without it, important evidence could be difficult to find, summaries could accidentally change the original facts, or events could be presented in the wrong order—making incidents harder to understand and harder to hold the right people and systems accountable.

To meet this expectation, the organisation must provide an on-demand capability that lets staff review, analyse, and create tailored reports for both routine checks and investigations after an incident. The original record content must remain exactly as it was, and the original time order must be preserved. Any reduced summaries and generated reports must be stored separately from the original records, and the reports must reflect the correct chronology.

The organisation must document how this is done, assign named responsibilities, restrict use to authorised personnel, and keep the relevant plans, procedures, design, and configuration information up to date.

##### Query-time audit record reduction with ordered, immutable reporting

- Category: Software
- Priority: Critical

###### WHAT
Set up an on-demand capability that turns existing audit records into analyst-friendly summaries and produces tailored reports for routine review and for investigating incidents after they have happened. This must keep the original audit record content unchanged and keep the original order of events in time.

###### WHY (control requirement)
AU-7 requires the organisation to **provide and implement audit record reduction and report generation capability** that supports **on-demand review and reporting**. It must use **advanced filtering and data mining** to spot unusual or suspicious behaviour. Importantly, it must ensure that **reduced outputs do not change or overwrite the original audit records**, and that **time order is preserved**—including when timestamp precision is not detailed enough.

###### HOW (specific steps/approach)
1. **Load audit logs into an unchangeable, append-only audit store** (for example, write-once storage such as WORM, or an immutable event store) so the source audit records cannot be rewritten by the reduction or reporting process.
2. **Standardise and enforce event identity and timestamp precision when data is received**:
   - Require a stable **event identifier** (for example, `event_id` or a deterministic hash) and keep the **original event timestamp**.
   - Ensure timestamp precision is sufficient to maintain correct ordering (prefer millisecond precision or better). If upstream systems provide lower precision, add a deterministic secondary ordering method (for example, an ingestion sequence number) while still keeping the original timestamp field unchanged.
3. **Do reduction using “read-only” views at query time** (or create non-destructive summaries stored separately):
   - Set up saved queries and dashboards that filter, combine, and enrich data to highlight anomalies, without altering the underlying immutable events.
   - Store reduced outputs (summaries, timelines, counts) in a **separate derived-data location**, with traceability back to the source `event_id` set.
4. **Create customised reports from query results**:
   - Use a report generator that runs parameterised queries (time window, system, account, event type) and produces reports that include the **ordering key** used.
   - Ensure report timelines and sequences reflect the preserved ordering (primary: event timestamp; secondary: deterministic tie-breaker).
5. **Limit and record who can run reduction and report generation**:
   - Use enterprise identity controls (role-based access control (RBAC) and least privilege) so only authorised Security Operations Centre (SOC), Incident Response (IR), and IT Audit roles can run reduction queries and export reports.
   - Record report generation activity (who/what/when/which filters were used) in a separate audit trail.
6. **Document the process and manage it through configuration control**:
   - Keep controlled documentation for the reduction and reporting process, including the query logic, ordering rules, timestamp handling, and how evidence can be traced back to sources.
   - Use version control for query and report definitions and manage changes through the organisation’s configuration and change management process.

###### WHO (role responsible)
- **Security Operations (SOC) / Incident Response Lead**: defines what analysts need in reports and which anomaly-focused filters to use.
- **Security Engineering / Security Information and Event Management (SIEM) / Audit Platform Engineer**: implements ingestion requirements, immutable storage, query-time reduction, and the report generator.
- **Identity and Access Management (IAM) / Platform Access Administrator**: sets up role-based access control (RBAC) and manages access approvals using least privilege.
- **Compliance / Assurance**: checks that evidence is audit-ready (traceability, correct ordering, no modification) and reviews the documentation.

###### Acceptance criteria (verifiable)
- Reduced views and generated reports are produced **without modifying** the immutable source audit records (show that the source store is append-only and that reduction writes only to derived-data and report outputs).
- Reports preserve correct chronology: for a test dataset with multiple events sharing the same timestamp, the report ordering matches the defined ordering key (timestamp precision plus deterministic tie-breaker).
- Each report includes traceability to the underlying events (for example, a list of `event_id` references or a verifiable mapping to source records).
- Only authorised roles can run reduction and report generation; all executions and exports are logged to a separate audit trail.
- Timestamp precision handling is documented and evidenced (ingestion contract plus configuration showing the enforcement and normalisation rules).

###### Actions Required for Compliance

- [ ] Set up an unchangeable, add-only audit record store to hold all collected audit records used for reduction and reporting.
- [ ] Define and enforce an ingestion agreement that requires each event to have a consistent, stable identifier and that the original event time is kept exactly, with enough detail (granularity) to distinguish events accurately.

### AU-8 — Time Stamps (Control)

This expectation is about making sure every official activity record has the correct time, so the records can be trusted and properly investigated. If system clocks are wrong or do not match the organisation’s approved time source, audit records may appear in the wrong order. To meet this expectation, the organisation must ensure audit records are time-stamped using the system’s own internal clock, but that clock must be accurate to within 10 milliseconds of the organisation-approved time source. The recorded time must be written in a clear and consistent way: either using Coordinated Universal Time (UTC), or using a fixed local time offset from UTC, or explicitly including that local offset as part of the timestamp.

The organisation should document how this is done and configure systems so the timestamp format and time source settings match the documented approach.

#### AU-9.1 — Hardware Write-once Media (Enhancement)

This requirement is about ensuring that your important records of system activity are written so they cannot be changed after the fact. These records are used to spot suspicious behaviour, investigate what happened, and report it. If the records can be altered, overwritten, or reused, wrongdoing could be hidden and investigations could be weakened.

To meet this expectation, the organisation must write the first set of audit trail records used for detection, analysis and reporting, and also any backups of those audit trails, onto hardware-enforced write-once, read-many media such as CD-R, DVD-R or BD-R. With this type of media, the media itself prevents changes once writing is finished.

The organisation must not use only write-protected or re-writable media such as USB drives, tape cartridges, CD-RW or DVD-RW for this purpose.

The organisation must document which media is used, where it is stored, the steps and responsibilities for writing and protecting it, and be able to provide evidence that the initial audit trail and backups were actually written to the correct write-once media.

##### Write initial audit trails and backups to hardware WORM media

- Category: Software
- Priority: Critical

###### WHAT
Create the **initial audit trail** (the set of audit records used for detection, analysis, and reporting) and its **audit trail backups** on **hardware-enforced write-once read-many (WORM) media** (for example, CD-R, DVD-R, BD-R). This ensures the media cannot be changed after writing is finished.

###### WHY (control requirement)
The National Institute of Standards and Technology (NIST) Special Publication (SP) 800-53 **AU-9(1)** enhancement requires: **“Write audit trails to hardware-enforced, write-once media.”** The guidance means this applies to the **first time audit trails are produced** (the audit trail packages) and the **backup of those audit trails**, and **does not** apply to raw audit records before they are written into an audit trail. It also explains that **WORM** is different from simply **write-protected** or **re-writable** media (for example, USB drives, tape, CD-RW, DVD-RW).

###### HOW (specific steps/approach)
Set up an auditable “package-to-WORM” process where the system makes only the **final write** to approved WORM media, using a clear **finalise/close** step that locks the content so it cannot be altered.

1. **Choose and document the allowed WORM media types**: Select the specific hardware-enforced WORM media types allowed for storing audit trails (for example, CD-R, DVD-R, BD-R). Record the exact media identifiers/part numbers and where the media will be stored.
2. **Define where the audit trail packaging starts and ends**: Make sure the system produces **audit trail packages** (time-bounded batches) that represent the initial audit trail used for detection, analysis, and reporting. Do not try to write the raw event outputs directly to WORM.
3. **Only write to WORM at the final stage**: Configure the security information and event management (SIEM) / logging system or audit archiving process so packages are prepared in a controlled area that can be changed, and then the **final write** happens only to the WORM device/media.
4. **Force hardware finalisation/closure**: Configure the WORM writing process to run the device-specific **finalise/close** operation after a successful write. If finalisation fails, treat it as a control exception and do **not** mark the package as “archived”.
5. **Back up to WORM using the same approach**: Copy or back up each completed audit trail package to a second WORM media set (or a second WORM target) using the same finalise/close process.
6. **Create and keep proof (evidence) for each package**: For every package, store an unchangeable record (manifest) that includes the package ID, time window, hash(es), WORM media identifier(s), writer/device identifier, and whether finalisation succeeded. Keep these manifests in an auditable location for review by the assessor.
7. **Stop use of non-write-once media**: Use technical and procedural controls to ensure the final write destination cannot be changed to USB, tape, CD-RW, or DVD-RW (for example, restrict allowed writer targets, enforce workflow permissions, and check the media type before the final write).

###### WHO
- **Security/Compliance Lead**: Approves the allowed WORM media types, the retention approach, and the evidence requirements.
- **Security information and event management (SIEM) / Logging Platform Owner** (or **Log Management Engineer**): Builds the package-to-WORM workflow and ensures finalise/close is enforced.
- **IT Operations / Archive Administrator**: Handles media in a controlled way and maintains the media inventory and storage locations.
- **Internal Audit / Assurance**: Checks the evidence (manifests, finalisation logs, media identifiers) during assessment.

###### Acceptance criteria
- The initial audit trail packages and their backups are written to **hardware-enforced WORM media** (approved CD-R/DVD-R/BD-R) and each package has a recorded successful **finalise/close**.
- No audit trail packages are marked as “archived” unless the WORM finalisation step succeeded.
- Evidence exists for each package: a manifest with hash(es), time window, WORM media identifier(s), writer/device identifier, and the finalisation result.
- The workflow prevents final writes to non-write-once media (USB, tape, CD-RW, DVD-RW) and raises exceptions if an invalid destination or media type is selected.
- Storage locations and responsibilities for handling media are documented and followed.

###### Actions Required for Compliance

- [ ] Define and get approval for the approved list of write-once, read-many (WORM) media types that are protected by hardware (for example, CD-R, DVD-R, and BD-R), and document where this data is stored and who is responsible for it.
- [ ] Set up an audit-trail packaging process that creates the first audit-trail packages as time-limited batches for detection, analysis, and reporting.
- [ ] Set up the logging and security information and event management (SIEM) export process so that packages are first prepared in a controlled, changeable staging area, and then written only once to the write-once, read-many (WORM) target.
- [ ] After every successful write to write-once, read-many (WORM) storage, complete a device-specific “finalise/close” step. If that finalise/close step does not succeed, stop and fail the archive process.
- [ ] Set up automatic backup and replication of each completed audit trail package to a second write-once, read-many (WORM) media set, using the same finalise and close steps.
- [ ] Create unchangeable, per-package evidence records (including hashes, the time window, write-once read-many (WORM) media identifiers, writer/device ID, and the finalisation outcome) and keep them for audit purposes.
- [ ] Set up technical allow-listing and checks so that final writes are only allowed to write-once media (for example, USB, tape, CD-R, DVD-R). If a selection is not valid, raise an exception.

This requirement is about keeping records of system activity in a location that is physically separate from the systems being monitored. - Priority: Critical

#### AU-9.3 — Cryptographic Protection (Enhancement)

This requirement is about making sure your audit records, and the tools used to create and check them, cannot be changed secretly without anyone noticing. Without this, someone could tamper with evidence of what happened (or change the software that reviews it), and you might not find out until it is too late—damaging investigations, compliance, and confidence in decisions.

To meet this expectation, the organisation must use approved cryptographic integrity protection. This means both the audit information and the audit tools are protected with digitally signed checks. A secret signing key is used to create a verifiable integrity mark, and a publicly available verification key allows authorised people to confirm later that nothing has been altered.

The secret signing key must be kept secure and protected from unauthorised access. The public verification information must be available for verification.

The organisation must document how this is done, store the integrity evidence together with the audit information and tools, and ensure that any integrity check failure is clearly flagged and recorded with enough detail to identify what was affected and when.

##### Asymmetric signatures for audit records and verification evidence

- Category: Software
- Priority: Critical

###### WHAT
Put in place cryptographic controls to protect the **integrity of audit information**. This means creating **signed hash evidence** for audit records and using automated checks that produce clear, auditable **pass/fail** results.

###### WHY (control requirement)
NIST AU-9(3) (Enhancement) requires: **“Implement cryptographic mechanisms to protect the integrity of audit information and audit tools.”** The guidance specifies using **signed hash functions with asymmetric cryptography**, where a **secret key** is used to sign and a **public key** is used to verify. This allows unauthorised changes to audit information to be detected.

###### HOW (specific steps/approach)
1. **Define what “audit information” covers**: decide which audit events and fields count for this application (for example: event type, user or system identity, target, time, source system, schema version, and a stable event identifier).
2. **Standardise the audit content before hashing**: convert each audit record into a consistent, repeatable format before creating the hash (for example, canonical JSON with a fixed key order, a consistent time format, and consistent encoding). This prevents attackers from bypassing checks by changing formatting.
3. **Create signed integrity evidence**: for each audit record (or a justified batch of records), calculate a hash of the standardised content and sign it using an **approved asymmetric algorithm** (for example, ECDSA or RSASSA-PSS with SHA-256/384). Store the signature (or signed hash) with the audit record.
4. **Safeguard the signing secret key**: perform signing through a dedicated signing service protected by a hardware security module (HSM) or key management service (KMS), or an equivalent enterprise key management approach. Use strict access controls and separate responsibilities.
5. **Make verification information available**: provide the matching **public verification key/certificate** to those who verify (for example, via a read-only certificate store or a signed metadata endpoint). Also record the key identifier and version with each signature.
6. **Verify and record outcomes**: run verification jobs (scheduled and as needed) that check signatures using the public key. Record the results as **separate auditable events** (pass/fail, key identifier, record identifier, time, and the reason for any failure).
7. **Treat verification failures as evidence**: if verification fails or signatures are missing, raise an alert and record enough detail to show what was affected and when (record identifier, source, and whether the signature was expected vs. present/valid).
8. **Document the approach**: document the signing and verification design, the standardisation method, who is responsible for key handling, and the operational steps for key rotation and handling verification failures.

###### WHO
- **Application owner / Security architect**: defines the audit scope and the integrity model.
- **Platform/infrastructure team**: implements the signing service integration with HSM/KMS and manages the key lifecycle.
- **Developers**: implement standardisation, attach the signatures, and integrate the verification job.
- **Security operations centre (SOC) / security information and event management (SIEM) operations**: monitor verification failure alerts and ensure evidence is retained.

###### Acceptance criteria
- Every selected audit record includes cryptographic integrity evidence (signed hash/signature) created using an asymmetric signing key.
- Verification using the published public key succeeds for records that have not been altered and fails for altered or missing-signature records.
- Signing keys are stored and used only through an approved key management mechanism (HSM/KMS) with restricted access.
- Verification results (pass/fail) are generated and kept as auditable events with traceable record identifiers and failure reasons.
- Documentation exists covering standardisation, signing and verification workflow, key handling, and key rotation/incident procedures.

###### Actions Required for Compliance

- [ ] Select and document which audit events and data fields are in scope for cryptographic integrity protection.
- [ ] Apply a consistent, repeatable format to audit records before creating the hash (a unique digital fingerprint).
- [ ] Use an approved digital signing service that uses hardware-backed key management (hardware security module (HSM) or key management service (KMS)) to sign a unique “fingerprint” (hash) for each record, or for a justified batch of records.
- [ ] Store the signature and the key identifier with every audit record in the audit store.
- [ ] Publish the matching public verification key or certificate, and make sure verifiers can reliably find and download it.
- [ ] Set up automated checks that verify signatures and record clear, auditable pass or fail results as verification events.
- [ ] Create procedures for raising alerts and handling evidence when a signature is missing or invalid, including the record identifier and the reason for the failure.

#### AU-9.4 — Access by Subset of Privileged Users (Enhancement)

This requirement is about ensuring that only a very small, approved group of people can control the system’s audit log settings and the process that records audit events. This matters because if other highly trusted staff could change or interfere with audit logging, they might accidentally or deliberately stop records being created, or change what the records show. That would make it harder to investigate issues or confirm what happened.

To meet this expectation, the organisation must formally authorise audit log management only for audit log administrators (the people who run the audit log collection and processing process) and the system security officer. Everyone else with privileged access must be blocked from making changes to audit logging.

The organisation must also keep an up-to-date list of who is allowed to manage audit logging, and ensure the system’s access permissions match that list exactly. The organisation must document the access rules clearly so that access can be checked and verified over time.

###### HOW (specific steps/approach)

2. **Use deny-by-default access for audit logging management** (the user interface, application programming interface (API), command line interface (CLI), or console used to manage audit logging). Give management permissions only to those two role categories, and make sure everyone else is explicitly denied.
3. **Assign those roles to the exact audit logging management permissions** in the audit logging platform(s) (for example, SIEM, log pipeline/orchestration, and audit log retention/forwarding controls). 5. **Keep an auditable, reconciled list of the “effective authorised subset”** by exporting (a) IdP group/role membership and (b) the audit platform’s effective management authorisations, then comparing them and alerting on any differences.

- The organisation can provide audit-ready evidence showing the authorised subset (IdP membership) and the effective management authorisations in the audit logging platform, including reconciliation results that show no drift.
- Audit logging management actions are recorded with enough detail (person, action, target, time) and are monitored with alerts for unauthorised attempts.

###### Actions Required for Compliance

- [ ] Create identity provider (IdP) roles and groups for `AuditLoggingAdmin` and `SystemSecurityOfficer` to match au-09.04_odp

- [ ] Require stronger “step-up” authentication and apply conditional access rules when making changes to audit logging settings.

#### AU-9.5 — Dual Authorization (Enhancement)

This requirement is about making sure that no single person can quietly change the records that show what happened on your most sensitive on-premises systems. Specifically, any movement or deletion of audit log records for security-relevant events on systems that handle Confidential and Secret data must be approved by two different authorised people.

Without this “two-person” safeguard, a dishonest or compromised insider could remove or alter evidence, making wrongdoing harder to detect, investigate, or prove.

To meet this expectation, the organisation must:
- clearly state that dual approval applies to both moving and deleting those audit records
- enforce a process that prevents the change unless two authorised individuals approve it
- keep evidence showing who approved the change and when

The organisation should also rotate which people carry out these dual approvals over time to reduce the risk of collusion. An exception should only be allowed when immediate action is needed to protect public and environmental safety. In that case, the reason for the emergency must be recorded, and the change must still be handled through an approved process.

##### Two-person approval for audit log movement/deletion (Conf/Secret)

- Category: Software
- Priority: Critical

###### WHAT
Put in place a “two-person control” process for **moving** and **deleting** **security-relevant audit log records** on **on-premises systems** that handle **Confidential** and **Secret** information. The system must **block the change** unless **two different authorised people** approve the **exact same action**.

###### WHY (control requirement)
This requirement comes from **NIST AU-9(5)**, which says: **“Enforce dual authorization for {{ param, au-09.05_odp.01 }} of {{ param, au-09.05_odp.02 }}.”** The guidance also requires two-person approval to reduce the risk of collusion. It allows **rotation of who performs the two approvals**, and allows an exception only when **immediate action is needed to protect public and environmental safety**.

###### HOW (specific steps/approach)
1. **Define what actions and systems are covered** in a way that can be audited:
   - Scope AU-09.05_odp.01 = **movement, deletion**.
   - Scope AU-09.05_odp.02 = **audit log records for security-relevant events** on the **on-premises systems** handling **Confidential** and **Secret** data.
   - Clearly state what counts as “movement” (for example: archiving/exporting/replicating to another storage location) and what counts as “deletion” (for example: purging or overriding retention rules).
2. **Set up one control point** for log movement/deletion on each eligible on-premises log platform (for example: the log storage system, the security monitoring system’s ingestion/archiving component, or privileged log management tools):
   - Stop people from making direct administrative changes.
   - Use a **controlled execution tool** (for example, an application programming interface (API) or command-line interface (CLI) wrapper) that only accepts a **short-lived, signed authorisation token**.
   - The tool must check the token and **refuse** the action if the required two-person approval evidence is missing or invalid.
3. **Create a two-person approval workflow** (using the organisation’s service management and approval system, plus orchestration):
   - Require **two different approvers** selected from an **authorised eligibility list**.
   - Record and link the approvals to the request, including: the target system, log type, record identifiers and/or time range, the destination for movement, and the deletion scope.
   - Ensure the workflow cannot reach an “execute” state until **both approvals** are present.
4. **Keep audit evidence linking approvals to the action**:
   - Store unchangeable records showing the request ID, both approvers’ identities, approval times, the exact parameters approved, and the token ID used to carry out the action.
   - Ensure the log platform also records that the operation happened and links it back to the workflow request ID.
5. **Rotate who can approve** to reduce collusion risk:
   - Use an eligibility list rotation schedule (for example, quarterly).
   - Only allow approvers from the current eligible set.
   - Keep evidence of rotation (the schedule and which individuals were eligible for each approval).
6. **Implement a limited emergency safety exception**:
   - Provide an emergency route only for **public/environmental safety**.
   - Require the emergency request to include a justification, while still producing auditable evidence.
   - After the emergency action, require a follow-up review/attestation through the two-person approval process (or an equivalent governance review) within a defined time window.

###### WHO
- **Security Governance Lead / Compliance Owner**: defines the scope, the exception conditions, and the rotation policy.
- **Platform Owner (security information and event management (SIEM)/log platform)**: implements the control point (the execution wrapper/API validation) and ensures actions are routed through it.
- **Identity and Access Management (IAM) Lead**: maintains the authorised approver eligibility list and the multi-factor authentication (MFA) requirements.
- **Security Operations Centre (SOC)/Operations Manager**: runs the workflow, ensures emergency exceptions are used correctly, and completes the post-emergency review.

###### Acceptance criteria
- For any in-scope **movement** or **deletion** of security-relevant audit logs on eligible on-premises **Confidential/Secret** systems, the operation **cannot be carried out** unless there are **two distinct authorised approvers**.
- Any execution attempt without valid dual-approval tokens must be **rejected** by the control point.
- For every executed operation, there must be **audit evidence** showing both approvers, timestamps, identities, and the exact approved parameters, and the log platform must reference the workflow request.
- Two-person approval duties must be **rotated** according to the defined schedule, and rotation evidence must be available for audit.
- Emergency exceptions must be available only for **public/environmental safety**, must include justification, and must have auditable follow-up review evidence.

###### Actions Required for Compliance

- [ ] Define and document what counts as “movement” and “deletion” in the in-scope security-relevant audit log records for Confidential and Secret on-premises systems.
- [ ] Set up a controlled process (wrapper/API) for moving or deleting logs that only allows the action when a valid, signed, short-lived authorisation token with approval from two people is provided.
- [ ] Set up a process that requires two different authorised approvers to approve each request, and make the approval apply only to the exact system and log details, time range, and destination specified.
- [ ] Set up permanent (immutable) audit logs that record: the identity of both approvers, the date and time of approval, the approved settings, and the token or request ID used to carry out the execution.
- [ ] Create and enforce an approved list of eligible approvers, with scheduled rotation and retained proof of approval records.
- [ ] Create an emergency “exception” route that can only be used to protect public or environmental safety, with a required written justification and a governance review after the action is taken.

### AU-10 — Non-repudiation (Control)

This requirement is about making sure the organisation can prove, with clear and hard-to-dispute evidence, who did what—for example, creating information, sending and receiving messages, approving information, signing documents, and confirming receipt of specific information. Without this, people could later claim they never created, sent, received, approved, or signed something, which would leave the organisation unable to resolve disputes, meet legal or regulatory duties, or investigate wrongdoing.

To meet this, the organisation must use reliable methods so that every relevant action is recorded with the person’s identity (or a clearly authorised process acting on their behalf), the exact time, and enough detail to identify what the action related to. The evidence must be stored in official system audit records (or equivalent controlled records), protected from unauthorised changes, and kept in a way that can be checked later.

The organisation must also document how the evidence is produced, assign named responsibilities, and include this requirement in its audit and accountability, system planning, and privacy planning where applicable.

##### Tamper-evident audit evidence for create/send/receive/approve/sign actions

- Category: Software
- Priority: Critical

###### WHAT must be done
Set up non-repudiation evidence for the organisation-defined **actions** (creating information, sending and receiving messages, approving information, signing documents, and receiving specific information). This must allow the organisation to provide **irrefutable evidence** that a specific person (or an authorised process acting for a person) carried out those actions.

###### WHY (control requirement)
NIST SP 800-53 **AU-10** requires: “Provide irrefutable evidence that an individual (or process acting on behalf of an individual) has performed {{param, au-10_odp}}.” The guidance explains that non-repudiation helps prevent someone from denying that they authored/sent/received/approved/signed. Evidence can be produced using methods such as **digital signatures** and **digital message receipts**.

###### HOW (specific steps/approach)
1. **Define the non-repudiation action catalogue** in the application workflow, mapping each AU-10 action to a specific evidence type:
   - **Signing / approving**: cryptographic digital signature evidence linked to the exact document/version (hash/fingerprint) and the signer’s identity.
   - **Creating information**: evidence recorded as an unchangeable “creation receipt” showing the creator’s identity, the time, and the identifier of the created record.

#### AU-10.1 — Association of Identities (Enhancement)

This requirement ensures that when confidential or secret information is created or sent, it is clearly and strongly linked to the exact person or system that produced it, and that the proof cannot be changed later without detection. Without this, someone could deny creating or sending a particular document or message, or the organisation could be unable to prove who was responsible if the information is misused, leaked, or disputed.

To meet this expectation, the organisation must use strong, verifiable digital signatures for confidential and secret information, and must store the identity of the producer and the supporting proof in records that cannot be altered once written. The organisation must also provide a way for authorised people to look up and verify who produced specific information, using the stored signatures and audit evidence, with access limited to those authorised to carry out these checks.

##### Cryptographically bind producer identity to Confidential/Secret content

- Category: Software
- Priority: Critical

###### WHAT

###### WHY (control requirement)

2. **Cryptographic signing**: create a **digital signature** for a standard (canonical) version of the item. #### AU-10.2 — Validate Binding of Information Producer Identity (Enhancement)

This requirement is about ensuring that every document or record you produce for later review clearly and permanently shows who originally created it, and that nobody can quietly change that creator information in the meantime. Without this check, someone could tamper with records so they appear to have been produced by a different person or system, which would undermine trust, accountability, and any investigation that depends on those records.

To meet this expectation, the organisation must, every time information is produced for review, verify that the original creator identity is correctly linked to the information using a method that can be repeated and independently checked. If the verification fails, the organisation must quarantine the affected information, notify the Security Operations Centre, and start incident response within 24 hours. It must also keep clear records of the validation results, the evidence used to reach those results, and the actions taken after any failure.

##### Cryptographically sign producer identity and verify at review time

- Category: Software
- Priority: Critical

###### WHAT
Set up a cryptographic link between each information item’s **creator identity** and the **content** (for example, by having the creator sign a hash of the content in a standard form). Then **check that link every time the item is prepared for review**.

If the check fails, the item must be **quarantined**, the **security operations centre (SOC)** notified, and **incident response started within 24 hours**.

###### WHY (control requirement)
The AU-10(2) enhancement guidance requires checking that the **information creator identity** is correctly linked to the information, to prevent changes between when it is produced and when it is reviewed. This check must happen **at the defined frequency** (organisation parameter: **every time information is produced for review**). The organisation must also define what happens when the check fails (organisation parameter: **quarantine, notify SOC, start incident response within 24 hours**). Cryptographic checksums and signatures are explicitly acceptable methods.

###### HOW (specific approach)
###### 1) Define a consistent “review payload” for hashing
- Create a standard way to convert the information that will be reviewed into a form suitable for hashing (for example, canonical JSON rules, consistent field ordering, and consistent encoding).
- Calculate a **content hash** (for example, SHA-256) from the standardised version.

###### 2) Link creator identity using cryptographic signing when producing the item
- When the information is produced, create a **creator identity assertion** by signing the following set:
  - `content_hash`, `producer_identity`, `signing_time`, and a `review_item_id` (or another equivalent unchangeable identifier).
- Save with the produced item:
  - `producer_identity`, `content_hash`, `signature`, `signing_time`, and (where applicable) a certificate chain reference.

###### 3) Validate the link every time the item is prepared for review
- Build an automated “review preparation” gate that, for each item:
  - recalculates `content_hash` from the item being prepared for review,
  - checks the signature matches the expected `producer_identity`,
  - records a **pass/fail** result and the verification evidence (hash, signature details, verification outcome, time of check).

###### 4) Quarantine and run the SOC/incident response process if validation fails
- If the check fails:
  - move the item to a **quarantine store** with restricted access and mark it **do not review**,
  - notify the **SOC** using the organisation’s standard alerting and automation process,
  - create/trigger an incident and ensure **incident response is started within 24 hours** (set an SLA timer in the workflow).

###### 5) Keep audit evidence for independent checking
- Record the validation results and the actions taken after failure in an **append-only/immutable audit record** (for example, write-once storage or immutable logging), including:
  - item identifier, recalculated hash, expected creator identity, verification result, and the times of quarantine/notification/incident actions.

- **Identity and public key infrastructure (PKI) team**: manage signing keys/certificates and ensure the correct mapping between creator identity and keys.

###### Acceptance criteria
- For every item produced for review, the system performs cryptographic verification of the creator identity-to-content link (no item ready for review can bypass the gate).
- For each review item, the system records verification evidence (recalculated hash, creator identity, signature verification result, and timestamps).
- If verification fails, the item is quarantined (cannot be used for review) and the SOC is notified; an incident is created and incident response is started within **24 hours**.
- Audit records show end-to-end traceability from production signing to review-time validation and any quarantine/SOC/incident response actions.

#### AU-10.3 — Chain of Custody (Enhancement)

This requirement is about ensuring that, every time someone reviews or releases information, the organisation can later prove exactly who did it and under which approved identity. This must be recorded as part of the item’s documented history. Without this, it is easier for the wrong person to be credited, for approvals to be impersonated or changed, or for an unapproved automated check to be used—potentially leading to unauthorised information being shared and making responsibility disputes difficult to resolve.

To meet this expectation, the organisation must keep a clear record of who handled each reviewed or released item. This means linking the reviewer and releaser identities (and their credentials) to the specific information item and its label, and recording when the action happened and why the information was transferred. If the review is automated, the system must ensure only approved automated review steps are used, and that the identity of the automated step is recorded before the information is released.

- Priority: Critical

   - (if applicable) `destination/recipient` as part of the transfer context

- Release is blocked if any required chain-of-custody fields are missing, or if the review step is not completed.

### AU-11 — Audit Record Retention (Control)

This requirement is about keeping a clear record of what happened, for long enough to prove it later. To achieve this, the organisation should have written rules and step-by-step procedures for how records are kept, stored, and disposed of. It should define common categories of records linked to these types of requests, assign named responsibilities, involve the appropriate privacy and information-handling roles in decisions, and ensure system administrators carry out the storage and retrieval work needed to keep records available.

#### AU-12.1 — System-wide and Time-correlated Audit Trail (Enhancement)

This requirement ensures that when important security events happen, the organisation can put together one complete, accurate account of what occurred across all its systems, with the timing aligned correctly. Without this, records from different systems may be missing, not match up in time, or be difficult to compare, which makes it harder to investigate incidents, confirm what happened, or identify suspicious activity in the correct order.

To meet this expectation, the organisation must collect security-relevant event records from every relevant source, including operating systems, applications, network devices, identity services, and cloud or on-premises gateways, and compile them into a single, organisation-wide audit trail. Each record must include a reliable date and time, using the same time basis across all sources, so that related events can be ordered correctly and their timestamps match within 5 seconds.

The organisation should also be able to provide evidence that all required sources are included and that the timing is accurate.

##### Time-correlated system-wide audit trail via normalised UTC ingest

- Category: Software
- Priority: Critical

###### WHAT
Set up a system-wide (logical) audit trail by collecting security-relevant audit records from all in-scope system components and sending them to one central logging and security information and event management (SIEM) platform. ###### WHY (control requirement)
AU-12(1) enhancement requires the organisation to “compile audit records from {{ insert: param, au-12.01_odp.01 }} into a system-wide (logical or physical) audit trail that is time-correlated to within {{ insert: param, au-12.01_odp.02 }}.” Time correlation means timestamps in individual records can be reliably linked to other records, so the correct time order can be established within the organisation’s allowed limits.

###### HOW (specific steps/approach)
1. **Define and enforce the correct time source**: Choose an approved authoritative time source (for example, redundant network time protocol (NTP) servers; consider precision time protocol (PTP) where needed). Require all in-scope systems that produce logs (operating systems, applications, network devices, identity services, and cloud or on-premises gateways) to synchronise to it. 3. **Use the event’s own time (do not replace it)**: Configure the intake process to read each event’s native event timestamp (not the time it was received), convert it to UTC, and store both **event_time_utc** and **event_time_original** (including timezone or offset details where available). 4. **Use one consistent audit trail structure**: Configure the SIEM/log platform to store all security events in one unified structure, with consistent fields for event time, source identity (host/device/service), event type, and user or session identifiers.
5. **Demonstrate time matching within the allowed limit (≤ 5 seconds)**: Use a repeatable test that performs coordinated security-relevant actions across multiple components (for example, identity sign-in → application access → operating system, network, and gateway audit events). Check the difference between the earliest and latest **event_time_utc** for the same test scenario, and show it is consistently within **5 seconds**.
6. **Run ongoing checks and keep proof**: Add automated checks that continuously monitor timestamp quality (for example, signs of clock drift, missing timestamp rates, and out-of-order detection). Keep the results of these checks as evidence for audit.

- For every collected event, a dependable timestamp is present and stored as **event_time_utc**, taken from the event’s native timestamp (not the time the log was received), with the original timestamp preserved.
- A repeatable test demonstrates that time ordering across representative component types is achievable with timestamp matching within **au-12.01_odp.02 (within 5 seconds)**.
- Timestamp quality monitoring is in place (for example, missing or invalid timestamp detection), and evidence that the validation tests were run is retained for audit.

###### Actions Required for Compliance

- [ ] Create a list that maps each asset to its log source, covering operating systems, applications, network devices, identity services, and cloud and on-premises gateways.

#### AU-12.4 — Query Parameter Audits of Personally Identifiable Information (Enhancement)

This requirement is about keeping a clear record of the exact search or retrieval criteria used whenever someone looks up personal information in your systems. Without this, it is difficult to spot misuse, investigate concerns, or show that only the right people accessed personal data for legitimate reasons—because you may know that a search happened, but not what it was searching for.

The organisation must set up and run an auditing feature that applies to any user or automated job that searches datasets known to contain personal identifiers. The system must capture the specific search details used and record them in the organisation’s audit record store for later review. The organisation must also document how and when these audit records are created, what they should include, and how the system is designed and configured to do this, including assigning named responsibilities to staff for creating and overseeing these records.

Finally, the organisation must be able to show that audit records are created automatically, are complete, and are kept for the required period.

##### PII query-parameter audit logging for qualifying query events

- Category: Software
- Priority: Critical

###### WHAT
Set up an automated auditing capability that records the **exact query settings** used in **user (or automated) query events** when those queries access **datasets that contain personal data (personally identifiable information (PII))**.

###### WHY (control requirement)
AU-12(4) enhancement requires the organisation to **provide and implement** the capability to **audit query settings** for **datasets containing PII**, so authorised staff can track and understand how PII is accessed, used, and shared.

###### HOW (specific approach)
1. **Define when auditing applies**: create a documented mapping from your data catalogue/data classification to a simple “PII dataset scope” flag (yes/no). Define “qualifying query events” as the application parts that run parameter-based data retrieval (for example, a user interface search, an application programming interface (API) query, or scheduled data extract jobs) against datasets within that scope.
2. **Add auditing to the query process**: add a middleware/component in the service that builds and runs queries (for example, a Structured Query Language (SQL) builder or search query builder). For qualifying events, it must create an audit record that includes:
   - event date and time
   - who did it (user or service account) and a session/request correlation identifier
   - which dataset was accessed (for example, table/index/report name or a logical dataset identifier)
   - type of query (search, filter, export, background retrieval)
   - the **exact query settings** in a structured format (for example, a list of {field, operator, value} or an equivalent representation)

   - encrypt query values when stored, using keys limited to the audit-review role; and/or
   - replace values with tokens (only reversible by approved key holders); and/or
   - use hashes when only “matches” (for example, equality/containment) need to be audited, while still recording the field/operator.
4. **Send audit records to the audit system**: send audit events to the organisation’s central audit pipeline/security information and event management (SIEM) ingestion endpoint configured for write-once, tamper-evident storage and restricted access. ### AU-13 — Monitoring for Information Disclosure (Control)

This requirement is about keeping a close daily watch on public places where information could be shared by accident or on purpose, such as public code websites, paste websites, and social networking pages. The goal is to spot any unauthorised release of the organisation’s information. Without this, sensitive details—such as personal information the organisation holds or valuable proprietary work—could be exposed to the public. This could harm customers, staff, and the business, and lead to legal and reputational damage.

To meet this expectation, the organisation must check these public sources every day for signs of unauthorised disclosure. If anything suspicious is found, the organisation must promptly alert the incident response lead and the owner of the affected system, escalate the issue to the organisation’s specialist response team, create an incident record, preserve evidence of what was seen and where, assess the potential impact of the disclosure, and begin requests to have the content removed from the relevant platform.

- Category: Software
- Priority: Critical

###### WHAT

###### WHY (control requirement)
AU-13 requires monitoring for information disclosure on open-source/public sites and ensuring that any suspected unauthorised disclosure leads to prompt notification and response. The organisation’s defined parameters specify: (1) which public sites to monitor, (2) the monitoring frequency (daily), (3) who must be notified, and (4) the follow-up actions (SOC escalation, incident creation, evidence preservation, impact assessment, and takedown requests).

###### HOW (specific steps/approach)
1. **Define monitoring scope and indicators (as code):** Create a version-controlled set of rules that lists the exact public sites to monitor (for example, GitHub/public code repositories, paste sites, and social networking sites) and the detection indicators for organisational information (for example, organisation identifiers, project names, customer names, known patterns that indicate proprietary material, and personal data (PII)/secret indicators).
2. **Run scheduled daily monitoring jobs:** Set up daily scheduled searches across the defined site categories using approved open-source intelligence (OSINT) and/or search tools and/or platform application programming interfaces (APIs). Make sure each category is checked every day.
3. **Combine results into a single evidence record:** For each match, record and store: source type (code/paste/social), URL, author/account (if available), time first seen/timestamp, matched indicator, and a confidence score.
4. **Start the SOC/SIEM/SOAR case workflow for suspected disclosures:** If results are above an agreed confidence threshold, automatically create a SOC case/ticket and link it to the incident workflow.
5. **Preserve evidence for audit purposes:** Automatically capture the permitted evidence items (for example, page snapshots/archived copies where allowed, plus metadata exports) and store them in an evidence store that cannot be changed, with retention aligned to incident handling requirements.
6. **Notify the required roles immediately:** Automatically notify the **incident response lead** and the **information system owner** with clear, actionable details (what was found, where, when, and the basis for the confidence/indicator).
7. **Carry out a structured impact assessment and start takedown actions:** Within the incident workflow, require a triage/impact assessment (for example, whether personal data (PII) is present, whether proprietary information/credentials are involved, and which systems are affected). Then follow a documented takedown playbook to submit takedown requests to the relevant platform and track submission IDs and outcomes.

###### WHO

###### Acceptance criteria

- For any suspected disclosure that meets the configured threshold, the system automatically:
  - notifies the incident response lead and information system owner,
  - escalates to the SOC and opens an incident,
  - preserves evidence (URL + timestamp + captured evidence items/metadata),
  - records an impact assessment outcome, and
  - initiates takedown requests with tracked status.
- Evidence records are unchangeable and can be retrieved for audit.
- Monitoring rules/indicators are version-controlled and include a change history.

###### Actions Required for Compliance

- [ ] Define and version-control the categories of open-source websites that are monitored, and the detection indicators used to identify relevant activity.
- [ ] Set up and run daily open-source intelligence (OSINT) monitoring tasks for each defined site category.
- [ ] Set up a standard evidence format that records the web address (URL), dates and times, the author or account, the relevant indicator, and the confidence level; and store the evidence in an unchangeable (immutable) way.
- [ ] Integrate monitoring results with the security operations centre (SOC) case workflow, using security information and event management (SIEM) and security orchestration, automation and response (SOAR), to automatically create incidents when they exceed the threshold.

#### AU-13.3 — Unauthorized Replication of Information (Enhancement)

This requirement is about actively checking whether other people are copying your information or pretending to be you, especially online. If unauthorised copying or impersonation is not noticed, customers and partners can be misled, scams can spread, and your organisation’s reputation and day-to-day operations can be harmed.

To meet this expectation, the organisation must set out a clear, documented approach for finding unauthorised copying by external parties. This includes defining what counts as unauthorised copying or impersonation. The organisation should use repeatable methods and supporting tools to look for signs of misuse, such as scanning external websites for lookalike pages and monitoring public sources like social media for suspicious accounts or posts.

Named staff must be responsible for carrying out these checks, with input from those responsible for information security and privacy. Relevant staff must also be trained to recognise warning signs and to know how to report and escalate any findings. - Category: Software
- Priority: Critical

- Unauthorised copying candidates are **sent through a documented escalation route** with recorded actions (removal requests or formal clearance).
- Scan scope and detection thresholds are **version-controlled**, and changes are reviewable.
- (If social media monitoring is included) social media candidates are handled through the same evidence-and-investigation workflow.

### AU-14 — Session Audit (Control)

This requirement is about being able to record and later review what a user did during a specific work session when there are serious concerns, such as a confirmed or suspected security incident, an insider threat, or unauthorised access involving confidential or highly sensitive information. Without this, the organisation may struggle to understand what happened, who did it, and what information was affected. That can delay containment, weaken investigations, and increase the risk of the same harm happening again.

The organisation must set up and run session auditing so that, in these defined situations, only the incident response team lead, the system security officer, and authorised legal or compliance investigators can record, view, and log the session content. It must develop, integrate, and use these activities with legal counsel, in line with all relevant laws, regulations, internal policies, and privacy requirements. It must also document the approach, procedures, system design and settings, and how records are stored and handled, while actively managing privacy risks that could arise from capturing personal information.

##### Case-gated session recording with RBAC and privacy/legal controls

- Category: Software
- Priority: Critical

###### WHAT must be done
Set up **session auditing** that can **record, view, and log the content of a user session** when the organisation-defined **circumstances** apply (a confirmed or suspected security incident, an insider threat, or unauthorised access involving **Confidential/Secret** data). Ensure only **authorised roles** can activate this capability and access the resulting records.

###### WHY (control requirement)
AU-14 requires the organisation to provide **session auditing** (as well as event logging) that can capture session content, and to **turn it on only in clearly defined situations**. The guidance also requires addressing **privacy risk** and consulting **legal counsel, civil liberties officials, and privacy officials** so that legal, privacy, and civil rights/civil liberties issues (including use of personal data) are properly handled.

###### HOW (specific steps/approach)
1. **Define and enforce authorised roles** for session audit content (record/view/log) using enterprise **role-based access control (RBAC)** linked to identity (for example, identity provider (IdP) groups):
   - Incident response team lead
   - System security officer
   - Authorised legal/compliance investigators
2. **Create a case-based activation workflow** in the incident/case management system that requires:
   - Case/incident reference
   - Data classification = Confidential/Secret
   - Circumstance code = confirmed versus suspected incident/insider/unauthorised access
   - Legal/privacy approval indicator (or a reference to an approval record)
3. **Integrate a session recording capability** (session capture technology) so that recording can be **started and stopped only when the case workflow is “armed”** and the classification/circumstance matches the required policy. Store session recordings as **retrievable records** linked to:

   - the user session identifier
   - the activating user (auditor)
4. **Protect access to recordings** using the principle of least privilege:
   - Separate permissions for **record**, **view**, and **log/export** actions
   - Require re-checking identity (step-up authentication) before viewing and exporting
   - Ensure every view/export/download action is written to an **immutable audit trail** (and linked to the case ID)
5. **Implement privacy risk mitigations** before and after capture:
   - Configure capture scope to minimise non-relevant content where possible
   - Use redaction workflows for personal data (using legal/privacy-defined rules)
   - Apply retention limits and secure handling in line with the privacy plan
6. **Document and keep evidence of the approach**:
   - System security plan/design/configuration records showing activation conditions, RBAC mapping, storage locations, retention, and audit trail design
   - Evidence of legal/privacy consultation and the approval criteria
   - Operational runbook for activation, review, redaction, retention, and access

###### WHO (role responsible)

- **Incident Response Team Lead**: runs the case-based activation process and ensures circumstances/classification are recorded correctly.
- **Authorised Legal/Compliance Investigator**: provides/records the legal approval criteria and participates in review/redaction decisions.
- **Privacy Officer / Legal Counsel**: checks privacy risk mitigations, retention, and personal data handling rules.

###### Acceptance criteria
- Only the defined authorised roles can **record, view, and log** session audit content.
- Session recording activates **only** when a case is opened/armed with circumstances matching the defined criteria and the data classification is **Confidential/Secret**.
- Session recordings are **retrievable** and are securely and operationally linked to a specific **case ID** and **user session**.
- All actions (activation, view, export, and deletion/redaction where applicable) are logged to an immutable audit trail.
- Privacy mitigations (minimisation/redaction/retention) are implemented and evidenced through documented legal/privacy approval and configuration.

###### Actions Required for Compliance

- [ ] Define role-based access control (RBAC) roles for viewing, recording, and logging session audit information, and link those roles to enterprise identity groups.
- [ ] Set up a case-based activation process that requires an incident reference, a circumstance code, and a classification of either Confidential or Secret.
- [ ] Set the session recording system to start and stop only when the case workflow is active (armed) and the required policy conditions are met.
- [ ] Link each session recording to the case identifier and the user session identifier, then store it in a secure, access-controlled repository that can be retrieved when needed.

#### AU-14.1 — System Start-up (Enhancement)

This requirement is about ensuring the system automatically starts recording user activity every time it is turned on, without anyone needing to remember to enable it. If this does not happen reliably, key evidence about who did what after a restart could be missing or incomplete. That would make it harder to spot misuse, investigate incidents, or prove what happened—particularly if someone tries to interfere with the recording.

To meet this expectation, the organisation must:
- set up and document clear procedures for recording user sessions; and
- formally state in its approved policy and plans that session recording must start automatically when the system starts up.

The organisation must also include this in its security and privacy planning. It must ensure the system is designed to begin the recording mechanism during start-up (boot), configure the system accordingly using documented settings, and be able to demonstrate that new session records are created after a reboot using real user activity.

##### Boot-time initiation of session audit with post-reboot verification

- Category: Software
- Priority: Critical

###### WHAT
Automatically start **session audits** when the system boots, so that user session activity is recorded without needing manual steps.

###### WHY (control requirement)
AU-14(1) (Enhancement) requires that **session audits are started automatically at system start-up**. This guidance helps ensure the information captured is complete and lowers the risk of missing activity or records being altered after a restart.

###### HOW (specific steps/approach)
Set up the audit system used to record user sessions so it starts at boot, and automatically collect proof that it is creating audit records after the system restarts.

1. **Set the scope of session auditing** for your environment (for example, interactive logon/logoff, remote session start/stop) and match it to the audit events your platform can produce.
2. **Update governance documents** so the requirement is clear and can be checked:
   - Audit and Accountability Policy: state that session auditing must start automatically at system start-up.
   - System Security Plan and Privacy Plan: describe what session data is collected, how it is protected, and privacy handling and retention expectations.
3. **Configure the audit service/agent to start at boot** using your platform’s built-in method:
   - Linux: make sure `auditd` (or an equivalent) is enabled and starts automatically at boot (for example, using systemd enablement), and that the audit rules for session events are loaded during start-up.
   - Windows: make sure the relevant auditing policy is set and the auditing system is enabled; ensure any required services are set to start automatically.
   - Containerised/microservices workloads: make sure the node/host auditing mechanism is enabled at boot, and that any application-level session logging components are started automatically by the orchestration layer.
4. **Apply the configuration centrally** (to prevent changes going unnoticed): use configuration management (for example, Ansible/Puppet/Chef or an equivalent) to apply the audit settings and ensure the audit service is enabled during provisioning and during scheduled checks for consistency.
5. **Verify after boot that auditing is working and producing evidence**:
   - Add a start-up check that confirms the audit service/agent is running and that at least one expected session audit event is created within a defined time window after reboot.
   - Trigger an alert if the audit service is not running, or if no session audit events appear within the window.
6. **Protect audit records and send them to central logging**:
   - Send logs to the enterprise security information and event management (SIEM) or logging platform using authenticated transport (for example, TLS) and monitor that the logs are being received.

#### AU-14.3 — Remote Viewing and Listening (Enhancement)

This expectation is about allowing only approved staff to quietly watch and listen to what is happening in a user session that is already running, as it happens. ### AU-16 — Cross-organizational Audit Logging (Control)

This requirement is about making sure that when your organisation asks an external organisation for a service, there is a clear and trustworthy record of who requested it, when it happened, where it came from, and what was requested. Without this, it becomes difficult to investigate problems, confirm what was authorised, or detect misuse across organisational boundaries. Records could also be incomplete, changed, or shared in ways that breach agreements or privacy expectations.

To meet this expectation, your organisation must set up a coordinated way to share audit information with external organisations. This should use a consistent event format that the other organisation can understand, protect the records digitally so they can be checked for tampering, and send the information only through secure channels covered by approved information-sharing agreements.

Your organisation must also define and document the process for coordinating these exchanges, assign named responsibilities for coordination and for information security and privacy, and ensure that the system design, configuration, plans, and stored audit records reflect how cross-organisation auditing is carried out.

##### Cross-org audit event schema, signing, and secure exchange pipeline

- Category: Software
- Priority: Critical

###### WHAT
Set up a coordinated, cross-organisation approach for recording and sharing audit information when it is sent between organisations. The approach must use a standard audit event format, include the person who made the request (requestor identity), the time the event happened (timestamps), the system that generated it (source system identifiers), and the relevant audit event details. It must also protect audit records so they cannot be altered without detection, and share them only through secure methods that are covered by approved information-sharing agreements.

###### WHY (control requirement)
AU-16 requires the organisation to “employ methods for coordinating audit information among external organisations when audit information is transmitted across organisational boundaries” and to coordinate the defined “audit information” (requestor identity, timestamps, source system identifiers, and audit event details). Guidance expects a coordinated approach because keeping the same identity across organisational boundaries can be difficult and may raise privacy and performance concerns. Therefore, the approach should usually capture the requestor identity at the first system and ensure later systems record that the request came from an authorised person. It also expects processes to coordinate audit information requirements and to protect audit information within the information exchange agreements.

###### HOW (specific steps/approach)
1. **Define the cross-boundary audit event standard**: create a standard audit event structure for cross-boundary service requests that, at minimum, includes: requestor identity, timestamp, source system identifier, and audit event details (as per `au-16_odp.02`). Add correlation identifiers so the original request can be linked to later processing.
2. **Set an authoritative identity capture point**: make sure the first system (or service gateway) records the requestor identity when the request enters the system, and that downstream components record that the request came from an authorised identity (for example, using a stable requestor identifier and/or signed token claims).
3. **Digitally protect audit records**: sign audit records (or use per-record hashes with verifiable supporting information) before exporting them. Use an organisation-controlled signing key (preferably one backed by a hardware security module) and include key identifiers and signature details so recipients can confirm integrity and authenticity.
4. **Use secure, agreement-governed exchange**: export signed audit events to each external organisation only under approved information-sharing agreements and using secure transport (for example, mutually authenticated endpoints or approved managed connectors). Keep a list of approved partners/destinations and enforce encryption while data is in transit.
5. **Coordinate requirements and protection in the agreements**: for each external organisation, document in the information exchange agreement the required schema version, required fields, signing and verification expectations, retention and handling expectations, and privacy/data minimisation rules for any requestor identity information.
6. **Make verification and evidence operational**: when events are received (where applicable), verify signatures before accepting them. If verification fails, quarantine or reject the events and alert the security operations centre. Provide audit-ready evidence that the process enforces the correct format, signing, and secure transport (for example, configuration snapshots, run logs, and signature verification performance measures).

###### WHO (role responsible)
- **Security Architect / Compliance Lead**: owns the AU-16 method definition, the schema standard, and the agreement requirements.

- **SOC/Monitoring Lead**: owns how verification failures are handled, alerting, and evidence collection.

###### Acceptance criteria
- Cross-boundary audit events exported to external organisations follow the agreed standardised schema and include **requestor identity**, **timestamps**, **source system identifiers**, and **audit event details**.
- Every exported audit record is **digitally signed** before transmission, and recipients (or the receiving pipeline) can **verify signatures**.
- Audit events are transmitted only via **approved information-sharing agreements** using **secure transport**, with partner/destination allowlisting enforced.
- For each external organisation, the information exchange agreement documents schema versioning, required fields, signing and verification expectations, and privacy/data protection handling for requestor identity.
- Signature verification failures are handled in a consistent way (quarantine/reject) and trigger operational alerts and audit evidence.

###### Actions Required for Compliance

- [ ] Define and publish a standard format for audit records shared across systems, including who made the request, the date and time, the identifiers of the source system, and the details of the audit event.
- [ ] Set up an official request capture process so that later records show each request came from an authorised person.
- [ ] Set up an audit export process that digitally signs each audit record (or creates a unique hash for each record) before sending it, and include the signature details needed to verify it.

#### AU-16.1 — Identity Preservation (Enhancement)

This requirement is about making sure that, when action records are shared between organisations, the person responsible for each action can still be clearly identified. Without this, wrongdoing or mistakes can be hidden behind unclear or missing identities, which makes it harder to investigate problems, assign responsibility, protect people’s privacy, and meet legal or contractual duties across organisational boundaries.

To meet this expectation, the organisation must ensure that audit records created or used across organisations always include enough information to identify the individual who carried out the action. It must also ensure that this identity information is not changed from start to finish when records are shared or accessed by another organisation.

The organisation should document how this is done in its audit and accountability policy and related procedures. Where personal data is involved, it should include this in its system and privacy planning. It should also describe it in system design and configuration documentation. Finally, it must ensure that the audit records themselves keep the identity details needed for authorised reviewers to trace each action back to the specific person.

##### Immutable actor identity in cross-org audit trails

- Category: Software
- Priority: Critical

###### WHAT
Set up an audit event design that keeps a reliable, unchanging identity for the person (or service) responsible for each action. Every audit record the application creates must include this authoritative identity (for example, an immutable identifier from the organisation’s identity provider). When audit records are exported, shared, or sent to other organisations, the same identity must be kept exactly the same from start to finish.

###### WHY (control requirement)
AU-16(1) (Enhancement) requires the organisation to **keep individuals’ identities consistent in audit trails that cross organisational boundaries**, so actions taken across organisations can still be traced back to the specific individual.

###### HOW (specific steps/approach)
1. **Define an “actor identity contract” for audit events**: create a fixed audit event structure that always includes unchangeable identity fields for the actor (for example, `actor.id` from the enterprise identity provider’s immutable identifier, `actor.type`, `actor.tenant/org`, and optional display details like `actor.name`). Also include event identifiers (`eventId`) and timestamps.
2. **Capture identity at the source from the authoritative identity information**: make sure the application takes actor identity from the authenticated session or token issued by the enterprise identity provider, not from information supplied by the client (such as headers or parameters). For service accounts, use a separate `actor.type`, while still ensuring the identity is stable and can be attributed.
3. **Lock identity so it cannot be changed later**: configure the audit logging and forwarding process so actor identity fields cannot be altered by later systems. For example, ignore any incoming attempts to override identity, store audit logs in an append-only way, and prevent any enrichment steps from overwriting `actor.id`.
4. **Secure cross-organisation sharing and data processing with checks**: when exporting to partners, regulators, or a security operations centre, or when importing into a third-party security information and event management (SIEM) system, apply controlled transformations. Validate the actor identity contract and reject or flag events where actor identity fields are missing, empty, or do not meet required format and consistency checks.
5. **Ensure audit records are usable as evidence**: document the identity-preservation approach in the system design and configuration. Confirm that each audit record includes enough information for an authorised reviewer to trace the same person across organisational boundaries.

###### WHO
- **Application Security Architect / Security Engineering Lead**: defines the actor identity contract and the rules that prevent changes.
- **Application Engineering Team**: implements identity capture from the enterprise identity provider context and ensures audit events follow the contract.
- **Platform/Logging Engineering (SIEM and ETL owners)**: implements schema validation, transformation safeguards, and identity immutability in the log pipelines.
- **Compliance/Assurance Lead**: checks that the audit evidence is sufficient for cross-organisation tracing.

###### Acceptance criteria
- In cross-organisational audit sharing flows, every exported or imported audit event includes a non-empty, stable `actor.id` that matches the authoritative identity provider’s immutable identifier.
- Actor identity fields (`actor.id`, `actor.type`, `actor.tenant/org`) remain unchanged from when the audit event is created through to export and import (confirmed by an end-to-end test comparing source and destination records).
- Events that do not contain valid actor identity fields are rejected or placed in a quarantine process (not silently turned into audit records with missing or unclear identities).
- An authorised reviewer can pick an audit event from the cross-organisational trail and trace it to the same individual using the preserved identity fields alone.

###### Actions Required for Compliance

- [ ] Set a standard audit event format (the “actor identity” definition), including an unchangeable `actor.id` taken from the organisation’s identity provider (IdP).
- [ ] Update the application’s audit event generation so that the actor’s identity is taken only from the claims in an authenticated session or token.
- [ ] Set up audit and log reporting so that the “actor identity” details cannot be changed after they are recorded (ignore or remove any later attempts to override them).
- [ ] Add data validation to the cross-organisation export and import (extract, transform, load) process to reject or quarantine any events where the actor identity is missing or not valid.
- [ ] Add end-to-end tests that compare the audit records at the source and destination to confirm that the actor’s identity has not changed.
- [ ] Publish system design and configuration documentation that explains how identity is kept consistent from start to finish for cross-organisation audit trails.

#### AU-16.3 — Disassociability (Enhancement)

This requirement is about sharing audit records without revealing who the person really is. When audit information is shared between organisations, there is a risk that names or other direct details could let others track, build profiles of, or single out individuals—especially if the same person’s activity can be connected over time.

To reduce this risk while still keeping the organisation accountable, the organisation must ensure that audit events shared across organisational boundaries use temporary coded identifiers instead of direct personal details. These codes must change over time using a defined rotating approach.

At the same time, the organisation must keep a secure, controlled way to link each temporary code to the real identity. This link must be owned and managed by the audit governance office. Only authorised audit governance staff should be able to look up the real identity when there is a legitimate need for accountability.

The organisation must also be able to demonstrate—through its policies, procedures, system design, configuration, and audit evidence—that the shared records contain only coded identifiers and that the codes rotate as expected.

##### Cross-boundary audit pseudonyms with rotating keys and governed mapping

- Category: Software
- Priority: Critical

###### WHAT
Implement privacy-enhancing cryptographic pseudonymisation for audit information that is shared between organisations. This means shared audit records will use coded identifiers that are not directly linked to a person’s real details, rather than including direct personal identifiers. Keep a tightly controlled and fully auditable link between these coded identifiers and the real identities, owned and managed by the audit governance office.

###### WHY (control requirement)
NIST AU-16(3) requires implementing **au-16.03_odp (measures to separate individuals from audit information shared between organisations)**. The guidance explains that keeping real identities in audit trails can create privacy risks (for example, tracking or profiling). These risks increase when information is shared across organisational boundaries. Privacy-enhancing cryptographic methods should reduce privacy risk while still supporting accountability.

###### HOW (specific steps/approach)
1. **Define the cross-boundary audit scope**: identify which audit events, and which identity-related fields (for example, user name, email, subject distinguished name (DN), employee ID) are included in exports to other organisations and security information and event management (SIEM) partners. Confirm these are the only fields that will be separated (pseudonymised).
2. **Implement a pseudonymisation mechanism (software)**:
   - Create an “Audit Pseudonymiser” component/library used by the audit export process.
   - For each identity value, generate a coded identifier using a keyed cryptographic pseudonymisation approach (for example, a pseudorandom function (PRF) or HMAC (hash-based message authentication code) over a standardised identifier), with **key versioning**.
   - Make the pseudonym **change over time** by tying it to a defined rotation window (for example, a time-based window such as daily or weekly, or event-based windows). This ensures the same person’s coded identifier changes across the defined periods.
3. **Apply transformation at export time (software)**:
   - Update the audit export process so that, for cross-boundary sharing, the exported audit record includes only the coded identifier(s) and removes direct identifiers.
   - Allow internal/local audit storage to keep direct identifiers if needed for internal operations, but ensure cross-boundary outputs are separated (pseudonymised).
4. **Govern and secure the reverse mapping (software + manual governance)**:
   - Store only the minimum required mapping information (or enable reverse lookup) in a restricted system owned by the **audit governance office**.
   - Use least-privilege access (role-based access control (RBAC) and attribute-based access control (ABAC)), separation of duties, and strong authentication for any lookup.
5. **Use a controlled lookup workflow (manual + software support)**:
   - Require a ticket and approval process for identity resolution requests, including justification and approval by authorised audit governance roles.
   - Log every lookup attempt, decision, requester, purpose, and the pseudonym/key version involved.
6. **Key rotation operations and proof (manual + software)**:
   - Set rotation frequency and triggers consistent with au-16.03_odp “rotating keys”.
   - Record key rotation events and ensure the pseudonymiser uses the correct active key version for each rotation window.

###### WHO (roles responsible)
- **Audit Governance Office**: owns the mapping system, approves lookup requests, sets rotation policy parameters, and reviews evidence.
- **Security Engineering / Platform Team**: builds the Audit Pseudonymiser, integrates it into the audit export process, and manages cryptographic configuration.
- **SIEM/Monitoring Integration Team**: ensures cross-boundary exports use the pseudonymised fields and checks that exported payloads contain the correct content.
- **Compliance/Audit Team**: checks evidence and performs disassociability checks during assessments.

###### Acceptance criteria
- Cross-boundary audit exports contain **only coded identifiers** for the selected identity fields; direct identifiers are not included in the transmitted payload.
- Pseudonyms **rotate** according to the defined rotation window (for example, daily or weekly), and the exported token includes (or corresponds to) the correct key version for that window.
- The reverse mapping is accessible only to authorised audit governance roles through an auditable, approved lookup process.
- Evidence is available for: (a) key rotation events, (b) pseudonymisation configuration and versioning, and (c) all mapping lookups (including approvals and justifications).

###### Actions Required for Compliance

- [ ] Define the scope for sharing audit records across organisational boundaries (which events and identity details are included) and confirm which identity fields must be removed from that shared data.
- [ ] Implement an audit pseudonymiser that creates coded identifiers using keyed cryptographic pseudonymisation, with key versioning.
- [ ] Add the pseudonymiser to the audit export process so that any information shared across boundaries does not include direct personal identifiers, and instead includes only coded identifiers.
- [ ] Set up and run rotating encryption keys by defining the key rotation time window and the conditions that trigger a change, and ensure the correct (active) key version is used within each window.
- [ ] Set up a restricted mapping store that is owned by the audit governance office, and use least-privilege access controls (only the minimum permissions needed).
- [ ] Set up an approved, auditable process for checking identities and keep a complete record of all identity-check activity.
- [ ] Collect and keep evidence of key rotations, the contents of exported data, and the results of mapping lookups for audit review.
