### CA-1 — Policy and Procedures (Control)

This requirement is about making sure your organisation has clear, up-to-date rules for deciding whether systems and business processes are suitable to run, granting permission for their use, and monitoring them to spot problems early. Without this, teams may use different approaches, miss legal or regulatory responsibilities, and fail to notice changes—raising the risk of data loss, service disruption, or security breaches that could have been prevented.

To meet this expectation, the organisation must create, document, and share an organisation-wide policy for assessment, authorisation, and monitoring, along with the supporting procedures. This work must be managed by the Security Governance Lead and shared with the Chief Information Security Officer (CISO), the Security Governance Lead, the Privacy Officer, system security officers, and the relevant workflow owners.

The policy must set out its purpose, what it covers, who is responsible for what, leadership commitment, how different parts of the organisation work together, and how compliance is checked. It must also align with relevant laws and internal standards.

The policy must be reviewed at least annually and after major audit findings, incidents, or breaches, relevant legal or regulatory changes, or major changes to systems or operational technology. The procedures must be reviewed at least annually and after changes to workflows, tools, or monitoring, changes to incident response or account reviews, or significant organisational restructuring.

##### Governance-owned CA-1 policy & procedure suite with review triggers

- Category: Manual
- Priority: Critical

###### WHAT must be done
Create and keep up to date an organisation-level (and, where necessary, mission/business process-level) **assessment, authorisation, and monitoring policy and procedures**. These must be **written down, shared with the right roles, and kept current**.

###### WHY (control requirement)
CA-1 requires that assessment, authorisation, and monitoring are governed by **policy and procedures** covering the CA family controls that are used in systems and organisations. The guidance highlights that the **risk management strategy** is a key input, that **security and privacy teams work together**, and that the **policy and procedures are reviewed and updated** when defined events occur (for example: audit findings, security incidents/breaches, changes to laws/regulations, and major changes to system or operational technology (OT) architecture).

###### HOW (specific steps/approach)
1. **Assign ownership and set who receives what**: The Security Governance Lead owns the policy/procedures lifecycle. Define which roles receive the policy (for example: Chief Information Security Officer (CISO), Security Governance Lead, Privacy Officer, system security officers) and which roles receive the procedures (for example: CISO, Security Governance Lead, Privacy Officer, and the owners of the assessment/authorisation workflow).
2. **Publish an auditable policy document (or a linked set of policies)** that includes: purpose, scope (organisation-level and/or mission/business process-level), roles and responsibilities, coordination between Security and Privacy, and a compliance approach aligned to relevant laws/directives/regulations/policies/standards/guidelines. Make sure it is **not just a repeat** of the CA controls.
3. **Create operational procedures** that explain how the policy is carried out (how assessments start, how authorisation decisions are handled, what monitoring expects and what evidence is kept, and how approvals/decisions are recorded). Procedures can be included in the system security and privacy plan(s) or in separate documents.
4. **Set up document control and sharing**: Use a controlled document management system with version numbers, effective dates, and a record of who the document was issued to and who received it (or an equivalent acknowledgement process) for the defined recipients.
5. **Set and enforce when reviews and updates happen, and what triggers them**:
   - **Policy**: review and update **annually**, and also when events occur such as **material audit findings**, **security incidents/breaches**, **changes to applicable laws/directives/regulations/policies/standards**, and **major changes to system or OT architecture**.
   - **Procedures**: review and update **annually**, and also when events occur such as **changes to assessment/authorisation workflows**, **updates to tools or logging/monitoring capabilities**, **changes to incident response or account review processes**, and **significant organisational restructuring**.
6. **Create a joint Security and Privacy approval step**: Require joint review and approval by the Security Governance Lead and the Privacy Officer (or their delegates) for any changes that affect privacy assurance or how data is handled.

###### WHO (role responsible)
- **Security Governance Lead**: owns the policy/procedures; manages drafting, approvals, publishing, sharing, and review cycles.
- **CISO (Chief Information Security Officer)**: governance sponsor/approver.
- **Privacy Officer**: co-reviews/co-approves privacy-relevant parts.
- **System security officers** and **assessment/authorisation workflow owners**: recipients and implementers of the procedures.

###### Acceptance criteria
- A current, controlled **assessment/authorisation/monitoring policy** exists, is approved by the defined leadership, and has been shared with the defined policy recipients.
- Current **assessment/authorisation/monitoring procedures** exist, are approved, and have been shared with the defined procedure recipients.
- The policy and procedures clearly state the **review frequency** (annual) and the **update triggers** that match the defined events.
- There is evidence that documents were shared and controlled (version history, effective dates, and issued-to/received-by records or equivalent acknowledgements).
- Security and Privacy teams show they work together through documented joint review/approval for policy/procedure changes.

###### Actions Required for Compliance

- [ ] Appoint the Security Governance Lead as the official owner responsible for managing the full lifecycle of the CA-1 policy and procedures.
- [ ] Define and record who should receive the policy and the procedures, including the responsible roles and workflow owners.
- [ ] Create and get approval for an organisation-wide policy covering assessment, authorisation, and monitoring. The policy should state its purpose, who it applies to, the responsibilities of different roles, how Security and Privacy teams coordinate, and how compliance will be handled.
- [ ] Create assessment, authorisation, and monitoring procedures that explain how the policy is put into practice, including how evidence and records are handled, rather than simply repeating the controls.
- [ ] Upload both documents to a controlled document management system with version control, effective dates, and records showing who they were issued to and who received them.
- [ ] Review and update the policy every year, and set automatic update triggers when there are audit findings, incidents or breaches, changes to legal or regulatory requirements, or major changes to the organisation’s systems or operational technology (OT) architecture.
- [ ] Review and update the procedure every year, and set automatic update triggers when there are changes to the workflow, the tools/logging/monitoring capabilities, incident response or account review processes, or when the organisation is restructured.

### CA-2 — Control Assessments (Control)

This requirement is about making sure your organisation regularly checks that the protections for company systems and people’s personal data are actually in place and working as intended. Without these checks, weaknesses could go unnoticed, leaving you exposed to data loss, privacy breaches, fraud, or service disruption, and making risk decisions based on outdated information.

To meet this expectation, the organisation must appoint the right person or team to carry out each assessment. Before any checking begins, it must also create a written assessment plan that clearly sets out what will be reviewed, how effectiveness will be judged, where the work will take place, who will do each task, and who is responsible for each part. This plan must be reviewed and approved first by the authorised official (or their designated representative).

The organisation must then assess both security and privacy protections every 90 days, produce a written report of the results, and share those results with the authorised official, the designated representative, the senior information security officer, and the system security and privacy leads.

##### CA-2: Qualified assessors and 90-day assessment plan/report workflow

- Category: Manual
- Priority: Critical

###### WHAT
Set up a repeatable process to ensure that control checks are planned, approved, carried out, and reported every **90 days** by **appropriately skilled and technically experienced assessors**. This must cover the system and the environment in which it operates (including hybrid/common/program management controls where applicable). Produce **written assessment reports** with enough detail, and share the results with the defined decision-makers.

###### WHY (control requirement)
CA-2 requires the organisation to ensure that control assessors have the right skills and technical knowledge to create effective assessment plans and to carry out assessments that fit the specific system. It also requires that assessments happen as part of initial and ongoing approvals (and continuous monitoring), and throughout the system development life cycle. Assessments must be carried out on the controls that have been implemented, as described in the security and privacy plans. The results must be recorded in enough detail and provided to the appropriate decision-makers.

###### HOW (specific steps/approach)
1. **Assessor capability and selection**: Keep a list of assessors (or a list of contracted assessors) matched to the system components they understand (hardware/software/firmware, deployment approach, and relevant security and privacy engineering). Require proof that they understand risk management and have hands-on experience with the specific technology used by the application.
2. **Consolidated assessment plan**: For each 90-day cycle, create or maintain a **security and privacy assessment plan** (either one combined plan or separate plans). It must clearly set out:
   - which controls are in scope (and any enhancements), and the assessment boundaries (system plus environment of operation)
   - the assessment steps and how effectiveness will be judged (for example, checking that controls are implemented correctly and working as intended)
   - the assessment setting (production/test/disaster recovery as applicable, and any constraints)
   - the roles and responsibilities of the assessment team and reviewers
3. **Pre-assessment approval gate**: Send the assessment plan to the **authorising official** (or **designated representative**) for review and recorded approval **before** any assessment work starts.
4. **Carry out assessments every 90 days**: Complete the assessment cycle on the implemented controls, using sampling or confirmation where appropriate, and ensure both **security** and **privacy** outcomes are assessed.
5. **Produce written reports and share results**: Create a written assessment report that records the results in enough detail to judge whether they are accurate and complete, and whether controls are implemented correctly and operating as intended. Share results with:
   - the authorising official
   - the designated representative
   - the senior agency information security officer
   - the system security and privacy leads

###### WHO
- **Assessment Lead / Assurance Manager**: responsible for choosing assessors, creating the plan, coordinating the work, and compiling the report.
- **Control Owners / System Security and Privacy Leads**: provide system context, access to evidence, and confirm the scope and interpretation.
- **Authorising Official / Designated Representative**: reviews and approves the assessment plan.
- **Assessor(s)** (internal assurance team or qualified external assessors): carry out the assessments and produce evidence-based findings.

###### Acceptance criteria
- A documented assessment plan exists for each 90-day cycle and includes scope, assessment steps/effectiveness criteria, assessment environment, and roles/responsibilities.
- The assessment plan is approved by the authorising official or designated representative **before** assessment activities start.
- Assessments are completed at least **every 90 days** and cover both **security** and **privacy** outcomes.
- Written assessment reports are produced with enough detail to support accurate and complete conclusions about correct implementation and operating effectiveness.
- Assessment results are provided to the defined roles (authorising official, designated representative, senior agency information security officer, system security and privacy leads).

###### Actions Required for Compliance

- [ ] Create and keep up to date an assessor capability matrix that lists, for the application’s hardware, software, firmware, and deployment approach, both the assessors’ knowledge of risk management and their practical, hands-on experience.
- [ ] Create the template for the combined security and privacy assessment plan, including the required sections for: scope, the procedures and how effectiveness will be judged, the assessment environment, and the roles and responsibilities.
- [ ] Set up a pre-assessment approval process that records approval from the authorised official or designated representative before any assessment work starts.
- [ ] Schedule and carry out control checks for the system and the environment in which it operates at least every 90 days, covering both security and privacy outcomes.
- [ ] Produce a written assessment report for each cycle, with evidence-based findings that are detailed enough to confirm whether the information is accurate and complete, and to verify that the controls are in place and working as intended.
- [ ] Share the assessment results with the authorising official, the designated representative, the senior agency information security officer, and the system security and privacy leads within the agreed reporting timeframe.

#### CA-2.1 — Independent Assessors (Enhancement)

This requirement is about making sure your checks are carried out by people who are genuinely independent, so the results can be trusted. Without independent assessors, the organisation risks “marking its own homework”, overlooking important gaps, or being influenced by people who built, run, or benefit from the system. This could lead to avoidable failures and harm to the organisation, its assets, or the people affected by its services.

To meet this expectation, the organisation must appoint independent assessors (or an independent team) to review how well its arrangements are working. These assessors must have no real or perceived conflicts of interest, must not review their own work, must not act as managers or employees for the system, and must not advocate for the organisation commissioning the work.

The organisation must decide how independent the assessors need to be based on the system’s risk level and classification. It must also ensure that any contracted assessors are similarly independent. Where independence is limited by the organisation’s structure, independent experts must validate the results for completeness, accuracy, trustworthiness, and reliability before the organisation uses them for key authorisation decisions.

##### Independent assessor cell with conflict checks and validation gates

- Category: Manual
- Priority: Critical

###### WHAT
Set up an internal, separately organised assurance function (an “independent assurance cell”) to plan and carry out control checks for the enterprise application. The people doing the checks must be impartial: they must not have conflicts of interest, must not check their own work, must not act as managers or employees of the system owner, and must not promote the commissioning organisation’s interests. If full independence is not practical (for example, because teams are small), require independent expert review of the assessment results before they are used.

###### WHY (control requirement)
CA-2(1) enhancement requires using independent assessors or independent assessment teams to carry out control checks. The guidance also requires impartiality, meaning there are no real or perceived conflicts of interest, no self-checking, no management or employee roles, and no advocacy. The authorising official must decide how independent the assessment needs to be based on the system’s security category and risk. It also states that where independence is limited, assessment results must be carefully reviewed and analysed by independent expert teams to confirm completeness, accuracy, integrity, and reliability.

###### HOW (specific steps/approach)
1. **Create an independent assurance cell** reporting to the Chief Information Security Officer (CISO) (or equivalent) and kept separate from application development, operations, and release/change management. Set clear decision rights so the cell can choose assessors, request evidence, and approve assessment outputs.
2. **Define an independence rulebook** based on the application’s system security category and risk (for example, confidentiality, criticality, and regulatory impact). The rulebook must state when internal assurance alone is enough and when independent expert validation is mandatory.
3. **Put conflict-of-interest checks in place** for every assessment: require annual and per-assessment confirmations covering involvement in design/build/run, change approvals, operational ownership, and any advocacy or consulting relationship with the commissioning organisation.
4. **Enforce separation of responsibilities**: ensure assessors are not the system owner, product/platform owner, change approver, or part of the operations team for the scope being assessed. Keep a record mapping which roles are allowed to assess which scopes.
5. **Add an independent validation step** when independence is limited: use a separate independent expert team to review and analyse the assessment results to confirm completeness, accuracy, integrity, and reliability before submission to the authorising official.
6. **Keep evidence showing independence and sufficiency**: store assessor confirmations, role eligibility checks, proof of where evidence came from, and validation outcomes in a central repository with tamper-evident audit trails. Require an “Assessor Independence and Sufficiency” sign-off by the authorising official, referencing the independence rulebook and the validation outcomes.

###### WHO
- **Chief Information Security Officer (CISO) / Authorising Official (AO):** approves the independence rulebook, sets the required level of independence, and confirms that assessor independence is sufficient for credible, risk-based decisions.
- **Assurance Cell Lead:** manages assessor selection, independence checks, assessment planning, and the validation workflow.
- **Independent Expert Validation Team (when required):** checks completeness, accuracy, integrity, and reliability of the assessment outputs.
- **System Owner / Application Owner:** provides evidence and supplies information only (not as an assessor or validator).

###### Acceptance criteria
- For each control assessment engagement, there is documented evidence that assessors meet impartiality requirements (no conflicts/no self-checking; not acting as management or employees; no advocacy) and are eligible under the separation-of-responsibilities rules.
- The independence rulebook is applied for each assessment based on the application’s security category and risk, and the authorising official records the decision that independence is sufficient.
- Where independence is limited, independent expert validation is completed and documented, explicitly covering completeness, accuracy, integrity, and reliability of the assessment results.
- Assessment outputs submitted for authorisation decisions include the independence sign-off and auditable records (confirmations, role checks, evidence provenance, validation outcomes).

###### Actions Required for Compliance

- [ ] Set up an independent assurance team, with reporting lines kept separate from application development, operations, and release or change management.
- [ ] Create and get approval for an independence assessment guide that links each system security category and risk level to the required assessor independence level and the validation checks needed.
- [ ] Put in place conflict-of-interest declarations for each assessment and for the year, covering any involvement in designing, building, or running the work, and any advocacy relationships.
- [ ] Maintain and enforce a segregation-of-duties eligibility matrix to prevent assessors from assessing areas they own, run, or approve changes for.
- [ ] Set up an independent expert review checkpoint for cases with limited independence, requiring documented checks that the information is complete, accurate, trustworthy, and dependable.
- [ ] Create an evidence repository that can be audited for assessor confirmations, role checks, proof of where evidence came from, and validation results, and require the authorising official to confirm that the evidence is sufficient.

#### CA-2.2 — Specialized Assessments (Enhancement)

This requirement is about making sure the organisation regularly and unexpectedly tests its defences to find weaknesses before criminals or careless insiders do. Without these quarterly, unannounced, detailed checks, problems such as software weaknesses that could be exploited, unusual or suspicious system behaviour, or misuse by people who already have legitimate access could go unnoticed for too long. This could lead to data loss, service disruption, or safety impacts.

Each quarter, the organisation must plan, approve, and record these assessments. They must use detailed monitoring supported by suitable monitoring tools, run tests that attempt to misuse systems, assess insider risk, and scan for known weaknesses. The organisation must also carry out any additional checks it defines, such as operational technology (OT) process safety and network segmentation validation.

The organisation must keep evidence of what it did, produce results reports, align the work with its system and privacy plans and with relevant laws and policies, and ensure that any vulnerabilities found are promptly passed into the organisation’s fix-and-track process.

##### Quarterly unannounced specialised assessments with evidence packs

- Category: Software
- Priority: Critical

###### WHAT
Carry out quarterly, unannounced specialist checks. These must include detailed system monitoring (using security monitoring tools), tests that try to act like a malicious user, checks for insider threat risks, and vulnerability scanning. Make sure all results and supporting evidence are recorded, formally authorised, and sent into the organisation’s vulnerability fixing process.

###### WHY (control requirement)
The National Institute of Standards and Technology (NIST) Special Publication 800-53 control CA-2(2) enhancement requires that, as part of control checks, the organisation includes: **(ca-02.02_odp.01)** specialist check frequency (set as **quarterly**), **(ca-02.02_odp.02)** checks carried out **unannounced**, and **(ca-02.02_odp.03)** detailed monitoring using security monitoring tools, malicious-user testing, insider threat assessment, and vulnerability scanning.

###### HOW (specific steps/approach)
1. **Set up a schedule that randomises when checks happen within each quarter** (for example, a random day and time window). Ensure system owners are not told in advance, so the checks are truly **unannounced** in day-to-day operation.
2. **Get the assessment approach pre-approved by the authorising official**, working with the organisation’s risk leadership function. Keep an approval record covering: rules for increasing monitoring, approved scanning settings, malicious-user test scenarios, and insider threat assessment questions and analysis.
3. **Increase monitoring during the check window** by turning on pre-defined monitoring and alert rules in the security information and event management (SIEM), endpoint detection and response (EDR), and cloud audit logs. Examples include misuse of privileged access, unusual sign-in behaviour, signs of lateral movement, and unusual access to sensitive data. Tag the check window in the logs so it can be traced during audits.
4. **Run vulnerability scanning during the check window** using the approved scanning settings, with rate limits to avoid disruption. Save the scan results (including targets, dates and times, tool versions, findings, and severity) as retained evidence.
5. **Carry out malicious-user testing using controlled, non-destructive test actions** (for example, attempts to access “canary” resources, checks that conditional access and multi-factor authentication (MFA) are enforced, tests of privilege boundaries, and tests of session or token controls). Use the system’s normal interfaces and follow the system’s authorisation rules.
6. **Complete an insider threat assessment during the window** using behaviour and identity analysis (for example, unusual privileged identity activity, abnormal patterns of data access, policy breaches, and indicators of segregation-of-duties issues). Record the assessment outputs.
7. **Produce an auditable evidence pack and control check report automatically** using templates (including check window details, authorisation reference, monitoring results, test results, scan results, and initial triage). Then **send all vulnerabilities/findings into the vulnerability remediation workflow**, with named ownership and tracking.

###### WHO (role responsible)
- **Authorising Official (AO)**: approves the check methods and safeguards (coordinated with the organisation’s risk leadership function).
- **Security Operations / Security Operations Centre (SOC) Lead**: runs the monitoring increase and carries out the malicious-user and insider threat checks.
- **Vulnerability Management Lead**: ensures scanning is carried out, evidence is captured, and results are sent into remediation.
- **Governance, Risk and Compliance (GRC) / Compliance Analyst**: maintains check documentation, report templates, and evidence retention records.

###### Acceptance criteria
- Evidence confirms **quarterly** execution of specialist checks (ca-02.02_odp.01).
- Evidence confirms checks were carried out **unannounced** (no advance notification to system owners; scheduling randomisation is documented) (ca-02.02_odp.02).
- For each check window, evidence includes outputs for: **detailed monitoring using security monitoring tools**, **malicious-user testing**, **insider threat assessment**, and **vulnerability scanning** (ca-02.02_odp.03).
- Each check includes an **AO authorisation reference** covering the methods, and is supported by a **control check report** and retained evidence.
- All vulnerabilities/findings are **entered into the vulnerability remediation process** with tracking status and named ownership, within the defined operational service level agreements (SLAs).

###### Actions Required for Compliance

- [ ] Set up a quarterly schedule to run assessments at randomly chosen times, without informing the system owners.
- [ ] Record approval from the Account Owner (with coordination from the risk executive) for monitoring rule intensification, scanning profiles, tests for malicious-user scenarios, and insider-threat analytics.
- [ ] Set up security information and event management (SIEM), endpoint detection and response (EDR), and audit logging to increase monitoring during the assessment period, and label each event with the assessment reference number.
- [ ] Run vulnerability scans automatically within the approved time window, and keep the scan results (including the tool version, date and time, and identified issues) as retained evidence.
- [ ] Run controlled tests that simulate malicious user activity during the agreed time window, and record the test results and outcomes.
- [ ] Run the insider threat assessment analysis during the agreed time window and keep the assessment results.
- [ ] Create an evidence pack and a control assessment report, then send all vulnerabilities and findings into the vulnerability remediation workflow, with tracking.

#### CA-2.3 — Leveraging Results from External Organizations (Enhancement)

This requirement is about using independent third-party assessment results properly, rather than re-checking everything from the beginning. This matters because, without a careful approach, an organisation could end up relying on testing that is incomplete, biased, or not relevant to the systems in scope. That could lead the organisation to believe its on-premises environment that includes mixed computer systems and operational technology is safer and compliant than it really is, creating avoidable risk to day-to-day operations, safety, and its regulatory position.

To meet this expectation, the organisation must maintain a list of accredited third-party assessment organisations and approved testing laboratories that have relevant experience. It must use their results only for the on-premises systems that fall within the organisation’s defined assessed boundary.

Before using any results, the organisation must:
- confirm the assessor was independent
- obtain and securely store the supporting written evidence
- verify the testing covered the same system scope and interfaces
- check that the assessment met all applicable Department of Defense (DoD) and Cybersecurity Maturity Model Certification (CMMC) requirements

The organisation must then record its decision and the source details, update its assessment reporting and system plans (and privacy plans where relevant), and document any gaps in a plan of action and milestones for what still needs to be done.

##### Assessment Reuse Register with eligibility, scope-match and evidence gates

- Category: Manual
- Priority: Critical

###### WHAT
Set up an auditable “External Assessment Reuse Register” and an approval process that only allows external assessment results to be used when the external organisation, the assessment evidence, and the assessment scope all meet the agreed requirements.

###### WHY (control requirement)
CA-2(3) requires using results from external organisations only when the assessment meets the defined criteria (external organisation(s), system, and requirements). This helps ensure the organisation does not accept incomplete, out-of-scope, or non-independent assessment outcomes.

###### HOW (specific steps/approach)
1. **Define eligible external organisations** in a controlled list (for example, accredited third-party assessment organisations and approved testing laboratories such as Common Criteria, Common Method Validation Program (CMVP), and Cryptographic Algorithm Validation Program (CAVP)), and link each one to the relevant assessment programme.
2. **Create an External Assessment Reuse Register** with mandatory fields aligned to CA-2(3):
   - external organisation name and eligibility reference
   - assessed system identifier(s) within the assessed boundary
   - assessment type/programme
   - evidence reference showing the assessor is independent
   - report ID/version and date
   - links to supporting documented evidence (full report and supporting materials)
   - claimed scope details (components, versions, interfaces, trust boundaries)
   - acceptance decision, reason for acceptance, and any remaining gaps
3. **Set up automated acceptance checks in the governance, risk, and compliance (GRC) workflow** so an assessment result cannot be marked “leveraged” unless all checks pass:
   - the external organisation is on the eligible list
   - the assessment evidence is present and complete (report plus supporting materials)
   - the scope matches the organisation’s system boundary (including interfaces and relevant configuration identifiers)
   - assessor independence is evidenced
   - assessment compliance is confirmed against the applicable Department of Defense (DoD) and Cybersecurity Maturity Model Certification (CMMC) requirements (as defined in the organisation’s CA-2(3) requirements setting)
4. **Store evidence in a controlled repository** (with access controls and retention rules), and ensure the register keeps immutable references (for example, document IDs, checksums, or versioned links) to the exact evidence used.
5. **Update assessment planning documents**: for each in-scope system, update the system security plan and assessment reporting to clearly state what the CA-2(3) evidence covers and what has not been assessed; create Plan of Actions and Milestones (POA&M) items for any gaps.

###### WHO (role responsible)
- **Governance, Risk, and Compliance (GRC)/Compliance Manager**: owns the reuse register, the approval workflow, and audit readiness.
- **Security Assessment Lead / Control Assessor**: checks scope match and evidence completeness.
- **System Owner / Operational Technology (OT)–Information Technology (IT) Integration Lead (if applicable)**: confirms the system boundary, interfaces, and configuration identifiers needed for scope matching.
- **Information Security Officer (ISO) / Compliance Approver**: gives final approval to use (leverage) the external results.

###### Acceptance criteria
- External assessment results are only marked “leveraged” when the external organisation is on the eligible list and the required documented evidence is present.
- For each leveraged result, the register records a documented scope-match reason covering the system boundary, components/versions, and relevant interfaces.
- Assessor independence and compliance with the applicable Department of Defense (DoD) and Cybersecurity Maturity Model Certification (CMMC) requirements are evidenced and referenced in the register.
- The system security plan and assessment reporting are updated to show coverage and any remaining gaps, with POA&M entries created where applicable.
- Evidence repository references are immutable/versioned and can be retrieved for audit within the defined retention period.

###### Actions Required for Compliance

- [ ] Create and keep an approved list of external assessment organisations, including accredited or authorised bodies that have relevant experience.
- [ ] Create an External Assessment Reuse Register with required information covering the system scope, whether the assessor is independent, references to the supporting evidence, and the reason the reuse is accepted.
- [ ] Set up governance, risk, and compliance (GRC) workflow “acceptance gates” to prevent use unless the following checks all pass: eligibility, complete supporting evidence, matching scope, independence, and compliance with the Department of Defense (DoD) and the Cybersecurity Maturity Model Certification (CMMC).
- [ ] Set up a controlled evidence repository that stores each external assessment evidence item with records that cannot be changed and that keep versions over time.
- [ ] For each external assessment, check and record that the assessment’s claimed scope matches the organisation’s in-scope system boundary and the interfaces it connects to.
- [ ] Update the system security plan and the assessment reporting to record what is supported by external evidence, and create a plan of action and milestones (POA&M) item for any gaps.

### CA-3 — Information Exchange (Control)

This requirement is about making sure that every time your organisation shares information with another system, there is a clear, written agreement that explains exactly how the information will be shared and how it will be protected. Without this, sensitive information could be sent to the wrong place, handled incorrectly, or exposed through privacy breaches because different systems and suppliers may follow different rules.

To meet this expectation, the organisation must approve and manage each information exchange using written information exchange security agreements. For each agreement, it must document:

- how the systems connect and exchange data  
- what security protections and privacy handling are required  
- what practical safeguards will be used  
- who is responsible on each side  

The organisation must also record the impact level of the information being shared, so the seriousness of any problem is understood.

Finally, the organisation must review and update these agreements at least every 90 days to keep them accurate as risks and services change.

##### Written information exchange security agreements with 90-day review

- Category: Manual
- Priority: Critical

###### WHAT
Create and maintain **written information exchange security agreements** for every system-to-system information exchange (internal and external). Each agreement must record the **interface details**, **security requirements and safeguards**, **privacy requirements**, **who is responsible**, and the **impact level** of the information being shared. Each agreement must be **reviewed and updated at least every 90 days**.

###### WHY (control requirement)
CA-3 requires that system information exchanges are governed by authorised officials, using appropriate risk mitigation. This must be done through **agreements** that explain how information is shared and protected, including interface details, security and privacy requirements, responsibilities, and the **impact level**. The guidance also requires a defined review/update frequency (set by the organisation) to keep agreements current.

###### HOW (specific steps/approach)
1. **Create an information exchange register** (information technology/operational technology) listing each exchange (for example: application programming interface (API)/web service, file transfer, database sharing, email, cloud service, virtual private network (VPN)/leased line). Include the participating systems, the direction of the exchange, and the endpoints/protocols.
2. For each exchange, **create a written information exchange security agreement** using standard templates, tailored by exchange type and data impact level. The agreement must include:
   - **Interface details** (how the systems connect and share information: endpoints/identifiers, protocols, data formats, direction of flow, and any network route such as VPN/peering).
   - **Security requirements** and the **safeguards** to apply to the exchange (for example: encryption in transit, expectations for authentication and authorisation, expectations for logging and monitoring, and any additional safeguards required by the risk assessment).
   - **Privacy requirements** for the exchanged data (for example: handling expectations aligned to the organisation’s privacy and security plans).
   - **Responsibilities** for each party/system (including operational ownership and expectations for incident handling).
   - **Impact level** of the information being communicated.
3. **Get authorisation**: make sure the agreement is approved by the appropriate authorised official(s) for the systems exchanging information. If different authorised officials apply, ensure the agreement (or equivalent agreement content) is completed for the relevant systems.
4. **Link agreements to the exchange register** and ensure only exchanges with a **signed/approved** agreement are marked as active.
5. **Run a 90-day review process**: schedule reviews for each agreement at least every 90 days, record the result (updated or no changes needed), and keep evidence that the review took place.

###### WHO
- **Information System Owner / Application Owner**: starts exchange entries and drafts the agreement content for their system.
- **Authorising Official (AO)**: approves the risk and the required agreement content.
- **Security and Privacy Officers**: check the security and privacy requirements and ensure they match the organisation’s security and privacy plans.
- **Contract/Legal (if applicable)**: ensures agreements are formally signed where required.
- **Compliance/Governance, Risk and Compliance (GRC)**: maintains the register, ensures review timing is followed, and keeps evidence.

###### Acceptance criteria
- For every identified system-to-system exchange, there is a **signed written information exchange security agreement** linked to the exchange register.
- Each agreement includes documented **interface details**, **security requirements/safeguards**, **privacy requirements**, **responsibilities**, and the **impact level**.
- Evidence is available showing each agreement has been **reviewed and updated (or explicitly confirmed as unchanged) at least every 90 days**.
- No exchange is marked active in the register without an approved/signed agreement.

###### Actions Required for Compliance

- [ ] Create an information exchange register that lists every system-to-system data exchange, both inside and outside the organisation. This includes cloud services, web services, file transfers, database sharing, and virtual private network (VPN) or leased line connections.
- [ ] Define and publish standard information-sharing security agreement templates that include mandatory details for: the characteristics of each interface, the security requirements and controls, privacy requirements, responsibilities, and the impact level.
- [ ] Create a written agreement for every registered exchange and get approval from the relevant authorised official(s).
- [ ] For each active exchange shown in the register, link it to the signed and approved agreement document. Do not allow activation of any exchange that does not have an approved agreement.
- [ ] Review the agreement at least every 90 days. Keep a record showing the review took place and whether any updates were needed.
- [ ] Keep the signed agreement and the supporting review evidence in the organisation’s document repository so it can be checked during audits.

#### CA-3.6 — Transfer Authorizations (Enhancement)

This requirement is about ensuring that only approved people and approved partner systems can send information into your protected systems, and that the receiving system checks this before it accepts anything. Without this, an unauthorised sender could put in data they are not allowed to write, which could corrupt records, change settings, or help them gain access through trusted connections. This includes routine “infrastructure” messages such as routing and name lookups, as well as service messages such as mail relays.

To meet this expectation, the organisation must:
- set clear rules for who is allowed to send what to each part of the system, and what permission level is required;
- ensure the receiving system independently verifies the sender’s authorisation using a reliable method, rather than trusting claims;
- apply the same checks to both normal data and control and service traffic;
- document the connection and protection approach;
- reflect these expectations in agreements with partners;
- configure the systems accordingly; and
- keep audit records showing whether each incoming attempt was accepted or rejected based on authorisation.

##### Receiving-side transfer authorisation checks for all inbound data

- Category: Software
- Priority: Critical

###### WHAT
Set up a receiving-side control that, before the system accepts any incoming data (including control-plane and service traffic), independently checks that the authenticated person or system is authorised for the specific transfer situation (destination, transfer type, and the required permission).

###### WHY (control requirement)
CA-3(6) requires verification that people or systems moving data between connected systems have the necessary authorisations (for example, write permissions/privileges) **before** accepting that data. Further guidance requires the protected system to verify authorisation using **independent checks**, and to apply the same verification to **control-plane traffic** (for example, routing/DNS) and **services** (for example, authenticated SMTP relays).

###### HOW (specific approach)
1. **Define a transfer authorisation policy model** in the receiving system that links:
   - **Verified sender identity** (a human identity or workload identity/service account/workload credential)
   - **Destination** (the specific API resource, database table/schema, message topic/queue, file drop location, etc.)
   - **Transfer type/context** (data write/update, configuration/control update, DNS/routing update, authenticated relay submission, etc.)
   - **Required privilege** (the write/update permission scope)

2. **Use deny-by-default at the receiving boundary** (for example, an API gateway, message consumer, relay service, DNS/routing controller endpoint, etc.). The receiving component must:
   - Authenticate the sender using enterprise identity and certificate-based methods.
   - Check authorisation against the policy model **before** accepting, storing, queueing, or applying any payload or making any control/service change.
   - Reject unauthorised transfers.

3. **Use independent verification**: do not rely on any sender information provided inside the payload or message metadata. Base the decision on the **authenticated identity from the connection** (for example, token claims from the identity provider, the identity from a mutual TLS (mTLS) client certificate, or signed workload credentials).

4. **Apply the same enforcement to control-plane and service traffic** by implementing the receiving endpoints for:
   - **DNS/routing updates** (authorise which identities can update which zones/routes)
   - **Authenticated SMTP relays** (authorise which identities/hosts can submit/relay to which destinations)
   using the same decision point (or an equivalent authorisation service that is governed consistently).

5. **Keep audit evidence for every attempt**: record allow/deny decisions at minimum with sender identity, destination, transfer type/context, request identifier, and the reason for the outcome (for example, permission denied/unauthorised relay).

###### WHO
- **Application/Platform Security Engineer**: designs the policy model and where the checks are enforced.
- **Application Owner / Backend Engineers**: implement the receiving-side checks at the relevant points (API/message/relay/DNS/routing endpoints).
- **Identity and Access Management (IAM) Team**: ensures correct integration for authenticated sender identity and certificate/token issuance.
- **Security Operations Centre (SOC)/Security Information and Event Management (SIEM) Engineer**: ensures audit logs are sent to the right place, kept for the required period, and can be searched to provide evidence for CA-3(6).

###### Acceptance criteria
- For every inbound transfer endpoint, the system **rejects** requests where the authenticated sender does not have the required permission for the specific destination and transfer type.
- Authorisation is checked **before** data is accepted (stored/queued/applied).
- Decisions are based on **independently verified authenticated identity** tied to the connection, not on sender fields provided in the payload.
- Control-plane (DNS/routing) and service traffic (authenticated SMTP relay) are covered using the same authorisation verification approach.
- Audit logs exist for both allowed and denied attempts, including sender identity, destination, transfer context/type, and the outcome reason.

###### Actions Required for Compliance

- [ ] Create a transfer authorisation policy model that links a verified sender’s identity to which destinations and transfer types they are allowed to use.
- [ ] Set up “deny by default” access control at every point where data is received (for example, the application programming interface (API) gateway, the message consumer, and the relay/domain name system (DNS)/routing endpoint).
- [ ] Set up the receiving points to use enterprise authentication (tokens or certificates), and make access decisions based on the identity confirmed during the connection.
- [ ] Add checks before accepting a transfer, so unauthorised transfers are rejected before they are saved, queued, or used to apply any control or service changes.
- [ ] Apply the same permission checks to both control-plane traffic (domain name system (DNS) and routing updates) and service traffic (authenticated simple mail transfer protocol (SMTP) relay submission).
- [ ] Set up audit logs for every decision to allow or block a transfer, recording the sender identity, destination, the transfer context or type, the request ID, and the reason code.
- [ ] Use automated tests to confirm that messages from unauthorised senders are rejected, and that messages from authorised senders are accepted for the correct destination and context.

#### CA-3.7 — Transitive Information Exchanges (Enhancement)

This requirement is about making sure you can identify and control the “hidden routes” where information you send to an external service can be passed on to other systems. You must then stop that flow if you cannot show that the connected systems are being kept secure and are being handled properly.

Without this, your organisation could unknowingly take on risk from partners or downstream systems. This could lead to sensitive information being exposed, misused, or mishandled because safeguards are missing, out of date, or not working as expected.

To meet this expectation, the organisation must identify and record which downstream systems receive information from, or pass information through, the external systems it already uses. This includes what information is shared and the route it takes.

The organisation must keep these downstream information exchanges running only while it can verify that the downstream safeguards are in place and up to date. If evidence is missing, out of date, or cannot be verified, the organisation must take practical steps to stop the exchanges. There must be clear responsibility for monitoring, stopping, and only restarting once verification is restored and approved.

##### Transitive exchange register with evidence-based stop/go for downstream links

- Category: Manual
- Priority: Critical

###### WHAT
For each already-approved external system, create and keep up to date a **transitive (downstream) information exchange register**. This should list the **downstream systems reached directly or indirectly**, the **information/data shared**, and the **route/path** the information takes. Then put in place an **evidence-based stop/go process** so that downstream information sharing is **stopped whenever the required downstream security and privacy checks cannot be confirmed** (for example, evidence is missing, out of date, or cannot be obtained).

###### WHY (control requirement)
The CA-3(7) enhancement guidance requires identifying **transitive/downstream information exchanges** and making sure the organisation can **understand and manage the security and privacy risks passed on through these connections**. It also requires that sharing is **stopped when downstream protections cannot be confirmed**, because organisational systems can inherit risk from downstream systems.

###### HOW (specific steps/approach)
1. **Create a transitive exchange register** for each external system already identified under CA-3a. For each entry, record:
   - downstream system(s) (direct and indirect),
   - direction (send/receive),
   - data/information types,
   - protocols/applications,
   - the **path via the CA-3a external system**.
2. **Populate the register using technical dependency sources** (for example, API gateway logs, iPaaS (integration platform as a service) connector inventories, service-to-service dependency maps, cloud flow logs, and SaaS (software as a service) administrator integration logs) and confirm details with the system owners.
3. For each downstream system, create a **downstream evidence pack** with proof that security and privacy protections are in place (for example, a current security assessment/assurance summary, privacy handling documentation, evidence from access control reviews, monitoring/logging evidence, and relevant test/assessment summaries). Record an **evidence expiry date** and how often evidence will be checked (**verification cadence**).
4. Implement a **stop/go workflow** in the organisation’s IT service management (ITSM) or ticketing system:
   - sharing stays enabled only while evidence is **up to date and can be checked**;
   - if evidence is **missing/out of date/cannot be obtained**, the workflow raises a **request to stop the connection/integration** to the connection/integration owner, including required outcome details (for example, disable the integration job, revoke API keys, block network traffic rules, pause message routing).
5. Add **operational monitoring** to detect cases where information exchange is **still happening after evidence has expired**, using security information and event management (SIEM) and integration monitoring data (for example, API gateway performance metrics, integration job status, message bus activity, and cloud network traffic flows). Escalate as an incident and trigger a stop when mismatches are found.
6. Assign named responsibilities (system owner plus security/privacy approver) and ensure the workflow treats **loss of evidence verifiability as a stop trigger**, with sharing only allowed again after explicit approval once evidence is restored.

###### WHO
- **Application/Integration Owner**: maintains the register entries for their integrations and ensures stop actions are carried out.
- **Security/Privacy Governance (CA-3 owner)**: sets what evidence is acceptable, how often it must be checked, and approves restarting.
- **ITSM/Workflow Administrator**: sets up the stop/go workflow, expiry triggers, and escalation routes.
- **Security Operations Centre (SOC) / SIEM Operations**: monitors for continued information exchange during evidence expiry and escalates where needed.

###### Acceptance criteria
- A complete transitive exchange register exists for each CA-3a external system, showing downstream systems and the path via the external system.
- For every downstream system listed in the register, there is an evidence pack with an expiry date and a defined verification cadence.
- The stop/go workflow clearly triggers stop actions when evidence is expired, missing, or cannot be obtained.
- Monitoring rules detect and alert when information exchange continues despite evidence expiry, and the organisation can provide audit-ready evidence of stop/go decisions and stop actions.
- Restarting any downstream information exchange requires documented approval after evidence is restored and verified.

###### Actions Required for Compliance

- [ ] Set up a register of data exchanges that works through each CA-3a external system, and record which downstream systems are involved, what data types are exchanged, the direction of the data flow, and the route taken through the external system.
- [ ] Fill in and check the accuracy of register entries using information from integration inventory sources, including the application programming interface (API) gateway, integration platform as a service (iPaaS), dependency maps, cloud workflow logs, and software-as-a-service (SaaS) integration logs.
- [ ] Set which types of evidence are acceptable for each downstream system, and define when that evidence expires and how often it must be checked.
- [ ] Set up an information technology service management (ITSM) “stop/go” process that triggers actions to stop work when required evidence is missing, has expired, or cannot be obtained.
- [ ] Assign named owners and approvers for maintaining the register, approving its cessation, and approving its resumption.
- [ ] Set up security information and event management (SIEM) and integration telemetry monitoring to alert if exchanges keep happening after the supporting evidence has expired, and escalate to take action to stop them.

### CA-5 — Plan of Action and Milestones (Control)

This requirement is about making sure the organisation has a clear, written recovery plan when problems are found, including when each fix will be carried out. Without this, security weaknesses and known gaps could remain in place, allowing the same mistakes to happen again, leaving incidents unresolved, and causing avoidable harm to people, services, or finances.

To meet this expectation, the organisation must create and maintain a documented action plan for the system. This plan must set out what remediation work is planned to address any weaknesses or deficiencies identified during reviews, and to reduce or remove known vulnerabilities. The plan must also state who is responsible for delivering and maintaining it, include specific checkpoints and planned completion dates, and be updated at least every quarter.

Each quarterly update must reflect new information from control assessments, independent audits or reviews, and ongoing monitoring. The organisation must also keep records showing the updates were completed and what findings led to the changes.

##### System remediation plan with quarterly ITSM governance and evidence

- Category: Software
- Priority: Critical

###### WHAT
Create and maintain a documented **Plan of Action and Milestones (POA&M)** for the enterprise business application. The POA&M must list planned fix actions to correct **weaknesses/deficiencies** and **reduce or remove known vulnerabilities**. It must include **milestones and planned timing**, and it must name who is responsible for creating, carrying out, and keeping the plan up to date.

###### WHY (control requirement)
CA-5 requires an organisation to have a **plan of action and milestones** to track planned corrective actions, and to **update it at least quarterly** using results from **control assessments, independent audits or reviews, and ongoing monitoring**. The organisation must also keep records showing the updates were made and which findings led to the changes.

###### HOW (specific approach)
Set up a **system-focused remediation backlog** in the organisation’s IT service management (ITSM) and vulnerability management tools, supported by a quarterly governance process that produces an auditable POA&M update pack.

1. **Define the “system” scope** for the application (including production, test, and disaster recovery boundaries) and link it to the system security plan, so every POA&M item can be traced to the correct system.
2. **Create the initial POA&M record** in ITSM (or a controlled repository linked from ITSM) with the required fields:
   - Finding identifier and source type (**assessment**, **independent audit/review**, **ongoing monitoring**)
   - Weakness/vulnerability category
   - Specific remediation action
   - Accountable owner (role/person/team)
   - Start date and **milestone dates / target completion dates**
   - Dependencies and what evidence is needed to confirm closure
3. **Add findings to the backlog**:
   - Import control assessment findings as work items linked to the original assessment evidence.
   - Import independent audit/review findings as a separate type of work item linked to audit evidence.
   - Import ongoing monitoring outputs (for example, vulnerability scan results, configuration posture findings, and security information and event management (SIEM) detections that indicate weaknesses) as work items linked back to the scan/detection evidence.
4. **Keep clear traceability and closure rules**:
   - For each POA&M item, require closure evidence (for example, ticket closure showing the fix was done, re-test results from scans, configuration differences, and approval records).
   - Ensure each item is either completed with evidence or explicitly postponed with an approved reason and updated milestone timing.
5. **Run a quarterly POA&M update cycle** (at least quarterly, aligned to the organisation’s reporting schedule):
   - Review newly added findings from all three sources.
   - Re-set priorities and milestone dates based on risk and operational constraints.
   - Close completed items and update the POA&M record to show the changes.
   - Record which findings drove each change (new items, milestone changes, postponements, and closures).
6. **Produce an auditable “POA&M update pack”** each quarter containing:
   - The updated POA&M version
   - A summary of changes (what changed since the previous quarter)
   - The set of findings used for the update (by source type)
   - Evidence that closed items were completed

###### WHO
- **Application Owner / System Owner**: responsible for the POA&M content and for delivering the remediation.
- **Information Security / governance, risk and compliance (GRC) Lead**: ensures the POA&M meets CA-5 requirements, the quarterly update cycle is followed, and the evidence is sufficient.
- **ITSM Process Owner / Service Management Lead**: maintains the workflow, required fields, and reporting exports.
- **Cybersecurity Engineering / Vulnerability Management Lead**: ensures ongoing monitoring findings are correctly added and linked to the right remediation actions.

###### Acceptance criteria
- A documented POA&M exists for the application and includes remediation actions for identified weaknesses/deficiencies and known vulnerabilities, with **milestones and planned timing** and an assigned accountable owner.
- The POA&M is **updated at least quarterly**, and each update includes findings from **control assessments, independent audits/reviews, and ongoing monitoring**.
- For each quarterly update, the organisation can provide evidence showing **what changed** and **which findings led to the changes**.
- For closed POA&M items, the organisation can provide **closure evidence** showing remediation completion (for example, re-scan results, configuration proof, or approved remediation records).

###### Actions Required for Compliance

- [ ] Define the scope of the “application system” and the limits of its environment to support proof of action and milestones (POA&M) item traceability.
- [ ] Create the initial plan of action and milestones (POA&M) record in IT service management (ITSM), with fields for the source of the finding, the remediation action, the responsible owner, and the milestone dates.
- [ ] Set up ingestion mappings so that, automatically, programme of actions and milestones (POA&M) work items are created from assessment findings, independent audit or review findings, and continuous monitoring results.
- [ ] Set clear closure requirements that include proof the issue has been fixed (for example, re-scans and updated configuration evidence), and require a written reason for any deferral, with updated target dates (milestones).
- [ ] Run a quarterly plan of action and milestones (POA&M) governance review to add new issues, update target dates, close items that are finished, and reset the priority plan.
- [ ] Export and keep a quarterly POA&M update pack that shows the updated plan version, a summary of what changed, and the findings that led to the updates.

#### CA-5.1 — Automation Support for Accuracy and Currency (Enhancement)

This requirement is about keeping your improvement plan up to date automatically, so nothing important becomes outdated or is missed. Without this, the organisation’s plan of actions and milestones could become inaccurate, out of date, or unavailable when people need to review it. That could delay fixing weaknesses and cause effort and resources to be directed at the wrong things.

To meet this expectation, the organisation must use automated workflow and ticketing tools that continuously check the status of each item in the plan, automatically update the plan when statuses change, and send scheduled reminders for actions that are coming up or are due—while also recording that the reminders were sent. The tools must also automatically collect and attach supporting evidence for any updates, link that evidence to the correct plan item, and keep a clear, easy-to-review record of what changed and when.

Finally, the plan must remain reliably available for authorised reviewers to access and update. The organisation should also use the latest evidence-based information to identify recurring issues and respond promptly.

##### Automate POA&M updates via ticket status, reminders, and evidence

- Category: Software
- Priority: Critical

###### WHAT
Set up automated processes to keep the system’s Plan of Action and Milestones (POA&M) accurate, up to date, and easy to access. This includes:
- continuously updating each POA&M item’s status based on the related work or ticket status
- automatically sending scheduled reminders
- automatically collecting and attaching supporting evidence when POA&M updates are made

###### WHY (control requirement)
The National Institute of Standards and Technology (NIST) Control CA-5(1) enhancement requires the organisation to “ensure the accuracy, currency, and availability of the plan of action and milestones for the system” by using the defined **automated mechanisms** (parameter **ca-05.01_odp**). Automated workflow and ticketing integration are intended to keep POA&M information accurate and current, make it available for sharing, and support coordination of security and privacy information.

###### HOW (specific steps/approach)
1. **Define POA&M as a structured record managed by automation**: create a POA&M data structure with fields for item and milestone identifiers, owner, due dates, status, the linked ticket/work item ID, and evidence references.
2. **Set up a reliable link to the ticket/work system**: for each POA&M item, create a permanent one-to-one link to the underlying ticket/work item (for example, Jira Service Management, ServiceNow, or Azure DevOps). Use application programming interfaces (APIs) and webhooks to receive status changes and/or run updates when events occur.
3. **Continuously check status and map it in a consistent way**: configure an automation service to continuously review the ticket/work status and translate it into the correct POA&M status and milestone dates (for example, “In Progress” means the milestone has started; “Done/Implemented” means the milestone is achieved). Record when the automation ran and the ticket status used for each update.
4. **Automate reminders, with a record of what was sent**: create reminders based on POA&M due dates (for example, reminder windows such as 7/14/30 days and reminders when overdue). Keep an auditable log of reminder creation and delivery results (who received it, how it was sent, when, and whether it was delivered successfully).
5. **Automate evidence collection and ensure it is linked correctly**: set up evidence sources such as ticket attachments, continuous integration/continuous delivery (CI/CD) outputs, scan reports, configuration snapshots, and items in the document repository. When a POA&M update is due, automatically collect the relevant evidence and attach it to the correct POA&M item using evidence IDs and standardised evidence details.
6. **Only allow POA&M updates to complete when evidence is available**: require evidence to be present (or record an explicit confirmation that evidence exists) before automation can move POA&M items into completion/achievement states. This helps ensure updates are accurate and can be justified.
7. **Make POA&M available and restrict access appropriately**: store POA&M in a central system with single sign-on (SSO) and role-based access control (RBAC) for authorised reviewers. Also monitor system uptime and raise alerts if the POA&M platform or automation service is not available.

###### WHO (role responsible)
- **POA&M owner / Governance lead**: defines the POA&M item structure, status definitions, reminder timing, and evidence requirements.
- **Security engineering / governance, risk, and compliance (GRC) automation engineer**: builds the integration, automation workflows, evidence collection, and evidence-based update checks.
- **Service management platform administrator (IT service management (ITSM)/DevOps)**: maintains ticket/work item fields, ensures webhooks/APIs are available, and keeps links accurate.
- **Identity and access management (IAM)/platform operations**: ensures authorised access (SSO/RBAC) and monitors availability.

###### Acceptance criteria
- Each POA&M item has a permanent link to the related ticket/work item.
- POA&M status and milestone dates update automatically within a defined time after ticket status changes, with recorded automation run times and the ticket status used.
- Reminders are generated automatically for upcoming and overdue milestones, and delivery is logged with timestamps and outcomes.
- Evidence is automatically collected and attached to the correct POA&M item for each update, including evidence IDs and evidence details.
- POA&M completion/achievement changes are blocked unless the required evidence (or confirmation) is present.
- Authorised reviewers can reliably access POA&M, and the POA&M platform/automation service has monitored availability with alerting.

###### Actions Required for Compliance

- [ ] Define the plan of action and milestones (POA&M) data model, including: statuses, milestone fields, due dates, evidence requirements, and the ticket or work identifiers linked to each milestone.
- [ ] Set up and enforce a one-to-one link between every POA&M item and a corresponding ticket or work item in the chosen IT service management (ITSM) or DevOps platform.
- [ ] Set up automated webhook and application programming interface (API) processes to keep ticket status continuously updated in the plan of action and milestones (POA&M), including milestone dates, and record details about each automation run.
- [ ] Set up scheduled reminder rules based on the plan of action and milestones (POA&M) due dates, and record a complete audit trail of each reminder sent (who sent it, when, which channel was used, and the result).
- [ ] Set up automated evidence collection sources and attach the evidence to the correct plan of action and milestones (POA&M) item, using consistent evidence reference IDs and supporting information (metadata).
- [ ] Add evidence-based checks so that when a plan of action and milestones (POA&M) moves to completion or achievement, there must be evidence available or an attestation provided before automation can write any updates.
- [ ] Set up single sign-on (SSO) and role-based access control (RBAC) for authorised reviewers, and put in place monitoring and alerts to track whether the POA&M platform and the automation workflow are available.

### CA-6 — Authorization (Control)

This requirement ensures that a senior, accountable leader formally approves (1) whether a system is allowed to run and (2) whether any shared “building-block” services the system depends on are allowed to be reused across the organisation. Without this, systems could be launched without the right people explicitly accepting the risks, which could lead to avoidable harm such as data loss, service disruption, or privacy breaches.

To meet this expectation, the organisation must appoint:
- one senior official to be responsible for approving the specific system, and
- another senior official to approve the shared common services that other systems may use.

Before the system starts operating, the system approving official must formally accept the shared services it will inherit and formally approve the system to operate. The shared-services approving official must formally approve those shared services for use by organisational systems.

Finally, these approvals must be reviewed and updated at least every 90 days, and clear records of the decisions must be kept.

##### 90-day authorisation refresh with inherited common-controls acceptance

- Category: Manual
- Priority: Critical

###### WHAT
Set up a governance process and an auditable authorisation record that (1) names the senior authorising officials for each system and for any shared “common controls” that the system can rely on, (2) requires clear, written acceptance of any inherited common controls and clear, written authorisation for the system to operate before it can go live, and (3) updates authorisations at least every **90 days**, using evidence from continuous monitoring.

###### WHY (control requirement)
Control CA-6 requires that authorising officials formally authorise system operation and clearly accept the associated risk, including when common controls are inherited. Authorisations must be issued or updated using evidence from continuous monitoring, and the organisation’s required update frequency is **every 90 days** (ca-06_odp).

###### HOW (specific steps/approach)
1. Create and maintain an **authorisation register** (single source of truth) that, at minimum, includes: system identifier, inherited common-controls identifiers, named **system authorising official**, named **common-controls authorising official**, approval/acceptance dates, and references to the evidence pack used.
2. Define standard **authorisation evidence packs** that are automatically filled using continuous monitoring outputs (for example: current security and privacy posture summaries, assessment results, and current POA&M status where applicable), so the authorising official bases decisions on up-to-date evidence.
3. Configure the governance workflow so the system authorising official cannot complete “authorise system to operate” until the workflow records a separate, explicit acceptance of **inherited common controls**, and the inherited scope matches what is listed in the register.
4. Configure the workflow so the common-controls authorising official must explicitly approve the common controls for **inheritance** by organisational systems, and that approval must be linked to the inherited common-controls identifiers used by the system.
5. Enforce the **90-day refresh cadence**: set automated reminders and prompts for re-approval; require re-approval (or a documented risk decision with evidence) when continuous monitoring shows material changes.
6. Keep auditable records for every decision: who approved or accepted, what scope was authorised or inherited, the evidence pack reference, and the decision date (including the 90-day refresh).

###### WHO
- **Authorising officials (where applicable, federal employees)**: provide formal acceptance of inherited common controls and authorise system operation; approve common controls for inheritance.
- **Governance workflow owner (for example, CISO office / compliance lead)**: maintains the authorisation register, workflow rules, evidence-pack templates, and refresh scheduling.
- **System owner / security engineering**: ensures continuous monitoring evidence is current and correctly mapped into evidence packs.

###### Acceptance criteria
- No production go-live happens unless there is a completed workflow record showing: (a) common-controls authorisation for inheritance, and (b) system authorising official acceptance of inherited common controls plus explicit authorisation to operate.
- Every system and inherited common-controls set has an authorisation record refreshed **at least every 90 days**, with evidence-pack references linked to continuous monitoring outputs.
- An audit review can trace each authorisation decision to: the named authorising officials, the authorised/inherited scope, the decision date, and the evidence pack used.

###### Actions Required for Compliance

- [ ] Create an authorisation register that includes: the system ID, the IDs of any inherited common controls, the names of the officials who authorise the system and the common controls, and references to the evidence packs.
- [ ] Create authorisation evidence pack templates and link them to continuous monitoring results, so the evidence is up to date for each 90-day update cycle.
- [ ] Set up the governance process so that “authorise the system to operate” cannot be approved until the acceptance of the inherited common controls has been clearly recorded, and the scope matches what is listed in the register.
- [ ] Set up the workflow so that organisational systems must get explicit approval for “common controls” before those controls can be inherited, and ensure no system can reference those common controls until that approval is in place.
- [ ] Set up and enforce a review every 90 days. Use automated reminders, and require re-approval if continuous monitoring shows any significant change in performance or behaviour.
- [ ] Keep auditable records for every decision, including who approved it, what was approved (the scope), the decision date, and the reference to the supporting evidence pack, to ensure readiness for audit.

#### CA-6.1 — Joint Authorization — Intra-organization (Enhancement)

This expectation is about making sure an important decision about a system is not approved by only one person. Instead, it must be reviewed and approved by more than one senior decision-maker within the same organisation. In practice, this helps prevent a situation where a single person’s judgement, mistake, or bias goes unchallenged—allowing the system to be used with avoidable weaknesses or privacy problems.

To meet this expectation, the organisation must set up a joint approval process for each system. For each approval, multiple authorising officials from the same organisation must independently review the information used to decide whether the system should be approved. They must then record their approval together, rather than relying on a single sign-off.

The organisation must document how this joint approval works in its relevant policies and procedures. Where applicable, it must also include this in the system’s security and privacy planning documents. The organisation must keep clear records showing who approved, that each person reviewed independently, and that the approvals relate to the same approval cycle.

#### CA-6.2 — Joint Authorization — Inter-organization (Enhancement)

This requirement is about making key “go/no-go” decisions for a system when there is more than one responsible person, including at least one decision-maker from outside your organisation. The risk it addresses is that an internal decision-maker working alone might approve something that is too risky, or overlook important issues because they are too close to the system—particularly when other organisations are affected by the outcome.

To meet this expectation, your organisation must use a joint authorisation process for the system. This means multiple authorising officials work together, and at least one authorising official must come from an external organisation. This includes the assessment findings, any required improvement plan, and the system’s security plan (and privacy plan where relevant).

### CA-7 — Continuous Monitoring (Control)

This expectation is about keeping a regular watch on each system so problems are found early, not months later. Without it, weaknesses could grow unnoticed, software might not be updated, unusual or unauthorised access could go undetected, harmful activity could spread, settings could be changed without permission, and important records might stop being collected—leaving the organisation exposed and unable to make timely decisions about risk and privacy.

To meet this expectation, the organisation must create a monitoring plan for each system that follows the organisation’s overall approach. It must then run the plan by checking key indicators every week, including the results of vulnerability checks, whether updates are applied, unusual privileged access, alerts about suspicious activity, malware detections, unexpected changes to settings, and whether logs are being successfully collected.

Every quarter, the organisation must review how well these measures are working, combine and analyse the findings, take timely action to fix issues, and report the system’s security status to the Chief Information Security Officer every month and its privacy status to the Privacy Officer every quarter to support ongoing authorisation decisions.

##### System-level continuous monitoring control plane with weekly KPIs

- Category: Software
- Priority: Critical

###### WHAT
Set up a continuous monitoring programme for the application that regularly checks the system’s security and privacy position. Use agreed system-level measures to do this, with **weekly monitoring** of the indicators that matter for controls, **quarterly checks** to see how well those controls are working, and **risk-based actions** that follow the results. Provide **monthly security updates** to the **Authorising Official (AO)/Chief Information Security Officer (CISO)** and **quarterly privacy updates** to the **Privacy Officer** to support ongoing authorisation decisions.

###### WHY (control requirement)
National Institute of Standards and Technology (NIST) Special Publication (SP) 800-53 **Control CA-7** requires the organisation to create a **system-level continuous monitoring strategy** that matches the organisation’s overall strategy, and to **carry out continuous monitoring** that supports decisions based on risk. The guidance explains that “continuous/ongoing” means monitoring often enough to support risk-based decisions, that monitoring results must lead to **risk response actions**, and that controls and risks should be assessed and monitored often enough to support **ongoing authorisation decisions**. The organisation’s agreed parameters specify the system-level measures to use, weekly monitoring, quarterly checks of control effectiveness, and the reporting roles and timing.

###### HOW (specific steps/approach)
Create a “control plane” approach that starts with measures (metrics). This standardises how data is collected, cleaned and prepared, scored, supported with evidence, and reported for the application across all environments (production, testing, and disaster recovery). It should also connect with existing enterprise tools, including: security information and event management (SIEM)/log platforms, vulnerability scanning, patch management, identity and access management (IAM) monitoring, intrusion detection system/intrusion prevention system (IDS/IPS), endpoint detection and response (EDR), and configuration baseline tools.

1. **Define the application monitoring data structure** by mapping each required **system-level metric** to its data source, the evidence items it produces, the severity thresholds, and who is responsible for fixing issues (for example: vulnerability scan results → evidence for ticketing; patch compliance → patch management reports; unusual privileged access → identity and access management (IAM)/privileged access management (PAM) logs; IDS/IPS alerts → network security event records; malware detections → EDR alerts; configuration changes outside the baseline → baseline comparison results; log collection success rate → SIEM ingestion health measures).
2. **Build automated evidence collection pipelines** to gather and standardise the measures above into one system-level dataset (per environment). Use consistent identifiers (system ID, environment, timestamp) and evidence links that can be audited (such as scan report IDs, ticket IDs, and SIEM query IDs).
3. **Run weekly monitoring** (at least weekly) to calculate each measure’s current value, direction of change (trend), and whether it breaches thresholds. Automatically raise alerts and create or update remediation tickets when thresholds are breached (including failures in “log collection success rate” to ensure monitoring continues reliably).
4. **Complete quarterly control effectiveness assessments** by linking measure trends to control outcomes (for example: patch compliance delays versus vulnerability exposure; unusual privileged access versus successful authorisation/approval outcomes; configuration drift versus incident or alert rates). Record root-cause analysis when multiple related controls or capabilities appear ineffective.
5. **Carry out risk response actions** based on the assessment results: prioritise fixes according to risk (system importance plus measure severity), track actions until completion (or until a time-limited exception is documented), and ensure the outcomes feed back into the monitoring thresholds and baselines.
6. **Produce the required reporting**: create **monthly security status** for the **Authorising Official (AO)/CISO** and **quarterly privacy status** for the **Privacy Officer**, including: measure status, a summary of the control effectiveness assessment, key incidents or near-misses, remediation progress, and any exceptions that could affect authorisation.

###### WHO (roles responsible)
- **Security Engineering / Security Operations Centre (SOC) Engineering**: implement the control plane, integrations, weekly measure calculations, and evidence pipelines.
- **Application Owner / Infrastructure Owner**: own the triage and closure of remediation tickets for findings relevant to the application.
- **Privacy Officer**: receives quarterly privacy status reporting and reviews trends that could affect privacy.
- **Authorising Official (AO) / CISO**: receives monthly security status reporting to support ongoing authorisation decisions.
- **Governance, Risk and Compliance (GRC) / Risk Management**: ensures reporting outputs and exceptions are recorded to support authorisation decisions.

###### Acceptance criteria
- The application has an approved **system-level continuous monitoring strategy** aligned to the organisation-level strategy.
- The system-level measures defined in the organisation’s parameters are implemented and monitored **at least weekly** (vulnerability scan results, patch compliance, unusual privileged access, IDS/IPS alerts, malware detections, configuration drift, log collection success rate).
- A **quarterly control effectiveness assessment** is completed and documented, including correlation/analysis and root-cause analysis where grouped controls or capabilities appear ineffective.
- Risk response actions are triggered from monitoring results, with evidence of ticket creation, prioritisation, and closure (or time-limited exceptions).
- **Monthly security status** is produced for the **Authorising Official (AO)/CISO** and **quarterly privacy status** is produced for the **Privacy Officer**, each including measure status, assessment outcomes, and remediation progress.
- Monitoring continuity is demonstrated by alerting on **log collection success rate** and evidence that any ingestion failures are addressed.

###### Actions Required for Compliance

- [ ] Approve a continuous monitoring approach at the system level for the application, aligned with the organisation-wide strategy.
- [ ] Define the system monitoring data model that links each required metric to its data sources, the threshold levels for action, and the supporting evidence records.
- [ ] Bring together vulnerability scanning results, patch management status, identity and privileged access monitoring data, intrusion detection and prevention alerts, endpoint detection and response (EDR) signals, configuration change monitoring, and security information and event management (SIEM) reporting health into one consistent, standardised dataset.
- [ ] Set up weekly automated calculations of key metrics and alerts for when thresholds are exceeded, so that remediation work tickets are created or updated.
- [ ] Carry out a quarterly review of how effective each control is, by linking changes in performance measures to the results of the controls, and record the underlying cause analysis when it is needed.
- [ ] Set up risk response workflows to monitor fixes until they are completed, or until a time-limited, documented exception is approved. Use these updates to inform ongoing authorisation decisions.
- [ ] Produce a monthly security status report for the Authorising Official/Chief Information Security Officer (CISO) and a quarterly privacy status report for the Privacy Officer, including audit-ready evidence.

#### CA-7.1 — Independent Assessment (Enhancement)

This requirement is about making sure an independent person regularly checks that the organisation’s protective measures are working properly in day-to-day practice. Without independent, impartial checking, there is a risk that problems are missed, biased views are introduced, or the same people who set up the protections end up judging their own work. This can hide weaknesses and delay fixes.

To meet this expectation, the organisation must appoint independent assessors, or an independent assessment team, to monitor how well the organisation’s security protections are performing on an ongoing basis—not as a one-off review.

These assessors must:
- have no conflicting interests with the organisation
- not monitor any areas they designed, built, configured, maintained, or directly produced
- not act as the organisation’s managers or employees
- not take an advocacy role that would favour the organisation’s interests over an honest assessment

#### CA-7.3 — Trend Analyses (Enhancement)

This expectation is about keeping your ongoing checks effective by learning from what is actually happening. If the organisation does not regularly review real-world evidence, it may continue using the same security measures, the same monitoring timetable, and the same types of monitoring even as threats change, new weaknesses are found, or earlier problems keep coming back. This can leave gaps that attackers could take advantage of.

To meet this expectation, the organisation must regularly review measured evidence and trends, including:
- recent threat activity
- how successful different attacks have been
- newly discovered weaknesses in relevant technologies
- changes in how people are being deceived (for example, phishing or impersonation)
- whether security settings are working as intended
- results from multiple internal assessments
- findings from auditors and Inspectors General

Based on what the evidence shows, the organisation must decide whether to update how security is put in place, how often monitoring happens, and which monitoring activities are used. The organisation must record these decisions and any changes made.

##### Evidence-driven trend analyses to tune control and monitoring

- Category: Manual
- Priority: High

###### WHAT must be done
Set up a repeatable trend-analysis capability that uses real-world evidence to decide whether (1) how controls are implemented, (2) how often continuous monitoring is carried out, and (3) the kinds of monitoring activities used in continuous monitoring need to be changed.

###### WHY (control requirement)
CA-7(3) enhancement requires using trend analyses based on real-world evidence to decide whether continuous monitoring needs to be changed. This includes changes to how controls are implemented, how frequently monitoring happens, and what types of monitoring activities are used. The trend analysis must take into account recent threat information (including threat event types and how often attacks succeed), new and emerging vulnerabilities, changing social engineering tactics, how well configuration settings are working, results from multiple control assessments, and findings from Inspectors General or auditors.

###### HOW (specific steps/approach)
1. **Create a standard trend-analysis template** for each system/environment (production, test, and disaster recovery as applicable). It must have three decision sections: **control implementation**, **monitoring frequency**, and **monitoring activity types**. Each section must record: evidence inputs, what was observed, a recommendation (change/increase/decrease/keep), and the impact/risk reason for the recommendation.
2. **Bring together and standardise evidence** into one dataset for analysis, at minimum including:
   - Recent threat and incident intelligence, linked to threat event types and changes in social engineering.
   - Indicators of whether attacks succeed (for example, compromise rates, rates of successful phishing leading to credential use, or other success measures defined by the organisation).
   - Emerging vulnerabilities relevant to the application environment (for example, newly identified vulnerabilities, including signals about how easily they can be exploited and how long fixes take).
   - Results showing whether configurations are effective (for example, repeated configuration drift, baseline compliance failure rates, and whether failures line up with incidents or detections).
   - Results from multiple control assessments (internal assessments and control testing outcomes).
   - Inspector General and auditor findings, mapped to the control areas affected.
3. **Set decision thresholds and triggers** (documented and controlled for version changes) that convert evidence into required actions, for example:
   - If configuration effectiveness shows repeated failures for the same control objective across consecutive cycles, recommend updating the control implementation and/or the monitoring checks.
   - If detection success measures fall below the target (or if coverage gaps are found), recommend changing the monitoring activity types and/or the monitoring frequency.
   - If assessment, audit, or Inspector General findings show recurring weaknesses, recommend changing monitoring procedures and/or the control implementation.
4. **Run the trend analysis on a defined schedule** (for example, quarterly or based on system risk). Produce an auditable **Recommendations Register** with unique IDs, including: evidence summary, decision made, and whether changes were implemented or postponed (with the reason).
5. **Make traceable updates** to continuous monitoring materials when recommendations require change:
   - Update monitoring rules/detections and telemetry sources (monitoring activity types).
   - Update monitoring schedules/intervals (monitoring frequency).
   - Update control implementation/configuration baselines where configuration effectiveness shows they are not working.
   - Record outcomes and link them to change-control items and any status or plan records (where the organisation uses them).

###### WHO (role responsible)
- **Continuous Monitoring Lead (Cybersecurity) / Security Operations Centre (SOC) Engineering Lead**: owns the trend-analysis process, evidence mapping, and recommendations.
- **Application Owner / System Owner**: checks whether recommended changes are practical and what operational impact they would have.
- **Governance, Risk and Compliance (GRC) / Compliance Manager**: ensures documentation is ready for audit, remains traceable, and aligns with assessment/audit/Inspector General findings.
- **Change Manager / Engineering Change Board**: approves and implements authorised changes.

###### Acceptance criteria
- Trend analyses are completed on the defined schedule and include all required evidence categories (threat information, attack success indicators, emerging vulnerabilities, social engineering evolution, configuration effectiveness, multiple assessment results, and auditor/Inspector General findings).
- For each system/environment, the Recommendations Register records: the evidence used, the decision outcome for control implementation/frequency/monitoring activity types, the rationale, and whether changes were implemented.
- Any implemented changes are traceable to the continuous monitoring materials they affect (monitoring schedules, telemetry/detections, and relevant configuration/control implementation updates). They must also be linked to change-control and any plan/status records the organisation uses.
- Evidence and decisions are kept in an auditable form suitable for review by internal audit and external assessors.

###### Actions Required for Compliance

- [ ] Create and publish a standard template for analysing trends, including how controls are put in place, how often they are monitored, and what kinds of monitoring activities are used.
- [ ] Identify and bring together the required evidence sources—such as threat intelligence and incident post-mortems, vulnerability trends, results showing how effective configurations are, assessment results, and findings from auditors or Inspectors General—into one analysis dataset.

Where applicable, link each update to the change-control records and the plan of action and milestones (POA&M) and status records.

#### CA-7.4 — Risk Monitoring (Enhancement)

This requirement is about making sure the organisation keeps checking, as things change, whether its plans for managing risk are still working. Without this ongoing checking, the organisation may only find out too late that the actions it put in place are no longer effective, that required safeguards are missing, or that changes to systems or the way they operate have quietly increased security or privacy risk.

To meet this expectation, the organisation must build risk monitoring into its regular, ongoing monitoring activities, so it can: (1) regularly test whether actions that reduce risk are still effective, (2) confirm that the required risk-reducing actions have actually been carried out and that security and privacy requirements are being met, and (3) identify and assess changes to systems and their operating environment that could affect security and privacy risk.

This monitoring should be carried out in line with the level of risk the organisation has agreed it is willing to accept, and the results should be used to inform ongoing reviews and action planning.

##### Risk monitoring control-plane integrated into continuous monitoring

- Category: Software
- Priority: Critical

###### WHAT
Build risk monitoring into the organisation’s ongoing continuous monitoring programme by implementing a “risk monitoring control-plane” that continuously checks **effectiveness**, **compliance**, and **changes**, using evidence that matches the organisation’s **risk tolerance**.

###### WHY (control requirement)
NIST CA-7(4) enhancement requires that risk monitoring is part of continuous monitoring, including:
- **Effectiveness monitoring** to confirm that the risk response measures being used are still working.
- **Compliance monitoring** to confirm that the required risk response measures are in place and that security and privacy requirements are being met.
- **Change monitoring** to spot changes to systems and operational environments that could affect security and privacy risk.
Monitoring must follow the organisation’s agreed **risk tolerance**.

###### HOW (specific steps/approach)
Set up a central control-plane that connects each risk response to (a) the security and privacy requirements it is meant to meet and (b) the evidence sources and checks used to monitor it. Use automated evidence collection and rule-based checks to provide continuous status for effectiveness, compliance, and change impact.

###### 1) Build risk-to-evidence mappings (control-plane model)
- For each risk response measure, define: the related security and privacy requirements, what the monitoring is trying to achieve (effectiveness/compliance/change), and the evidence sources (for example: security information and event management (SIEM) alerts, identity provider logs, vulnerability scan results, configuration baseline drift, application audit logs, and cloud audit logs).
- Make sure the mappings cover the application’s production, test, and disaster recovery (DR) environments, and include third-party integrations where relevant.

###### 2) Implement effectiveness monitoring rules
- Define measurable key performance indicators (KPIs) and thresholds based on risk tolerance (for example: authentication success rates, detection coverage, how quickly vulnerabilities are fixed, and how often encryption settings meet requirements).
- Continuously review trends and thresholds to confirm that risk response measures remain effective.

###### 3) Implement compliance monitoring checks
- Continuously confirm that required risk response measures are in place and that security and privacy requirements are met.
- Use configuration drift detection and policy-violation checks (for example: adherence to access control policies, logging and retention settings, encryption at rest and in transit settings, and coverage of privileged access monitoring).

###### 4) Implement change monitoring and impact assessment
- Link change events (continuous integration/continuous delivery (CI/CD) releases, infrastructure-as-code changes, configuration changes, identity and access management (IAM) policy changes, dependency and version updates, and vendor onboarding/offboarding) to the risk responses and requirements they could affect.
- Start reassessment workflows when changes exceed agreed impact thresholds.

###### 5) Risk tolerance-driven escalation and reassessment workflow
- Set escalation rules so that when effectiveness falls below thresholds, compliance checks fail, or change impact is high, the system automatically:
  - creates or updates remediation tasks,
  - triggers a risk reassessment,
  - updates programme of actions and milestones (POA&M) and status reporting with evidence links, responsible owners, and due dates.

###### 6) Produce audit-ready monitoring outputs
- Keep monitoring results with timestamps, evidence references, evaluation logic version details, and named accountable owners.
- Provide ongoing reporting views for security and privacy stakeholders and governance.

###### WHO (role responsible)
- **Security Engineering Lead / governance, risk and compliance (GRC) Lead**: owns the risk-to-evidence mappings, threshold definitions, and escalation logic aligned to risk tolerance.
- **Platform/Cloud Security Architect**: builds evidence collection processes and integrates with SIEM, observability tooling, and configuration management.
- **Application Owner / Engineering Manager**: ensures application-level monitoring data and configuration signals are available and correctly set up.
- **Security Operations Centre (SOC)/Monitoring Operations**: checks that alerts, triage processes, and remediation actions are working correctly.

###### Acceptance criteria
- For each defined risk response measure, there is a documented mapping to security and privacy requirements and at least one automated evidence source.
- Effectiveness monitoring produces continuous pass/fail (or graded) results against risk tolerance-informed thresholds, and triggers reassessment and remediation when thresholds are breached.
- Compliance monitoring continuously confirms that required risk response measures are in place and that security and privacy requirements are met, with evidence kept for audit.
- Change monitoring identifies relevant changes to systems and environments and triggers reassessment/remediation workflows based on agreed impact criteria.
- Monitoring outputs feed continuous monitoring reporting and update POA&M/remediation tracking with evidence links, owners, and due dates.

###### Actions Required for Compliance

- [ ] Create a mapping that links each identified risk to the security and privacy requirements for the application and its connected services.
- [ ] Define effectiveness monitoring key performance indicators (KPIs) and set clear pass/fail thresholds based on the organisation’s risk tolerance.
- [ ] Carry out ongoing compliance checks by looking for changes from the approved configuration and using evidence of policy breaches, with the results kept for audit purposes.
- [ ] Link change events (continuous integration and continuous delivery (CI/CD), infrastructure as code (IaC) changes, identity and access management (IAM) and configuration changes, and software dependency updates) to risk-and-response rules.
- [ ] Set up automatic escalation for remediation and a reassessment of risk when effectiveness or compliance fails, or when the impact of a change is high.
- [ ] Publish monitoring reports that are ready for audit, and for each result show the supporting evidence, the version of the evaluation logic used, and the accountable owner responsible.

#### CA-7.5 — Consistency Analysis (Enhancement)

This requirement is about making sure your security and privacy rules are applied in the same way across all systems, and that different safeguards do not accidentally interfere with each other. Without this, you could end up with hidden gaps where some systems are not protected, or where one safeguard (for example, protecting data while it is being sent) prevents another safeguard (for example, recording activity), leaving you unable to see what is happening or exposed to risk.

To meet this expectation, the organisation must regularly run automated checks to compare settings and how policies are applied across all relevant environments. It must confirm that each policy requirement has been turned into the correct safeguards, and review system configurations to ensure they are consistent. It must also test how key safeguards work together, check the results for consistency, record any issues found with clear details of where they occurred and why, fix them, and then re-test to confirm the fixes worked.

Finally, the organisation must keep clear evidence of what was tested, what was found, who carried out the work, and how any fixes were tracked and verified over time.

##### Automated policy-to-control consistency and non-interference testing

- Category: Software
- Priority: Critical

###### WHAT must be done
Set up and run repeatable checks to confirm that: (1) the required security and privacy policies are in place and have been correctly turned into real-world controls, and (2) those controls work in a consistent, coordinated way across all relevant environments, without interfering with each other. Any mismatches or suspected clashes between controls must be fixed and then re-checked, with proof that can be audited.

###### WHY (control requirement)
NIST Special Publication 800-53 (CA-7(5)) enhancement requires using the organisation’s defined actions to confirm that policies are in place and that the implemented controls work consistently (parameter: **ca-7.7.5_prm_1**). The guidance notes that adding controls step-by-step can create unacceptable security and privacy gaps or cause controls to interfere with each other (for example, encryption preventing monitoring; incomplete monitoring across dual-stack internet protocol versions).

###### HOW (specific steps/approach)
Create an automated “consistency analysis” process that combines:

1) **Policy-to-control mapping checks**: confirm that every policy requirement has a matching implemented control or configuration in each environment (including privacy controls where relevant). If anything is missing or wrong, the result must clearly state the policy requirement, the expected control/configuration item, and the environment/component where the link is missing or incorrect.

2) **Configuration and policy consistency testing across environments**: run automated configuration reviews and policy consistency tests (for example, baseline compliance checks, cloud configuration checks, and infrastructure-as-code policy checks) to detect changes over time (“drift”) and inconsistent application of the same control intent.

3) **Targeted interoperability and non-interference testing**: run controlled tests that specifically check known interaction risks for the technology being used, such as:
   - encryption settings in transit do not disable or block required telemetry, logging, or monitoring;
   - logging and monitoring collectors keep working for all relevant network protocols (for example, Internet Protocol version 4 and version 6 where applicable);
   - security components (web application firewall, reverse proxy, application programming interface gateway, and transport layer security termination) do not stop the events needed by monitoring and security information and event management (SIEM).

4) **Fix verification and re-testing**: for each issue found, record the remediation, apply the fix, and run the same checks again to confirm the problem is resolved. Only then is the issue considered closed.

5) **Evidence pack generation**: for each run, produce an auditable evidence bundle that includes: the scope (environments/components), policy-to-control mapping results, configuration test outputs, interoperability test results, the remediation actions taken, and proof from the re-test.

###### Concrete implementation approach for this enterprise application context
- Use a central policy repository (policy-as-code or an equivalent approach) and a versioned **policy-to-control mapping** document.
- Add automated checks to continuous integration and continuous delivery (CI/CD) for change testing, and to scheduled jobs for drift detection, across development, testing, production, and disaster recovery.
- For interoperability and non-interference, run realistic test traffic through the application’s real network path (for example, via application programming interface calls and representative web requests), while confirming that security information and event management (SIEM) and monitoring/observability data ingestion is complete for both encrypted and unencrypted traffic where applicable.
- If operational technology (OT) is not in scope, focus on information technology (IT), the enterprise application, and its network and security integration points (identity, SIEM, monitoring/observability, gateways/proxies, databases, messaging, and backup).

###### WHO (role responsible)
- **Security Engineering / Governance, Risk and Compliance (GRC) Automation Lead**: owns the consistency analysis process design, the mapping documents, and the evidence pack structure.
- **Application Owner / Platform Engineering**: implements the fixes and ensures the re-tests pass.
- **Cybersecurity Monitoring and Detection Engineering**: checks that interoperability and non-interference test results match the expected behaviour in SIEM and monitoring/observability.
- **Privacy Officer (where applicable)**: confirms that privacy policy requirements are included in the mapping and in the scope of the consistency checks.

###### Acceptance criteria
- For every policy requirement in scope, the policy-to-control mapping check produces a positive result in every required environment, or there is an approved exception record.
- Automated configuration and policy consistency tests show no unresolved drift for the mapped controls across environments.
- Targeted interoperability and non-interference tests show that required monitoring and telemetry are produced and not blocked by encryption or other security components (with results captured in the evidence pack).
- Every issue includes: the remediation action, the responsible owner, the implementation date, and successful re-test evidence; no issue is closed without re-test confirmation.
- Evidence packs are kept according to the organisation’s audit and retention requirements, and can be reproduced (same scope and test versions).

###### Actions Required for Compliance

- [ ] Define which environments are in scope, which application components are included, and which security and privacy policies will be assessed for consistency.
- [ ] Create and version a policy-to-control mapping document that links each policy requirement to the expected implemented control or configuration for each environment.
- [ ] Set up automated checks to confirm that configurations and policies are consistent. Run these checks across all in-scope environments and produce a clear pass or fail result for each environment.
- [ ] Design and carry out focused tests to confirm systems work together without causing unwanted effects—for example, check that encrypted traffic still produces the required logs and monitoring data, and that monitoring covers all relevant communication protocols—and record the results.
- [ ] Set up a remediation process that requires the same checks to be run again before any issue is closed, with the responsible owner and supporting evidence recorded.
- [ ] For each run, create and keep an evidence pack that can be audited. It must include the scope, the mapping results, the test results, any remediation actions taken, and confirmation that re-testing was completed.

#### CA-7.6 — Automation Support for Monitoring (Enhancement)

This requirement is about making sure the information your organisation uses to monitor a system is correct, up to date, and available when needed. Without these checks, staff could make decisions based on monitoring information that is missing, damaged, or out of date. They might also not realise that the automated process that collects and reports monitoring data has stopped working, so problems could go unnoticed for longer than necessary.

To meet this expectation, the organisation must use automated checks to confirm that monitoring results are valid before they are treated as usable. It must also verify that the results are recent by comparing their timestamp against an agreed “freshness” time window. In addition, it must automatically detect and report failures in the monitoring process to the right responsible people, with enough detail for them to take action.

These automated checks and alerts must run continuously or at the agreed frequency. The organisation must also be able to demonstrate, through records and procedures, that monitoring results remain accessible and that the validation checks, freshness checks, and failure alerts are carried out and evidenced.

##### Automated validation and freshness gating for monitoring outputs

- Category: Software
- Priority: Critical

###### WHAT
Set up automated checks that confirm monitoring results are correct, up to date, and still accessible. This includes using timestamps (and a defined “freshness” time window) and automatically detecting and alerting on failures in the monitoring process.

###### WHY (control requirement)
NIST SP 800-53 CA-7(6) enhancement requires: “Ensure the accuracy, currency, and availability of monitoring results for the system using {{ insert: param, ca-07.06_odp }}.” The organisation-defined parameter **ca-07.06_odp** requires **automated mechanisms** for “automated monitoring data validation, timestamp/currency checks, and alerting for monitoring pipeline failures.”

###### HOW (specific steps/approach)
1. **Define an agreed monitoring data standard for each system** (per environment: production, testing, disaster recovery) including required fields, rules for structure and format, acceptable value ranges, and an organisation-defined **freshness window** (for example: “results must be updated within X minutes”).
2. **Add validation checks into the monitoring process** so monitoring results are only published or marked “usable” when automated checks pass:
   - structure/format validation (for example: JSON structure checks or Avro contract checks)
   - completeness checks for required metrics and events
   - checks for corrupted or malformed content (for example: successful parsing and non-null values for critical fields)
   - **timestamp/freshness checks** comparing event or metric timestamps to the publish time and enforcing the freshness window
3. **Automatically detect monitoring pipeline failures** using health signals from each stage (collection, processing/normalisation, storage/indexing, and export to dashboards, security information and event management (SIEM), and observability):
   - stalled jobs or missing expected data volume
   - connector or export failures
   - dead-letter or failed message queues
   - exporter timeouts or repeated ingestion errors
4. **Automatically raise alerts** when validation checks or pipeline health checks fail, sending them to the right monitoring or incident channels with clear, usable details:
   - system or component affected
   - failure type (validation failed versus stale data versus pipeline unhealthy)
   - first and last failure time, and last successful publish time
   - affected environment(s)
   - correlation identifiers (job or run id, connector name)
5. **Maintain availability using fail-safe behaviour**:
   - if validation fails or data becomes stale, stop showing “green/healthy” indicators and publish a clearly flagged status (for example: “monitoring unavailable/invalid”), while still raising alerts
   - ensure the validation and alerting mechanism itself is monitored (meta-monitoring) so failures in the checking layer are also detected
6. **Keep audit evidence** by storing validation outcomes and pipeline health events (for example, tamper-resistant logs or metrics) so the organisation can demonstrate the accuracy, currency, and availability of monitoring results.

###### WHO (role responsible)
- **Application Owner / Monitoring Engineering Lead**: defines the monitoring data standard and freshness window for each system.
- **Platform/Observability Engineer**: implements the pipeline validation checks, failure detection, and alert routing.
- **Security Operations Centre (SOC) / SIEM Operations (or Security Monitoring Team)**: checks that alerts are useful and ensures correct ownership and triage processes.

###### Acceptance criteria
- Monitoring results for the system are **only marked usable** when automated structure/format, completeness, and timestamp freshness checks all pass.
- If results are stale or invalid, the pipeline **automatically flags** the monitoring status and **generates alerts** that include system/component, failure type, and timing (first/last failure and last successful publish).
- If the monitoring pipeline fails (collection, processing, or export), automated detection triggers alerts within the organisation-defined monitoring frequency or continuous monitoring expectation.
- Validation outcomes and pipeline health events are retained as evidence and can be produced for audit (for example, logs or metrics showing validation pass/fail and freshness evaluation).

###### Actions Required for Compliance

- [ ] Define a monitoring data agreement for each system, including the data format (schema), the required fields, and the rules for acceptable values and ranges, and set an organisation-approved time limit for how up-to-date the data must be (freshness window).
- [ ] Set up automated checks in the monitoring process so that only results that have been verified and are up to date are published as “usable”.
- [ ] Add checks for the time and currency of results by comparing each result’s timestamp with the publish time, and enforce a freshness window to ensure the information is recent.
- [ ] Set up automated checks to detect whether each pipeline stage is working correctly (collection, processing, storage and indexing, and export) and categorise any failures.
- [ ] Set up automated alerts for validation failures, out-of-date (stale) data, and pipeline failures, including the relevant system or component and when the issue occurred.
- [ ] Set up fail-safe behaviour to prevent misleading “healthy” signals, and publish a flagged status showing that monitoring is unavailable or the data is invalid.
- [ ] Store the results of validation checks and pipeline health events as audit evidence, and set up “meta-monitoring” for the validation and alerting layer.

### CA-8 — Penetration Testing (Control)

This requirement is about deliberately testing your public-facing digital services to see how well they resist real-world attacks. Without this, weaknesses in your website, your application programming interfaces (APIs), or other internet-accessible services may go unnoticed until criminals exploit them. This could lead to data theft, fraud, service disruption, or privacy breaches.

To meet this expectation, the organisation must commission skilled, hands-on penetration testing at least every year, and also after any major change, or after any significant vulnerability is identified, in those internet-facing web applications, APIs, or externally accessible network services.

The testing must be carried out by people with suitable experience. There must be clear, agreed limits on what they are allowed to do, and there must be a plan, a written report, and retained evidence showing what was tested and what was found.

The organisation must then review the results and either fix the issues with clear ownership and dates recorded, or formally accept the risk with documented approval.

##### Annual external penetration tests with change/significant-vuln triggers

- Category: Manual
- Priority: Critical

###### WHAT
Commission and carry out penetration testing on the organisation-defined scope of **internet-facing web applications, application programming interfaces (APIs), and externally accessible network services** at a defined **frequency** of **once a year**, and again **after major changes or after significant vulnerabilities are identified**.

###### WHY (control requirement)
National Institute of Standards and Technology (NIST) Special Publication (SP) 800-53 **CA-8** requires: “Conduct penetration testing {{ insert: param, ca-08_odp.01 }} on {{ insert: param, ca-08_odp.02 }}.” The guidance also requires that penetration testing is **specialist and hands-on**, carried out by **teams with proven skills and experience**, using **rules for how testing will be carried out** that are agreed before testing starts, and with **planning, reporting, and supporting evidence** kept on record. The results must be used to support fixing issues or formally accepting the risk.

###### HOW (specific approach)
1. **Define and keep up to date the list of in-scope assets** for ca-08_odp.02 by linking the internet edge inventory (domain name system (DNS)/edge routing, web application firewall (WAF)/API gateway/load balancers) to the application/service catalogue, ensuring each item is classified as a web application, API, or externally accessible network service.
2. **Set up an ongoing annual testing arrangement** with a suitably skilled and authorised penetration testing provider (or an internal red team). Require in the contract/statement of work (SOW) that testing includes **manual, exploit-focused checks** beyond automated scanning, covering network, operating system (OS)/application, and/or application-layer weaknesses where relevant.
3. **Create standard Rules of Engagement (RoE) templates** and require a signed RoE for each testing engagement, including: testing time windows, rate limits, clear **stop conditions** (availability/impact thresholds), prohibited actions (for example, destructive testing), and limits for handling any sensitive/protected information that may be exposed during testing.
4. **Use two triggers to commission targeted additional penetration testing**:
   - **Major change trigger (ca-08_odp.01):** when a release/change affects in-scope endpoints/services (for example, a new API version, changes to authentication/authorisation, or major edge infrastructure/network changes), automatically check whether exploitability validation is needed and commission an add-on test for the affected scope.
   - **Significant vulnerability trigger (ca-08_odp.01):** when a critical/high vulnerability is confirmed (from vulnerability management, incident findings, or other credible sources), commission an add-on test to confirm exploitability and verify that remediation is effective.
5. **Produce and keep audit-ready evidence** for each engagement: engagement letter, signed RoE, pre-test analysis/plan, test execution evidence (for example, logs/screenshots where appropriate), the final penetration test report, and a mapping of findings to remediation actions (including owners and due dates).
6. **Run a governance process for results**: review findings, ensure remediation is tracked and verified with follow-up testing, or formally record risk acceptance with approval where remediation is not practical.

###### WHO (roles responsible)
- **Information Security (Security Testing Lead):** owns RoE templates, the trigger workflow, and review of results.
- **Application Owners / Service Owners:** provide technical context, fix findings, and support verification.
- **Procurement/Legal (or Governance):** ensures contracts/SOW include RoE, evidence requirements, and constraints.
- **Penetration Test Provider / Internal Red Team:** performs hands-on testing within the agreed RoE.

###### Acceptance criteria
- At least **one penetration test per year** is completed for all assets in ca-08_odp.02.
- For each **major change** and each **confirmed significant vulnerability**, a documented decision is made, and where required an **add-on penetration test** is commissioned and completed for the affected scope.
- Each engagement has **signed RoE** with clear constraints and stop conditions, and testing is carried out by a **clearly skilled** team using hands-on exploit validation.
- A complete evidence pack exists for each engagement (plan, report, and retained records). Findings are either **fixed and verified** or **formally risk-accepted** with documented approval.

###### Actions Required for Compliance

- [ ] Keep an up-to-date, authoritative list of all internet-facing web applications, application programming interfaces (APIs), and externally accessible network services that fall within the CA-8 scope.
- [ ] Commission a standing annual penetration testing engagement that requires practical, hands-on proof that any security weakness can actually be exploited, as set out in the statement of work (SOW).
- [ ] Create and approve standard “Rules of Engagement” templates, including when to stop and how to handle limits for sensitive information.
- [ ] Set up a major-change trigger process to commission targeted add-on penetration tests for any in-scope endpoints or services that are affected by the change.
- [ ] Set up a process that, when a serious security weakness is found, automatically commissions targeted add-on penetration tests to confirm whether the weakness can be exploited and to check that the fixes work effectively.
- [ ] For each engagement, store and keep an evidence pack containing the signed rules of engagement (RoE), the plan, the report, and the test evidence. Link each finding to a remediation ticket, including the responsible owner and the due date.
- [ ] Review the security results to confirm that any fixes have been verified, or that any remaining risk has been formally accepted and approved.

#### CA-8.1 — Independent Penetration Testing Agent or Team (Enhancement)

This requirement is about hiring an independent team to try to break into your systems. The goal is to find weaknesses before criminals do. Without impartial testing, serious problems may be missed because the people most closely involved in building or running the system could unintentionally overlook issues, or because testing may focus on what is easiest rather than what is most risky.

To meet this expectation, the organisation must commission an external or otherwise independent penetration testing agent or team to test the relevant system, or clearly defined parts of it. The organisation must also ensure the testers have no actual or perceived conflicts of interest with the system’s development, day-to-day operation, or management.

The organisation should have clear written procedures for planning and running this testing. Before testing starts, it should agree a testing plan that sets out the scope and approach. The organisation should then keep the test report and a summary of findings, along with supporting evidence, in an organised way.

The organisation should assign internal responsibility for overseeing the testing, and ensure the activity is included in the system’s security planning, and privacy planning where applicable.

##### Independent, conflict-free penetration testing with controlled approval

- Category: Manual
- Priority: Critical

###### WHAT
Commission an independent penetration testing provider (or team) to carry out penetration testing on the in-scope system and/or system components. Ensure the provider is impartial (with no actual or perceived conflicts of interest) and that the work is carried out under documented, approved procedures.

###### WHY (control requirement)
NIST Special Publication 800-53, control CA-8(1) enhancement requires the organisation to **use an independent penetration testing provider or team** to test the system/components, with **impartiality** (no conflicts of interest with the development, operation, or management of the target). The guidance also expects controlled planning and execution, and that reports and supporting evidence are kept.

###### HOW (specific approach)
1. **Select and contract an independent provider**: Use an external penetration testing provider (or an internal team that can clearly demonstrate independence). Put this in a contract or statement of work (SOW) that explicitly prevents involvement in the target system’s development, day-to-day operation, or management. Require disclosure of any previous involvement and confirm that any subcontractors are also independent.
2. **Create evidence of impartiality**: Get a signed, scope-specific independence and conflict-of-interest declaration from the provider (and any subcontractors) during onboarding, and again immediately before each engagement. This must confirm there are no actual or perceived conflicts for the named system/components.
3. **Produce a written penetration testing plan for approval**: For each engagement, create an assessment plan that sets out the scope (system/components and relevant environments such as production or test), the approach (for example, authenticated or unauthenticated testing), the timing and testing windows, the rules of engagement, who can escalate or stop testing, and what will be delivered (a penetration test report and an assessment summary).
4. **Get formal approval before testing starts**: Send the plan for approval to the system owner and the information security lead (and the privacy lead where applicable). Record the approval details (date and time, scope identifiers, named authorised contacts, and the test window).
5. **Run the work with controlled oversight**: Provide a named organisational point of contact, define how incidents are escalated and who has authority to “stop testing”, and record key engagement communications and any approvals needed for disruption or downtime.
6. **Review results and decide on remediation**: Hold a structured review of findings (severity, how easily issues can be exploited, which assets are affected, business impact, and who will fix the issues and by when). If risk acceptance is proposed, ensure it is documented and approved through the organisation’s risk processes.
7. **Keep audit-ready evidence**: Store the approved plan, approval records, independence declarations, penetration test report(s), assessment summary, and evidence supporting the findings in a controlled repository. Link this to the system security planning records (and privacy planning records where applicable).

###### WHO
- **Information Security Lead / Security Governance**: owns the impartiality governance, approval process, and evidence retention requirements.
- **System Owner**: approves the assessment plan and authorises testing for the in-scope system/components.
- **Privacy Lead (if applicable)**: reviews plan elements that may affect personal data.
- **Independent Penetration Testing Provider/Team**: performs the testing and provides reports and supporting evidence.
- **Security Operations / Incident Response Contact**: provides escalation and “stop testing” coordination during execution.

###### Acceptance criteria
- A scope-specific independence and conflict-of-interest declaration exists for the engagement and is kept with the engagement record.
- A written penetration testing plan exists and is formally approved before any testing starts, including scope, approach, timing, rules of engagement, and escalation/“stop testing” authority.
- Penetration test reports and an assessment summary are produced and kept in an auditable repository linked to the system.
- Evidence of controlled oversight exists (named authorised contacts, escalation/“stop testing” process, and logged engagement communications).
- Findings are reviewed with documented remediation owners and timelines (and documented risk acceptance where used).

###### Actions Required for Compliance

- [ ] Choose an independent penetration testing provider or team, and document the contractual independence requirements—meaning they must not be involved in development, operations, or management for the target.
- [ ] Before each engagement, obtain and keep a written declaration from the provider (and any subcontractors) confirming they are independent and have no conflicts of interest, specific to that scope.
- [ ] Create a written penetration testing plan that sets out the testing scope, method, schedule and time windows, rules for how testing will be carried out, and who has authority to escalate issues or stop the testing.
- [ ] Before any testing begins, get formal approval for the plan from the system owner and the information security lead (and the privacy lead, where applicable).
- [ ] Appoint named organisational contacts to oversee activities and to escalate incidents, and record all engagement communications and any approvals for disruptions or downtime.
- [ ] Review the results of the penetration test in a structured meeting, assign who will fix each issue and by when, and record any decisions to accept risks.
- [ ] Keep the approved plan, authorisation records, proof of independence, penetration test reports, and assessment summaries in an auditable storage location linked to the system.

#### CA-8.2 — Red Team Exercises (Enhancement)

This requirement is about running a yearly, realistic “stress test” where trained challengers try to break into the organisation’s systems and trick people, in the same way a real attacker would. Without this, weaknesses may stay hidden until it is too late, leaving the organisation exposed to data loss, service disruption, financial harm, and damage to trust.

To meet this expectation, the organisation must plan and carry out at least one red-team exercise each year. The exercise must include both:
- technology-based attempts (targeting the systems and processes themselves), and
- social engineering attempts (such as phishing-style messages, phone calls, in-person conversations, or other ways to influence people).

All activity must be carried out only under pre-approved rules of engagement. These rules must be clearly documented and followed.

The organisation must also:
- create an assessment plan setting out what will be tested and how,
- involve the right system and network administrators where relevant,
- produce a report of findings, and
- keep evidence and supporting records showing the exercise was properly planned, authorised, carried out, and reported in line with its security and privacy plans.

##### Annual red-team exercise with RoE-approved technical and social attacks

- Category: Manual
- Priority: Critical

###### WHAT
Run at least one red-team exercise every year that simulates attempts to compromise an organisation, using **both**:
- **technology-based attacks** (against hardware, software, firmware, and mission or business processes), and
- **social engineering-based attacks** (for example: email, telephone, shoulder surfing, and personal conversations),

strictly following **pre-approved rules of engagement (RoE)**.

###### WHY (control requirement)
CA-8(2) enhancement requires the organisation to use red-team exercises to simulate compromise attempts **in line with the applicable rules of engagement** (parameter: **ca-08.02_odp**). The guidance also requires realistic simulation of both **technical** and **social engineering** attack attempts, and that the results are used to improve the organisation’s security and privacy posture.

###### HOW (specific approach)
1. **Define and approve RoE (pre-approval required):** Record what is allowed and what is not allowed, which communication channels can be used, the time windows, what areas are in scope, any data-handling and privacy limits, and clear **stop conditions** (for example: no destructive actions; no disruption to live services without emergency approval). Get formal approval before starting.
2. **Create an assessment plan for each annual exercise:** Set out the scope (systems, business processes, and user groups), the attack goals and storylines (initial access → gaining higher privileges → moving to other systems/remaining present → attempts to cause impact), the success measures, and what evidence must be captured and kept (for example, which logs or monitoring data).
3. **Run a combined technical + social engineering exercise:**
   - **Technical:** Carry out controlled intrusion attempts against the in-scope application and infrastructure components and business processes, with safety limits and staged approvals.
   - **Social engineering:** Run consented, RoE-bounded simulations of realistic work activities (for example: helpdesk, finance, and vendor onboarding) using approved channels (email/phone/in person) and collecting only the interaction information needed for evidence.
4. **Assign named roles and involve system/network administrators:** Make sure system and network administrators provide technical approval for the in-scope components, and that security and privacy oversight is in place to confirm compliance with the RoE and privacy limits.
5. **Produce an audit-ready report and keep evidence:** Provide a red-team assessment report summarising what was done, weaknesses found, likely impact, gaps in defensive controls, and remediation recommendations. Keep the RoE, assessment plan, approvals, execution evidence, and final findings.

###### WHO
- **Red team lead / engagement manager:** Owns the RoE, assessment plan, coordination of the exercise, and reporting.
- **Information Security (and Privacy) governance owner:** Approves the RoE and confirms privacy and data-handling constraints.
- **System/Network administrators (for in-scope systems):** Provide technical approval and operational safety constraints.
- **Security Operations Centre (SOC) / security information and event management (SIEM) monitoring owner:** Ensures the right logging and monitoring data is captured to meet evidence needs.

###### Acceptance criteria
- Evidence shows **at least one** red-team exercise was completed within the last 12 months.
- The exercise includes **both** technology-based attack simulations and social engineering attack simulations.
- A documented **RoE** exists, with **pre-approval** before any activity, and the exercise followed the stop conditions and prohibited actions.
- A documented **assessment plan** exists for the exercise (scope, approach, success criteria, and evidence requirements).
- A **red-team assessment report** exists, and supporting evidence (approvals, RoE, plan, execution records, and findings) is kept for audit.

###### Actions Required for Compliance

- [ ] Document and obtain formal pre-approval for the annual rules of engagement (RoE), covering which actions are allowed or not allowed, when operations must stop, and the privacy and data-handling requirements.
- [ ] Create an annual red-team assessment plan that sets out the scope, the planned attack goals and scenarios, what counts as success, and the evidence and system records (telemetry) needed to prove results.
- [ ] Plan and coordinate the in-scope technical testing with the system and network administrators to support step-by-step authorisation and protect day-to-day operations.
- [ ] Run rules-of-engagement (RoE)-limited social engineering simulations using approved communication channels and realistic business processes, and record only the interaction information that is required.
- [ ] Collect and keep proof that the work was carried out as approved (including approval records, records showing compliance with rules of engagement, system logs and performance data, and the results of participant interactions) so it can be checked during an audit.
- [ ] Produce and distribute a red-team assessment report that includes: the findings, likely effects (impact hypotheses), and remediation recommendations, each linked to the specific weaknesses in defensive controls.

#### CA-8.3 — Facility Penetration Testing (Enhancement)

This requirement is about checking, in a realistic way, whether someone could enter your premises by getting past or defeating the physical entry protections. Without this kind of check, weaknesses at doors, gates, visitor entry points, or access barriers might not be noticed until they are used, putting people, assets, and sensitive work at risk.

The organisation is expected to carry out authorised, unannounced attempts to bypass or get around the facility’s physical access protections at least once every year, and again after any major change to those physical access arrangements. Each test must be planned in advance, with a written plan and clear, approved rules for how the testers will operate—setting out what they may and may not do—and it must be carried out under those approved arrangements.

The organisation must produce and keep the test reports and supporting evidence, so the results can be reviewed and any needed improvements can be identified.

##### Annual unannounced facility access red-team with RoE and evidence

- Category: Manual
- Priority: Critical

###### WHAT
Carry out an authorised penetration testing process (often called a “red team” exercise) that includes **unannounced attempts** to **bypass or get around physical security controls** at **facility entry points** at least **every year**, and **again after any major change** to physical access controls. Keep complete, auditable records, including an assessment plan, **rules of engagement (RoE)**, required approvals, and a retained set of supporting evidence and a final report.

###### WHY (control requirement)
NIST SP 800-53 **CA-8(3)** requires a penetration testing approach that includes **(1) the defined testing frequency** and **(2) unannounced attempts** to bypass or get around controls linked to **facility entry points**. The guidance notes that testing entry points can uncover serious weaknesses in real working environments and helps improve physical security.

###### HOW (specific steps/approach)
1. **Define scope and triggers**: Write down what counts as “facility entry points” (for example: main entrances, gates, loading bays, visitor entry points/turnstiles, mantraps, badge readers, secure doors). Also define “major physical access control change” (for example: replacing access control equipment, changing access procedures, adding new perimeter barriers, introducing a new visitor management process).
2. **Set governance and approvals**: Use a repeatable approval process that requires named authorisation for each exercise (for example: Physical Security Lead plus Information Security/Compliance plus Legal/Health and Safety where applicable). Make sure the exercise is formally authorised before it starts.
3. **Create an assessment plan and RoE for each test**: For every exercise, produce an assessment plan and **rules of engagement (RoE)** that clearly state:
   - which entry points are in scope,
   - the goal is to **bypass or get around physical security controls**,
   - what “success” means (for example: getting in without valid authorisation/escort where required),
   - safety limits, when to stop, and who to contact if issues arise,
   - what evidence must be collected.
4. **Run unannounced attempts**: Conduct the exercise **without advance notice** to site staff (only share details on a need-to-know basis for safety and oversight). Make attempts that realistically test how well the site resists bypassing or getting around controls (for example: resisting tailgating, testing weaknesses in badge validation, checking whether visitor escort rules are enforced, testing how gate interlocks behave).
5. **Collect evidence and produce reports**: Gather an evidence pack (time-stamped observations, photos/video where allowed, observer notes, notes linking alarms/incidents to events, and a “path-to-entry” narrative). Produce a penetration test report with findings, severity, and recommendations for fixes, and keep the supporting evidence for audit.
6. **Fix issues and confirm improvements**: Monitor remediation actions until completion, with responsible owners and deadlines. Arrange follow-up checks where high-risk bypass routes are found.
7. **Test after major changes**: Link this to the organisation’s physical security change management process so that when a “major physical access control change” happens, an unannounced exercise is started at the next practical opportunity.

###### WHO (roles responsible)
- **Physical Security Lead / Facilities Security Manager**: owns the scope definitions, safety limits, and mapping of entry points.
- **Information Security / Security Assurance**: owns compliance with CA-8(3), **rules of engagement (RoE)** governance, and report quality.
- **Authorised Red Team / Penetration Test Team** (internal or a controlled contractor): carries out the unannounced bypass/get-around attempts.
- **Health and Safety / Site Operations** (where required): confirms safety and stop conditions are appropriate.
- **Change Management Owner**: triggers additional testing after major physical access control changes.

###### Acceptance criteria
- At least **one unannounced** facility access penetration test is carried out **every year**.
- An additional unannounced test is carried out **after each major physical access control change** (as defined in the organisation’s scope/triggers document).
- Each test has an approved **assessment plan** and **rules of engagement (RoE)** that explicitly target **bypassing or getting around physical security controls**.
- The evidence pack and penetration test report are retained and show that the test was unannounced, what was tested, the results, and the remediation recommendations.
- Remediation actions are tracked to completion for any identified high/critical bypass routes.

###### Actions Required for Compliance

- [ ] Define the facility’s physical entry points and explain what counts as a major change to physical access controls.
- [ ] Set up a CA-8(3) governance workflow that requires authorisation and approval of the per-test assessment plan.
- [ ] Create clear, per-exercise rules of engagement (RoE) that include any allowed bypass or circumvention aims, when the exercise must stop, and the safety limits that must not be exceeded.
- [ ] Schedule and carry out an unannounced facility access “red team” exercise at least once every year.
- [ ] After each major change to physical access controls, carry out an additional unannounced exercise immediately.
- [ ] Collect an evidence pack that can be audited and produce a penetration test report, including prioritised remediation actions.

### CA-9 — Internal System Connections (Control)

This expectation is about making sure only approved devices are allowed to connect to your internal systems, and that each connection is clearly understood, properly protected, and kept only for as long as it is genuinely needed. Without this, unauthorised devices or outdated settings could be used, which could lead to sensitive information being leaked, accessed by the wrong people, or shared in ways that breach privacy rules.

To meet this expectation, the organisation must allow internal connections only from approved servers, workstations, printers, scanners, copiers, and mobile phones or tablets that use approved standard settings. It must also keep an up-to-date record of what is authorised.

For every internal connection, the organisation must document:
- how the connection works,
- what protections are required,
- which privacy rules apply, and
- what type of information will be sent.

When permission is withdrawn, the business need ends, or the system owner no longer requires it, the organisation must disable and remove the connection immediately. It must also check at least every 90 days whether each connection is still needed.

##### Internal connection register with automated allow-list enforcement

- Category: Software
- Priority: Critical

###### WHAT
Set up an authoritative **Internal System Connections Register** for the application. This register must record and control **approved internal connections** (including groups of similar components) and the documentation needed for each one. It must then enforce these permissions automatically using **approved connection allow-lists**.

###### WHY (control requirement)
CA-9 requires that internal system connections are **authorised** (including by **class**, where connections share the same key characteristics/configurations). The organisation must keep an **authorisation list/register**. Each connection must be **documented**, including interface details, security and privacy requirements, and what types of information are involved. Connections must be **ended immediately** when authorisation or the mission need is no longer valid. The ongoing need must be **reviewed at least every 90 days**.

###### HOW (specific steps/approach)
1. **Define approved component classes and baseline IDs** for the application’s internal connections (for example: servers, workstations, printers, scanners, copiers, mobile phones/tablets). Link each class to an approved **baseline configuration**.
2. Create and maintain an **Internal System Connections Register** (either as an extension to a configuration management database/asset system, or as a dedicated register). For each connection (or for class-based authorisation), include at least:
   - **System component/class** and **baseline configuration ID**
   - **Interface characteristics** (for example: network segment/VLAN/SSID, ports/protocols, API endpoints, service names, routing path, and any relevant physical/operational technology interface identifiers)
   - **Security requirements** (for example: required authentication method, encryption requirements, allowed user/service accounts/groups, required device compliance posture)
   - **Privacy requirements** (for example: data categories involved and any applicable privacy handling, retention, or sharing constraints)
   - **Type/nature of information communicated** (for example: print jobs, scanned documents, telemetry, customer/employee data categories)
   - **Authorisation status** and **system owner**
3. Apply **policy-as-code enforcement** so that only register-approved connections are allowed:
   - Enforce **network/service allow-lists** (for example: micro-segmentation, zero trust network access, and firewall rules) based directly on register entries.
   - Enforce **identity and device compliance** for mobile and endpoint-originated connections using conditional access and device posture (for example: only devices matching the baseline/compliance profile can access the documented services).
   - Make enforcement aware of the environment (for example: production, test, and disaster recovery separation) so permissions do not carry over between environments.
4. Implement **automated termination workflows**:
   - When a register entry is revoked (authorisation is removed, mission need ends, or the system owner no longer requires it), automatically remove the related allow-list rules and revoke the associated identity/device permissions.
   - Quarantine or block the endpoint/device where applicable (for example: mobile device management, endpoint detection and response, and conditional access). Record the termination time to demonstrate it happened **immediately**.
5. Implement a **90-day review workflow**:
   - Schedule reviews for each connection entry at least every **90 days**.
   - Require confirmation from the system owner and a security/privacy review when data categories or security requirements are affected.
   - Record review outcomes as evidence: **continued need**, **changed requirements** (with updated enforcement), or **remove** (which triggers termination automation).

###### WHO
- **System Owner**: responsible for authorising connections, deciding whether there is continued need, and keeping the register accurate.
- **Security Engineering / Identity and Access Management (IAM) and Network Security**: implements policy-as-code enforcement and automated termination.
- **Privacy Officer / Data Protection Lead (as applicable)**: checks that privacy requirements and data-category handling are correct.
- **IT/Operational Technology (OT) Operations (or Platform Team)**: ensures baseline configurations and enforcement pipelines are working.

###### Acceptance criteria
- A complete **Internal System Connections Register** exists for the application, with entries (or class-based entries) for all internal connections, including documented interface characteristics, security requirements, privacy requirements, and information types.
- Enforcement is clearly based on the register (for example: changes in register entries update network/service allow-lists within an agreed operational timeframe).
- When an entry is revoked, the corresponding connection is blocked/removed, and evidence shows termination happens **immediately** (measured and logged).
- A scheduled review process runs and provides auditable evidence that each connection entry is reviewed **at least every 90 days**, with recorded outcomes and any resulting enforcement updates/removals.

###### Actions Required for Compliance

- [ ] Define the approved internal connection component types and link each type to an approved baseline configuration ID.
- [ ] Create the **Internal System Connections Register**. Include fields for **interface characteristics**, **security requirements**, **privacy requirements**, and the **type of information**.
- [ ] Use policy-as-code to automatically create network and service allow-lists, and to run identity and device compliance checks based on information in the register entries.
- [ ] Set up automated offboarding workflows to remove any approved allow-lists and immediately cancel the related permissions as soon as the registration is revoked.
- [ ] Set up a 90-day scheduled review process that records the results and updates or removes enforcement actions based on those outcomes.

#### CA-9.1 — Compliance Checks (Enhancement)

This requirement is about making sure that any two parts of your systems are only connected after you have checked that they are safe and that they handle personal information properly. Without these checks, you could accidentally connect components that are not approved to be linked, that are set up incorrectly, or that do not meet your organisation’s required settings. This could result in data leaks, unauthorised access, or privacy breaches.

To meet this expectation, before any internal connection is put in place, the organisation must:
- identify the exact components being connected
- confirm they are on the approved list for internal connections
- carry out both security and privacy compliance checks against the organisation’s required baseline settings

The results must be recorded in an assessment report, supported by relevant evidence such as audit records and the organisation’s security and privacy plans. The checks must be completed and documented before the connection is established.

##### Gate internal connections with security & privacy baseline checks

- Category: Software
- Priority: Critical

###### WHAT
Set up a mandatory “connection request” process that checks **security and privacy compliance** for the **specific building blocks** proposed for an internal connection **before** the connection is switched on. The checks must confirm the proposal matches the **relevant approved baseline configuration**.

###### WHY (control requirement)
National Institute of Standards and Technology (NIST) Special Publication (SP) 800-53 **CA-9(1)** requires the organisation to **carry out security and privacy compliance checks on the component building blocks before establishing the internal connection**. These checks must include **verifying the relevant baseline configuration**. This helps prevent connecting components that are incorrectly set up, not authorised, or do not meet security and privacy requirements.

###### HOW (specific steps/approach)
1. **Create a connection request record** in the organisation’s change management / information technology service management (ITSM) workflow for every proposed internal connection (for example: network route, firewall rule, application programming interface (API) integration, message bus subscription, identity and access management (IAM) trust, data pipeline).
2. **Identify and record the constituent components** (component identifiers/classes, source and target, environment, and the intended connection type). Require the request to reference the **authorised internal connections register** (allowed component types and connection patterns, including any exceptions and expiry dates).
3. **Run security baseline compliance checks** automatically (or through controlled automation) against the relevant baseline configuration for each component/class (for example: approved hardening settings, required logging and monitoring settings, encryption requirements, identity and authorisation settings, and vulnerability/patch status where the baseline drives the checks).
4. **Run privacy baseline compliance checks** automatically (or through controlled automation) against the relevant privacy baseline for each component/class (for example: alignment of data classification and purpose tagging, encryption in transit and at rest for personal data, access control and logging for personal data, and retention/handling settings consistent with the privacy baseline).
5. **Produce an assessment report** for each request that records the results against each baseline, any exceptions, and the supporting evidence (for example: configuration snapshots, scan results, extracts from audit logs, and outputs from policy checks). Link the report to the change record.
6. **Apply a process gate**: the workflow must **stop the connection from being activated** until both the security and privacy checks finish successfully (or are explicitly approved with documented exceptions under the organisation’s governance process).

###### WHO (role responsible)
- **Change Manager / ITSM Owner**: ensures the connection request workflow and the activation gate are enforced.
- **Security Engineering / Governance, Risk and Compliance (GRC) Analyst**: maintains the baselines and the authorisation register; validates exceptions.
- **Privacy Officer / Data Protection Lead**: maintains the privacy baselines and validates the privacy check results.
- **Platform/Cloud/Operational Technology (OT) Integration Engineers**: provide component identifiers/classes and fix any findings.

###### Acceptance criteria
- For every internal connection request, the system produces an **assessment report** containing **security and privacy baseline verification results** before activation.
- Connection activation is technically and/or process-wise **blocked** unless the security and privacy checks pass (or approved exceptions are recorded).
- Each check result includes **evidence references** sufficient for audit (for example: scan/configuration snapshot outputs and policy evaluation logs).
- The request can be traced to an entry in the **authorised internal connections register** for the relevant component classes/patterns.

###### Actions Required for Compliance

- [ ] Define the authorised internal connections register mapping that links each component class to the allowed connection patterns and any exceptions.
- [ ] Set up an information technology service management (ITSM) change-management process for requesting connections, with required fields to identify the constituent components and the environment.
- [ ] Turn the security baseline configuration requirements into automated, computer-checkable rules (policy-as-code) and link them to the existing configuration and vulnerability scanning sources.
- [ ] Turn the privacy baseline requirements into computer-checkable “policy as code” rules that match expectations for how personal data is handled, encrypted, accessed, and kept.
- [ ] Set up automated production of an assessment report that includes results for each baseline, the supporting evidence references, and links to the change record.
- [ ] Apply a workflow checkpoint that prevents activating a connection until the required security and privacy checks have passed, or until an approved exception has been recorded.
