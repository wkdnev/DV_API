### AT-1 — Policy and Procedures (Control)

This requirement is about making sure everyone in the organisation understands what is expected of them to protect the organisation’s information and personal data, through clear written guidance and regular updates. Without an agreed policy and practical procedures, people may not know their responsibilities, training may vary between teams and suppliers, and important changes in law or lessons learned from incidents may be missed—raising the risk of data loss, privacy breaches, and failure to comply with requirements.

The organisation must create and document an organisation-wide awareness and training policy. This policy must explain its purpose, what it applies to, who is responsible for what, the commitment from senior leadership, how different parts of the organisation work together, and how compliance will be achieved. It must also be consistent with relevant laws and the organisation’s own rules.

The Chief Information Security Officer, together with the Security Awareness Programme Manager and the Privacy Officer, must manage producing, documenting, and distributing the policy and the supporting procedures.

The policy must be reviewed at least every year, and also after security incidents, audit findings, or relevant legal or policy changes. The procedures must be reviewed at least every year, and also after major organisational or system changes, and after incidents or audit findings.

The policy must be shared with all employees, contractors, and third parties who have access to the organisation’s systems and data. The procedures must be shared with all personnel with security or privacy responsibilities, including system owners, operational technology (OT) operators, and data stewards.

### AT-2 — Literacy Training and Awareness (Control)

This requirement is about making sure everyone who uses the organisation’s systems understands, in plain terms, how to protect information and people’s privacy, and what to do if something looks wrong. Without regular, practical reminders and training, staff may fall for common scams, share access by mistake, mishandle sensitive information, or miss early warning signs of a security incident—leading to data breaches, legal issues, financial loss, and harm to individuals.

The organisation must provide security and privacy training when people first start using the systems. It must then repeat security training every 180 days and privacy training every 180 days. It must also provide extra training after major system changes, new threats, audit findings, security incidents or breaches, or changes to relevant laws and policies. This includes specific follow-up training after events such as phishing, malicious software (malware), sharing login details, repeated failed login attempts, suspected data leaving the organisation, privacy handling mistakes, signs of health or data-protection breaches, or access to records that they are not authorised to view.

The organisation should reinforce learning using posters, messages when people log in, emails, awareness events, and reminder materials. It must update the training content every quarter and after major triggers, and include lessons learned from real incidents.

#### AT-2.1 — Practical Exercises (Enhancement)

This expectation is about making staff training feel real by running practical literacy and security-awareness exercises that copy what attackers try to do. Without realistic, hands-on practice, people may only remember advice in theory and then fail when under pressure—for example, by sharing sensitive information, trying to access something they should not, or being tricked by messages that lead them to open harmful email attachments or click harmful links.

To meet this expectation, the organisation must provide practical exercises for the relevant people and make sure each exercise is based on a realistic situation that looks like a real incident. At least one exercise must be run without warning and include a safe, training-only attempt to influence participants to collect information and to try to gain unauthorised access, without affecting real systems.

The organisation must also include separate scenarios that simulate the harmful impact of opening malicious attachments and clicking malicious links, with at least one delivered in a targeted way. The organisation must document how it will do this, keep training materials and records, and name specific people responsible for delivering and running the exercises.

#### AT-2.2 — Insider Threat (Enhancement)

This requirement is about making sure everyone in the organisation understands what warning signs of harmful insider behaviour can look like and how to report a concern early. Without this, a trusted employee or contractor could misuse their access or cause harm—for example, by showing long-term dissatisfaction, trying to access information they do not need for their role, having unexplained financial problems, bullying or harassing colleagues, threatening or carrying out violence in the workplace, or breaking serious workplace rules and policies.

To meet this expectation, the organisation must provide training that is appropriate to each role for all relevant staff. This should help them recognise these warning signs and understand exactly how and where to report them using the organisation’s established reporting routes. Staff must follow the organisation’s internal procedures and should not try to investigate concerns themselves.

The organisation should clearly assign responsibility for keeping the training up to date, maintaining the training materials and course content, and keeping records that show the required people completed the training.

#### AT-2.3 — Social Engineering and Mining (Enhancement)

Without regular, practical training, staff may be persuaded to share confidential information, click on harmful messages, or pass on internal information to someone who is pretending to be legitimate. This can lead to fraud, loss of data, or disruption.

To meet this expectation, the organisation must set out a clear training approach and a documented way to deliver it. This should include who must attend, who is responsible for running the training, and how it will be updated and tracked over time. The training must cover both recognising and reporting social tricks (attempts to manipulate people) and social information-gathering (attempts to collect information through people), using plain-language examples. It must also explain clearly how and where staff should report concerns, using the organisation’s established reporting channels and procedures.

The organisation should keep evidence that the required people completed the training and that it included both recognising issues and reporting them.

The organisation must assign responsibility for delivering the basic training and for reviewing or approving the training content, include the training in relevant planning documents, keep evidence that staff received the training, and ensure trained staff can correctly recognise the warning signs and carry out the correct reporting actions.

### AT-3 — Role-based Training (Control)

This requirement ensures that the right people receive the right security and privacy training for their specific responsibilities before they are allowed to access systems or handle information, and that the training is kept up to date. Without this, staff and contractors may misunderstand how to protect data and privacy, follow the wrong procedures, or miss important updates—raising the risk of data leaks, privacy breaches, and costly incidents.

The organisation must:
- identify which roles need security and privacy training;
- provide the correct security and privacy training before anyone in those roles is authorised to access systems or carry out their duties; and
- repeat the training at least every 12 months.

It must also update the training content at least every quarter, and whenever any of the following occur:
- changes to systems;
- assessment or audit findings;
- security incidents or breaches;
- major changes in laws or policies; or
- major changes to systems or architecture.

Finally, it must include lessons learned from past incidents in the training, and keep records showing who completed the required training and when, including contractors with relevant duties.
