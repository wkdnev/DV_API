### PM-1 — Information Security Program Plan (Control)

This requirement is about having a clear, organisation-wide security plan that everyone can follow. Without it, different parts of the organisation may protect information in different ways, creating gaps that could lead to data loss, fraud, disruption to services, reputational damage, or harm to people and partners.

The organisation must create and share a formal programme plan that explains:
- what security outcomes must be achieved
- what management arrangements and shared safeguards are already in place (or are planned)
- who is responsible for making the plan work, including a clear commitment from senior leadership

The plan must be coordinated across all relevant parts of the organisation, set out how compliance will be achieved, and be approved by a senior official who is accountable for the risks to the organisation’s mission, assets, people, other organisations, and the Nation.

The plan must be reviewed and updated at least every 12 months, and also after any organisation-wide audit or assessment findings, security incidents or breaches, changes in laws or government directions, major changes to the organisation or its mission, or material changes in risk for confidential or classified information.

Finally, the plan must be protected so that only authorised people can view it or change it.

### PM-2 — Information Security Program Leadership Role (Control)

This requirement is about making sure your organisation has a clearly named senior leader who is responsible for running the organisation-wide information security work. Without this, security duties can become split across different teams, decisions may take longer, and key protections may not be planned, put in place, or kept up to date—leaving the organisation more exposed to data loss, fraud, disruption, and regulatory or reputational harm.

To meet this expectation, the organisation must formally appoint a senior information security officer and give them a clear mandate, along with enough authority and resources to coordinate, develop, implement, and maintain the programme across the whole organisation. The organisation should also produce a programme plan explaining how it will be developed, rolled out, reviewed, and updated over time, and how the plan will be coordinated with relevant internal and external parties. Finally, responsibility for planning and delivering the programme must be assigned to named individuals, including the senior information security officer.

### PM-3 — Information Security and Privacy Resources (Control)

This requirement ensures that when the organisation plans and approves major spending, it also plans and documents the resources needed to protect both information and people’s privacy. Without this, important security and privacy work may be left out of budgets, which can lead to avoidable incidents such as data being exposed, misused, or handled in ways that breach legal and regulatory duties.

To meet this expectation, the organisation must include, in every capital planning and investment request, the resources needed to run both its information security programme and its privacy programme. It must also clearly document any exceptions, including what will be done instead.

The organisation must prepare and keep the required documentation showing how information security and privacy will be addressed in those requests, in line with all applicable laws, government directions, internal policies, regulations, and standards. It must also make sure the planned resources are available for spending as scheduled.

### PM-4 — Plan of Action and Milestones Process (Control)

This requirement is about keeping a clear, up-to-date action plan to fix known problems in how the organisation protects information, handles personal data, and manages risks from suppliers. Without these plans, issues may continue for longer than necessary, be dealt with in the wrong order, or be missed altogether—causing harm to the organisation, its assets, the people affected, other organisations, and potentially wider national interests.

The organisation must create and keep documented action plans and milestones for each of these areas (and any relevant systems). Each plan must set out the specific fixes needed, who is responsible, and when progress will be made. The organisation must update the plans based on findings from reviews and ongoing checks, report progress in line with its required reporting rules (including Office of Management and Budget (OMB) / Federal Information Security Management Act (FISMA) expectations where applicable), and regularly review the plans to ensure they still align with the organisation’s overall approach to risk and its organisation-wide priorities for responding to risk.

### PM-5 — System Inventory (Control)

This requirement is about keeping a clear, up-to-date list of the organisation’s main systems, so you always know what you run and who is responsible for it. Without this, important systems can be missed when changes are made, when problems are investigated, or when reporting is needed. This increases the risk of outages, incorrect decisions, and delays in responding to issues.

The organisation must define, in writing, what counts as a “system”, and must record each system in a consistent way in an organisation-wide inventory (not just individual parts). Named people or teams must be responsible for creating and maintaining the inventory. The inventory must be updated every quarter, and the organisation must keep evidence showing the updates were completed.

The organisation must also have written procedures explaining how systems are added, changed, and retired; how quarterly updates are reviewed and approved; and what records are kept. This is to allow the organisation to demonstrate the inventory and its quarterly updates for reporting purposes.

#### PM-5.1 — Inventory of Personally Identifiable Information (Enhancement)

This requirement is about keeping a clear, up-to-date record of every place in the organisation where information that can identify a person is collected, used, stored, shared, or deleted. Without this, the organisation can easily lose track of where personal details are held or handled. That increases the risk of using personal information for the wrong purpose, not giving people the correct privacy information, or missing when a system should be changed or stopped.

To meet this expectation, the organisation must create an inventory that covers all systems, applications, and projects that handle personal data. It must clearly define what counts as handling personal data, and assign named people to be responsible for owning and maintaining the inventory.

The inventory must be kept current through an ongoing update process. It must be reviewed and updated at least once every quarter, with new or changed items added and items that are no longer relevant removed. This must be supported by appropriate documentation showing how the inventory was created and how it stays accurate.

### PM-6 — Measures of Performance (Control)

This requirement is about showing, with clear results, whether your organisation’s privacy and information protection work is actually effective. Without regular tracking and reporting focused on outcomes, you may only find problems after harm has already happened—for example, personal data being handled incorrectly, preventable incidents occurring, or privacy duties being missed—because you never checked whether the approach worked in the first place.

To meet this expectation, the organisation must put in place practical measures for both privacy and information protection that demonstrate how effective and efficient the programme and its safeguards are. It must also document how these measures are set up, how the organisation collects the information, how it monitors it, and how it reports it. Named individuals must be assigned responsibility for carrying out this work.

The organisation must then regularly review the results and report them to the appropriate audiences, making sure the measures remain aligned with the level of risk the organisation has said it considers acceptable in its risk planning. Finally, it must keep evidence of the work, such as reports and supporting data.

The plan must also ensure that information protection and privacy are built in from the start, and that it explicitly considers the knock-on effects and risks to day-to-day operations, important assets, real people, other organisations, and the wider public interest.  

Without this, security and privacy needs can be overlooked until something goes wrong. That can lead to misuse of data, disruption to services, expensive rework, loss of trust, and harm that goes beyond the organisation itself.  

To meet this expectation, the organisation must develop and keep current an enterprise architecture that explicitly includes both information security and privacy requirements, and explains how they connect to business activities. It must document how the architecture is created and maintained, name specific people responsible for producing it and for carrying out related risk checks, perform and record risk assessments linked to the architecture, and use the recorded results to update the architecture over time.

#### PM-7.1 — Offloading (Enhancement)

This requirement is about keeping everyday “support” activities—such as printing, copying files, and distributing software updates—separate from the systems that run your most important services.

The risk it addresses is that if these support functions run on the same systems as critical business functions, then a mistake or misuse in the support area could give an attacker an easier way to reach the parts of the organisation that matter most.

To meet this expectation, the organisation must:
- identify which services are not essential, and
- decide where each one should run—either on separate, non-critical systems or components, or with a specialist external provider where this is feasible.

The organisation must record its decisions and the reasons for them, carry out and document risk checks for these design choices, and keep up-to-date records of the architecture showing that the separation is in place.

It must also have clear, repeatable procedures for identifying suitable services and moving them to the agreed locations. These procedures must include named responsibilities and involve information security and privacy planning staff. Finally, the organisation must be able to prove that the services are actually hosted where it agreed.

### PM-9 — Risk Management Strategy (Control)

#### PM-30.1 — Suppliers of Critical or Mission-essential Items (Enhancement)

This requirement is about knowing exactly which suppliers are most important to keeping your business running, and checking that they are safe and reliable. If you do not clearly identify and rank the companies behind your most critical technologies, products, or services, you may face unexpected delays, poor quality, failure to meet required rules, or sudden changes that disrupt operations or prevent you from delivering your mission.

To meet this expectation, the organisation must:
- write down what it considers critical or mission-essential,
- identify the suppliers that provide those items, and
- keep an up-to-date list of suppliers and the specific critical items each supplier provides.

It must link each supplier to the relevant critical items, prioritise suppliers based on how important they are, and record the reasons for those priorities. It then needs to review and assess the risk posed by the higher-priority suppliers using its established supplier review and supply-chain risk approach, and keep dated records of the assessment results and any required follow-up actions. The organisation must also name the people responsible for maintaining the supplier list, carrying out the assessments, and ensuring everything stays aligned with the organisation’s wider risk plans.

### PM-31 — Continuous Monitoring Strategy (Control)

This requirement is about keeping a steady, organisation-wide watch on how well your systems are protected and how well privacy is being handled, so problems are spotted early and fixed quickly. Without this ongoing oversight, weaknesses such as out-of-date software, missing extra login checks, slow fixing of known security issues, poor visibility of suspicious activity, incomplete reviews of who has powerful access, or missing records of system events could go unnoticed until they are exploited, harming customers, staff, and the business.

The organisation must set up and run a continuous monitoring programme overseen by the Chief Information Security Officer. It must define and regularly track key measures, including: software patch compliance, extra login check coverage, fixing vulnerabilities within agreed timeframes, endpoint protection coverage, completion of privileged access reviews, success in collecting system event records, and the rate of critical alerts. It must set targets for how effective patching, extra login checks, and access reviews are, and separately assess how effective it is at fixing issues within time and at collecting records. It must then compare and analyse the results, take timely action to address findings, and report security status weekly to the Security Operations Centre manager and system security officers, and privacy status monthly to the Privacy Office lead and system privacy officers.

### PM-32 — Purposing (Control)

This requirement is about making sure the organisation’s most important computer systems are used only for the specific jobs they were designed to do. Over time, systems can gradually start supporting other activities. This can put sensitive information and critical services into the wrong environment, increasing the risk of misuse, disruption, or compromise, and ultimately harming the mission or business function that depends on them.

To meet this expectation, the organisation must keep:
- a documented list of the essential services and functions,
- a record of the mission-essential systems and the supporting components for each, and
- a clear statement of the intended purpose for each system or component.

The organisation must then regularly check how each system or component is actually being used, compare it with its intended purpose, identify any use outside that purpose, assess how this could increase risk, record what it found, and take corrective action to bring use back in line.

The organisation must update its records after any changes and ensure this work is reflected in its information security and privacy planning and its risk management approach.
