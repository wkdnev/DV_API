### PS-1 — Policy and Procedures (Control)

This requirement is about making sure your organisation has clear, up-to-date rules for protecting people who hold security clearance, and the practical steps to follow those rules. Without a written personnel security policy and supporting procedures, different teams could work in different ways, important legal and regulatory duties could be missed, and lessons from incidents, audits, or changes in the law might not be acted on quickly—leaving the organisation exposed to preventable security and privacy risks.

To meet this expectation, the organisation must develop, document, and share an organisation-level personnel security policy for roles within the Department of Defense (DoD) personnel security programme and for human resources (HR) and personnel security staff. The policy must set out its purpose, who is responsible for what, management commitment, how different parts of the organisation work together, and how compliance is checked. It must also align with all applicable laws, directives, regulations, and internal guidance.

The organisation must appoint a named Personnel Security Policy Owner within the Security Office. This person is responsible for creating, maintaining, and sharing the policy and procedures with all security-cleared employees, contractors, and relevant supervisors. They must also share the procedures with HR and personnel security staff, personnel security programme managers, and site security leads.

The policy must be reviewed and updated every year, and within 60 days of any event that triggers a change. The procedures must be reviewed and updated every year, and within 30 days of any triggering event. Updates must also be made after security incidents or breaches, audit findings, or changes to the requirements that apply to the organisation.

##### PS-1 policy/procedure lifecycle with PSPO ownership and timed updates

- Category: Manual
- Priority: Critical

###### WHAT
Set up and keep an **organisation-wide personnel security policy** and supporting **personnel security procedures** for the PS family controls used in the organisation.

###### WHY (control requirement)
PS-1 requires that personnel security policy and procedures:
- exist for the PS family controls used in systems and organisations
- are **owned**, **documented**, **shared with the right people**, and **kept up to date**

Updates must happen **every year** and within set time limits after certain events happen (for example: incidents or breaches, audit findings, or changes to relevant laws, directives, or regulations). Simply repeating the controls is not enough.

###### HOW (specific steps/approach)
1. **Create an organisation-level policy document** (or a set of coordinated policies) that includes: purpose, scope, roles and responsibilities, management commitment, coordination between security and privacy programmes, and how compliance is checked. Make sure the policy is **consistent with applicable laws, executive orders, directives, regulations, policies, standards, and guidance** (i.e., it is explicitly linked to requirements, not just listed).
2. **Create separate procedures** that explain **how** the policy is carried out for the relevant personnel security programme roles (for example: human resources (HR)/Personnel Security staff, security programme managers, and system/operational technology (OT) site security leads). Procedures must be practical and step-by-step, not just a repeat of the PS controls.
3. **Appoint a named Personnel Security Policy Owner (PSPO)** in the Security Office and give them end-to-end responsibility for: keeping the policy and procedures up to date, starting reviews, managing approvals, and ensuring they are shared with the right people.
4. **Use a controlled document process** in an enterprise repository (for example, a governance, risk, and compliance (GRC) or document management system): version control, approval workflow, an audit trail, and controlled publication.
5. **Set service-level agreements (SLAs) for reviews/updates and define what triggers them**:
   - Policy: review/update **annually** and within **60 days** of a triggering event.
   - Procedures: review/update **annually** and within **30 days** of a triggering event.
   - Triggering events include security incidents or breaches, audit findings, and changes to applicable laws, directives, or regulations.
6. **Share the documents**:
   - Policy to **all cleared employees, contractors, and supervisors** who have personnel security responsibilities.
   - Procedures to **HR/Personnel Security staff, security programme managers, and system/OT site security leads**.
   Use controlled sharing methods (for example, a single sign-on (SSO)-protected intranet page and/or tracked email) and keep proof that people acknowledged receipt or accessed the current documents.

###### WHO
- **Personnel Security Policy Owner (PSPO)**, Security Office: document ownership, starting reviews, coordinating approvals, sharing the documents.
- **HR/Personnel Security staff** and **security programme managers / system-OT site security leads**: provide input to the procedures and confirm they work in practice.
- **Security and Privacy programme representatives**: work together to ensure the policy and procedures support both security and privacy assurance.
- **Legal/Compliance** (as required by the organisation): confirm alignment with applicable laws, directives, and regulations.

###### Acceptance criteria (evidence)
- A published **organisation-wide personnel security policy** exists, with an identified PSPO and documented scope, roles, coordination approach, and compliance approach.
- A published **personnel security procedures** document set exists that explains the implementation steps for the defined personnel/roles.
- Evidence shows the documents were shared with the required audiences (policy: cleared employees/contractors/supervisors; procedures: HR/Personnel Security staff, security programme managers, system/OT site security leads) and that recipients can access the current versions.
- Document process evidence shows: the policy is reviewed/updated **annually** and within **60 days** of triggering events; the procedures are reviewed/updated **annually** and within **30 days** of triggering events.
- Triggering events (incidents/breaches, audit findings, legal or regulatory changes) are linked to documented review/update actions and version changes.
- The policy and procedures are clearly aligned to applicable laws, directives, and regulations through explicit mapping or justification (not just a reference list).

###### Actions Required for Compliance

- [ ] Draft and get approval for an organisation-wide personnel security policy. Ensure it has a named owner (personnel security policy owner), a clearly defined scope, assigned roles and responsibilities, coordination arrangements, and a documented mapping showing how it complies with all relevant laws, directives, and regulations.
- [ ] Develop personnel security procedures that set out the step-by-step actions for human resources (HR) and personnel security staff, security programme managers, and system/operational technology (OT) site security leads (do not repeat existing controls).
- [ ] Set up a controlled document repository for the policy and procedures, with version history, an approval process, and an audit trail.
- [ ] Appoint the Personnel Security Policy Owner (PSPO) in the Security Office and publish their responsibilities for maintaining and sharing the documents.
- [ ] Review and update service level agreements (SLAs): policy reviewed at least once a year and within 60 days of any event that triggers a review; procedures reviewed at least once a year and within 30 days of any event that triggers a review.
- [ ] Set up a process to capture “trigger events” (incidents or breaches, audit findings, and legal or regulatory changes) and link the evidence from each event to the specific policy or procedure updates that are required.
- [ ] Share the current policy with all security-cleared employees, contractors, and supervisors who have personnel security responsibilities. Also share the procedures with human resources (HR) and personnel security staff, security programme managers, and site security leads for information technology (IT) and operational technology (OT). Keep records showing who received the information and who acknowledged it.

### PS-2 — Position Risk Designation (Control)

This expectation is about making sure every job role in your organisation is clearly labelled with the level of risk it could create, and that the right checks are carried out for the people appointed to those roles. Without this, unsuitable people could be placed in roles where they could cause serious harm through misconduct, or where their access to sensitive information should have been handled more carefully.

To meet this expectation, the organisation must:
- assign a risk level to every job role and keep an up-to-date record of those risk levels;
- set clear, role-specific screening requirements for anyone filling each role, ensuring the requirements match that role’s responsibilities for handling information;
- review the risk level for each job role at least every 90 days, and update it when the review shows it is no longer accurate, keeping evidence of both the reviews and any changes.

##### Maintain a Position Risk Register with 90-day review and screening criteria

- Category: Manual
- Priority: Critical

###### WHAT
Set up and keep an organisation-wide **Position Risk Register** that assigns an OPM-aligned **position risk designation** to **every** organisational position. For each position, define **matching screening requirements** for people appointed to it (including **clear requirements for information security role appointments**). Carry out and record a **review and update at least every 90 days**.

###### WHY (control requirement)
PS-2 requires that position risk designations are assessed and kept up to date as the basis for a consistent suitability and personnel security programme. The assessment results must decide the **level of investigation (screening)** and what permissions people receive for access to organisational information and information systems. The organisation’s required review frequency is **every 90 days**.

###### HOW (specific steps/approach)
1. **Create the Position Risk Register** as the official source of record (for example, a governance, risk and compliance module or a secure HR system). It must include, at minimum: position identifier, job family/role, assigned risk designation, sensitivity/risk reason, linked screening requirements, and review details.
2. **Set a designation-to-screening rule** based on the OPM Position Designation System (PDS) so that each risk designation clearly determines the required screening/investigation level.
3. **Include explicit information security role appointment requirements** in the screening requirement templates (for example, requirements for roles that administer or operate security capabilities such as identity and access management (IAM) administration, security monitoring/security information and event management (SIEM) operations, vulnerability management, security engineering, and privileged access administration). Make sure these requirements are included in the criteria pack attached to appointments.
4. **Link to HR systems for onboarding** so that when someone is appointed to a position, the correct screening requirements pack is automatically selected. The appointment record must link to the position’s risk designation and screening requirements.
5. **Run an automated review process every 90 days** that creates review tasks for every position (or position category) at least every 90 days. Capture reviewer decisions, keep evidence (who/when/what changed), and update the register when designations are no longer accurate.
6. **Add change-trigger reviews** (in addition to the 90-day schedule) when duties or responsibilities change significantly (for example, system ownership changes, expansion of privileged access scope, or new information security responsibilities).
7. **Document the governance process** and reference it in the security planning documents for the application/system context. This should include roles and responsibilities for maintaining the register, carrying out reviews, and ensuring screening requirements are applied.

###### WHO (role responsible)
- **HR Security/Suitability Lead**: owns the Position Risk Register process and ensures every position is designated and reviewed.
- **Chief Information Security Officer (CISO)/Information Security Governance Lead**: confirms the mapping for information security role appointment requirements.
- **System/Application Owners (and operational technology (OT)/operations owners where applicable)**: provide input on duties and responsibilities for positions linked to the application’s information systems.
- **Compliance/Governance, Risk and Compliance (GRC) Analyst**: ensures evidence is suitable for audit and can be traced.

###### Acceptance criteria
- The organisation can produce a **complete list of all positions** with an assigned **position risk designation**.
- For each position, the organisation can show the **screening requirements** derived from the designation, including **explicit information security role appointment requirements**.
- Evidence shows that **every position has been reviewed at least every 90 days**, with recorded outcomes and updates where changes were needed.
- Appointment/onboarding records show that the **correct screening requirements pack** is applied based on the position’s designation.
- The process is **documented and referenced** in security planning documents for the relevant application/system context, with named responsibilities and an auditable workflow.

###### Actions Required for Compliance

- [ ] Create and keep up to date an official Position Risk Register that covers every role within the organisation.
- [ ] Define and get approval for a mapping from job designations to screening criteria, aligned with the PDS concepts
- [ ] Add clear requirements for appointing information security roles into the screening criteria templates.
- [ ] Integrate the human resources information system (HRIS) with identity onboarding so that job appointments are linked to the role’s risk rating and the required screening criteria pack.
- [ ] Set up a process (workflow) to start and record evidence for position risk designation reviews at least once every 90 days.
- [ ] Keep records of each review for audit purposes, including the reviewer’s name, the date, the result, and any updates to designations or screening.
- [ ] Update the system security planning documents to reference the PS-2 process, the assigned roles, and the evidence sources.

### PS-3 — Personnel Screening (Control)

This requirement ensures the organisation checks the right people before they are allowed to use the system, and that those checks are repeated when circumstances change. Without this, someone who should not have access could gain it, or a person’s situation could change in a way that increases risk—leaving the organisation exposed to fraud, misuse, or unauthorised handling of sensitive information.

The organisation must screen every individual before granting access, using background checks and other appropriate checks. It must also keep records showing that screening was completed and the date it was completed.

The organisation must rescreen people when required:
- at least every year for anyone who can access Classified (Secret) information
- at least every five years for anyone who can access Confidential information
- within 30 days after a significant change in their role or the risk level of their position, including when someone leaves and is then rehired into a role that is covered by this requirement.

##### IAM screening gate with event-driven rescreening SLAs

- Category: Software
- Priority: Critical

###### WHAT
Set up an identity and access “screening gate” that stops initial account set-up until personnel screening is approved. It must also enforce when people must be re-screened, based on the organisation’s rules for Secret and Confidential access, and when a person’s job role or risk level changes.

###### WHY (control requirement)
PS-3 requires personnel screening and re-screening to match the criteria for the person’s assigned position, including the organisation’s defined re-screening triggers and timing. The organisation’s rules require:
- re-screening for access to Secret and Confidential data,
- re-screening after material job role or risk level changes, and
- re-screening within 30 days after those material changes (including when someone leaves and is rehired into a covered role).

This approach ensures screening and re-screening are completed and recorded before access is granted or allowed to continue.

###### HOW (specific steps/approach)
1. **Define screening-to-access rules (policy-as-code):** Create an official mapping that links application access areas to data classifications (Secret/Confidential), and links HR/role/risk designations to the correct re-screening rule.
2. **Create screening status information in identity and access management (IAM):** Keep tracking details such as `screeningDecision` (approved/denied/pending), `screeningLevel` (Secret/Confidential), `screeningExpiry`, `lastRescreenDate`, and `triggerReason` (for example: role change, risk designation change, termination followed by rehire).
3. **Link screening results to account set-up:** Connect the screening workflow outcome to the IAM system and identity provider (IdP) so that **account set-up and assignment to privileged groups are blocked** unless `screeningDecision=approved` and `screeningLevel` matches the access classification being requested.
4. **Enforce re-screening timing and service expectations:**
   - **Secret access:** schedule re-screening at **least once per year**, and stop continued access when screening is due or has expired until a new decision is recorded.
   - **Confidential access:** schedule re-screening **every 5 years**, and apply the same access stop-and-wait rule when due or expired.
   - **Material role/risk changes:** trigger re-screening **within 30 days**, and restrict access (at least to the minimum necessary) until the updated decision is recorded.
5. **Cover termination and rehire into covered roles:** Use human resources (HR) events to detect termination and later rehire into a covered role. Trigger the **30-day** re-screening process and ensure access is not set up again for covered areas until approval is recorded.
6. **Produce audit-ready evidence:** Store an unchangeable audit record for each person showing the screening result, approval/completion date, trigger reason, and the access set-up event it enabled. Keep logs in line with the organisation’s enterprise logging and retention standards.

###### WHO (role responsible)
- **IAM/Identity Architect:** designs the screening gate, the information model, and the account set-up integration.
- **Security Governance and Compliance Lead:** checks that the mapping and re-screening rules match PS-3 organisation-defined parameters.
- **HR Systems Owner / HRIS Analyst:** ensures HR event feeds (role/risk changes, termination/rehire) are complete and correctly mapped.
- **Application Owner:** confirms that application access areas are correctly classified and mapped to IAM access areas.

###### Acceptance criteria
- No user or contractor is set up for Secret or Confidential application access areas unless screening is recorded as **approved** in IAM.
- For **Secret access**, re-screening happens at least **annually**; for **Confidential access**, at least **every 5 years**. Access is restricted when screening is due or expired until a new approved decision is recorded.
- For **material role/risk changes** (and termination followed by rehire into covered roles), re-screening is started so that the completion/decision is recorded **within 30 days**, with access restricted until approval is recorded.
- For each access authorisation, an auditor can retrieve evidence showing the screening outcome, completion/approval date, trigger reason, and the access set-up event.
- The solution is tested in a scenario for each trigger type (Secret expiry, Confidential expiry, material role change, material risk designation change, termination+rehire).

###### Actions Required for Compliance

- [ ] Create an official mapping of which applications can access which data categories (Secret or Confidential), and the rescreening rule that must be applied.
- [ ] Set up identity and access management (IAM) / identity provider (IdP) screening status details—decision, level, expiry date, last rescreen date, and trigger reason—based on information from the screening workflow.
- [ ] Set up user provisioning and assignment to privileged groups so it is blocked unless the screening decision is **approved** and the screening level meets the required access classification target.
- [ ] Link human resources (HR) changes—such as job role changes, changes to risk status, termination and rehire—to start re-screening processes. Apply a 30-day service level agreement (SLA) for material changes.
- [ ] Set up automatic re-checks for **Secret** information every year and **Confidential** information every 5 years, and block access when the re-check is due or has expired.
- [ ] Store unchangeable audit records that link the screening result to the date the access was completed or approved, and to the access authorisation or provisioning event.

#### PS-3.1 — Classified Information (Enhancement)

This requirement is designed to ensure that only people who have been properly approved and properly briefed can handle the most sensitive government information. Without it, someone could be allowed to view or work with classified material without the correct level of security clearance, or without being formally told what they are allowed to access. This increases the risk of unauthorised disclosure, misuse, or accidental mishandling.

To meet this requirement, the organisation must clearly identify which people are permitted to access systems that store, process, or transmit classified information. Before any access begins, the organisation must confirm that each person has the required security clearance and has been formally briefed to the highest classification level of information they are allowed to access on that system.

The organisation must keep clear records of the security clearance checks and the briefing level, including dates. It must repeat the clearance and briefing checks whenever a person’s permitted access level increases.

##### Enforce clearance and highest-level indoctrination before access

- Category: Software
- Priority: Critical

###### WHAT
Set up an access control that checks, for every person trying to use the application, that they have a **valid security clearance** and have been **formally briefed (indoctrinated)** to the **highest information classification level** they are authorised to access on the system.

###### WHY (control requirement)
NIST PS-3(1) Enhancement requires the organisation to **verify** that people accessing a system that handles **classified information** are **cleared** and **briefed (indoctrinated)** to the **highest classification level** of information they are allowed to access. This must be enforced **before access is granted**, and the checks must be supported with evidence that can be audited.

###### HOW (specific steps/approach)
1. **Define which clearance level allows which access**: create an official mapping between (a) the application’s classified data access levels and (b) the maximum classification level allowed for each access role (for example, “Secret”, “Top Secret” equivalents). Keep this mapping up to date for each environment (production, testing, disaster recovery) and for each part of the system (user interface, application programming interface, administration functions).
2. **Create official identity details in the organisation’s central identity service** (or equivalent): store clearance and briefing information for each person using attributes such as:
   - `clearanceLevel` and `clearanceValidUntil`
   - `indoctrinationLevel` and `indoctrinationCompletedOn`
   - `indoctrinationRecordId` (or equivalent reference)
   - `clearanceRecordId` (or equivalent reference)
   Only the clearance and briefing authorities should be able to change these attributes, and every change must be fully recorded for audit purposes.
3. **Connect the clearance and briefing processes**: link the central identity service to the clearance management system and the formal briefing/indoctrination system, so that when people join or their roles change, the attributes above are updated automatically, including dates/times and source information.
4. **Apply conditional access checks when authorising (and at session start where needed)**: deny access unless all conditions are met:
   - `clearanceLevel` is **at least** the required maximum classification level for the requested role and resource
   - `clearanceValidUntil` is a future date (not expired)
   - `indoctrinationLevel` is **at least** the required maximum classification level for the requested role and resource
   - `indoctrinationCompletedOn` is on or before the time access is granted/activated
   - the user is a real person (not a generic service identity) where this control applies; for service accounts, ensure they are managed through approved human sponsorship and authorisation processes
5. **Re-check when access increases (movers)**: if a person’s allowed access level increases, automatically re-check both clearance and briefing before completing the role change or enabling access to higher-classified resources.
6. **Produce audit-ready evidence**: record the access decision and the attribute values used (for example, in central identity service logs and/or application audit logs), linked to `clearanceRecordId` and `indoctrinationRecordId`, including timestamps.
7. **Record how the checks work in the system security plan**: document the access-control logic, where the clearance/briefing information comes from, who is responsible (central identity service administrator, security clearance authority, training/indoctrination authority, application/system owner), and how evidence is kept.

###### WHO
- **Identity and Access Management (IAM) Engineer**: set up central identity service attributes, conditional access policies, and audit logging.
- **Security Clearance / Personnel Security Officer**: ensure clearance validity and clearance record references are correct and exported properly.
- **Security Training / Indoctrination Programme Owner**: ensure briefing/indoctrination completion and record references are correct and exported properly.
- **Application Owner / System Owner**: define the mapping between classification levels and roles/resources, and ensure the system security plan reflects the enforcement.

###### Acceptance criteria
- When any user attempts to access classified resources, the system denies access unless both clearance and briefing meet or exceed the required maximum classification level for that access.
- Access is denied if clearance is expired, or if briefing is not at the required level (or not completed before access is activated).
- Role increases (movers) cannot be completed unless the clearance and briefing checks both pass.
- Audit logs show, for each access decision, the attribute values used and references to the underlying clearance and briefing records, including timestamps.
- The enforcement approach and how evidence is retained are documented in the system security plan.

###### Actions Required for Compliance

- [ ] Define the application’s levels of access to classified information, and link each access role and resource to the highest classification level it is allowed to access.
- [ ] Create official identity provider (IdP) attributes for clearance and indoctrination, including the level, validity and completion dates/times, and record references, with strict change control.
- [ ] Link the clearance and indoctrination processes to the identity provider (IdP) so that updates to user attributes are automatic, recorded with the time and date, and their source is kept for audit purposes.
- [ ] Set conditional access and authorisation rules to block access unless the person’s security clearance and indoctrination meet or exceed the required highest classification level, and the security clearance has not expired.
- [ ] Set up role-change (joiner/mover) processes to re-check security clearance and required indoctrination before allowing access to higher-classified information.
- [ ] Enable audit logging for authorisation decisions, including the specific attribute values used and how they link to the relevant clearance and indoctrination record references.
- [ ] Update the system security plan to describe how security rules are applied, where the information comes from, who is responsible, and how long evidence is kept.

#### PS-3.2 — Formal Indoctrination (Enhancement)

This requirement is about ensuring that only people who have been properly briefed can handle sensitive government information, and that they receive the required briefing for every type of information they are allowed to access. Without this, someone could be given access to information they have not been formally trained or authorised to understand, increasing the risk of accidental mishandling, unauthorised disclosure, or serious compliance failures.

To meet this expectation, the organisation must first define which types of classified information on the system require formal indoctrination (including Special Access Program (SAP), Restricted Data (RD), and Sensitive Compartmented Information (SCI)). It must then identify everyone authorised to access the system and specify exactly which information types each person is allowed to access. Before access is granted, each person must complete the formal indoctrination for every relevant information type, and the organisation must keep clear, person-specific records showing that this has been completed.

The organisation should document this process in its personnel and system plans, and be able to check that the required briefing coverage is in place when making access decisions and during reviews.

##### Type-specific indoctrination gating for SAP/RD/SCI access

- Category: Software
- Priority: Critical

###### WHAT
Put in place an identity and permissions check so that access to system resources that handle **SAP, RD, and SCI** is allowed only after a person has completed **formal indoctrination for each specific classified information type** they are authorised to access.

###### WHY (control requirement)
NIST PS-3(2) (enhancement) requires proof that people who access a system that processes, stores, or transmits classified information types that require formal indoctrination are **formally indoctrinated for all relevant types** they can access. This must be verifiable and specific to each person, covering **SAP, RD, SCI** (and any other indoctrination-required types identified by the organisation).

###### HOW (specific steps/approach)
1. **Define indoctrination-required types and link them to resources**: Create a list (taxonomy) of indoctrination-required information types (at minimum **SAP, RD, SCI**) and link each type to the application, data, and programming interfaces (application programming interfaces (APIs)), plus datasets, storage locations, and administrative functions that process, store, or transmit them.
2. **Create type-specific access permissions**: Treat each indoctrination-required type as its own permission (for example, `SAP_ACCESS`, `RD_ACCESS`, `SCI_ACCESS`) rather than using one general “classified” flag.
3. **Send indoctrination completion evidence into identity management**: Set up a secure connection from the organisation’s formal indoctrination/briefing record system (or human resources (HR)/security training system) into the enterprise identity provider (identity provider (IdP)) or an authorisation decision service. This should give each user **type-specific, person-specific** attributes such as `SAP_INDOCTRINATION_COMPLETE=true` (and equivalents for RD/SCI).
4. **Block access before it is granted**: Configure conditional access and permission rules so that permissions and/or access to the linked resources are blocked unless the correct **type-specific completion attribute** is present and valid. Apply this for both **joiner and mover** events (role changes) and for privileged access routes.
5. **Create audit evidence for coverage**: Produce audit-ready records showing, for each user and each indoctrination-required type: (a) what permissions were granted, (b) the linked completion record, and (c) the completion date/time and validity period (if applicable).
6. **Update the system security plan**: Document the access-check approach and named responsibilities (for example, identity and access management (IAM) owner, security training/indoctrination owner, application owner) so the system security plan clearly explains how PS-3(2) is met.

###### WHO (role responsible)
- **IAM/Identity Architect**: designs permissions (entitlements), the attribute model, and the access-check rules.
- **Security Training/Personnel Security Lead**: owns the formal indoctrination record source and ensures the evidence is accurate and type-specific.
- **Application Owner / Security Engineer**: links information types to resources and ensures access checks match the permissions.
- **Compliance/Audit Lead**: checks that the evidence is sufficient for PS-3(2) during audits.

###### Acceptance criteria
- For every user granted access to any resource linked to **SAP, RD, or SCI**, the system can show that the user has **completed formal indoctrination for each corresponding type**.
- Requests to access SAP/RD/SCI resources are **rejected** when the relevant type-specific completion attribute is missing or invalid.
- Audit reports show, for each user, the **permissions** and the **linked type-specific completion records** (including completion dates).
- The **system security plan** documents the access-check approach and responsibilities.

###### Actions Required for Compliance

- [ ] Define an information classification system that specifies which information requires indoctrination (at minimum: SAP, RD, and SCI) and link each classification to the relevant application resources.
- [ ] Create separate identity and access management (IAM) permissions for each type of access that requires indoctrination (for example, SAP_ACCESS, RD_ACCESS, SCI_ACCESS).
- [ ] Add formal records showing completion of required training (“indoctrination”) into the identity provider and authorisation service as attributes that are both type-specific and person-specific.
- [ ] Set conditional access and authorisation rules to prevent entitlement being issued and/or access to resources unless the matching “type completion” attribute is valid.
- [ ] Set up joiner and mover processes so that when new or expanded SAP (Systems, Applications and Products) / RD / SCI permissions are granted, the system checks whether the required pre-access indoctrination has been completed before access is allowed.
- [ ] Create and check audit reports that link every granted entitlement to the matching type-specific completion record and its date.
- [ ] Update the system security plan to describe the PS-3(2) approval step and assign named responsibilities.

#### PS-3.3 — Information Requiring Special Protective Measures (Enhancement)

This requirement is about ensuring that only the right people can access systems that hold especially sensitive information, and that those people have been properly checked for the work they are expected to do. Without this, an unauthorised or unsuitable person could access sensitive material, or someone whose checks are no longer valid could continue working with it. This increases the risk of data misuse, fraud, or serious harm to the organisation and others.

To meet this expectation, the organisation must first identify which systems hold information that needs special protection. It must then set out who is allowed to access those systems based on legitimate official government duties, and keep evidence that each person’s access is up to date, approved, and clearly linked to their job. The organisation must also ensure that anyone accessing information at Secret or Confidential level meets the required suitability and background screening standards before access is granted. It must complete and record any required periodic re-checks. If someone’s screening is no longer valid, their access must be suspended or removed unless there is an approved exception that is limited to a specific time period.

##### Screening-validity gate for Secret/Confidential system access

- Category: Software
- Priority: Critical

###### WHAT
Set up an access gate based on identity and authorisation, so that only people with **valid access authorisations** and **current Secret/Confidential position sensitivity background screening** (including suitability checks and periodic re-checks) can access systems that process, store, or transmit information that needs extra protection measures (for example, controlled unclassified information).

###### WHY (control requirement)
PS-3(3) enhancement requires confirmation that people who access these systems meet **personnel security requirements** (including **position sensitivity background screening requirements**) and that access is linked to the person’s authorised role.

###### HOW (specific steps/approach)
1. **Tag special-protection systems**: Create an official list of applications/systems and assign a “special-protection” label and required screening level (for example, Secret versus Confidential) to each system.
2. **Define role/position-to-access mapping**: In the identity and access management (IAM) authorisation model, create roles/groups that reflect official duties, and link them to the required screening level and the specific system access areas (scopes).
3. **Integrate authoritative screening status**: Connect the organisation’s personnel security/human resources (HR) clearance-suitability system (or clearance management service) to the organisation’s enterprise identity provider (IdP)/IAM, so each user has machine-readable attributes for:
   - screening level (Secret/Confidential)
   - validity dates
   - re-check due/overdue status
   - current suitability status
   - any approved exception indicator (if your organisation allows time-limited exceptions)
4. **Apply conditional access at sign-in and when setting up access**:
   - Configure the IdP to block sign-in to any “special-protection” application when the screening status is not valid for the required level.
   - Ensure automated provisioning only grants group/application access when screening is approved and valid.
   - Configure automated deprovisioning/suspension when screening becomes invalid or the re-check is overdue.
5. **Keep evidence that can be audited**:
   - Record access decisions using the screening details used (level, validity, due/overdue) and the resulting access outcome.
   - Keep clear records linking the user, the system access scope, the access approval, and the screening record (including dates and any exception references, where applicable).
6. **Run exceptions and reviews in a controlled way**:
   - If exceptions exist, implement them as time-limited, approval-controlled flags that still create audit records and trigger re-validation when they expire.
   - Schedule periodic checks to identify any mismatch between IAM access and screening status.

###### WHO
- **Information Security/Compliance Lead**: owns how the control is interpreted, what evidence is required, and audit readiness.
- **IAM Engineer / Identity Architect**: implements IdP conditional access, provisioning/deprovisioning processes, and attribute mapping.
- **Personnel Security/HR Security Team**: provides the authoritative screening information and manages exceptions.
- **Application Owner**: ensures the application is correctly labelled and connected to the IAM access gate.

###### Acceptance criteria
- For every tagged special-protection system, access is denied unless the user’s screening status is **valid** for the required level (Secret/Confidential) at the time of sign-in.
- If screening becomes invalid or the re-check is overdue, access is automatically suspended/revoked within the defined operational timeframe.
- Audit logs for access attempts and provisioning events show the user identity, system scope, the screening level/validity details used, and the access outcome.
- An evidence package can be produced showing the link between the access scope and the screening records (including dates and any approved exceptions).

###### Actions Required for Compliance

- [ ] Create and keep an official list (inventory) of special-protection systems, including the required security clearance level (Secret or Confidential).
- [ ] Define identity and access management (IAM) roles and groups that match official job duties to system access areas (scopes) and the required background screening levels.
- [ ] Integrate the official personnel security and human resources (HR) screening system with the organisation’s identity provider and identity and access management (IdP/IAM) so that each user’s screening level, validity period, and whether a reinvestigation is due are available as user details (attributes).
- [ ] Set up conditional access rules to block sign-in to specially protected applications when the identity check is not valid at the required level.
- [ ] Set up automated processes to grant access only after screening has been approved and is still valid, and to suspend access when screening is invalid or overdue.
- [ ] Enable audit logging for access decisions and user provisioning events, including the screening details used and the final authorisation result.
- [ ] Carry out regular checks to compare who has access (identity and access management) with the latest screening status, and fix any differences.

#### PS-3.4 — Citizenship Requirements (Enhancement)

This requirement is about making sure only the right people can handle highly sensitive information. In everyday terms, it stops unauthorised or unsuitable people from accessing systems that store, process, or send **Confidential** and **Secret** information. Without this control, there is a risk of serious data leaks, fraud, or harm to customers, partners, or national interests.

To meet this requirement, the organisation must clearly define who is eligible to access these systems. It must require that every authorised person is either a **United States citizen** or a **lawful permanent resident**, and also holds the specific level of security clearance needed for that access.

Before access is granted, the organisation must check both eligibility and the required clearance. If either requirement is not met, access must be refused or blocked. The organisation must also keep clear records showing that these checks were completed and when access was authorised.

Finally, the organisation must document the screening criteria and the step-by-step process for any additional checks. It must ensure these requirements are included in its system plans and access procedures.

##### Pre-access citizenship & clearance gate for Confidential/Secret apps

- Category: Software
- Priority: Critical

###### WHAT
Set up an “identity-first” access check that, for every person trying to use systems that process, store, or transmit the defined **information types** (for example, *Confidential and Secret*), confirms they meet the defined **citizenship requirements** (for example, *United States citizenship or lawful permanent resident status, with the required security clearance*). Access must be refused unless **both** citizenship eligibility and the required security clearance are confirmed.

###### WHY (control requirement)
PS-3(4) (Enhancement) requires the organisation to **verify** that people accessing a system that processes, stores, or transmits the specified **information types** meet the specified **citizenship requirements**. This verification must happen **before** access is granted, and the decision must be capable of being checked later through an audit trail.

###### HOW (specific steps/approach)
1. **Define the eligibility rule mapping** in the access policy model: for each relevant application/system, record the required **information types** and the matching **citizenship requirements** (United States citizen or lawful permanent resident *and* the required security clearance).
2. **Use authoritative sources** in the identity governance layer:
   - Human resources (HR) or personnel system data for citizenship/lawful permanent resident (LPR) status.
   - Clearance management and authoritative clearance records for clearance level.
   - Make sure these data items are versioned and include start and end dates (effective/expiry dates).
3. **Apply pre-access conditional checks** in the identity layer (identity provider (IdP) / access management):
   - Refuse sign-in (or refuse entitlement assignment) to the Confidential/Secret applications unless **both** required attributes are valid.
   - Default to **deny** if attributes are missing, out of date, or cannot be verified.
4. **Set up an entitlement approval process with an auditable sequence**:
   - Require an approval step that records “citizenship eligibility verified” and “clearance verified” (including references/identifiers to the evidence) before the access decision is recorded as approved.
   - Link the evidence references to the access authorisation record.
5. **Handle exceptions explicitly**:
   - If citizenship/clearance information is missing or inconsistent, automatically create a remediation ticket and keep access in a denied/quarantined state until verification is completed.
6. **Regularly check and remove access when no longer allowed**:
   - Run periodic re-checks (for example, daily/weekly) and automatically revoke access when security clearance is downgraded/expired or when citizenship eligibility changes.

###### WHO (role responsible)
- **Identity and Access Management (IAM) Lead**: owns the identity provider (IdP) and entitlement gate implementation, and the policy-as-code/conditional access configuration.
- **Security Clearance and Personnel Data Owner**: ensures authoritative data feeds and attribute accuracy for citizenship/LPR and clearance records.
- **Application Owner**: ensures each Confidential/Secret system is correctly labelled and mapped to the required clearance/citizenship requirements.
- **Compliance/Audit Lead**: checks that the evidence is sufficient and that the audit trail is complete.

###### Acceptance criteria
- For every Confidential/Secret-labelled application, access is **denied** unless the user’s identity attributes confirm **United States citizenship or lawful permanent resident status** and the user holds the **required security clearance**.
- The authorisation workflow records an auditable decision trail showing verification was completed **before** access is marked approved, including evidence references/identifiers.
- If citizenship/clearance attributes are missing or out of date, access is denied and a remediation ticket is created.
- Periodic checks lead to automatic revocation when eligibility no longer applies.

###### Actions Required for Compliance

- [ ] Tag all systems that process, store, or transmit **Confidential** or **Secret** information with the required citizenship and clearance rule.
- [ ] Add employee “citizenship” and “lawful permanent resident (LPR)” details, along with official clearance records, into the central identity governance system, including the start (effective) and end (expiry) dates.
- [ ] Set up identity provider (IdP) conditional access (or an equivalent control) to block sign-ins and the issuing of access tokens to Confidential/Secret applications unless both citizenship eligibility and the required security clearance have been verified.
- [ ] Set up an approval process for access requests that records the evidence references for both citizenship checks and security clearance checks before the access request is marked as approved.
- [ ] Apply a “deny by default” rule when citizenship or clearance information is missing, out of date, or cannot be verified, and raise remediation tickets to fix the issue.
- [ ] Set up regular re-checks of eligibility and automatically remove access when eligibility changes (for example, when a security clearance is downgraded or expires, or when citizenship status changes).

### PS-4 — Personnel Termination (Control)

This expectation requires that a person’s access is stopped immediately when their job ends, and that nothing valuable or sensitive is left behind. Without it, a former employee could still sign in to systems, use old cards or keys, or keep control of information and tools they should no longer manage. This creates a real risk of unauthorised access, misuse of data, or loss of security assets.

Within 1 hour of employment ending, the organisation must switch off the person’s access to organisational systems and remove every way they could prove their identity. This includes digital access and physical items such as keys, identification cards, building passes, and security tokens. The organisation must also get back all security-related property the person had, and hold an exit interview where practicable. The exit interview should cover any ongoing responsibilities under non-disclosure agreements, the need to return security items, how classified information must continue to be protected, and any limits on future employment.

Finally, the organisation must ensure that any information and systems the person previously controlled remain protected and are managed by the organisation, with proper records kept of what was done.

##### HR-driven termination workflow to disable access within 1 hour

- Category: Software
- Priority: Critical

###### WHAT
Set up an organisation-wide, event-driven process for ending access when someone leaves. This process must: (1) disable the person’s system access within the agreed time period (default: **within 1 hour of termination**), (2) remove all authentication methods and credentials (digital and physical), (3) arrange an **exit interview where practicable** covering required security topics, and (4) ensure the organisation keeps and manages any information or systems the person previously had, with evidence suitable for audit.

###### WHY (control requirement)
PS-4 requires that **when employment ends**, the organisation quickly removes access and credentials, retrieves security-related property (including hardware authentication tokens, keys, identification cards, building passes, and system administration technical manuals), and carries out an **exit interview where practicable**. The exit interview should cover reminders about non-disclosure agreements (NDAs), returning security-related property, continued protection of classified information, and limits on future employment. The guidance also stresses that **timely action** is essential, including for terminations for cause.

###### HOW (specific steps/approach)
1. **Set the target time** in the workflow so system access is disabled **within 1 hour of termination**, as defined by the organisation.
2. **Create an official HR termination event** (including the termination time and reason) that automatically triggers removal of access in the enterprise identity provider (identity provider (IdP)) and connected access systems.
3. **Automate removing digital access**: disable the user account, end active sessions and remove refresh tokens, and remove or disable multi-factor authentication (multi-factor authentication (MFA)) methods (for example: remove FIDO2 keys, disable authenticator app factors, and revoke SMS/voice factors where used). Ensure access is blocked immediately using access/session controls, not just at the next sign-in.
4. **Automate removal of physical property and other non-IdP items**: create work orders for Security/Facilities/IT to collect **hardware authentication tokens, keys, identification cards, building passes, and any system administration technical manuals** assigned to the person leaving. Keep a property-return status list linked to the termination case.
5. **Exit interview where practicable**: include an exit-interview checklist in the termination workflow. If an exit interview is not practicable (for example, supervisor unavailable, illness, job abandonment), record the reason and use an alternative accountability method (for example, a signed acknowledgement or a recorded briefing) to maintain accountability for NDA and ongoing protection of classified information.
6. **Maintain organisational control of information and systems**: automatically remove or transfer any application roles, group memberships, service account ownership, and administrative privileges previously held by the person. Ensure access is reassigned to authorised staff and that the former user no longer has permissions.
7. **Create audit-ready evidence**: record the HR termination time, the time the identity was disabled, the timestamps for session and MFA removal, the list of affected systems/applications/roles, the outcomes of property return, and whether the exit interview was completed (or why it was not practicable).

###### WHO (role responsible)
- **HR Operations / People Services**: ensures the termination time is correct and triggers the termination event.
- **Identity and Access Management (Identity and Access Management (IAM)) team**: implements IdP removal of access, ends sessions and removes MFA, and enforces access controls.
- **Security Operations / Facilities**: manages collection of physical security property (tokens, keys, IDs, building passes, manuals).
- **Application Owners / System Administrators**: confirm role/ownership transfer and removal of any remaining permissions.
- **Compliance / Information Security Governance**: checks that the evidence is complete and that the exit-interview topics are covered.

###### Acceptance criteria
- In a test termination, the person’s access to enterprise applications is disabled **within 1 hour** of the HR termination timestamp.
- Active sessions are ended and MFA methods are removed/disabled so the person cannot sign in again to re-authenticate.
- All identified categories of security-related property (hardware authentication tokens, keys, identification cards, building passes, system administration technical manuals) are either returned or have a documented exception with compensating controls.
- An exit interview is completed **where practicable** and covers: NDA obligations, return of security-related property, continued protection of classified information, and limitations on future employment. If not practicable, the workflow records a documented reason and an alternative accountability method.
- The evidence package includes timestamps and the affected systems/roles, and confirms removal/transfer of application/system access previously held by the person leaving.

###### Actions Required for Compliance

- [ ] Set up the process for ending access so that system access is turned off during the agreed time window (target: within 1 hour of termination).
- [ ] Link human resources (HR) termination events to the organisation’s identity provider (IdP) so that, automatically, user accounts are disabled, active log-in sessions and refresh tokens are revoked, and multi-factor authentication (MFA) sign-in methods are removed or disabled.
- [ ] Use conditional access and session controls to block access immediately, so access is prevented before the next sign-in.
- [ ] Create and link work orders to retrieve property used for hardware authentication, including tokens, keys, identification cards, building passes, and system administration technical manuals.
- [ ] Set up an exit-interview checklist as a required step to confirm: the person’s non-disclosure agreement (NDA) duties are met; all security-related property is returned; classified information remains protected; and there are limits on future employment. If any part cannot be followed, record how this was handled as not practicable.
- [ ] Automate the removal or transfer of the leaver’s application roles, group memberships, and administrative privileges.
- [ ] Create an evidence bundle showing the timing of the employee’s termination, the time access was disabled, the time multi-factor authentication (MFA) and session access were revoked, which systems and roles were affected, the status of returned company property, and the result of the exit interview.

#### PS-4.1 — Post-employment Requirements (Enhancement)

This requirement is about making sure that when people leave the organisation, they are clearly told what they must do after they go, and that they confirm they understand it. Without this, a leaver could accidentally or deliberately misuse or disclose organisational information, which could cause legal, financial, and reputational harm to the business.

To meet this expectation, the organisation must keep an up-to-date list of the post-leaving obligations that apply to each person, and ensure those obligations can be legally enforced (for example through contract terms, the law, or other binding agreements). During the exit process, the organisation must provide the departing individual with written notice of the obligations that apply to them before they leave, or at the time they leave, and require them to sign a statement confirming they received and understood what they were told.

The organisation should also have clear internal procedures for handling terminations, assign named responsibilities for managing this, record the approach in its security planning, and involve its legal advisers to confirm which obligations are legally binding.

##### Versioned post-employment requirements pack with signed acknowledgement

- Category: Software
- Priority: High

###### WHAT
Set up a termination process that, for every person leaving the organisation, (1) identifies the post-employment information-protection duties that apply, (2) gives them a written, legally binding notice during the termination, and (3) records a signed confirmation that they received and understood the notice—keeping evidence that can be checked later.

###### WHY (control requirement)
PS-4(1) (Enhancement) requires the organisation to consult the Office of the General Counsel about post-employment requirements for people who leave. In practice, this means the organisation must make sure the post-employment duties it tells people about are legally correct and can be evidenced at the time of termination.

###### HOW (specific steps/approach)
1. **Create and keep an up-to-date, approved “post-employment requirements list”** owned by Information Security and **legally checked by the Office of the General Counsel** (or legal counsel acting for it). Each duty must include: the legal basis/reference (for example, contract/policy/law), the roles/contract types/regions it applies to, and the start dates.
2. **Link this to the HR termination workflow (HR information system)** so that when termination starts, the system selects the correct duties based on employee details (for example, contract type, access level, types of data handled, and region).
3. **Create a “requirements pack” at the time of termination** that includes:
   - a **written notice** listing the applicable duties and clearly pointing to the legal basis/version; and
   - an **acknowledgement form** for the leaver to confirm they received and understood the notice.
4. **Only allow exit completion after the leaver signs**, using an electronic signature process that keeps an audit trail (who signed, when, and a document reference such as a document hash/version). Stop the system from completing deprovisioning/exit steps until the acknowledgement is recorded.
5. **Keep the proof with the termination record**, including: the notice version, the requirements list version, the acknowledgement form version, signature details, and the workflow timing.
6. **Document termination procedures and responsibilities** (HR personnel security owner and Information Security owner) and include the process in security planning, making sure Legal checking is part of the controlled template/requirements-list update process.

###### WHO
- **Information Security**: owns the requirements list content and how it maps to roles and data access; ensures version control and that it is built into the workflow.
- **HR (Personnel Security)**: starts the termination workflow and ensures the pack is issued during the termination process.
- **Office of the General Counsel / Legal Counsel**: confirms which duties are legally binding and approves the notice/acknowledgement templates.
- **System/Workflow Owner (IT/Platform team)**: builds the HR information system workflow link and the electronic-signature “gate” that prevents exit completion.

###### Acceptance criteria
- For **100%** of terminations processed through the workflow, a requirements pack is created and issued **before or on the termination date**.
- Each pack contains the correct duties based on the employee’s details and references the **approved, legally checked** requirements list version.
- The leaver’s signed acknowledgement is captured with an auditable signature record and is **stored in the termination record**.
- Exit completion (or the equivalent final deprovisioning step) cannot finish unless the acknowledgement has been recorded.
- There is evidence that Legal has checked and approved the requirements list and templates (for example, approval records linked to the relevant versions).

###### Actions Required for Compliance

- [ ] Create a version-controlled list of post-employment requirements and get approval from the Office of the General Counsel for each requirement and template.
- [ ] Set up the human resources (HR) termination process so it automatically chooses the correct obligations based on employee details, such as contract type, access level, types of data involved, and region.
- [ ] Create a written requirements pack (notice plus acknowledgement form) using only the latest versions that have been approved by Legal.
- [ ] Enable electronic signatures for the acknowledgement, and require a workflow step so exit completion cannot be completed without a signature.
- [ ] Store proof of termination in the termination record, including the notice version, the requirements list version, the acknowledgement version, signature details, and the relevant dates and times.
- [ ] Publish and keep up to date the staff termination procedure, and assign named owners for human resources (HR) personnel security and information security responsibilities. Then refer to this procedure in your security planning.

#### PS-4.2 — Automated Actions (Enhancement)

This expectation is about ensuring that, when someone leaves the organisation, the correct people are alerted automatically and the person’s access is removed automatically as well—without relying on anyone to remember. Without this, there is a real risk that the human resources (HR) team and the IT security team are informed too late or not at all, and that a departing employee could continue to access systems and information for longer than intended. This could create opportunities for mistakes or misuse.

To meet this expectation, the organisation must use automated processes, aligned with its own leaver (termination) procedures, to:
- send prompt alerts to the HR team and the IT security roles for the specific type of termination, and
- automatically disable the individual’s access to the relevant system resources for those same termination actions.

The information system owner is responsible for making sure these automated actions are in place, correctly configured, documented, and supported by records showing that notifications and access changes were completed for each termination event.

##### HR leaver workflow to auto-notify HR/Sec and disable access

- Category: Software
- Priority: Critical

###### WHAT
Set up automated processes that (a) alert the correct people or job roles about each individual termination action and (b) automatically stop the terminated person’s access to the specified system resources. Both actions must be triggered by the same termination event.

###### WHY (control requirement)
PS-4(2) Enhancement requires: **“Use automated mechanisms to notify personnel or roles of individual termination actions and/or to disable access to system resources.”** This reduces the risk that alerts are missed or delayed, and that terminated individuals keep access longer than intended.

###### HOW (specific steps/approach)
1. **Define how the termination event is recorded**: configure the HR system of record (HR information system) to send a termination event that includes a unique termination reference, employee identifier, termination action type, and effective time.
2. **Create an automated “leaver” workflow** in the organisation’s orchestration layer (for example, an identity and access management workflow engine or integration platform). This workflow must start automatically when the HR termination event is received (no manual start).
3. **Automated notifications**: during the same workflow run, send alerts to the **defined recipients** (for example, the HR team and IT security roles). Include the termination reference, employee identifier, termination action type, and effective time.
4. **Automated access disabling**: during the same workflow run, automatically remove access by applying the agreed scope of system resources for the same employee identifier and termination reference. This may include disabling the account in the identity provider, removing group memberships and entitlements, revoking tokens and sessions, and enforcing a sign-in block and/or conditional access.
5. **Evidence and linking**: make sure the workflow creates an auditable record (an execution log) that can be matched to the identity provider audit logs and the notification delivery logs. Include timestamps and the termination reference.
6. **Operational controls**: add retry and alerting if the workflow fails, so notifications and access disabling are not silently missed. Also set a measurable expectation for how quickly actions must be completed, aligned to the organisation’s termination procedures.

###### WHO
- **Information system owner**: responsible for ensuring the automated processes are defined, configured, and supported (per ps-04.02_odp.03).
- **IAM (identity and access management) / identity engineering lead**: implements identity provider account disabling, group and entitlement removal, and workflow integration.
- **HR operations / HR information system owner**: ensures termination events are sent correctly, including the termination reference and action type.
- **IT security / security operations centre representative**: confirms the notification recipients and checks that access disabling covers the agreed resource scope.

###### Acceptance criteria
- For each HR termination event, the workflow runs automatically and produces (1) notification delivery to the defined HR and IT security roles, and (2) automated access disabling for the terminated user.
- Notifications and access disabling are linked to the **same termination reference** and employee identifier.
- Evidence exists for each termination event: workflow execution log + identity provider access-disable audit trail + notification delivery records.
- No manual triggering is required for the notification and access disabling actions.

###### Actions Required for Compliance

- [ ] Define the official human resources (HR) system termination message details—termination reference, employee identifier, termination action type, and the effective date/time—and ensure the HR information system (HRIS) sends it reliably.
- [ ] Set up an automated “leaver” process that starts automatically when the human resources (HR) termination event happens, without anyone having to start it manually.
- [ ] Set up automated notifications to the agreed human resources (HR) and information technology (IT) security contacts, including the termination reference, employee identifier, type of action, and the effective date and time.
- [ ] Set up automated account disabling in the identity provider and identity and access management system for the same employee identifier and termination reference. This should include disabling the account, removing any access rights and group memberships, ending any active sessions or tokens, and blocking sign-in where applicable.
- [ ] Enable logging of workflow activity, and link (correlate) those workflow logs with identity provider (IdP) audit logs and notification delivery logs for each termination event.
- [ ] Test the full end-to-end process for several types of termination actions, and confirm that the response times meet the organisation’s expectations in its termination procedure.

### PS-5 — Personnel Transfer (Control)

This requirement ensures that when someone changes job roles, they keep only the access they genuinely need, and any access from their previous role is removed quickly. If this is not done, they may still be able to enter buildings or use systems they no longer require, or they may have the wrong permissions. This can lead to mistakes, unauthorised access, or disruption to day-to-day work.

For every permanent or long-lasting reassignment, the organisation must:
- review and confirm that the person’s current building and system access is still needed for their new role; and
- within 24 hours of the formal transfer, remove any old access, issue new keys, cards, or passes where required, close old system accounts and create new ones if they are used, update what the person is allowed to do, and transfer access to any official records they need for the new role.

The organisation must also adjust access if operational needs change, and notify the owner responsible for the affected systems and the facility security officer within 24 hours. It must keep records showing what was checked and what changes were made.

##### Automated transfer access lifecycle with 24-hour SLA

- Category: Software
- Priority: Critical

###### WHAT
Set up an identity-first process for moving staff between roles. When Human Resources (HR) records a formal transfer or reassignment, the process will automatically: (1) confirm what access the person needs in their new role, (2) remove their old access to systems and physical areas where applicable, (3) issue new keys, cards, or passes where applicable, (4) close old system accounts and create new ones if the organisation’s account approach requires it, (5) update permissions to match the new role, (6) move access to the official records needed for the new role, and (7) inform the information system owner and the facility security officer within the organisation’s required time period.

###### WHY (control requirement)
PS-5 requires that for permanent or long-term staff transfers/reassignments, the organisation defines and carries out actions that match the type of transfer. This includes: getting back old keys/identifiers/passes and issuing new ones; closing old system accounts and setting up new ones; changing system access permissions; and giving access to the official records that the person previously could access at the old location/account.  

The organisation’s required timing parameters mean these transfer actions must be completed within the defined time period (set here to **within 24 hours** of the formal transfer action). They also require that notification to the **information system owner** and the **facility security officer** happens within the defined time period (set here to **within 24 hours** of the reassignment/transfer).

###### HOW (specific steps/approach)
1. **Create an official trigger**: connect HR systems’ “transfer/reassignment” events to an identity and workflow process (event bus plus workflow engine).
2. **Define which roles need which access**: keep an auditable role-to-access table for each application and for physical access where systems are connected through an application programming interface (API) (for example, badge access systems such as badge access control systems (PACS)). The workflow must calculate what access changes before and after the transfer.
3. **Build a 24-hour service target into the workflow**: start a timer at the time of the formal transfer. Ensure that removing old access, setting up new access, and updating permissions and accounts are queued and completed within **24 hours**.
4. **Carry out access changes across the full lifecycle (automated where possible)**:
   - Remove old access (remove group membership and permissions; disable sessions or tokens where supported).
   - Issue new keys/cards/passes where applicable through connected systems.
   - Close old system accounts and create new accounts when the organisation’s account approach requires it.
   - Update permissions to match the new role (only what is needed).
   - Move access to official records (for example, repository/case/document permissions) under the new account setup.
5. **Notify required roles within 24 hours**: create notifications or tickets to the **information system owner** and the **facility security officer**. Include the employee identity, transfer time, and a summary of what access changes were started and completed.
6. **Keep evidence suitable for audit**: store workflow records showing (a) the outcome of the review/confirmation, (b) the time of each action, (c) what access changed before and after, and (d) notification times and recipients.
7. **Re-check when operational needs change**: add a follow-up re-evaluation step after the transfer (for example, an access review event or a change in manager/role) that repeats the same role-to-access mapping and access-difference logic when needs change.

###### WHO (role responsible)
- **Identity and Access Management (IAM) / Identity Engineering Lead**: responsible for designing the workflow, integrating systems, and maintaining the role-to-access mapping.
- **Information System Owner**: checks that the role-to-access mapping for their systems is correct and receives transfer notifications.
- **Facility Security Officer**: checks physical access changes where applicable and receives notifications.
- **HR Operations / HR Information System (HRIS) Owner**: ensures transfer events are accurate and include correct timestamps.

###### Acceptance criteria
- For a test transfer, the workflow starts the required access changes **within 24 hours** of the formal transfer action.
- Old system access is removed and permissions are updated to match the new role; old accounts are closed and new accounts are created where required by the account approach.
- New keys/cards/passes are issued where the badge/PACS integration exists; otherwise, the workflow creates an auditable exception that requires manual action.
- Access to official records is moved so the person can access only what is required for the new role.
- Notifications to the **information system owner** and **facility security officer** are sent **within 24 hours** and include enough detail to take action.
- Audit records show before/after access evidence and timestamps for the review, changes, and notifications.

###### Actions Required for Compliance

- [ ] Add human resources information system (HRIS) transfer or reassignment events into an identity and access management (IAM) orchestration process, using a formal transfer date and time.
- [ ] Create and keep an auditable record that maps each role to the access it should have (including the changes before and after entitlements) for every application and every integrated physical access system.
- [ ] Set up the workflow so it completes certificate revocation, user provisioning, privilege updates, and account changes within a strict 24-hour service level agreement (SLA).
- [ ] Set up automated transfer of permissions for official records to the new account context, or create documented, auditable exceptions for any permissions that must be handled manually.
- [ ] Within 24 hours of any reassignment or transfer, set up automated notifications to the information system owner and the facility security officer.
- [ ] Enable audit logging to record the review result, the entitlements before and after the change, the time the action was taken, and proof that notifications were sent.
- [ ] Add a trigger after the transfer to re-check and re-run the mapping when operational needs change

### PS-6 — Access Agreements (Control)

This requirement is about making sure that anyone who is authorised to use the organisation’s information and systems has clearly agreed, in writing, to follow the rules. Without current, signed agreements, people may misunderstand what is expected, handle sensitive information in the wrong way, or keep access they no longer need after their job changes. This increases the risk of data leaks, misuse, and unauthorised access.

The organisation must create and keep written access agreements for its information and systems. This includes the relevant types, such as non-disclosure agreements, acceptable use agreements, rules of behaviour, and conflict-of-interest agreements. These agreements must be reviewed and updated at least every 90 days, and whenever changes are needed or when a person’s role changes.

Anyone who needs access must sign the appropriate agreement before access is granted. They must also re-sign to keep access when agreements are updated or when their role changes. The organisation must keep records showing the reviews, updates, and re-signing.

##### Agreement-gated access with 90-day re-sign enforcement

- Category: Software
- Priority: Critical

###### WHAT
Put access agreements (for example: non-disclosure agreement (NDA), acceptable use, rules of behaviour, conflict-of-interest) in a controlled, version-controlled format. Users must **sign them (electronic signature permitted where allowed)** before they are given access to the organisation’s systems. Users must **sign again** when the agreement is updated or when their job role changes.

###### WHY (control requirement)
NIST PS-6 requires that access to organisational systems and information is governed by **documented access agreements**. Individuals must **acknowledge they have read, understood, and agree** to follow the stated constraints. These agreements must be **reviewed and updated at least every 90 days**, and individuals must **sign again** when agreements are updated or when their role changes.

###### HOW (specific steps/approach)
1. **Create agreement templates and mapping**: Create standard templates for each agreement type. Define which agreement(s) apply to each application/system role (for example: corporate user, privileged administrator, contractor). Keep the acknowledgement text that confirms the signer has read, understood, and accepted the constraints.
2. **Centralise version-controlled agreements**: Store agreements in a controlled repository with an unchangeable history of versions, effective dates, and an approval process (for example: information security (InfoSec), legal, human resources (HR), as applicable). Make sure each version has a unique identifier.
3. **Link to the organisation’s identity and access system for signing before access**: Set up the access process so users cannot be given access to the mapped application/system role until they complete electronic signature acceptance of the correct agreement version.
4. **Enforce 90-day reviews and re-sign triggers**: Automatically run a 90-day review cycle for each agreement version. When a new version is published, mark affected users as having an expired agreement and block access (or remove the role) until they electronically sign the updated version.
5. **Require re-signing when roles change**: Link human resources information system (HRIS) role-change events (or identity and access management (IAM) role assignment changes) to the agreement mapping. If a user’s role changes to a different set of mapped agreements, require electronic signature before the new role becomes active.
6. **Create audit-ready evidence**: For each signer and each agreement version, record: the signer’s identity, agreement type and version, date and time, acknowledgement text, signing method (electronic signature), and the reason for re-signing (agreement update and/or role change). Keep these records according to the organisation’s retention policy.

###### WHO
- **Information Security (control owner)**: Owns the agreement content, review schedule, and acknowledgement text.
- **Legal/HR (as applicable)**: Approves NDA/acceptable use/rules of behaviour/conflict-of-interest content.
- **IAM/Identity Engineering**: Implements the identity and access workflow controls (signing before access), role-change triggers, and audit logging.
- **Application Owner**: Ensures the correct mapping between application roles and the required agreements.

###### Acceptance criteria
- For the target application/system, every user who is given access has a recorded electronic signature for the correct agreement type and version **before** access is granted.
- Agreement versions are reviewed and updated at least every **90 days**, and there is evidence for each review outcome.
- When an agreement is updated, affected users cannot keep access to the mapped roles until they re-sign the updated version; evidence shows the re-sign event and the reason.
- When a user’s role changes to a different mapped agreement set, access to the new role is blocked until re-signing is completed; evidence shows the re-sign event and the reason.
- Audit logs provide version-controlled, unchangeable evidence of signing and re-signing for auditors.

###### Actions Required for Compliance

- [ ] Define the agreement templates (non-disclosure agreement (NDA), acceptable use, rules of behaviour, and conflict-of-interest) and the required acknowledgement wording.
- [ ] For each application or system role, match the required agreement type(s) and the agreement version identifier.
- [ ] Set up the organisation’s identity provider and identity and access management (IdP/IAM) process so that user account provisioning is paused until the user electronically signs the correct version of the agreement.
- [ ] Set up an automated 90-day review process for each agreement, then publish updated versions with new effective dates.
- [ ] Set up re-sign enforcement so that when an agreement is updated, the affected users’ access expires until they re-sign the updated version.
- [ ] Link human resources information system (HRIS) and identity and access management (IAM) role-change events to trigger a requirement for the user to re-sign when the set of agreements mapped to that user changes.
- [ ] Turn on audit logging and set retention for signing and re-signing events, including the signer’s identity, the agreement version, the date and time, and the reason.

#### PS-6.2 — Classified Information Requiring Special Protection (Enhancement)

This requirement is about making sure only the right people can access the most sensitive, specially protected information. Without these checks, confidential material could be seen by someone who does not genuinely need it, by someone who has not been properly assessed, or by someone who has not clearly agreed to keep it secret—putting people, day-to-day operations, and national interests at serious risk.

To meet this expectation, the organisation must first define which information it treats as specially protected. It must then allow access only when all three of the following conditions are met:
- the person has valid access authorisation that is justified by their official duties
- the person has completed the required personnel security checks set out in the relevant rules and policies
- the person has read, understood, and signed a non-disclosure agreement before access is granted

The organisation should also keep clear records showing that each of these conditions has been satisfied for every person given access.

##### Enforce an access triad gate for collateral/SAP/SCI data

- Category: Software
- Priority: Critical

###### WHAT
Set up an access “triad gate” so that access to classified information that needs special protection (collateral, SAP, SCI) is granted to a requesting person **only if all** of the following are true:
1) They have **valid access authorisation** that is **supported by their official job duties**.
2) They meet the required **personnel security checks**.
3) They have **signed a non-disclosure agreement (NDA)** (with proof).

###### WHY (control requirement)
PS-6(2) enhancement requires checking that access to classified information needing special protection is given only to people who meet all the stated conditions. This approach makes sure the organisation applies the three requirements together (not separately) and creates evidence suitable for audit.

###### HOW (specific steps/approach)
1. **Define special-protection entitlements and mapping**: Create access entitlements for each category (collateral/SAP/SCI or the organisation’s equivalent terms) and link them to the application’s protected items (for example: document libraries, database structures, application programming interface (API) access permissions, message topics, and user interface routes).
2. **Use identity details for the triad**: Extend or use identity information (from human resources (HR) and security systems, and the NDA process) such as:
   - `duty_justification_reference` (or equivalent)
   - `access_authorisation_status` (valid/expired)
   - `personnel_security_status` (meets criteria/approved)
   - `nda_signed_timestamp` and `nda_document_id` (or a verifiable reference)
3. **Create a central authorisation gate**: Configure the application’s access-control layer (or the upstream identity provider (IdP) / authorisation service) to allow access **only when all** triad details are valid at the same time. Deny access by default.
4. **Capture proof and keep audit records**: For every access approval, store unchangeable proof references (or checksums) showing duty justification, the personnel security decision, and completion of the NDA. Send the records to the organisation’s security information and event management (SIEM) system for retention and tamper-evidence.
5. **Automate re-checking and removal of access**: Set entitlement and session end times, and perform periodic re-checks based on clearance and NDA validity. Automatically remove entitlements and revoke access if any triad requirement becomes invalid.

###### WHO (role responsible)
- **Application Owner / IAM Lead**: define entitlements, map them to resources, and implement the triad enforcement logic.
- **Security/Personnel Security Officer**: confirm where personnel security criteria come from and what “approved” means.
- **HR/Security Clearance Administrator**: ensure clearance status updates are accurate and timely.
- **Legal/Contracting (NDA Owner)**: ensure the NDA process produces verifiable completion references.
- **Platform/Cloud Engineer**: implement the integrations, logging, and automated revalidation.

###### Acceptance criteria
- For any request to a collateral/SAP/SCI-protected resource, access is **denied** unless the system verifies (a) valid duty-justified access authorisation, (b) approved personnel security criteria, and (c) signed NDA evidence.
- Audit records show, for each access approval, verifiable evidence references for all three requirements.
- If clearance or NDA status becomes invalid/expired, access is automatically revoked within the configured revalidation time window.
- A test user missing any one requirement (for example, NDA not signed) cannot get access even if the other requirements are valid.

###### Actions Required for Compliance

- [ ] Define the collateral, SAP, and SCI access permissions (entitlements) and link each entitlement to the specific application resources it must protect.
- [ ] Integrate identity details to support duty-based authorisation status, personnel security approval status, and evidence that a non-disclosure agreement (NDA) has been signed.
- [ ] Set the application and authorisation check to block access unless all three “triad” attributes are valid at the same time.
- [ ] Set up tamper-proof audit logs that record the evidence used to justify access, support personnel security decisions, and confirm completion of the non-disclosure agreement (NDA) for each access grant.
- [ ] Set up automatic checks to re-validate and remove access when any required condition in the triad expires or is no longer valid.
- [ ] Run tests that check both allowed and blocked access to confirm that if any one required prerequisite is missing, access is prevented.

#### PS-6.3 — Post-employment Requirements (Enhancement)

This expectation is about making sure that, before anyone is given access, they are clearly told what they must do to protect the organisation’s information even after they leave, and that they formally agree to it. Without this, a departing employee or contractor could later claim they did not know about their ongoing responsibilities, and the organisation may find it harder to enforce rules on confidentiality, returning or destroying information, and other legally binding duties—putting sensitive information at risk.

To meet this expectation, the organisation must:
- keep an up-to-date list of the legally binding post-employment requirements that apply to different roles,
- decide which individuals are covered, and
- notify each person of the specific requirements before their first access is granted.

Where a written acknowledgement is required, the organisation must obtain the person’s signed confirmation before access is provided, and keep the signed record on file. The organisation should also involve its legal team when deciding which post-employment requirements apply.

##### IAM access gating for post-employment obligations with signed acknowledgement

- Category: Software
- Priority: High

###### WHAT
Set up an identity-and-access process that, **before any first access** to covered organisational information is allowed, shows the person the **post-employment requirements that are legally binding** and—where needed—collects a **signed acknowledgement**. Keep audit records that clearly link the notification and acknowledgement to the access approval.

###### WHY (control requirement)
PS-6(3) (enhancement) expects organisations to **consult the Office of the General Counsel** about **post-employment requirements** for people whose employment has ended. The practical result is that post-employment obligations are confirmed with legal input, communicated before access is granted, and formally acknowledged where required.

###### HOW (specific steps/approach)
1. **Legal determination and maintaining a requirements catalogue (with OGC input):** Create and keep a version-controlled catalogue of post-employment requirements by job role and contract type, and (where relevant) by access category. Make sure the Office of the General Counsel (OGC) is consulted on what is legally binding and on the wording to use.
2. **Link roles to the right requirements:** Configure access and approval rules so each access role or group points to the correct catalogue entries, including whether a signed acknowledgement is required (or not applicable).
3. **Pre-access notification pack:** When an access request starts, automatically generate and send a notification that includes the specific post-employment obligations for that person, including the catalogue version and the date those requirements take effect.
4. **Signed acknowledgement (when required):** Require the requester to complete an electronic acknowledgement through the organisation’s identity provider (identity-verified), including a timestamp and the identity of the person signing. Store the signed record in a way that cannot be altered.
5. **Access gating:** Put a firm stop in the workflow: **initial access cannot be approved or completed** until the acknowledgement record exists (or until an approved, documented decision of “not applicable” is recorded).
6. **Audit evidence linkage:** In the access request and approval record, store references to (a) proof that the notification was delivered/received and (b) the signed acknowledgement record. This allows auditors to confirm that the notification happened **before** access, and the signed acknowledgement happened **before** access.

###### WHO (role responsible)
- **Office of the General Counsel (OGC):** confirms which obligations are legally binding and approves the wording.
- **Information Security / Compliance:** owns how the catalogue is governed, how the mapping rules work, and what audit evidence is required.
- **Identity and Access Management (IAM) / Identity Engineering:** builds the workflow stop, captures the electronic acknowledgement, and links the evidence.
- **Human Resources (HR) / Procurement (as applicable):** ensures employees and contractors are classified correctly and that the correct contract types feed into the mapping.

###### Acceptance criteria
- For a sample of new hires/contractors across the mapped roles, the system shows: (1) the notification was delivered with the correct catalogue version **before** access is granted, and (2) the signed acknowledgement was captured **before** access is granted where required.
- The catalogue is version-controlled, reviewed and approved by OGC, and referenced in both the notification and acknowledgement records.
- Access requests cannot be completed without the required acknowledgement (or a recorded, approved reason for “not applicable”).
- Audit reports can be produced that link each access approval to the corresponding notification and signed acknowledgement evidence.

###### Actions Required for Compliance

- [ ] Create an Office of Government Commerce (OGC)-reviewed, version-controlled list of post-employment requirements, organised by job role, contract type, and access category.
- [ ] Link identity and access management (IAM) roles and groups to the catalogue entries, and state whether a signed acknowledgement is required or not applicable.
- [ ] Set up the access request process so that, before anyone is given initial access, a customised notification pack is created and sent out.

### PS-7 — External Personnel Security (Control)

This requirement is about making sure that people working for outside organisations are properly checked before they are given access, and that their access to your sites and systems is removed quickly when they change jobs or leave. Without clear rules, an external worker could keep using an organisation badge or access rights after they have moved on, or the wrong people could be given access in the first place. This increases the risk of unauthorised access, fraud, or misuse of data.

To meet this expectation, the organisation must:
- clearly define and document personnel security responsibilities for external providers;
- require those providers to follow the organisation’s own personnel security procedures; and
- include these expectations in relevant contracts and procurement documents.

The organisation must also require external providers to notify the Facility Security Officer and the Information System Security Manager within 24 hours of any transfer or termination of external personnel who hold organisation-issued badges or credentials and/or have system privileges.

Finally, the organisation must actively monitor and check that external providers are complying with these personnel security requirements.

- Priority: Critical

3) notify the **Facility Security Officer** and the **Information System Security Manager** of any reportable external personnel transfers or terminations **within 24 hours**.

###### WHY (control requirement)
PS-7 requires the organisation to include external provider personnel security requirements in procurement documents, set expectations for reporting roles, transfers, and terminations, and ensure notifications of external personnel changes are made so that credentials and system privileges are promptly withdrawn when appropriate. - **who must be notified**: Facility Security Officer and Information System Security Manager  
- **time period**: within 24 hours

### PS-8 — Personnel Sanctions (Control)

This requirement ensures that serious failures to follow your organisation’s rules for handling information and protecting people’s privacy are dealt with through a clear, formal disciplinary process. Once the formal process begins, it must notify the Office of the General Counsel, the Chief Information Security Officer, and the HR Director within 24 hours. The notification must name the person involved and state the specific reason for the sanction.

### PS-9 — Position Descriptions (Control)
