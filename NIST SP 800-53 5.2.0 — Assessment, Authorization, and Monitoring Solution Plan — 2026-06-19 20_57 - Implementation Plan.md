### CA-1 — Policy and Procedures (Control)

This requirement is about making sure your organisation has clear, up-to-date rules for deciding whether systems and business processes are suitable to run, granting permission for their use, and monitoring them to spot problems early. Without this, teams may use different approaches, miss legal or regulatory duties, and fail to notice when something changes—raising the risk of data loss, service disruption, or security breaches that could have been prevented.

To meet this expectation, the organisation must create, document, and share an organisation-wide policy for assessment, authorisation, and monitoring, along with supporting procedures. This must be managed by the Security Governance Lead and shared with the Chief Information Security Officer (CISO), the Security Governance Lead, the Privacy Officer, system security officers, and the relevant workflow owners.

The policy must set out its purpose, what it covers, who is responsible for what, leadership commitment, how different parts of the organisation work together, and how compliance is checked. It must also align with relevant laws and internal standards.

The policy must be reviewed at least once a year and after major audit findings, incidents, or breaches, relevant legal or regulatory changes, or major changes to systems or operational technology. The supporting procedures must be reviewed at least once a year and after changes to workflows, tools, or monitoring, changes to incident response or account reviews, or significant organisational restructuring.

### CA-2 — Control Assessments (Control)

This requirement is about making sure your organisation regularly checks that the protections safeguarding company systems and people’s personal data are actually in place and working as intended. Without these checks, weaknesses could go unnoticed, leaving you exposed to data loss, privacy breaches, fraud, or service disruption, and decisions about risk could be based on outdated information.

To meet this expectation, the organisation must appoint the right person or team to carry out each assessment. Before any checking begins, it must also create a written assessment plan that clearly sets out what will be reviewed, how effectiveness will be judged, where the work will take place, who will do what, and who is responsible for each part. This plan must be reviewed and approved first by the authorised official (or their designated representative).

The organisation must then assess both security and privacy protections every 90 days, produce a written report of the results, and share those results with the authorised official, the designated representative, the senior information security officer, and the system security and privacy leads.

#### CA-2.1 — Independent Assessors (Enhancement)

This requirement is about making sure your checks are carried out by people who are genuinely independent, so the results can be trusted. Without independent assessors, the organisation risks “marking its own homework”, overlooking important gaps, or being influenced by people who built, run, or benefit from the system. This could lead to avoidable failures and harm to the organisation, its assets, or the people affected by its services.

To meet this expectation, the organisation must appoint independent assessors (or an independent team) to review how well its arrangements are working. These assessors must have no real or perceived conflicts of interest, must not review their own work, must not act as managers or employees for the system, and must not advocate for the organisation commissioning the work.

The organisation must decide how independent the assessors need to be based on the system’s risk and classification. It must also ensure that any contracted assessors are similarly independent. Where independence is limited by the organisation’s structure, independent experts must validate the results for completeness, accuracy, trustworthiness, and reliability before the organisation uses them for key authorisation decisions.

#### CA-2.2 — Specialized Assessments (Enhancement)

This requirement is about making sure the organisation regularly and unexpectedly tests its defences to find weaknesses before criminals or careless insiders do. Without these quarterly, unannounced, in-depth checks, issues such as software weaknesses that could be exploited, unusual or suspicious system behaviour, or misuse by people who have legitimate access could go unnoticed for too long. This could lead to data loss, service disruption, or safety impacts.

Each quarter, the organisation must plan, approve, and record these assessments. They must use detailed monitoring supported by appropriate monitoring tools, run tests that attempt to misuse systems, assess insider risk, and scan for known weaknesses. The organisation must also carry out any additional checks it decides are needed, such as operational technology (OT) process safety and network segmentation validation.

The organisation must keep evidence of the work, produce assessment results reports, and make sure the assessments align with its system and privacy plans and with relevant laws and policies. Any vulnerabilities found must be promptly passed into the organisation’s fix-and-track process.

#### CA-2.3 — Leveraging Results from External Organizations (Enhancement)

This requirement is about using independent third-party assessment results properly, rather than re-checking everything from the beginning. It matters because, without a careful approach, an organisation could rely on testing that is incomplete, biased, or not relevant to the systems in scope. This could lead the organisation to believe its on-premises mixed computer and operational technology environment is safer and compliant than it really is, creating avoidable risk to day-to-day operations, safety, and its standing with regulators.

To meet this expectation, the organisation must keep a list of accredited third-party assessment organisations and approved testing laboratories that have relevant experience, and it must use only their results for the on-premises systems within the organisation’s assessed boundary.

Before using any results, the organisation must:
- confirm the assessor was independent
- obtain and store the written evidence that supports the results
- check that the testing covered the same system scope and interfaces
- ensure the assessment met all applicable Department of Defense (DoD) and Cybersecurity Maturity Model Certification (CMMC) requirements

The organisation must also record its decision and the source details, update its assessment reporting and system plans (and privacy plans where relevant), and document any gaps in a plan of action and milestones showing what still needs to be done.

### CA-3 — Information Exchange (Control)

This requirement ensures that every time your organisation shares information with another system, there is a clear, written agreement that explains exactly how the information will be shared and how it will be protected. Without this, sensitive information could be sent to the wrong place, handled incorrectly, or exposed through privacy breaches because different systems and suppliers may follow different rules.

To meet this expectation, the organisation must approve and manage each information exchange using written information exchange security agreements. For each agreement, it must document:
- how the systems connect and exchange data  
- what security protections and privacy handling are required  
- what practical safeguards will be used  
- who is responsible on each side  

The organisation must also record the impact level of the information being shared, so the seriousness of any problem is understood. Finally, it must review and update these agreements at least every 90 days to keep them accurate as risks and services change.

#### CA-3.6 — Transfer Authorizations (Enhancement)

This requirement is about ensuring that only approved people and approved partner systems can send information into your protected systems, and that the receiving system checks this before it accepts anything. Without this, an unauthorised sender could send data they are not allowed to write, which could corrupt records, change settings, or help them gain access by using trusted connections. This includes routine “infrastructure” messages such as routing and name lookups, as well as service messages such as mail relays.

To meet this expectation, the organisation must set clear rules for who is allowed to send what to each part of the system, and what level of permission is required. The receiving system must then independently verify the sender’s authorisation using a reliable method rather than trusting the sender’s claims. The same checks must be applied to both normal data and control and service traffic. The organisation must document the connection and protection approach, reflect these expectations in agreements with partners, configure the systems accordingly, and keep audit records showing whether each incoming attempt was accepted or rejected based on authorisation.

#### CA-3.7 — Transitive Information Exchanges (Enhancement)

This requirement is about ensuring you can identify and control the “hidden routes” where information you send to an external service can pass on to other systems. You must also stop that flow if you cannot show that the connected systems are secure and being handled properly.

Without this, your organisation could unknowingly take on risk from partners or downstream systems. Sensitive information could be exposed, misused, or mishandled because protections are missing, out of date, or not working as expected.

To meet this expectation, the organisation must:
- identify and record which downstream systems receive information from, or pass information through, the external systems it already uses; and
- record what information is shared and the route it takes.

The organisation must then keep these downstream information exchanges running only while it can verify that the downstream protections are in place and up to date. If evidence is missing, out of date, or cannot be verified, the organisation must take practical steps to stop the exchanges. There must be clear responsibility for monitoring, stopping, and only restarting once verification has been restored and approved.

### CA-5 — Plan of Action and Milestones (Control)

This requirement is about making sure the organisation has a clear, written recovery plan when problems are found, including when each fix will be carried out. Without this, weaknesses and known security gaps could remain, allowing the same mistakes to happen again, leaving incidents unresolved, and causing avoidable harm to people, services, or finances.

To meet this expectation, the organisation must create and keep a documented action plan for the system. This plan must set out what remedial work is planned to address any weaknesses or deficiencies found during reviews, and how it will reduce or remove known vulnerabilities. The plan must also state who is responsible for delivering and maintaining it, include specific checkpoints and target dates for completion, and be reviewed and updated at least every quarter.

Each quarterly update must reflect new information from control checks, independent audits or reviews, and ongoing monitoring. The organisation must also keep records showing that the updates were made and what findings led to the changes.
