### at-1 — Policy and Procedures (Control)

This requirement is about making sure everyone in the organisation understands what is expected of them to protect the organisation’s information and personal data. This should be done through clear written guidance and regular updates. Without an agreed policy and practical procedures, people may not know their responsibilities. Training could vary between teams and suppliers, and important changes in law or lessons learned from incidents might be missed—raising the risk of data loss, privacy breaches, and failure to comply with requirements.

The organisation must create and document an organisation-wide awareness and training policy. This policy must explain its purpose, what it applies to, who is responsible for what, the commitment of senior leadership, how different parts of the organisation work together, and how compliance will be achieved. It must also be consistent with relevant laws and the organisation’s own rules.

The Chief Information Security Officer, together with the Security Awareness Programme Manager and the Privacy Officer, must manage the production, documentation, and distribution of the policy and the supporting procedures.

The policy must be reviewed at least every year, and also after security incidents, audit findings, or relevant changes to laws or policies. The procedures must be reviewed at least every year, and also after major organisational or system changes, and after incidents or audit findings.

The policy must be shared with all employees, contractors, and third parties who have access to the organisation’s systems and data. The procedures must be shared with all people with security or privacy responsibilities, including system owners, operational technology (OT) operators, and data stewards.

##### CISO-owned awareness & training policy with versioned procedures

- Category: Manual
- Priority: Critical

###### WHAT
Create and document an organisation-level **security and privacy awareness and training policy** and **supporting awareness and training procedures**. Share them with the defined audiences and keep them up to date.

###### WHY (control requirement)
AT-1 requires that awareness and training **policy and procedures** exist, are **documented**, and are **kept up to date**. The guidance also makes clear that simply repeating existing controls is not enough. The policy and procedures must explain how the organisation’s expectations for awareness and training are put into practice, how they align with the organisation’s risk management strategy, and how security and privacy activities are coordinated.

###### HOW (specific steps/approach)
1. **Create one authoritative organisation-level policy** in a controlled document repository. The **Chief Information Security Officer (CISO)** must own it. The policy must include required content that reflects how the security and privacy programmes work together (for example: purpose, scope, roles and responsibilities, leadership commitment, coordination approach, and a compliance approach consistent with relevant laws and standards).
2. **Create supporting procedures** that explain how the policy is carried out (for example: how training is planned, delivered, monitored, and recorded). Ensure procedures cover the **people and roles with security or privacy responsibilities**, including **system owners, operational technology (OT) operators, and data stewards**.
3. **Define how information is shared**:
   - Publish the **policy** to **all employees, contractors, and third parties who use organisation systems and data**.
   - Publish the **procedures** to **all personnel with security or privacy responsibilities** (as listed above).
4. **Set review and update triggers exactly as the organisation defines**:
   - Policy: review and update **annually**, and also after security incidents or breaches, assessment or audit findings, or changes to relevant laws, executive orders, directives, regulations, policies, standards, or guidelines.
   - Procedures: review and update **annually**, and also after any major organisational or system change. Also update after incidents or breaches, audit findings, or changes to relevant requirements.
5. **Keep audit-ready records** by using controlled document versions (for example, document management with an approval workflow) and keeping records of when documents were published and what review outcomes were.

###### WHO (role responsible)
- **CISO**: responsible for managing the development, documentation, and distribution of the awareness and training policy and procedures.
- **Security Awareness Programme Manager** and **Privacy Officer**: jointly develop and coordinate the security and privacy content, and ensure it aligns with the organisation’s risk management strategy.

###### Acceptance criteria
- An organisation-level **Awareness & Training Policy** exists, is approved by the CISO (with input from the security and privacy programmes), and is available to **all employees, contractors, and third parties who have access**.
- Awareness & Training **Procedures** exist, are approved and available to **all personnel with security or privacy responsibilities** (including system owners, OT operators, and data stewards).
- The policy and procedures include defined **review and update schedules** and **event triggers** that match the organisation’s defined parameters.
- Evidence exists that documents are published under controlled processes (version history and approvals) and that at least the most recent scheduled review status is recorded (or the initial publication date if newly implemented).

###### Actions Required for Compliance

- [ ] Create an organisation-wide awareness and training policy in a controlled document repository. The policy should be owned by the Chief Information Security Officer (CISO) and co-authored with the Security Awareness Programme Manager and the Privacy Officer.
- [ ] Define and document the policy’s scope and purpose, the roles and responsibilities of each party, how the security and privacy programmes will work together, and how the policy aligns with all applicable requirements for compliance.
- [ ] Create awareness and training procedures that explain how the policy is put into practice for people responsible for security and privacy (including system owners, operational technology (OT) operators, and data stewards).
- [ ] Share the policy with all employees, contractors, and third parties who have access to the organisation’s systems and data. Share the procedures with the specific security and privacy responsibility roles that have been assigned responsibility.
- [ ] Set review and update schedules for:

- **The policy**: at least **every year**; and also **after incidents or data breaches**, **after assessment or audit findings**, and **when legal, policy, or standards requirements change**.

### at-2 — Literacy Training and Awareness (Control)

This requirement is about making sure everyone who uses the organisation’s systems understands, in plain terms, how to protect information and people’s privacy, and what to do if something looks wrong. Without regular, practical reminders and training, staff may fall for common scams, share access by mistake, mishandle sensitive information, or miss early warning signs of a security or privacy incident—leading to data breaches, legal issues, financial loss, and harm to individuals.

The organisation must provide security and privacy training when people first start using the systems. After that, it must repeat security training every 180 days and privacy training every 180 days. It must also provide extra training after major system changes, new threats, audit findings, security incidents or breaches, or changes to relevant laws and policies.

Extra training is specifically required after events such as phishing, malware, sharing login credentials, repeated failed login attempts, suspected data leaving the organisation, mistakes in how privacy is handled, signs of health or data-protection breaches, or access to records that should not be accessed.

The organisation should reinforce learning using posters, messages at login, emails, awareness events, and reminder materials. It must update the training content quarterly and after major triggers, and include lessons learned from real incidents.

##### Automated security & privacy literacy training with trigger-based refresh

- Category: Software
- Priority: Critical

###### WHAT
Set up an identity-linked security and privacy learning programme for **all users of the system** (including managers, senior executives, and contractors) to ensure they understand what they must do to protect information:

- **Initial training** when they first get authorised access
- **Security training every 180 days** and **privacy training every 180 days**
- **Extra training** when defined security or privacy events happen
- **Updates to the training content at least every 3 months (quarterly)**, and also after defined triggers such as major system changes, new threats, audit findings, incidents/data breaches, and changes to relevant laws, directives, or policies

The programme must also reinforce learning through the agreed awareness methods (posters, messages on the sign-in screen, email updates, awareness events, and reminder materials) and include knowledge checks so the organisation can confirm users have understood.

###### WHY (control requirement)
This requirement is based on **AT-2**, which expects the organisation to provide both basic and advanced security and privacy learning and awareness for system users, including:

- training content covering:
  - security and privacy expectations
  - what users should do
  - how to respond if they suspect an incident
  - operational security expectations
  - how to handle personal data (personally identifiable information)
- awareness methods (for example, sign-in messages and email updates, and posters)
- **repeat training at a minimum frequency required by applicable rules** (here: **every 180 days** for security and privacy)
- updates triggered by defined events (for example, phishing leading to compromise, suspected unauthorised data copying, and mistakes in handling personal data)
- regular content updates (here: **quarterly**) and after major triggers

###### HOW (specific steps/approach)
###### 1) Define training types and who gets them
- Create **security** and **privacy** course tracks (basic vs advanced), matched to:
  - the type of system access each user has
  - their job role (including privileged users and contractors)
- Ensure course content clearly covers:
  - security and privacy expectations
  - what users must do to protect security and personal privacy
  - how to respond if they suspect an incident
  - operational security expectations
  - handling personal data (personally identifiable information)

###### 2) Automate enrolment and enforcement using the organisation’s Identity Provider and Learning System
- Connect the organisation’s **Identity Provider (IdP)** to the **Learning Management System (LMS)** so that:
  - users receive **initial training** when they first gain authorised access to the application/system
  - users are automatically enrolled for **security training every 180 days** and **privacy training every 180 days**
  - users are automatically enrolled for **extra security training** when security events occur (phishing compromise, malware infection, sharing credentials, not following policy, repeated failed sign-in attempts)
  - users are automatically enrolled for **extra privacy training** when privacy events occur (suspected unauthorised data copying, personal data handling errors, indicators of a breach involving HIPAA/GDPR, access to records they are not authorised to view)
- Where appropriate, use IdP group membership (for example, application access groups and remote-working cohorts) to tailor what users receive.

###### 3) Add knowledge checks and track results
- Include an assessment after each course with:
  - a defined pass mark
  - retention of results for each user for each training instance, so the organisation can show evidence during audits
- For advanced courses, include questions based on realistic scenarios related to suspected-incident response and personal data handling.

###### 4) Update content quarterly and when triggers occur
- Put in place a content approval and update process to ensure training materials are updated:
  - at least **quarterly**
  - and also when triggers occur (major system changes, new threats, audit findings, security incidents/data breaches, and changes to relevant laws, directives, or policies)
- Track the version of training content and record which version was delivered to which users.

###### 5) Run awareness activities continuously
- Set up ongoing awareness using the agreed methods:
  - **sign-in screen messages** (IdP sign-in banner)
  - **email updates** from security and privacy officials (targeted by role)
  - **posters** and **reminder materials** (digital signage/print packs)
  - **awareness events** (scheduled sessions/tabletop exercises for advanced users)

###### WHO
- **Security Awareness Programme Manager** (programme owner; ensures content coverage, assessment design, and governance)
- **Privacy Officer / Data Protection Lead** (privacy curriculum and event triggers)
- **Identity and Access Management (IAM) Engineer / IdP Administrator** (automation of enrolment/enforcement and sign-in messaging)
- **LMS Administrator** (course setup, assessment settings, reporting)
- **Security Operations Centre (SOC) / Security Operations (SecOps) and Incident Response Lead** (provides incident/event triggers into the training automation)

###### Acceptance criteria
- Evidence exists that **all authorised users** of the application/system receive **initial security and privacy training** before or at their first access.
- Evidence exists that security training is completed at least **every 180 days** and privacy training at least **every 180 days** for the user population.
- Evidence exists that when any defined security/privacy event occurs, affected users are enrolled for **additional** relevant training within an agreed operational timeframe.
- Evidence exists that training content is updated **quarterly** and after each defined trigger; delivered content versions can be traced.
- Evidence exists of **knowledge checks** with recorded results and defined pass criteria.
- Awareness activities are clearly active (for example, current sign-in banner text, recent email updates, and scheduled awareness events).

###### Actions Required for Compliance

- [ ] Set up security and privacy training programmes (basic and advanced) linked to application access categories and job roles, including remote-working groups where relevant.
- [ ] Set up learning management system (LMS) courses to include post-training assessments, define the pass/fail criteria, and keep the assessment results for audit purposes for each user for each training instance.
- [ ] Set up identity provider (IdP) to learning management system (LMS) automation to automatically enrol users in their initial training the first time they are authorised to access the system, and to schedule recurring security and privacy training every 180 days.
- [ ] Set up automated re-training workflows that start when the defined security and privacy events occur, using the security operations centre (SOC) and security operations (SecOps) teams and privacy incident signals.
- [ ] Set up a quarterly review and update of content, including mandatory updates after major system changes, new threats, audit findings, incidents or breaches, and any legal or policy changes.
- [ ] Use awareness techniques, including: messages shown on the current logon screen, emails tailored to specific roles, and planned awareness events supported by communications.

#### at-2.1 — Practical Exercises (Enhancement)

This expectation is about making staff training feel real by running practical literacy and security-awareness exercises that copy what attackers try to do. Without realistic, hands-on practice, people may only remember guidance in theory and then fail when under pressure—for example, by sharing sensitive information, trying to access something they should not, or being tricked by messages that lead them to open harmful email attachments or click harmful links.

To meet this expectation, the organisation must provide practical exercises for the relevant people and make sure each exercise is based on a realistic scenario that looks like a real incident. At least one exercise must be run without warning and must include a safe, training-only attempt to influence participants to collect information and to try to gain unauthorised access, without affecting any real systems.

The organisation must also include separate scenarios that simulate the harmful impact of opening malicious attachments and clicking malicious links, with a targeted delivery style for at least one of them. The organisation must document how it will do this, keep the training materials and records, and name specific people responsible for delivering and running the exercises.

##### No-notice literacy exercises with safe phishing and link/attachment sims

- Category: Software
- Priority: Critical

###### WHAT
Deliver practical literacy training exercises that mimic realistic events and incidents, including:
- At least one **unannounced social engineering** attempt that tries to **collect information** and to **gain unauthorised access** using **training-only** methods.
- Separate scenarios that show the **harm caused by opening malicious email attachments** and **clicking malicious web links**, with at least one scenario delivered in a **targeted spear-phishing style**.

###### WHY (control requirement)
The National Institute of Standards and Technology (NIST) **AT-2(1) Practical Exercises (Enhancement)** requires practical exercises in literacy training that **simulate events and incidents**. The guidance says practical exercises must include **unannounced social engineering** attempts to collect information and try unauthorised access, and must also simulate the **harm** caused by opening malicious attachments and using malicious web links (including spear-phishing delivery for at least one scenario).

###### HOW (specific approach)
Set up a repeatable “training lane” using **training-only** systems and measurable results:
1. **Define exercise audiences and roles**: create a training matrix for relevant groups (for example, all staff, contractors, privileged users, helpdesk, finance, and OT/vendor contacts if applicable). Assign named responsibilities for designing, running, and overseeing information security.
2. **Create training-only targets**: set up training-only accounts/roles and isolated resources (for example, a training-only web page, a restricted training-only document library, and a training landing page that explains the correct response). Make sure these resources cannot access production data or carry out real actions.
3. **Run at least one unannounced campaign per cycle**: deliver a scenario that attempts both:
   - **Information collection** (for example, a form requesting non-production “details” such as a training-only code), and
   - **Unauthorised access** (for example, a link that takes the participant to a training-only page simulating access to a restricted resource, and records whether they try to access it).
   The campaign must be **unannounced** to participants.
4. **Simulate the impact of malicious attachments**: send a realistic email with a training attachment that, when “opened”, takes the participant to a training landing page explaining the harm and the correct reporting/response steps. Record whether participants opened/attempted to open and whether they reported.
5. **Simulate the impact of malicious links (spear-phishing style)**: deliver at least one targeted email to a defined group using realistic personalisation (role/team context) and a malicious link that takes the participant to a training landing page. Record link-click attempts and reporting behaviour.
6. **Safety and evidence**: ensure the simulation does not bypass real security controls or affect real systems. Instead, measure user behaviour (reporting, clicking/opening attempts, and interaction with training-only resources). Keep audit-ready records: the reason for each scenario, delivery dates, audience lists, and outcome metrics.

###### WHO
- **Information Security Awareness Lead**: owns the exercise programme, scenario design, and the evidence pack.
- **Security Operations / SOC (or delegated security team)**: checks that the simulation stays within safe boundaries and that reporting and monitoring data are captured.
- **IT/Identity and Email Platform Owner**: builds the training-only delivery methods (email/link/attachment simulations) and ensures separation from production.
- **Business/Human Resources (HR) Training Owner (where applicable)**: confirms participant groups and supports communications for coaching after the exercise (without undermining the requirement for no advance notice).

###### Acceptance criteria
- At least **one unannounced** social engineering exercise is carried out in the defined cycle and includes both **information collection** and **unauthorised access attempts**, with all activity limited to **training-only** resources.
- At least one exercise simulates the **harm from malicious attachments**, and at least one simulates the **harm from malicious links**.
- At least one link scenario is delivered in a **targeted spear-phishing style** to a defined subgroup.
- For each scenario, the organisation keeps evidence of: the audience, delivery method, scenario type (unannounced social engineering / attachment / link), and measurable outcomes (for example, click/open/report rates).
- No simulation results in access to production systems/data or carries out any real unauthorised actions.

###### Actions Required for Compliance

- [ ] Define participant groups and assign named responsibilities for exercise design, delivery, and oversight of information security
- [ ] Set up training-only user accounts and roles, and use separate web or document targets that cannot access production data.
- [ ] Plan and carry out at least one surprise (no-notice) social engineering test that tries to gather information and gain unauthorised access.
- [ ] Set up email, landing page, and attachment simulations so that any clicks or downloads go to training-only landing pages, and so that the results are recorded.
- [ ] Create separate test scenarios for the impact of a malicious attachment and for the impact of a malicious link. Include at least one targeted spear-phishing style campaign.
- [ ] For each exercise, collect and keep evidence that is ready for audit (who attended, scenario details, dates, and results). Use the results to provide targeted coaching.

#### at-2.2 — Insider Threat (Enhancement)

This requirement is about making sure everyone in the organisation understands what warning signs of harmful insider behaviour can look like and how to report a concern early. Without this, a trusted employee or contractor could misuse their access or cause harm—for example, by showing long-term dissatisfaction, trying to access information they do not need for their role, having unexplained financial problems, bullying or harassing colleagues, threatening or carrying out violence in the workplace, or breaking serious workplace rules and policies.

To meet this expectation, the organisation must provide training that fits each person’s role to all relevant staff. This training should help them recognise these warning signs and explain clearly how and where to report them using the organisation’s established reporting routes. Staff should follow the organisation’s internal procedures and not try to investigate concerns themselves.

The organisation should also clearly assign responsibility for keeping the training up to date, maintaining the training materials and programme, and keeping records to show that the required people completed the training.

##### Role-based insider threat literacy training with reporting routes

- Category: Manual
- Priority: Critical

###### WHAT
Provide training, matched to each role, for all relevant people so they can spot possible insider threat warning signs and report concerns quickly through the organisation’s agreed reporting routes—without trying to investigate themselves.

###### WHY (control requirement)
The National Institute of Standards and Technology (NIST) Special Publication (SP) 800-53 **AT-2(2)** enhancement requires the organisation to **provide awareness training** on how to recognise and report possible insider threat warning signs. The guidance includes example warning signs, such as: long-term job dissatisfaction; attempts to access information that is not needed for the person’s job; unexplained access to financial resources; bullying or harassment; workplace violence; and serious breaches of policies, procedures, or other rules. It also requires training to include **how to report concerns** using **established reporting channels**, in line with the organisation’s policies and procedures.

###### HOW (specific steps/approach)
1. **Set the training scope and role groups** within the insider threat awareness programme: at minimum **Employees/Contractors** and **Managers/Team Leads** (optionally add a **Security/Privacy Champion** group for people who receive escalations). Link each group to their job responsibilities.
2. **Create training modules on warning signs** using clear, plain language and short examples. Make sure each module covers the guidance examples: long-term job dissatisfaction; attempts to access information that is not needed; unexplained access to financial resources; bullying or harassment; workplace violence; and other serious breaches of policies, procedures, directives, regulations, rules, or practices.
3. **Include reporting instructions in every module**: teach people to **spot warning signs first, report promptly, and not investigate themselves**. Include the exact reporting channels and escalation or backup routes the organisation has set up (for example: line manager/HR/security contact/confidential portal or hotline), aligned to internal procedures.
4. **Deliver training in a way that can be checked during audits** for the organisation’s main systems (for example: completion tracking through a learning management system for office staff; facilitated sessions or toolbox talks for shift-based or on-call roles). Use scenario-based checks to confirm people understand both the warning signs and the correct reporting routes.
5. **Manage and update the training content**: appoint an owner (for example, an HR Security Awareness Lead with input from Security and Legal), use version control, and set review and update dates (for example, after policy changes or lessons learned). Ensure training materials stay aligned with current reporting procedures.
6. **Keep proof of completion**: record completion status, date and time, learner identity, role group, and the training version in the learning management system (LMS) or human resources information system (HRIS), so the organisation can show that required people completed the training.

###### WHO
- **HR Security Awareness Lead / People Operations**: owns the training programme, content, and proof of completion.
- **Information Security / Insider Threat Programme Lead**: ensures the warning sign content is accurate and that reporting/escalation instructions match the programme.
- **Legal/Compliance (if needed)**: confirms the wording for reporting and the boundaries around confidentiality.
- **Line Managers**: reinforce reporting expectations and ensure their teams complete the required training.

###### Acceptance criteria
- All in-scope people (employees and contractors where applicable) complete the correct **role-based** insider threat awareness training.
- The training includes the guidance warning sign examples and teaches people to report concerns through **established organisational channels**.
- The training clearly instructs people **not to investigate themselves**.
- Scenario checks show that learners can choose the correct reporting route for representative warning signs.
- Records exist showing completion, training version, and timestamps for audit purposes.

###### Actions Required for Compliance

- [ ] In the insider threat awareness policy, define which people are included (for example, employees and contractors), and specify the role categories they fall into (for example, managers and team leads, plus any specialist roles).
- [ ] Develop role-based training modules that cover the guidance indicator examples using plain-language scenarios.
- [ ] Publish and include the organisation’s established reporting channels and escalation or backup routes in every training module.
- [ ] Use scenario-based knowledge checks to confirm that learners can spot the warning signs and choose the correct reporting route, without having to investigate the issue themselves.
- [ ] Assign responsibility for the programme and set up a controlled process for updating the curriculum, including when reviews must happen and how changes are tracked using version control.
- [ ] Deliver the training using a method that can be audited (for example, a learning management system (LMS) and/or facilitated sessions), and keep proof of completion that includes the curriculum version and the date and time (timestamps).

#### at-2.3 — Social Engineering and Mining (Enhancement)

This requirement is about making sure everyone in the organisation can recognise and respond to attempts to trick people or to quietly collect information for future attacks. Without regular, practical training, staff may be persuaded to share confidential information, click on harmful messages, or pass on internal information to someone who is pretending to be legitimate. This can lead to fraud, loss of data, or disruption.

To meet this expectation, the organisation must set out a clear training approach and a documented way to deliver it. This should include who must attend, who is responsible for running the training, and how it will be updated and tracked over time. The training must cover both recognising and reporting social tricks (for example, manipulation to get information) and social information-gathering (for example, attempts to collect information by talking to people). It must use plain-language examples and explain exactly how and where staff should report concerns using the organisation’s established reporting channels and procedures.

The organisation should keep evidence that the required people attended the training and that it covered both recognition and reporting.

##### Enterprise “Social Engineering & Social Mining” literacy training

- Category: Manual
- Priority: High

###### WHAT
Provide a documented, role-based literacy training programme that teaches staff to **recognise** and **report** both **social engineering** (for example: phishing, pretexting, impersonation, baiting/“quid pro quo”, thread-jacking, social media exploitation, tailgating) and **social mining** (gathering information about the organisation to support future attacks).

###### WHY (control requirement)
NIST SP 800-53 **AT-2(3)** enhancement requires the organisation to **provide literacy training** on recognising and reporting **potential and actual** instances of social engineering and social mining, including how to raise concerns through **organisational channels** in line with established policies and procedures.

###### HOW (specific steps/approach)
1. **Define training governance and scope**: create and approve a training standard that sets who must be trained (employees, contractors, and partners where applicable), the minimum content (recognition and reporting for both social engineering and social mining), and how often training is refreshed (for example, an initial baseline plus periodic refresh).
2. **Document reporting pathways**: publish clear “report concern” instructions (for example, a security email address, ticket category, reporting form, or security operations centre intake process) and the immediate actions expected (for example, do not click or reply; keep any relevant evidence; report using the named channel), aligned to existing incident and reporting procedures.
3. **Build scenario-based training**: create short, repeatable modules with realistic examples covering both categories (social engineering and social mining), and include “choose the next action” exercises to test correct recognition and correct reporting behaviour.
4. **Deliver by role**: tailor scenarios and emphasis by role (for example, finance/procurement for vendor impersonation; HR for identity and benefits scams; helpdesk/IT for credential reset pretexting; executives for CEO fraud; site access roles for tailgating warning signs).
5. **Track completion and keep evidence**: deliver the training through the organisation’s learning system and onboarding process; record completion status and assessment results as audit evidence, including catch-up for new starters.
6. **Refresh and improve**: update training at the agreed frequency and after relevant incidents or campaign patterns; include anonymised lessons learned from reported attempts.

###### WHO
- **Information Security / Cybersecurity Awareness Lead**: owns the training content, ensures reporting instructions align, and manages the refresh cycle.
- **HR/People Operations (or Learning and Development)**: ensures onboarding and catch-up, and tracks completion.
- **SOC/Security Operations or Incident Response Team**: checks that the reporting process is usable and provides feedback on reported items.
- **Application/Business Owners** (for high-risk roles): support attendance and ensure role-relevant scenarios.

###### Acceptance criteria
- Training content clearly covers **both recognition and reporting** of **social engineering and social mining**.
- Reporting instructions point to the organisation’s **established channels and procedures** and include immediate “what to do” guidance.
- All in-scope staff complete the training within the defined onboarding and refresh timeframes, with **documented evidence** (completion records and scenario/knowledge check results).
- Training is updated at the defined frequency and/or after relevant incidents, with evidence of version control and refresh delivery.

###### Actions Required for Compliance

- [ ] Approve a documented literacy training standard that covers how to recognise and report both social engineering and social mining.
- [ ] Publish and share the exact reporting channels for the organisation, and the immediate actions to take for both potential and confirmed incidents.
- [ ] Create scenario-based training modules that include “choose your next action” checks to test both recognition and reporting.
- [ ] Provide role-based training (including onboarding catch-up) to all people covered by this requirement, using the organisation’s learning management system (LMS) and human resources (HR) process.
- [ ] Record and keep proof that each required training session was completed and record the assessment results for it.
- [ ] Review and update the training materials on the agreed schedule and after relevant incidents or recurring campaign patterns.

#### at-2.4 — Suspicious Communications and Anomalous System Behavior (Enhancement)

This requirement is about making sure everyone in the organisation can recognise warning signs of suspicious messages and unusual computer behaviour, before they accidentally help something harmful spread. Without this, staff may open unexpected email attachments or click strange links, trust messages that pretend to be from a familiar sponsor or contractor but are actually from an unfamiliar sender, or dismiss odd message quality such as poor grammar and unusual wording. They may also miss early warning signs such as repeated failed login attempts or unexpected programmes running on their device, which can allow malicious code to spread.

To meet this expectation, the organisation must create and keep up to date a written training policy, procedures, training plan (curriculum), and learning materials. These must clearly set out the specific warning signs staff should look for and what staff must do when they see them, including how to report suspicious emails or web communications using the organisation’s agreed reporting route. The organisation must assign responsibility for delivering the basic training and for reviewing or approving the training content, include the training in relevant planning documents, keep evidence that staff have received the training, and ensure trained staff can correctly recognise the warning signs and carry out the correct reporting actions.

##### Scenario-based training for suspicious comms and anomalous behaviour

- Category: Manual
- Priority: High

###### WHAT
Deliver role-based literacy training that teaches staff to recognise **suspicious messages** and **unusual system activity**, using the organisation-defined indicators in **{{ insert: param, at-02.04_odp }}** (for example: unexpected email attachments/links, messages that pretend to be from known sponsors but do not match, poor spelling/grammar, repeated failed login attempts, unexpected programmes/processes) and to respond using the organisation’s agreed reporting route.

###### WHY (control requirement)
AT-2(4) Enhancement requires the organisation to provide literacy training on recognising suspicious messages and unusual activity in organisational systems, using **{{ insert: param, at-02.04_odp }}**. The training must help staff understand what counts as suspicious messages and how to respond, so that early recognition can provide warning and support technical detection.

###### HOW (specific steps/approach)
1. **Define training indicators and response actions** in the training materials, clearly linking each indicator from **at-02.04_odp** to:
   - recognition cues (what the learner will notice), and
   - immediate actions (what to do / what not to do), including reporting via the organisation’s named route.
2. **Create two scenario tracks** delivered as short modules (microlearning) with realistic examples:
   - Track A: suspicious messages (unexpected attachments/links, messages pretending to be from known sponsors/contractors, poor spelling/grammar/odd tone/pressure or urgency).
   - Track B: unusual activity (repeated failed login prompts/alerts, unexpected programmes/screens appearing without the user taking action).
3. **Include “response cards”** in each module (for example: do not open/click/reply; close the message/tab; report using the agreed channel) and ensure the reporting route matches the organisation’s incident/reporting process.

- Security: simulated attacks based on common software weaknesses and spear/"whale" phishing aimed at senior leaders/executives.
- Privacy: quizzes on recognising and handling PII, and scenario-based practice for completing PIAs.

###### HOW (specific steps/approach)
1. **Create two repeatable training tracks with role assignments**
   - Track A (Security): assign to software developers and senior leaders/executives.
   - Track B (Privacy): assign to roles responsible for handling personal data and/or supporting PIAs (for example, product owners, data stewards, privacy leads, developers).
2. **Deliver the Security practical activities**
   - **Secure coding workshop for developers (simulated weakness exploitation):** run a guided workshop where participants review a deliberately vulnerable component (for example, injection, broken access control, insecure deserialisation) and create a secure fix (for example, corrected code/configuration plus evidence such as test results or a workshop worksheet).
   - **Executive spear/"whale" phishing simulation:** run targeted simulations using realistic executive work patterns (for example, board pack/invoice approval-style messages). Set up the platform to capture measurable results (for example, whether it was reported, time taken to report, whether links were clicked, and whether the participant followed the correct reporting process).
   - **Mandatory debrief:** after each simulation/workshop, run a short debrief that links what people did to the organisation’s expected response steps (for example, how to report suspected phishing; how to fix the weakness).
3. **Deliver the Privacy practical activities**
   - **PII identification and handling quizzes:** run scenario-based quizzes where participants decide whether the information is PII and select the correct way to process/handle it for each scenario.
   - **PIA scenario practice:** provide a structured PIA case (for example, a new feature, new data sharing, or a new processing purpose) where participants complete the required PIA steps and produce the required outputs (for example, documented processing purpose, data categories, lawful basis/justification, risk assessment, and mitigation actions) using an organisation-approved PIA template.
   - **Automated or rubric-based scoring:** score the quizzes and PIA outputs using a scoring guide (rubric) aligned to the organisation’s privacy requirements.
4. **Track participation and record results for governance evidence**
   - Use the training platform (or an integrated learning record system) to record: participant identity, role, module completion, scores/results, and timestamps.
   - Keep the activity records needed for audit (for example, workshop submission evidence, quiz results, PIA submission records, and confirmation that debriefs were completed).
   - Run periodic reviews to update scenarios based on common failure patterns.

###### WHO (role responsible)
- **Security Training Owner / Cybersecurity Awareness Lead:** owns Track A security scenarios (phishing and secure coding workshops) and the debrief content.
- **Privacy Officer / Data Protection Officer (DPO) or Privacy Programme Lead:** owns Track B privacy quizzes and the PIA scenario templates/rubrics.
- **Learning Management System (LMS) Administrator / Training Platform Owner:** sets up tracking, scoring, and reporting exports.
- **Application Security Lead (for developer workshops):** checks that the workshop scenarios are realistic and that the expected remediation is appropriate.

###### Acceptance criteria
- Security activities include (a) simulated attacks based on common software weaknesses for software developers and (b) spear/"whale" phishing simulations aimed at senior leaders/executives, with recorded measurable results and a debrief.
- Privacy activities include (a) quizzes that test recognising PII and handling it correctly, and (b) scenario-based PIA practice where participants complete the required PIA steps/outputs using an approved template.
- For each activity, the organisation can produce audit-ready evidence showing who completed what, when, and the recorded results/scores (including workshop submissions and PIA outputs where applicable).
- Scenario content is based on roles and aligned to the organisation’s security and privacy training goals, with a documented process for updating scenarios based on results.

###### Actions Required for Compliance

- [ ] Define which roles are assigned to which training modules for: developers (vulnerability labs), senior leaders and executives (spear‑phishing and “whale” phishing), and privacy-relevant roles (personal data quizzes and privacy impact assessment (PIA) practice).
- [ ] Set up the training platform to run phishing simulations for executives and to capture measurable results (for example, whether they reported it, whether they interacted with it, and how quickly they reported it).
- [ ] Develop and publish secure coding training labs for developers that simulate attacks using common software weaknesses, and require participants to submit evidence showing how they fixed the issues securely.
- [ ] Create quizzes to test how people identify and handle personal data (PII) using realistic scenarios, and use scoring and marking guidelines (rubrics) to assess whether they correctly identify it and take the right actions to process it.
- [ ] Run personal information assessment (PIA) scenario practice using an organisation-approved PIA template, and score participants’ answers against a defined marking guide (rubric).
- [ ] Hold mandatory debrief sessions after every exercise, and update the scenario guidance based on the failure patterns you observe.
- [ ] Export and keep audit-ready records showing, for each exercise, who completed it, their score or result, the date and time it was submitted, and any files or other materials they submitted.

#### at-3.5 — Processing Personally Identifiable Information (Enhancement)

This expectation is about making sure everyone who may handle personal information understands exactly how to do it lawfully and openly. Without this, staff could use people’s details for the wrong reason, share them when they should not, or fail to meet the organisation’s commitments about how that information will be used. This can lead to complaints, legal issues, and real harm to individuals.

The organisation must identify every role that has access to personal information, including system owners, data stewards, and privacy officers. These people must receive initial training before they start handling personal information, and refresher training every year.

The training must explain what counts as personal information, the risks and responsibilities of handling it, and the organisation’s authority and permitted reasons for using it. It must also cover the organisation’s transparency expectations and direct staff to the relevant privacy and data-sharing documents, such as privacy notices and policies, official notices about records, privacy statements, assessments, sharing agreements, and contracts.

##### Identity-gated PII training with enforced initial and annual refresh

- Category: Software
- Priority: Critical

###### WHAT
Provide role-based training for all staff who have access to personally identifiable information (PII), and make sure the initial training is completed before anyone can process PII. Then require annual refresher training.

###### WHY (control requirement)
NIST SP 800-53 AT-3(5) (enhancement) requires the organisation to provide **{{ insert: param, at-03.05_odp.01 }}** with **initial** and **{{ insert: param, at-03.05_odp.02 }}** training on how to handle PII processing and transparency controls in their work. Training must cover, at minimum, what counts as PII, the related risks and responsibilities, and the organisation’s **authorisation and reasons (purposes)** for processing. This should align with the organisation’s privacy and transparency documents (for example: privacy policies/notices, system of records notices, privacy impact assessments (PIAs), privacy statements, and contracts/information sharing agreements (ISAs)/memorandums of agreement (MDAs)).

###### HOW (specific steps/approach)
1. **Define the PII-access role list**: identify all staff and roles that can access PII (including system owners, data stewards, privacy officers, and any other PII-access roles). For each role, list the systems and datasets where PII is processed.
2. **Create role-based training courses in the learning platform**:
   - **Core module for all PII-access roles**: what counts as PII; risks and responsibilities; expectations for transparency; where to find and how to use privacy notices/policies and other relevant guidance.
   - **System-owner/data-steward module**: the authority to process PII and the purposes for processing; how to interpret system-specific privacy documents (for example: PIAs/privacy statements/system of records notices) and ensure processing matches those purposes.
   - **Privacy-officer module**: governance, expectations for checking and escalation, and how to ensure transparency requirements are met.
3. **Set proof of training completion and timing**:
   - Require completion of **initial** training before any PII access rights are enabled.
   - Require completion of **annual refresher** training (per {{ at-03.05_odp.02 }} = Annual refresher training) within the agreed time window.
4. **Enforce through identity and access rules** (policy-as-code / conditional access):
   - Use conditional access or access-enablement rules so PII processing permissions are granted only when the user’s training completion records show initial training is completed and the annual refresher is up to date.
   - Apply enforcement to both normal user access and any application programming interface (API) access routes that allow PII processing permissions (including service accounts where relevant).
5. **Automate updates and enable auditing**:
   - Connect the learning platform to the organisation’s identity provider (for example, using system for cross-domain identity management (SCIM)/application programming interface (API)) so training completion status is recorded in the user attributes or groups used by access rules.
   - Produce audit-ready evidence: training completion dates and times, the course assigned for each role, and access decision logs showing that access was enabled only after training requirements were met.

###### WHO (role responsible)
- **Privacy Officer / Data Protection Lead**: owns the training requirements, including mapping of authority/purpose and the governance documents.
- **Identity and Access Management (IAM) Lead / Identity Architect**: implements identity-based gating and conditional access/access-enablement enforcement.
- **System Owners / Data Stewards**: confirm that system-specific authority/purpose and transparency expectations are correctly reflected in the training.
- **Learning Platform Administrator**: runs course delivery, tracks completion, and manages the integrations.

###### Acceptance criteria
- All personnel identified in {{ at-03.05_odp.01 }} who can access PII have completed initial PII training before any PII processing access rights are granted.
- All such personnel complete annual refresher training within the defined annual window {{ at-03.05_odp.02 }}.
- Training content explicitly covers: (a) what counts as PII, (b) risks and responsibilities, (c) the organisation’s authorisation and purposes for processing, and (d) transparency requirements, with references to relevant privacy/transparency documents.
- Identity/access enforcement clearly prevents PII processing permissions when training is missing or out of date, and audit logs show the enforcement decision and the training completion evidence.

###### Actions Required for Compliance

- [ ] Create and approve a catalogue of roles that lists which roles can access personally identifiable information (PII) and which PII systems or datasets they can access.
- [ ] Develop role-based personal information (PII) training that covers what personal information is, the risks and legal responsibilities, the authority and purpose for using it, and transparency controls, with references to the relevant privacy governance documents.
- [ ] Set up the learning platform to record when each role completes the initial training and each annual refresher.
- [ ] Integrate the learning platform with the organisation’s identity provider (IdP) to publish training completion status to the user attributes or groups used to make access decisions.
- [ ] Set rules for when access and entitlements can be activated so that permissions to process personal information (PII) are only granted after initial training is completed and an annual refresher training is completed each year.
- [ ] Run a test to confirm that users who do not have valid training cannot enable personal information (PII) processing permissions, including any application programming interface (API) or service access routes where applicable.

### at-4 — Training Records (Control)

This requirement is about making sure everyone in the organisation is properly trained to protect information and personal data, and that there is clear written evidence showing the training took place. Without this, people may not understand what they should (or should not) do, the same mistakes could happen again, and the organisation may not be able to prove who was trained and when—leaving it more exposed to data loss, privacy breaches, and avoidable incidents.

The organisation must record and keep track of all information security and privacy training. This includes general awareness training for everyone, as well as extra training tailored to specific job roles. It must also regularly check that training is completed and follow up when it is missing or overdue.

Finally, the organisation must keep each person’s training records for at least 3 years, in a way that allows the records to be retrieved and verified when needed.

##### LMS-based security/privacy training records retained for 3 years

- Category: Software
- Priority: Critical

###### WHAT must be done
Set up a single, central, auditable way to create, keep, and store each person’s information security and privacy training records (including general awareness and role-based/specialised training) for at least the organisation’s defined retention period.

###### WHY (control requirement)
AT-4 requires keeping training records and retaining them for a defined time period (organisation-defined parameter: **3 years**). The guidance also notes that specialised training records may be kept by individual supervisors at their discretion, but the organisation must still be able to prove training was completed and retrieve the records.

###### HOW (specific steps/approach)
1. **Use a learning management system (LMS) as the official record (system of record)** for security/privacy awareness and role-based training, linked to the organisation’s main identity source so each completion is tied to a named person.
2. **Set up training catalogues** for (a) security awareness and (b) privacy awareness, plus **role-based security/privacy modules** matched to job families/roles.
3. **Make records complete when training is finished**: each training completion record must, at minimum, record the **person**, **training/topic**, and **completion date** (and completion status/evidence as supported by the LMS).
4. **Monitor and detect gaps**: run recurring reports that show people who are overdue or missing required training, with the ability to drill down to the underlying completion records.
5. **Create an auditable follow-up process** for overdue/missing training (for example, automated reminders and escalation to line managers), and record follow-up actions in a workflow or ticketing system linked to the person.
6. **Set retention to at least 3 years** for training completion records (including records near the end of the retention period), and define an export/archive process so records can be retrieved and checked during audits.
7. **Permit supervisor-held specialised training evidence only as an exception**: define a documented process for supervisors to upload/attach evidence to the LMS (or to a controlled repository) so the organisation can still retrieve a complete set of records.

###### WHO (role responsible)
- **Compliance/Information Security Training Owner**: sets training requirements, retention expectations, and what audit evidence must show.
- **LMS Administrator / HR Learning Operations**: configures courses, completion evidence fields, reporting, and retention settings.
- **Line Managers**: ensure assigned training is completed and respond to overdue escalations.
- **Internal Audit / Compliance Assurance**: periodically checks that training records can be retrieved and are complete.

###### Acceptance criteria
- For a sample of staff across job families, the organisation can retrieve an auditable training record showing **who** completed **what** training and **when**, for both awareness and role-based modules.
- Reports reliably identify **overdue/missing** training and link to the underlying completion records.
- Training completion records are retained and can be retrieved for **at least 3 years** (per the organisation-defined parameter).
- If supervisor-held specialised training evidence is used, it is captured through the defined exception process and is retrievable as part of the audit evidence set.

###### Actions Required for Compliance

- [ ] Choose and set up a learning management system (LMS), or an integrated human resources (HR) learning module, to be the official system that records completion of security and privacy training.
- [ ] Connect the learning management system (LMS) to the organisation’s identity system so every course completion is linked to a specific named person.
- [ ] Set up security awareness, privacy awareness, and role-based training programmes, matched to job families and job roles.
- [ ] Require completion records to include the training topic or training name, the completion date, the completion status, and the identity of the person who completed it.

### at-6 — Training Feedback (Control)

This requirement ensures that the people responsible for training are told, promptly and clearly, how well staff have learned what they need to know. After each round of security training finishes, the organisation must share the results within 30 days with the Chief Information Security Officer, the person who manages the training programme, and the owner of the relevant information system. This includes both the results from general security awareness training and the results from training tailored to specific roles.

The real-world risk is that if poor performance—especially in critical roles—is not identified quickly, the organisation may miss an early warning sign and leave itself exposed to preventable mistakes or misuse. To meet this expectation, the organisation must provide feedback detailed enough to assess the outcomes and improve and update the training programme, rather than only confirming that training was completed.

##### Automated AT-6 training feedback packs to required owners

- Category: Software
- Priority: Critical

###### WHAT
After each security training completion cycle, create and send an outcome-focused training feedback package (including both awareness training results and role-based training results) to the **Chief Information Security Officer (CISO)**, the **training programme manager**, and the **information system owner**.

###### WHY (control requirement)
National Institute of Standards and Technology (NIST) Special Publication (SP) 800-53 **AT-6** requires the organisation to **share feedback on organisational training results** with the people named by the organisation’s defined rules **at-06_odp.01 (frequency)** and **at-06_odp.02 (personnel)**. The guidance also says the feedback should include **awareness training results and role-based training results**, and that any failures in **critical roles** must be raised so that the organisation can take the right follow-up actions.

###### HOW (specific steps/approach)
Set up an automated process that works with the Learning Management System (LMS) and a workflow or ticketing system to:
1. **Set the “cycle complete” trigger** (end of each completion cycle) and run it as a scheduled job so the feedback is produced **within 30 days after each training completion cycle** (at-06_odp.01).
2. **Pull the training outcomes from the LMS** for each completion cycle, including:
   - Awareness training results (for example, completion and assessment outcomes)
   - Role-based training results (for example, results by role and/or module)
   - Identification of failures, especially for **critical roles** (as defined in your training programme mapping)
3. **Put the outcomes into a standard, auditable format** that supports programme review and updates (for example, pass/fail or proficiency outcomes, completion rates, and links to the LMS reports/exports used as evidence).
4. **Work out who should receive the feedback in a consistent way**:
   - Always include the **CISO** and **training programme manager** (at-06_odp.02)
   - Determine the **information system owner** using an authoritative system-to-owner mapping (for example, a governance register or service catalogue) based on which information systems the role-based modules apply to.
5. **Create a “feedback pack”** (a portal view and/or PDF) showing the outcomes and evidence links, and **send it through a workflow queue** with a service level agreement (SLA) timing control to ensure delivery by day 30.
6. **Automatically escalate** if delivery is at risk (for example, if pack creation or sending fails), and **store proof of delivery** (time sent, recipients, pack identifier) in a records repository so it can be audited.

###### WHO
- **Training programme manager**: sets the training cycle definitions, the critical-role mapping, and approves the feedback content.
- **CISO**: receives the feedback and confirms that critical-role failures are clearly highlighted.
- **Information system owner**: receives the role-based training outcomes relevant to their systems.
- **Platform/Integration engineer (IT)**: builds the LMS integration, recipient mapping, workflow automation, and evidence recording.

###### Acceptance criteria
- For every training completion cycle, a feedback pack is delivered to the **CISO**, **training programme manager**, and the correct **information system owner(s)** **within 30 days**.
- Each feedback pack includes **awareness training results** and **role-based training results**, with enough detail to support evaluation and updates to the training programme.
- **Critical-role failures** (as defined in the programme) are clearly identified and included.
- Audit evidence is available showing pack creation/delivery times, recipients, and links to the underlying LMS outcome evidence.

###### Actions Required for Compliance

- [ ] Set the criteria and timetable for when the training completion cycle is considered finished, and schedule the feedback report job to run immediately after the cycle is closed.
- [ ] Integrate the learning management system (LMS) with an orchestration and workflow system to extract awareness and role-based training results for each cycle.
- [ ] Create a standard feedback-pack template that includes outcome details and links to supporting evidence, so it can be checked and verified during audits.
- [ ] Set up recipient-mapping rules so that emails always include the Chief Information Security Officer (CISO) and the training programme manager, and so the information system owner is taken from an official, authoritative register.
- [ ] Set up the workflow so the feedback pack is delivered within 30 days under a service level agreement (SLA), and automatically escalate the matter if deadlines are at risk.
- [ ] Store proof of delivery (date and time, who received it, and the parcel identifier) in a records repository that matches the retention requirements for training records.
