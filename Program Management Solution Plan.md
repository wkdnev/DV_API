### PM-1 — Information Security Program Plan (Control)

This requirement is about having a clear, organisation-wide security plan that everyone can follow. Without it, different parts of the organisation may protect information in different ways, creating gaps that could lead to data loss, fraud, disruption to services, reputational damage, or harm to people and partners.

The organisation must create and share a formal programme plan that explains:
- what security outcomes must be achieved,
- what management arrangements and shared safeguards are already in place (or planned),
- who is responsible for making the plan work, including a clear commitment from senior leadership.

The plan must be coordinated across all relevant parts of the organisation, explain how compliance will be met, and be approved by a senior official who is accountable for the risks to the organisation’s mission, assets, people, other organisations, and the Nation.

The plan must be reviewed and updated at least every 12 months, and also after any organisation-wide audit or assessment findings, security incidents or breaches, changes in laws or government directions, major changes to the organisation or its mission, or significant changes in risk for confidential or classified information.

Finally, the plan must be protected so that only authorised people can view it or change it.

##### Governed organisation-wide information security programme plan

- Category: Manual
- Priority: Critical

###### WHAT
Create and maintain an organisation-wide information security programme plan (as a single document or a controlled set of documents). The plan should explain the organisation’s security requirements, the programme management controls and common controls (already in place or planned), the compliance approach, and who is responsible for running and managing the programme.

###### WHY (control requirement)
This requirement (PM-1) asks the organisation to create, approve, share, and keep up to date an information security programme plan. The plan must provide enough detail about programme management and common controls (including the rules for how assignments/selections are made, either directly or by reference) so that compliance and risk can be determined clearly and without ambiguity. The plan must be updated at least every 12 months and whenever defined triggering events occur. It must also be protected from unauthorised disclosure and unauthorised changes.

###### HOW (specific steps/approach)
1. **Create one “single source of truth” programme plan** using a standard template with required sections: scope and overview of security requirements; programme management controls; common controls appendix (or references to where common controls are described); roles and responsibilities (including governance ownership and deputies); compliance approach (how compliance is achieved and how non-compliance is handled); and a senior approval statement.
2. **Set the review/update schedule and triggering events** in the plan and in the governance process: review and update **every 12 months** and after triggers such as organisation-wide assessment or audit findings, security incidents or breaches, changes in laws and executive orders, directives, regulations, policies, standards, or guidelines, major organisational or mission changes, and material changes in risk for Confidential or Classified data.
3. **Use a controlled approval and change process**: require that all changes are proposed through the governance workflow, assessed for their impact on programme/common controls and risk, and then approved by the **senior official accountable for risk** before the updated plan is published.
4. **Share the approved plan only with authorised stakeholders** using controlled access channels (for example, an enterprise document management system with sensitivity labels), ensuring stakeholders always use the latest approved version.
5. **Protect the plan from unauthorised disclosure and changes** by using role-based access control, restricting privileged access, and keeping an auditable record of versions and changes. This record must include: the trigger type, the reason for the change, which sections were affected, and the approval decision.
6. **Keep evidence for audit purposes** by retaining the approval record, change requests, and the mapping between programme/common control references and system security plans where inheritance is used.

###### WHO (role responsible)
- **Information Security Programme Owner / Chief Information Security Officer (CISO) (or a delegated senior official accountable)**: accountable for risk and final approval.
- **Information Security Governance team**: maintains the template, workflow, and change logs; coordinates updates.
- **Compliance/Assurance function**: provides assessment and audit findings and their compliance implications.
- **System owners / common controls owners**: provide input on common controls and planned changes.

###### Acceptance criteria
- An approved organisation-wide programme plan (or controlled document set) exists and includes: an overview of security requirements, programme management controls, common controls (in place or planned), roles and responsibilities, the compliance approach, and senior approval.
- The plan is reviewed and updated **at least every 12 months**, and it is clearly updated after each defined triggering event.
- All updates are made through an auditable workflow with senior official approval and a recorded reason for the change.
- Access to the plan is limited to authorised roles; unauthorised disclosure and unauthorised changes are prevented; and version history and change logs are retained for audit.

###### Actions Required for Compliance

- [ ] Create the organisation-wide information security programme plan using a standard template that includes the required sections: requirements overview, programme management controls, common controls appendix and references, roles and responsibilities, compliance approach, and a senior approval statement.
- [ ] Set out the review/update schedule and the list of events that trigger a review in the plan. Then configure the governance process to ensure these triggers are followed, at least once every 12 months, including after: assessment or audit findings; incidents or breaches; changes to legal, regulatory, policy, or standards; major mission or organisational changes; and material changes to the organisation’s Confidential or Classified risk posture.
- [ ] Use a controlled change process that includes an impact assessment and approval from the senior official responsible for risk before any update is released.
- [ ] Store the plan in the organisation’s document management system using role-based access control, sensitivity labelling, and an unchangeable version history with auditable change records.
- [ ] Share the approved plan only through approved, controlled channels with authorised people, and clearly label the current version as the single source of truth.
- [ ] Create and keep a cross-reference between the common controls listed in the programme plan and the related system security plans. This supports control inheritance and provides audit evidence.

### PM-2 — Information Security Program Leadership Role (Control)

This requirement is about making sure your organisation has a clearly named senior leader who is responsible for running the organisation-wide information security work. Without this, security responsibilities can become split across different teams, decisions may take longer, and key protections may not be planned, put in place, or kept up to date—leaving the organisation more exposed to data loss, fraud, disruption, and regulatory or reputational harm.

To meet this expectation, the organisation must formally appoint a senior information security officer and give them a clear role, along with enough authority and resources to coordinate, develop, implement, and maintain the programme across the whole organisation. The organisation should also produce a programme plan that explains how the programme will be developed, rolled out, reviewed, and updated over time, and how the plan will be coordinated with relevant internal and external parties. Finally, responsibility for planning and delivering the programme must be assigned to named individuals, including the senior information security officer.

##### Appoint SISO with mission, authority, resources and programme plan

- Category: Manual
- Priority: Critical

###### WHAT must be done
Formally appoint a senior agency information security officer (SISO) as an organisation official, and give them a clear mission, enough authority, and dedicated resources to coordinate, develop, implement, and maintain an organisation-wide information security programme.

###### WHY (control requirement)
PM-2 requires the organisation to appoint a senior information security officer with the mission and resources to coordinate, develop, implement, and maintain an organisation-wide information security programme.

###### HOW (specific steps/approach)
1. **Appoint the SISO formally** (for example, a human resources role letter, an executive appointment memo, or board/leadership meeting minutes) stating they are the senior agency information security officer responsible for the organisation-wide information security programme.
2. **Set out the SISO’s mission and decision-making authority** in a Security Governance Charter. This should cover: scope (organisation-wide), authority to coordinate across business and technology teams, escalation routes to executive leadership, and authority to drive adoption of security requirements and standards.
3. **Provide and record resources** (people, budget, tools, and time) sufficient to run the programme through its full lifecycle (development, implementation, maintenance, and review). Include a resourcing approach and an annual review process.
4. **Create an Information Security Programme Plan** that the SISO owns and keeps up to date. The plan must include: procedures for developing and implementing the programme, how often it is reviewed and updated (and what triggers updates, such as major incidents, regulatory changes, or major changes to the organisation’s architecture), and procedures for coordinating with relevant internal and external parties.
5. **Assign responsibilities to named roles** using a RACI (or equivalent) covering programme planning and implementation activities. Make sure the SISO is included in the roles responsible for accountability and planning, and that other security-related functions are explicitly named.
6. **Set an operating schedule and keep an evidence record** (for example, monthly governance meetings, quarterly risk and control reviews, and an annual assurance cycle). Store version-controlled documents (charter, programme plan, RACI, meeting minutes, and resourcing approvals) in a controlled repository.

###### WHO (role responsible)
- **SISO (Senior Agency Information Security Officer):** accountable for leading, coordinating, and maintaining the programme.
- **Executive leadership / accountable governance body:** approves the appointment, charter, and resourcing.
- **Security governance office / Chief Information Security Officer (CISO) office (if separate):** supports programme plan development and managing evidence.
- **Information technology (IT), risk, legal/compliance, procurement, and (where applicable) operational technology (OT) stakeholders:** provide named responsibilities as set out in the RACI.

###### Acceptance criteria
- A formally documented appointment exists identifying the SISO as the senior agency information security officer.
- A Security Governance Charter exists that states the SISO’s mission and authority to coordinate and drive the organisation-wide programme.
- Documented resourcing exists (staffing, budget, tools, and time) sufficient for programme development, implementation, and maintenance, with an annual review.
- An Information Security Programme Plan exists, is version-controlled, and includes review/update frequency and coordination procedures with relevant internal and external parties.
- A RACI (or equivalent) exists with named roles for programme planning and implementation, including the SISO, and evidence of the governance operating schedule (minutes/approvals) is available.

###### Actions Required for Compliance

- [ ] Formally appoint the chief information security officer (SISO) using an executive or human resources (HR) appointment document that states they are responsible for the organisation-wide information security programme.
- [ ] Publish a security governance charter that sets out the Security Information and Security Officer’s (SISO) role, responsibilities, coverage, decision-making authority, and how issues are escalated.
- [ ] Create and get approval for a resourcing plan (staff, budget, tools, and time) to develop, implement, and maintain the programme
- [ ] Create and keep under version control an information security programme plan, owned by the Senior Information Security Officer (SISO), including how often it is reviewed and updated, and what events trigger updates.
- [ ] Set up a RACI (Responsible, Accountable, Consulted, Informed) for programme planning and delivery, with named roles for each task. Make sure the Senior Information Security Officer (SISO) is accountable for planning the key activities.
- [ ] Set up a regular governance schedule (for example, monthly, quarterly, or annually) and keep proof of decisions and changes (such as meeting minutes, approvals, and plan change logs) in a secure, controlled document repository.

### PM-3 — Information Security and Privacy Resources (Control)

This requirement ensures that when the organisation plans and approves major spending, it also plans the money and documentation needed to protect both information and people’s privacy. Without this, important security and privacy work may be left out of budgets, which can lead to avoidable incidents such as data being exposed, misused, or handled in ways that do not meet legal and regulatory duties.

To meet this expectation, the organisation must include, in every capital planning and investment request, the resources needed to run both its information security programme and its privacy programme. It must also clearly document any exceptions, including what will be done instead.

The organisation must prepare and keep the required documentation showing how information security and privacy will be addressed in those requests, in line with all applicable laws, government directions, internal policies, regulations, and standards. It must also make sure the planned resources are available for spending as scheduled.

##### Gate capital requests with costed InfoSec/Privacy resource evidence packs

- Category: Manual
- Priority: Critical

###### WHAT
Set up a mandatory, policy-approved “Information Security and Privacy Resources” evidence pack that must be included with every capital planning or investment request. If the required resources cannot be provided as planned, attach a structured, authorised exception record instead.  

Each pack must include:
- costed information security programme resources, and
- costed privacy programme resources,  
plus documentation that clearly links the request to the relevant laws, government directions, internal policies, regulations, and standards.

###### WHY (control requirement)
PM-3 requires the organisation to consider appointing champions and to include the necessary resources (with specialist expertise where needed) as part of capital planning and investment control. It also expects the required security and privacy documentation to be prepared and kept up to date for approval, and that any exceptions are recorded and authorised.

###### HOW (specific steps/approach)
1. **Create two standard evidence packs** that must be submitted to the Investment Review Board (or equivalent):
   - **Information Security Resources Pack**: sets out the security work to be funded, names the security roles/champions, includes delivery milestones, and provides a **costed schedule** for the information security programme activities required by the investment.
   - **Privacy Resources Pack**: sets out the privacy work to be funded, names the privacy roles/champions, includes outputs from a data protection impact assessment (DPIA) or privacy impact assessment (PIA) (or references to them), covers lawful basis or consent considerations where relevant, sets out the retention and deletion approach, and provides a **costed schedule** for the privacy programme activities required by the investment.
2. **Add requirement-alignment documentation** inside each pack:
   - Include a mapping or checklist that clearly links the proposed investment’s security and privacy activities to the relevant **laws, government directions, internal policies, regulations, and standards**.
3. **Add a submission gate** to the capital request process:
   - The process must prevent submission unless both packs are present and complete, or unless an approved exception record is attached.
4. **Create a structured Exceptions Register process**:
   - If any required resource is missing or cannot be delivered as scheduled, require an exception record that includes: what is missing, the reason, compensating actions, acceptance of any remaining risk, and **approval evidence** from the investment governance body.
5. **Confirm planned resources are available for spending**:
   - Require finance or procurement checks in the process (for example, budget or cost centre, and a procurement route set-up reference) so the request cannot be approved if funding is not schedulable or available at the planned time.

###### WHO (role responsible)
- **Security champion / Information Security lead**: owns the Information Security Resources Pack template, checks completeness, and ensures the requirement mapping quality.
- **Privacy champion / Data Protection lead**: owns the Privacy Resources Pack template, ensures the DPIA/PIA links are included, and ensures the requirement mapping quality.
- **Investment Review Board / investment governance body**: authorises exceptions and approves capital requests.
- **Finance and Procurement partner**: confirms budget/cost centre/procurement route availability for the planned schedule.
- **Programme or Portfolio management (workflow owner)**: implements the submission gate and the Exceptions Register process.

###### Acceptance criteria
- 100% of capital or investment requests submitted to the Investment Review Board include **both** the Information Security and Privacy evidence packs with **costed schedules**.
- For any request with missing or late resources, an **authorised exception record** exists with the required information (missing items, reason, compensating actions, acceptance of residual risk, approval evidence).
- Each pack includes a clear mapping or checklist linking to the relevant **laws, government directions, internal policies, regulations, and standards**.
- Process enforcement is demonstrated: requests cannot be submitted or approved without complete packs or an authorised exception.
- Finance and procurement checks confirm the planned resources are available for spending at the scheduled time (with evidence recorded in the request record).

###### Actions Required for Compliance

- [ ] Define and publish the required information security resources pack and privacy resources pack templates for all capital and investment requests.
- [ ] Update the capital request process so that a request cannot be submitted unless both document packs are complete, or an authorised exception record is attached.
- [ ] Create an Exceptions Register process that records any missing resources, the reasons they are missing, the compensating actions taken, the decision to accept any remaining risk, and evidence that governance approval has been obtained.
- [ ] Add requirement-alignment mapping and checklists in both packs, referencing the relevant laws, government instructions, internal policies, regulations, and standards.
- [ ] Add finance and procurement checks to approval steps, so approvals only happen when there is evidence that the planned resources will be available to spend at the scheduled time.
- [ ] Appoint and support security and privacy champions to check that each submission pack is complete and consistent before it is sent for governance approval.

### PM-4 — Plan of Action and Milestones Process (Control)

This requirement is about keeping a clear, up-to-date plan for fixing known problems in how the organisation protects information, handles personal data, and manages risks from suppliers. Without this, issues could continue for longer than necessary, be dealt with in the wrong order, or be missed completely—causing harm to the organisation, its assets, the people affected, other organisations, and potentially wider national interests.

The organisation must create and maintain documented action plans and milestones for each of these areas (and any relevant systems). Each plan should set out the specific steps needed to put things right, who is responsible, and when progress will be made. The plans must be updated based on findings from reviews and ongoing checks, reported in line with the organisation’s required reporting rules (including Office of Management and Budget/Federal Information Security Management Act expectations where applicable), and regularly reviewed to ensure they align with the organisation’s overall approach to risk and its organisation-wide priorities for responding to risk.

##### Enterprise POA&M workflow driven by risk findings and continuous monitoring

- Category: Software
- Priority: Critical

###### WHAT
Create and maintain documented Plans of Action and Milestones (POA&Ms) for information security, privacy, and supply chain risk at the right organisation and system levels. Each POA&M must set out the corrective actions needed, who is responsible, the target dates, and what proof will be used to show progress and completion. POA&Ms must be kept up to date using results from control assessments and ongoing monitoring.

###### WHY (control requirement)
PM-4 requires the organisation to run and maintain a POA&M process with an organisation-wide view. This includes prioritising risk response actions, keeping the approach aligned with organisational goals and objectives, updating POA&Ms based on assessment results and ongoing monitoring, and supporting required reporting.

###### HOW (specific steps/approach)
Set up an integrated, auditable POA&M workflow in the organisation’s governance, risk, and compliance (GRC) / information technology service management (ITSM) tools. Use one “single source of truth” and drive updates from risk findings.

1. **Define a POA&M data structure and templates** that match the organisation’s risk response needs. This must include, at minimum: scope (organisation/system/process level), linked risk and/or finding identifier(s), specific corrective action(s), measurable outcomes, accountable owner (role), milestone dates, dependencies, current status, and links to evidence/verification.
2. **Set up workflow stages** (for example: Draft → Review → Approve → Execute → Verify → Close → Report) and use role-based access control (RBAC) so only authorised roles can approve or report.
3. **Create a repeatable update process** that takes inputs from ongoing monitoring and control assessments (for example: vulnerability management findings, configuration compliance gaps, identity and access review findings, privacy assessment findings, and supplier/supply chain assessment findings). It must either:
   - create new POA&M entries, or
   - update existing POA&M entries (milestone dates, status, or corrective actions) when findings change.
4. **Check completeness and consistency before approval or reporting** using automated validation rules (policy-as-code) that prevent approval if required information is missing (for example: milestone dates, owner, linked risk/finding identifier, corrective action description, and the plan for verification evidence).
5. **Produce reporting packs from the same workflow** to meet organisational reporting requirements (including any internal reporting calendar aligned to Office of Management and Budget (OMB) / Federal Information Security Management Act (FISMA), where applicable). Ensure the reported POA&M status comes from the current system of record.
6. **Hold regular governance reviews** (for example, monthly or quarterly) to confirm POA&Ms remain aligned with the organisation’s enterprise risk management approach and organisation-wide risk response priorities. Record review outcomes and approval decisions.

###### WHO (role responsible)
- **Information Security / Chief Information Security Officer (CISO) office (POA&M process owner):** owns the POA&M process, templates, and governance schedule.
- **Governance, Risk, and Compliance (GRC) / Compliance Manager:** configures the workflow, validation rules, and reporting outputs.
- **System/Application Owners and Privacy/Supply Chain Risk Owners:** provide corrective actions, accountable ownership, and committed milestone dates.
- **Continuous Monitoring / Security Operations (SOC/engineering):** provides findings and evidence used for updates and verification.
- **ITSM/GRC Platform Administrator:** maintains integrations and access controls.

###### Acceptance criteria
- POA&Ms exist for relevant areas of information security, privacy, and supply chain risk at the appropriate levels.
- Each POA&M entry includes: linked risk/finding identifier(s), specific corrective action(s), accountable owner, milestone dates, and an evidence/verification link.
- POA&M entries are updated based on assessment findings and ongoing monitoring outputs, supported by an audit trail showing updates.
- Approval and reporting are prevented unless required fields are complete and consistent.
- Reporting outputs are generated from the workflow system of record and match the latest POA&M status.
- Governance review records show periodic confirmation that POA&Ms align with enterprise risk response priorities.

###### Actions Required for Compliance

- [ ] Define the plan of action and milestones (POA&M) structure and the required information, including: scope; the linked finding or risk identifier; the corrective actions; measurable results; the person responsible for delivery; key milestone dates; any dependencies; and links to supporting evidence and how it will be checked.
- [ ] Set up the plan of action and milestones (POA&M) workflow in the governance, risk and compliance (GRC) and information technology service management (ITSM) system, with role-based access control (RBAC) for who can approve and who can view reports.
- [ ] Put validation checks in place to stop approval and reporting of the plan of action and milestones (POA&M) when required fields are missing or do not match each other.
- [ ] Automatically combine information from continuous monitoring and assessments to create or update plan-of-action-and-milestones (POA&M) entries for vulnerabilities, configuration issues, identity issues, privacy issues, and supplier findings.
- [ ] Set up automated production of programme of actions and milestones (POA&M) reporting packs from the organisation’s main workflow system, timed to the organisation’s reporting calendar.
- [ ] Schedule and document regular governance reviews to confirm that plans of action and milestones (POA&Ms) still match the organisation’s enterprise risk management priorities, and record the results.

### PM-5 — System Inventory (Control)

This requirement is about keeping a clear, up-to-date list of the organisation’s main systems, so you always know what you run and who is responsible for it. Without this, important systems can be overlooked when changes are made, when problems are investigated, or when reporting is needed—raising the risk of outages, incorrect decisions, and delays in responding to issues.

The organisation must define, in writing, what counts as a system. It must also record each system in a consistent way in an organisation-wide inventory, not just individual components. Named people or teams must be responsible for creating and maintaining the inventory, and it must be updated every quarter. Evidence must be kept to show the updates were completed.

Written procedures must explain how systems are added, changed, and retired; how quarterly updates are reviewed and approved; and what records are kept. This allows the organisation to demonstrate the inventory and its quarterly updates for reporting purposes.

##### Quarterly system register with defined scope and approval workflow

- Category: Software
- Priority: Critical

###### WHAT
Create and maintain an organisation-wide **system inventory** (system register) that lists **organisational systems** (business or mission systems), not individual parts. Keep it **up to date on the agreed schedule** (pm-05_odp: **quarterly**) and keep **proof that can be checked during an audit**.

###### WHY (control requirement)
PM-5 requires the organisation to **create and update** an inventory of organisational systems using a consistent method, and to keep it updated on the organisation’s defined schedule (pm-05_odp). The inventory must support reporting and show that updates are actually carried out.

###### HOW (specific steps/approach)
1. **Define “system” in writing**: publish a short standard that explains what counts as an organisational system (for example: HR/Finance enterprise resource planning (ERP), customer portal, manufacturing execution system) and what does not (for example: a single virtual machine (VM), database instance, container, network segment). Include rules for system identifiers and a status list (Active/Inactive/Retired/Planned).
2. **Set up one authoritative system register** in an enterprise tool (for example: information technology service management (ITSM), configuration management database (CMDB), governance risk and compliance (GRC) registry) with a **consistent record format** and required information (system name, business owner, purpose, importance/criticality, hosting approach, high-level connections, data classification summary, and links to supporting evidence).
3. **Create a quarterly update process**: set up a workflow that runs **quarterly** to (a) add new systems, (b) update owner/status/importance changes, and (c) remove systems that are no longer in scope. The workflow must produce **quarterly snapshots with version numbers** (for example: Q2-2026) and store evidence of what changed.
4. **Assign named responsibility**: appoint specific roles or teams responsible for (a) maintaining the register and (b) reviewing and approving each quarterly update. Require approval before a quarterly snapshot is final.
5. **Use automation to suggest, not to decide scope**: connect to enterprise sources (for example: cloud application catalogues, identity and access management (IAM) application assignments, CMDB/ITSM service records, monitoring/observability service lists) to suggest possible entries and highlight systems that may be missing. However, people must confirm that each entry is a **system** (not a component) according to the written definition.
6. **Ensure data quality and audit readiness**: add checks (required fields, duplicate detection, allowed status changes, and “system vs component” checks). Make sure every change can be traced back to the original request (ticket/form) and an approval record.

###### WHO
- **System Inventory Owner (governance, risk and compliance (GRC)/information technology (IT) governance or security governance lead)**: responsible for the register and the quarterly schedule.
- **Business/Application Owners**: provide the purpose, importance/criticality, and confirm the system status.
- **ITSM/CMDB Administrator or Platform Owner**: sets up the register, the update workflow, and the integrations.
- **Approving Reviewer (IT/operational technology (OT) architecture or security governance approver)**: approves quarterly updates and finalises each snapshot.

###### Acceptance criteria
- The organisation has a documented definition of “system”, and it is used to decide what is included or excluded.
- There is one authoritative system register with consistent required fields for each organisational system.
- The register is updated **quarterly** (pm-05_odp) and produces **versioned quarterly snapshots**.
- Each quarterly update includes audit-ready evidence (tickets/forms, reviewer approvals, and change logs) showing that updates were made.
- Named roles are responsible for maintaining and approving the inventory updates.

###### Actions Required for Compliance

- [ ] Document and publish the organisation’s definition of an “organisational system”, including which systems are in scope versus out of scope, and the rules for assigning system identifiers.
- [ ] Set up an official system register with a consistent structure (schema) and required fields for every system entry.
- [ ] Set up a quarterly process that updates onboarding, changes, and retirements, and require a named person to review and approve the update before the final snapshot is completed.
- [ ] Bring together enterprise information sources (identity and access management (IAM) application catalogue, configuration management database/information technology service management (CMDB/ITSM) service records, and cloud/software-as-a-service (SaaS) application lists) to create proposed system records for review by people.
- [ ] Add validation rules to stop component-level entries and improve data quality, including required fields, duplicate checks, and correct status changes.
- [ ] Create and keep quarterly, versioned snapshots, with evidence that can be checked during audits (tickets or forms, approvals, and change records).

#### PM-5.1 — Inventory of Personally Identifiable Information (Enhancement)

This requirement is about keeping a clear, up-to-date record of every place in the organisation where information that can identify a person is collected, used, stored, shared, or deleted. Without this, the organisation can lose track of where personal details are held or handled. That increases the risk of using personal information for the wrong purpose, not giving people the correct privacy information, or failing to notice when a system should be changed or stopped.

To meet this expectation, the organisation must create an inventory that covers all systems, applications, and projects that handle personal data. It must clearly define what counts as handling personal data, and assign named people to be responsible for owning and maintaining the inventory.

The inventory must be kept current through an effective update process. It must be reviewed and updated at least once every quarter, with new or changed items added and items that are no longer relevant removed. This must be supported by appropriate documentation showing how the list was created and how it is kept accurate.

##### Quarterly PII inventory built from data mapping with discovery validation

- Category: Manual
- Priority: Critical

###### WHAT
Create and keep an auditable list of **all systems, applications, and projects that handle personally identifiable information (PII)**, and update it **at the organisation-defined frequency (quarterly)**.

###### WHY (control requirement)
The National Institute of Standards and Technology (NIST) Special Publication (SP) 800-53 **PM-5(1)** enhancement requires the organisation to **“set up, maintain, and update”** an inventory of systems, applications, and projects that handle PII, specifically **{{ insert: param, pm-05.01_odp }}**. The guidance explains that this inventory helps map how data is used, support privacy notices, and ensure PII handling remains relevant and necessary.

###### HOW (specific approach)
Set up a **data-mapping-led PII inventory** that is checked continuously using automated discovery signals, with a **quarterly review and sign-off** process.

1. **Define what counts as PII handling** in a standard mapping template (collect, store, use, share, delete; include purposes/authorised purposes; include data subjects and destinations/third parties where relevant). This standard becomes the basis for every inventory entry.
2. **Create an authoritative inventory record** (similar to a configuration management database (CMDB) / data catalogue) where each entry includes: system/application/project name, environment (production/testing/disaster recovery), system owner, application owner, PII categories/identifiers, processing actions, purpose/authorised basis, data stores and interfaces, and links to **evidence** (data flow diagrams, application programming interface (API) specifications, database schema notes, integration diagrams, and/or data mapping records).
3. Build the initial inventory from **authoritative data mapping** and project documentation, then check completeness using **automated discovery** sources appropriate to the environment (for example: cloud/software-as-a-service (SaaS) application inventory, database/storage inventory, API/integration inventory, and data catalogue signals).
4. Set up a **quarterly reconciliation workflow**:
   - Review discovery changes (new/changed SaaS applications, databases, storage locations, API routes, message topics/queues, and integration endpoints).
   - Assess changes to decide whether they introduce or change PII handling.
   - Update inventory entries accordingly (add/update/remove) and record the reason.
   - Get **named sign-off** from the system/application owner and privacy/security representatives.
5. Link this to change management so that when a project/system is added, changed, or retired, the inventory is updated (or queued for the next quarterly reconciliation) with traceable evidence.

###### WHO (roles responsible)
- **Privacy lead / Data protection officer (DPO)**: owns the PII handling definition and template, and chairs the quarterly sign-off.
- **Application/system owners**: provide evidence and confirm the accuracy of inventory entries for their systems.
- **Security architect / data security team**: checks discovery findings, access routes, and that purposes for processing are authorised.
- **Enterprise data governance / data catalogue team**: maintains the system of record for the inventory and the evidence links.

###### Acceptance criteria
- The organisation has an inventory covering **systems, applications, and projects** that handle PII, with each entry traceable to **documented evidence** and named owners.
- The inventory is **updated at least quarterly** (per pm-05.01_odp = quarterly), with documented reconciliation results showing additions/updates/removals.
- Quarterly sign-off records exist (privacy/security plus system/application owners) and show that discovery changes were reviewed.
- For a sample of in-scope systems, the inventory entry correctly identifies PII categories and processing actions (collect/store/use/share/delete) and links to the underlying mapping evidence.

###### Actions Required for Compliance

- [ ] Define and publish a standard template for processing personal data (PII) that sets out, for each activity: what is collected, stored, used, shared, and deleted; the purpose; the people affected; and the recipients or third parties involved.
- [ ] Create an official personal data (PII) inventory record for each system, application, or project, including mandatory details for the named responsible owners and links to evidence showing where the data is mapped.
- [ ] Create the initial inventory using authoritative data mapping and project documentation, and make sure every item can be traced back to supporting evidence.
- [ ] Set up automated discovery sources—cloud/software-as-a-service (SaaS) inventory, database and storage inventory, and integration/application programming interface (API) inventory—to produce quarterly change reports for reconciliation.
- [ ] Run a quarterly reconciliation process to add, update, or remove inventory records based on differences found during discovery and supporting mapping evidence.
- [ ] Record quarterly approval (sign-off) by the privacy and security team and by the system and application owners, including the reason for any changes and any exceptions.

### PM-6 — Measures of Performance (Control)

This requirement is about showing, with clear results, whether your organisation’s privacy and information protection work is actually working. If you do not regularly track progress and report on outcomes, you may only find problems after harm has happened—for example, personal data being handled incorrectly, preventable incidents occurring, or privacy duties being missed—because you never checked whether the approach was effective in the first place.

To meet this expectation, the organisation must put in place practical measures for both privacy and information protection that demonstrate how effective and efficient the programme and its safeguards are. It must document how these measures are set up, how information is collected, how performance is monitored, and how results are reported. It must also name specific people who are responsible for carrying out this work.

The organisation must then regularly review the results and share them with the appropriate audiences, making sure the measures remain aligned with the level of risk the organisation has said is acceptable in its risk planning. Finally, it must keep evidence of the work, such as reports and supporting data.

##### Outcome KPI framework for security & privacy performance reporting

- Category: Manual
- Priority: Critical

###### WHAT
Create an outcome-based set of information security and privacy performance measures. Then continuously check how well they are working and share the results with the intended audiences.

###### WHY (control requirement)
PM-6 requires the organisation to **develop, monitor, and report** the results of **information security and privacy performance measures**. These measures must be **outcome-based** (showing how effective and efficient the programme and controls are), and they should be **aligned to the organisation’s risk tolerance** set out in the risk management strategy.

###### HOW (specific steps/approach)
1. **Create a Security & Privacy Measures Register** for the application/programme. It should list outcome-based key performance indicators (KPIs) (for example: how effective fixes are, how effective detection and response are, how quickly privacy requests are handled, and how harm is reduced). Include the formula, data sources, how often the measure is taken, and how often it is reported.
2. **Link each KPI to risk tolerance** by mapping it to the relevant risk tolerance statement(s) (for example: the maximum unacceptable exposure time, the acceptable time to respond to data subject access requests (DSARs), and thresholds for confirmed privacy harm). Record the reason for each mapping.
3. **Set up KPI calculations and collect supporting evidence** using existing enterprise tools already connected to the application (for example: security information and event management (SIEM) and observability, vulnerability management, information technology service management (ITSM) ticketing, identity logs, and privacy case management). Make sure each KPI has evidence that can be checked, including the metric definition version, the data extracts/queries used, and the calculation results for each reporting period.
4. **Set up monitoring and governance routines**: define who reviews the KPI results, how unusual results are investigated, and how issues are escalated when thresholds are not met.
5. **Produce and share regular performance reports** (for example: monthly to the risk committee; quarterly to senior leadership; operational dashboards for control owners). Reports should show trends, whether targets were met, and any corrective actions triggered by missed thresholds.
6. **Control changes to the measures**: treat changes to KPI definitions (thresholds, formulas, data sources) as controlled changes with approvals, so reported performance stays consistent and can be audited over time.

###### WHO (role responsible)
- **Information Security Manager / Chief Information Security Officer (CISO) Office**: owns the security measures register, monitoring routine, and reporting to risk governance.
- **Data Protection Officer / Privacy Lead**: owns the privacy measures register, monitoring routine, and reporting to privacy governance.
- **Governance, Risk and Compliance (GRC) Analyst / Compliance Lead**: maintains the KPI register, evidence packs, and documentation linking measures to risk tolerance.
- **Application Owner / Engineering Lead**: provides and validates data sources, and ensures the application is set up to collect the needed measurement data (for example, instrumentation/logging).

###### Acceptance criteria
- A documented **Security & Privacy Measures Register** exists with outcome-based KPIs (security and privacy) including calculation rules, data sources, measurement frequency, owners, and mapping to risk tolerance.
- KPI results are **monitored** on the agreed schedule, with documented review and escalation steps when thresholds are missed.
- KPI results are **reported** to the agreed audiences using repeatable reporting outputs (dashboards and/or governance packs) for at least one full reporting cycle.
- For each KPI reporting period, an **evidence pack** is kept showing the metric definition version, data extracts/queries, and the calculated results.
- KPI definitions and thresholds are **change-controlled** (versioned and approved), providing audit-ready traceability.

###### Actions Required for Compliance

- [ ] Create a **Security and Privacy Measures Register** that includes outcome-based key performance indicators (KPIs), the rules for how each KPI is calculated, the data sources used, how often each KPI is measured, and the named person responsible for each measure.
- [ ] Match each key performance indicator (KPI) to the organisation’s risk tolerance statements in the risk management strategy, and record the reason for each match.
- [ ] Set out procedures for monitoring performance, reviewing results, and escalating issues when agreed targets are missed, including what corrective actions to take when those triggers occur.
- [ ] Create an initial baseline report covering one full reporting cycle, then share it with the intended groups: the risk committee, the chief information security officer (CISO) and privacy governance team, and the control owners.
- [ ] Set up an evidence pack for each reporting period (including the metric definition version, the data extracts or queries used, and the calculated results) and keep it for audit purposes.
- [ ] Place changes to metric definitions and thresholds under change control, with formal approvals and version tracking, to keep a complete audit trail.

### PM-7 — Enterprise Architecture (Control)

This requirement is about having a clear, up-to-date “big picture” plan showing how the organisation’s main systems and services work together. The plan must also ensure that information protection and privacy are built in from the start, and that it explicitly considers the knock-on effects on day-to-day operations, important assets, real people, other organisations, and the wider public interest.  

Without this, security and privacy needs can be overlooked until something goes wrong. That can lead to misuse of information, disruption to services, expensive rework, loss of trust, and harm that goes beyond the organisation itself.  

To meet this expectation, the organisation must develop and keep current an enterprise architecture that explicitly includes both information security and privacy requirements and explains how they connect to business activities. It must document how the architecture is created and maintained, name specific people responsible for producing it and for carrying out related risk checks, perform and record risk assessments linked to the architecture, and use those recorded results to update the architecture over time.

##### Security & privacy integrated EA guardrails with risk-linked governance

- Category: Manual
- Priority: Critical

###### WHAT
Develop and maintain an enterprise architecture (EA) that clearly includes **information security** and **privacy** requirements, and shows how architecture decisions address the resulting risks to **organisational operations and assets, individuals, other organisations, and the Nation**. Put in place a repeatable way to create and update the EA, including recorded risk assessment results that directly drive EA updates.

###### WHY (control requirement)
PM-7 requires the organisation to **develop and maintain an enterprise architecture** that takes account of **information security, privacy, and resulting risk** to organisational operations/assets, individuals, other organisations, and the Nation. Further guidance expects security and privacy architecture to be considered at a **system-of-systems level**, and for the integration to match the organisation’s **risk management strategy** and be applied through the **Risk Management Framework (RMF)**.

###### HOW (specific steps/approach)
1. **Create an EA repository and modelling standard** covering system-of-systems views, including: capability/service catalogue, application/service interactions, data flows, integration patterns, and deployment/environment views across production, test, and disaster recovery.

For each guardrail, link it to:
   - the **EA document types** that must show it in each view, and

   - the linked risk assessment result(s) and the mitigations.
4. **Use RMF-aligned risk assessment records linked to architecture decisions** (for example: risk register entries or RMF documentation) so that EA updates are driven by recorded risk outcomes. Handle any exceptions through a formal architecture decision record (ADR) that includes the risk reasoning and the mitigation plan.
5. **Assign named roles and responsibilities** for EA production and maintenance, security and privacy integration, and review of EA-related risk assessments (for example: EA owner, security architecture lead, privacy lead, and risk governance approver).
6. **Maintain the EA using a defined governance schedule** (versioning, periodic review, and change control) aligned to the security and privacy programme plans and the organisation’s risk management strategy. Produce audit-ready evidence packs showing the EA version/date, guardrail coverage, and links from risk records to the EA elements affected.

###### WHO (role responsible)
- **Enterprise Architect (EA Owner):** maintains the EA repository, modelling standard, and overall EA governance.
- **Security Architecture Lead:** owns the security guardrails and ensures they are built into EA documents.
- **Privacy Lead / Data Protection Officer (DPO) (where applicable):** owns the privacy guardrails, privacy impact triggers, and ensures privacy alignment in the EA.
- **Risk Management / RMF Governance Lead:** ensures risk assessment records follow RMF and are linked to architecture decisions.
- **Architecture Review Board (or equivalent):** approves exceptions and ADRs based on recorded risk outcomes.

###### Acceptance criteria
- EA documentation exists and is **up to date** (version-controlled) and includes explicit **security and privacy requirements** integrated into system-of-systems views.
- For a representative set of EA documents, there is clear **traceability** from EA elements to security and privacy guardrails, and to **recorded risk assessment outcomes**.
- Named roles exist for EA maintenance, security and privacy integration, and EA-related risk review; evidence of governance cadence and exception handling is available.
- EA updates are made based on recorded risk outcomes (exceptions require an ADR plus the linked risk record and mitigation plan).

###### Actions Required for Compliance

- [ ] Set up an enterprise architecture repository and a standard for how to model the organisation’s “system of systems”, including views of capabilities, applications and services, data movements, integrations, and where solutions are deployed.
- [ ] Create a security and privacy “guardrails” catalogue with clear, testable requirement statements. For each guardrail, show which enterprise architecture (EA) document types it applies to and which business processes or capabilities it supports.

- [ ] Set up an ADR (authorisation decision record) process so that any deviation from a guardrail must point to a dated, recorded risk assessment outcome that follows the Risk Management Framework (RMF), and include a mitigation plan.

- [ ] Set up and run a regular governance schedule that matches the security and privacy programme plans. Produce audit-ready evidence packs that link each enterprise architecture (EA) version to the relevant risk records and the specific EA elements that were affected.

#### PM-7.1 — Offloading (Enhancement)

This requirement is about keeping everyday “support” activities—such as printing, copying files, and distributing software updates—separate from the systems that run your most important services. The risk it addresses is that if these support functions run on the same systems as critical business functions, then a mistake or misuse in the support area could give an attacker an easier way to reach the parts of the organisation that matter most.

To meet this expectation, the organisation must:
- identify which services are not essential, and
- decide where each one should run—either on separate, non-critical systems or components, or, where possible, with a specialist external provider.

The organisation must record its decisions and the reasons for them, carry out and document risk checks for these design choices, and keep up-to-date architecture records that show the separation.

The organisation must also have clear, repeatable procedures for identifying suitable services and moving them to the agreed locations. These procedures must include named responsibilities and involvement from information security and privacy planning staff. Finally, the organisation must be able to demonstrate that the services are actually hosted where it agreed.

##### Offload non-essential services to separate tiers or providers

- Category: Manual
- Priority: Critical

###### WHAT
Move non-essential support functions and services (for example: printing, copying files, and distributing software updates) so they are not run on the same systems as mission- or business-critical functions. Where possible, move them to separate non-critical systems/components, or to an external provider.

###### WHY (control requirement)
This requirement (NIST SP 800-53 PM-7(1) enhancement) says: “Offload {{ insert: param, pm-07.01_odp }} to other systems, system components, or an external provider.” The guidance explains that keeping support functions alongside mission-essential services increases the number of ways an attacker could reach the critical services. Moving them to non-critical systems/components or specialist providers reduces that exposure.

###### HOW (specific approach)
1. **Define what will be offloaded**: Create and keep up to date an inventory of the organisation’s non-essential support services. Make sure it explicitly includes at least the examples in the parameter (pm-07.01_odp): printing, file copying, and software update distribution.
2. **Classify where critical services are hosted**: Create a criticality map for your enterprise application environments (for example: production/mission-critical tiers versus non-critical service tiers). If relevant, also separate cloud accounts/subscriptions or separate on-premises network areas.
3. **Choose offloading targets (start with internal separation)**:
   - Move printing services to a dedicated print service tier (for example, a print gateway/spooler tier) that is separated by network and identity from the mission-critical application tiers.
   - Move file copying/copy services to a dedicated managed file transfer/copy service tier with controlled entry and exit points.
   - Move software update distribution to a dedicated update distribution tier (for example, an internal update repository/distribution service) separated from mission-critical workloads.
4. **Build and deploy with separation enforced**:
   - Use network separation (for example, dedicated subnets/virtual private clouds, firewall rules, and restricted routing) and identity separation (dedicated service accounts/roles, and least-privilege access) so that day-to-day administration and operational access to the offloaded tiers is limited.
   - Use architecture safeguards (for example, policy checks in the delivery pipeline, continuous integration/continuous deployment checks, and infrastructure templates) to prevent these non-essential services being deployed onto critical workload hosts.
5. **Assess and record the decision for each service**:
   - For each offloaded service, record why it was moved, the chosen target (separate system/component or external provider), and any conditions or safeguards needed to maintain confidentiality, integrity, and availability.
   - Link each decision to the relevant architecture record and the associated risk assessment.
6. **Check and keep evidence of where services are hosted**:
   - Keep an auditable catalogue entry for each offloaded service showing where it is currently hosted (system/component, environment, and network boundary) and the linked risk assessment.
   - Regularly confirm (for example, using configuration inventories and deployment records) that the service remains hosted in the agreed offloaded location.

###### WHO (roles responsible)
- **Enterprise/solution architect**: owns the criticality map, reference architectures, and architecture safeguards.
- **Application/infrastructure engineering**: implements the offloaded service tiers and the deployment restrictions.
- **Information security (and privacy planning where relevant)**: reviews and approves risk assessments and separation safeguards.
- **Configuration management / IT operations**: maintains the service catalogue and provides evidence of current hosting.

###### Acceptance criteria
- The organisation has an up-to-date inventory of non-essential support services covering printing, file copying, and software update distribution.
- For each identified non-essential service, there is a documented offloading decision to a separate non-critical system/component (or an external provider where feasible), including the rationale and the linked risk assessment.
- Offloaded services are not deployed on mission/business-critical hosts (confirmed by deployment/configuration evidence).
- Network and identity separation safeguards are in place for each offloaded tier (restricted connectivity and least-privilege access).
- Architecture records and service catalogue entries show the current hosting location and are periodically checked.

###### Actions Required for Compliance

- [ ] Create and keep an up-to-date list (inventory) of non-essential support services, including printing, copying files, and distributing software updates.
- [ ] Define a system for classifying how critical each function is, and assign mission or business-critical functions to the appropriate protected tiers or zones.
- [ ] Choose where to move non-essential work (for each non-essential service, use separate non-critical systems or external providers where this is practical) and record the reasons for your choice.
- [ ] Carry out and record a risk assessment for each offloading decision, including any required safeguards or conditions.
- [ ] Update the architecture records and service catalogue entries to show the current hosting location, and link each entry to its risk assessment.
- [ ] Put in place deployment controls to stop non-essential services being installed on mission or business-critical servers.
- [ ] Periodically check (using configuration and deployment evidence) that the offloaded services are still hosted in the agreed separate locations.

### PM-9 — Risk Management Strategy (Control)

This requirement is about having a clear, organisation-wide plan for deciding which risks are acceptable and which must be fixed to protect the business and people. It covers two areas: the risk of harm from how the organisation uses its systems (including effects on other organisations and the wider public), and the risk to people’s privacy when personal information is handled as part of authorised work. Without a consistent approach, different teams could make conflicting decisions, which could mean serious problems are missed or not dealt with properly, and privacy concerns may be handled unevenly.

The organisation must document one strategy that sets its risk tolerance, explains how risks are assessed and reduced, how risks are checked against what is acceptable, and how risk will be monitored over time. A senior accountable leader must oversee that this strategy aligns with planning and budgets, and it must be applied consistently across the organisation. The strategy must be reviewed at least every year and updated within 30 days of major organisational or system changes (or sooner if needed). Records must be kept of relevant risk results.

##### Security+Privacy risk strategy with tolerance, workflow, and review cadence

- Category: Manual
- Priority: Critical

###### WHAT
Create and maintain a single, organisation-wide **Risk Management Strategy** that covers both **security** and **privacy** risk. The strategy must (1) state the organisation’s **security and privacy risk tolerance**, (2) set out the **acceptable ways to assess risk** and **how to reduce risk**, (3) define how the organisation will **evaluate security and privacy risk** against that tolerance, and (4) set out how risk will be **tracked over time**. The senior accountable official for risk management must align information security management activities with the organisation’s strategic plans, day-to-day operations, and budgeting.

###### WHY (control requirement)
PM-9 requires an organisation-wide risk management strategy that includes security and privacy risk tolerance, approved ways to assess and reduce risk, evaluation of risk against that tolerance, and ongoing risk monitoring. It must also align with planning and budgeting. It must be reviewed and updated **at least annually** and **within 30 days of major organisational or system changes**.

###### HOW (specific steps/approach)
1. **Set up governance and responsibility**: appoint the senior accountable official for risk management (risk executive function) and create a Security & Privacy Risk Executive forum to approve risk tolerance statements, assessment methods, and decisions to accept risk.
2. **Define risk tolerance (security + privacy)**: publish a plain-language risk tolerance statement covering both security impacts (on organisational operations/assets and on other organisations/individuals) and privacy impacts (on individuals from authorised processing of personal information). Include clear decision thresholds (for example, residual risk levels that require executive approval) and escalation triggers.
3. **Standardise the way risk is assessed**: adopt an organisation-approved risk categories and scoring approach that teams must use for both security and privacy assessments (for example, confidentiality/integrity/availability, plus privacy impact factors such as data minimisation and purpose limitation, and the likelihood and severity of harm).
4. **Define risk reduction options and what is acceptable**: document the approved ways to respond to risk (reduce/avoid/transfer/accept) and link them to common control types used across the organisation’s application lifecycle (for example, strengthening identity controls, encryption, logging and monitoring, secure software development lifecycle, vulnerability management, incident response, and controls from vendors/third parties).
5. **Put a risk evaluation process in place**: require every system or project risk assessment to (a) use the PM-9 strategy’s assessment approach, (b) record the assessed risk and the remaining risk after controls, (c) compare the remaining risk to the organisation’s tolerance, and (d) send the outcome to the correct decision-maker (accept/reduce/escalate) with a documented reason.
6. **Run ongoing monitoring and set update triggers**: define how risk will be monitored over time (for example, periodic reassessments, key risk indicators and key performance indicators, vulnerability and incident trends, and signals that controls are working). Ensure the strategy update process is linked to change governance so updates happen **within 30 days** of major organisational or system changes.
7. **Keep evidence and manage it over time**: keep auditable records of (a) the outcomes of annual reviews, (b) updates made within 30 days of major changes, (c) risk evaluation decisions and approvals, and (d) monitoring results used to support reassessments.

###### WHO (role responsible)
- **Senior Accountable Official for Risk Management (risk executive)**: owns the strategy, approves risk tolerance and the assessment approach, and ensures alignment with strategic plans, operational plans, and budgeting.
- **Information Security & Privacy Risk Leads**: maintain the strategy content, assessment approach, risk reduction patterns, and monitoring approach.
- **Application/System Owners and Risk Owners**: carry out assessments using the approved approach and submit decisions for evaluation against the risk tolerance.
- **Governance/Compliance function**: ensures audit evidence is complete and tracks whether reviews and updates are done on time.

###### Acceptance criteria
- A documented, organisation-wide **single** strategy exists covering **security and privacy** risk tolerance, assessment approaches, risk reduction approaches, risk evaluation against tolerance, and monitoring over time.
- The strategy is reviewed **at least annually** and updated **within 30 days** of major organisational or system changes; evidence of review/update dates is kept.
- The risk evaluation process is in place so that system/project risk assessments record assessed risk and remaining risk, compare them to the tolerance, and include documented approval/escalation outcomes.
- Monitoring outputs (for example, key risk indicators and key performance indicators, reassessment triggers, and trend-based signals) are defined and used to inform risk reassessments and strategy updates.

###### Actions Required for Compliance

- [ ] Appoint the senior accountable lead for risk management and set up a Security and Privacy Risk Executive forum to approve decisions.
- [ ] Publish an organisation-wide security and privacy risk tolerance statement that clearly sets the acceptable remaining risk levels (residual risk thresholds) and the specific situations that require escalation.
- [ ] Document and get approval for the organisation’s methods for assessing acceptable security and privacy risks, including the scoring system (taxonomy).
- [ ] Define approved security and privacy risk mitigation approaches—reduce, avoid, transfer, or accept—and the mitigation patterns linked to the organisation’s control capabilities.
- [ ] Set up a risk assessment process that records the remaining risk and compares it against the organisation’s risk tolerance, then routes decisions to either accept the risk, reduce it, or escalate it.
- [ ] Set up ongoing risk monitoring over time, including key risk indicators (KRIs) and key performance indicators (KPIs), clear triggers for when risks must be reassessed, and signals showing whether controls are working effectively. Then link this monitoring into change governance (the process for approving and managing changes).
- [ ] Create an evidence pack and a tracking process to show that the annual review is completed and updated within 30 days after any major organisational or system changes.

### PM-10 — Authorization Process (Control)

This requirement is about ensuring that every system, and every place it is used, is formally approved for both protecting information and protecting people’s personal data before it is allowed to operate. Without this, an organisation could unknowingly run tools or services that are unsafe or mishandle personal information, which could lead to financial loss, legal issues, reputational damage, or harm to customers and staff.

To meet this expectation, the organisation must use a structured approval process that considers security and privacy together. It must clearly name specific people who are responsible for making risk decisions, including a risk executive and the officials who approve each system. The organisation must then link these approval decisions into its wider risk management approach. It must also keep reviewing risks over time, so security and privacy risks are continuously understood and only accepted when appropriate. Any changes must be fed back into the ongoing risk programme.

##### Risk-based authorisation workflow for security & privacy decisions

- Category: Software
- Priority: Critical

###### WHAT
Set up a single, organisation-wide, risk-based approval process that produces a clear security and privacy approval decision for every system and every operating environment. These decisions must be recorded by named, accountable roles and reviewed continuously using results from ongoing monitoring.

###### WHY (control requirement)
PM-10 requires that approval processes for organisational systems and operating environments:
- are built into the organisation’s overall risk management process and the related security and privacy standards and guidance;
- name specific roles, including a risk executive and designated approving officials (and, where relevant, common control provider officials);
- are linked with ongoing monitoring so the organisation continues to understand and accept security and privacy risks to organisational operations and assets, people, other organisations, and the Nation.

###### HOW (specific approach)
Create an “Authorisation Workflow” (using governance, risk and compliance (GRC) and workflow tools) that produces an auditable approval pack and uses decision checkpoints:

1. **Define the approval scope model**: make sure every system and environment (including live, test, and disaster recovery (DR) where applicable) has its own unique approval scope entry, linked to the system/environment catalogue and data classification.
2. **Require two sets of evidence before any approval decision is issued**:
   - security assessment evidence (for example, whether required controls are in place, and a summary of remaining security risk);
   - privacy assessment evidence (for example, privacy impact considerations, and risks from how data is handled or processed);
   - a clear link to the organisation’s security and privacy standards and guidance.
3. **Use named sign-off for each role**:
   - the risk executive records and approves risk acceptance and the escalation rules;
   - the designated approving official(s) approve the approval decision for that system/environment;
   - assessors provide evidence, but do not make or issue the decision.
4. **Record the approval decision in the approval pack**:
   - approved / not approved / approved with conditions;
   - decision date, and the time period it applies to (if used), and the named approvers;
   - any conditions, including due dates and the evidence needed to prove the required fixes are complete.
5. **Link to ongoing monitoring triggers**:
   - configure the workflow to receive outputs from ongoing monitoring (for example, significant vulnerabilities, configuration changes, unusual behaviour involving identities or privileged access, and signals that privacy-relevant data may be exposed);
   - set thresholds that automatically start a “re-approval” process or “update conditions” actions.
6. **Maintain traceability**: ensure the approval pack links to the underlying assessment evidence and monitoring results that led to the decision, and to any later updates to conditions.
7. **Apply the same governance model to operational technology (OT) where applicable**: use the same workflow approach, but adjust evidence collection and monitoring inputs to fit OT constraints (for example, approved OT scanning methods, maintenance windows, and safety constraints), while still requiring security and privacy approval sign-off and ongoing monitoring linkage.

###### WHO
- **Risk executive**: owns how risk acceptance is governed, the escalation criteria, and ensures decisions align with the organisation’s overall risk management.
- **Designated approving officials (per system/environment)**: approve the approval decision outcome (including any conditions).
- **System owners / application owners**: ensure the system/environment scope is complete and that evidence is provided.
- **Security and privacy assessment leads**: produce the security and privacy assessment evidence.
- **GRC/workflow administrator**: sets up the workflow checkpoints, role mappings, and monitoring triggers.

###### Acceptance criteria
- For every system/environment in scope, there is an auditable approval record showing: scope, security assessment evidence, privacy assessment evidence, decision outcome, decision date, the named approving official(s), and (if applicable) conditions with due dates.
- Approval decisions cannot be issued unless both security and privacy evidence are provided and the required roles have signed off.
- Ongoing monitoring results that meet the defined thresholds must trigger documented workflow actions (re-approval or condition updates) and update the approval record accordingly.
- Evidence and decisions are traceable end-to-end (approval pack ↔ assessment evidence ↔ monitoring results).
- The workflow approach is clearly applied to both information technology (IT) and OT systems/environments (with OT-appropriate evidence and monitoring adjustments), while keeping the same decision governance and ongoing monitoring integration.

###### Actions Required for Compliance

- [ ] Create and keep a complete catalogue of systems and environments, with a unique authorisation scope identifier for each one and a named owner.
- [ ] Define how organisational security and privacy standards and guidelines map to specific workflow decision points, and encode those decision points as workflow “gates”.
- [ ] Set up a governance, risk and compliance (GRC) and workflow authorisation pack that requires proof from both a security assessment and a privacy assessment.
- [ ] Assign a named risk executive and designated authorising officials for each system and environment, and require approval through role-based sign-off (role-based access control (RBAC)).
- [ ] Set up continuous monitoring with agreed thresholds that automatically trigger re-authorisation or updates to conditions.
- [ ] Create clear links from each authorisation decision to the assessment evidence and the specific monitoring results that led to the updates.
- [ ] Run a trial using one information technology (IT) system and one operational technology (OT) system to confirm that the required evidence is complete, that the approval (sign-off) process works as intended, and that monitoring alerts trigger the expected actions.

### PM-11 — Mission and Business Process Definition (Control)

This requirement is about making sure the organisation’s mission and main ways of working are clearly written down, with the right safeguards built in from the start. Without this, important activities could be carried out in ways that expose sensitive information, mishandle personal data, or fail to protect people and partners. That could lead to financial loss, reputational damage, regulatory issues, and harm to individuals and other organisations.

To meet this expectation, the organisation must document its mission and business processes. For each one, it must clearly identify what information needs protection and what personal data handling is required, based on how serious the impact would be if the information were compromised. It must also keep records of the risk thinking used to make these decisions.

These documented definitions and protection needs must be reviewed at least every year, and updated whenever there are major changes to the mission, business, or systems.

##### Document mission/process protection needs with annual review triggers

- Category: Manual
- Priority: Critical

###### WHAT
Define and document each mission and business process in a repeatable way. From the potential harm that could happen if something is compromised or breached, explicitly work out and document (a) the information protection needs and (b) the personally identifiable information (PII) processing needs.

###### WHY (control requirement)
PM-11 requires that mission and business process definitions (and the related protection requirements) are documented in line with organisational policies and procedures. It also requires that protection and PII needs are derived from what organisational stakeholders need for their mission and business, and from the organisation’s risk management strategy.  

It further requires understanding the possible adverse impacts, including impacts on confidentiality, integrity, availability, and privacy. It requires using categorisation to identify potential impact, and using privacy risk assessments to prioritise risks to individuals from PII processing across the information life cycle.  

The definitions and related protection requirements must be reviewed and updated at the frequency defined by the organisation.

###### HOW (specific approach)
Set up a standardised “Process-to-Protection” governance workflow that produces outputs that can be audited and are controlled by version number for every mission/business process:

1. **Create a Process Definition Pack template** that requires: process description, roles, system touchpoints, information flows, data categories (including whether PII is processed), and clear sections for security and privacy considerations.
2. **Derive information protection needs** by linking each process to the categorisation results and the adverse impacts of compromise (confidentiality, integrity, availability, plus privacy). Then select the required protection needs from a controlled list (for example: encryption, logging and monitoring, secure disposal, and resilience/availability requirements).
3. **Derive PII processing needs** by running and recording the privacy risk assessment results for the process (including unintended privacy consequences across the information life cycle). Then select the required privacy controls (for example: minimisation, purpose limitation, access restrictions, retention and disposal, handling of individuals’ rights, and breach-related handling).
4. **Record traceability evidence**: each Process Definition Pack must point to the specific risk/categorisation and privacy risk assessment records (or include an auditable reason for “not applicable”).
5. **Set review and change triggers**: require review at least **annually**, and also when there are **major mission, business, or system changes** (as defined by the organisation). Require named approvers for security and privacy decisions, and record sign-off evidence.
6. **Store and control documents** in a controlled repository with version control, access controls, and retention periods aligned to organisational policy.

###### WHO
- **Process Owner / Business Owner**: keeps the Process Definition Pack up to date and ensures it is complete.
- **Information Security Officer / Security Architect**: checks that the categorisation-to-protection decisions are correct and that protection needs are derived properly.
- **Privacy Officer / Data Protection Lead**: checks that the privacy risk assessment linkage is correct and that PII processing needs are derived properly.
- **GRC/Compliance Manager**: ensures the workflow is followed, evidence is captured, and materials are ready for audit.

###### Acceptance criteria
- For every mission/business process in scope, there is a version-controlled Process Definition Pack that is complete against the template.
- Each pack clearly documents: (1) adverse impact considerations (confidentiality, integrity, availability, plus privacy) and (2) the derived information protection needs.
- Where PII is processed, each pack clearly documents the derived PII processing needs and references the relevant privacy risk assessment record(s) (or provides an auditable “not applicable” rationale).
- Evidence exists that the pack is reviewed **at least annually** and updated when there are **major mission/business/system changes**, with named approvals for security and privacy decisions.
- Traceability links to categorisation and privacy risk assessment records are present and can be checked by auditors.

###### Actions Required for Compliance

- [ ] Define and publish a standard “Process Definition Pack” template that covers the mission or business process, how information moves through the process, the impact on confidentiality, integrity, availability and privacy, and whether personal data is present.
- [ ] Set up a controlled process where every “Process Definition Pack” must point to the relevant categorisation and privacy risk assessment records, or include an auditable reason why they do not apply.
- [ ] Create a controlled list of information protection needs and a controlled list of personal data (PII) processing needs, and link each one to the relevant process outputs.
- [ ] Assign named responsibilities (process owner, security reviewer, privacy reviewer) and require proof of approval (sign-off evidence) for decisions about protection measures and personal information (PII) determinations.
- [ ] Set governance review triggers to happen at least once every year and also after major mission, business, or system changes, and ensure all updates are made using version control.
- [ ] Store all Process Definition Packs and the linked risk assessment evidence in a secure, controlled repository. Limit access using access controls and ensure audit-ready traceability (clear records of who accessed what and when).

### PM-12 — Insider Threat Program (Control)

This requirement is about spotting and dealing with suspicious behaviour by people who already have access to your organisation’s information, before it leads to theft, sabotage, or other harm. Without a clear insider threat programme and a team that is trained and practised, warning signs can be missed, responses may be inconsistent, and sensitive personal information (such as workplace or employment records) could be used in the wrong way—creating legal and trust risks.

The organisation must set up an insider threat programme with a senior responsible person who oversees it. It must also create a cross-discipline incident handling team that includes people responsible for information security and privacy, as well as legal support. The team must be able to receive and review concerns. The programme must bring together workplace-related information and evidence from relevant systems to assess risks in one joined-up process, while following privacy and legal rules.

Employees must receive awareness training on what to look for and how to report concerns. The programme must also be reviewed and improved through regular self-checks.

##### Insider threat hub with cross-discipline case handling workflow

- Category: Manual
- Priority: Critical

###### WHAT
Set up an insider threat programme operating model, with a cross-discipline insider threat incident handling team and a central “insider threat hub” case-management process. This should bring together both technical and non-technical information, apply privacy and legal safeguards, and produce decisions that can be independently checked through audit records.

###### WHY (control requirement)
PM-12 requires the organisation to run an insider threat programme that includes a cross-discipline insider threat incident handling team. It must centrally integrate and analyse both technical and non-technical information to identify potential insider threat concerns. Oversight must be provided by a senior responsible official, supported by documented policies and implementation plans, and backed by staff awareness and self-assessment.

###### HOW (specific steps/approach)
1. **Appoint senior oversight**: Nominate a senior official as the accountable owner for the insider threat programme, with documented authority to accept cases, escalate them, and close them.
2. **Form the cross-discipline team**: Define named roles and responsibilities across Information Security/security operations centre (SOC) liaison, Privacy, Legal (or legal counsel support), HR/workplace relations (as applicable), and the incident handling function (this can use existing incident response (IR) / computer security incident response team (CSIRT) processes).
3. **Create the insider threat policy and implementation plan**: Document:
   - what triggers a case to be opened,
   - the full case journey (intake → triage → assessment/investigation → decision → referral/closure),
   - how evidence must be handled,
   - how long information is kept,
   - privacy and legal approval steps before accessing or sharing sensitive HR/workplace information.
4. **Implement the insider threat hub (case management)**: Set up a central case-management workflow (for example, ticketing or orchestration linked with existing SOC/IR tools) that:
   - provides standard ways for people to report concerns,
   - links technical signals (identity and access management events, changes to privileged access, and unusual activity indicators from enterprise monitoring/security information and event management (SIEM)) with non-technical inputs (workplace/HR-relevant concerns provided through approved reporting routes),
   - requires privacy and legal approval before sensitive non-technical information is accessed or used,
   - keeps audit records of decisions, approvals, and requests for evidence.
5. **Define decision and escalation pathways**: Set out documented criteria for when cases are escalated to disciplinary or legal processes, referred to law enforcement (where applicable), or closed with the reasons recorded.
6. **Run awareness and self-assessment**: Provide insider threat awareness training to employees (including how to report concerns) and carry out periodic self-assessments of how well the programme is operating, producing evidence for governance review.

###### WHO (role responsible)
- **Senior responsible official**: programme owner and oversight authority.
- **Insider threat incident handling team**: Information Security/SOC liaison (technical triage), Privacy lead (privacy approval checks), Legal counsel (legal approval checks and advice), HR/workplace relations liaison (non-technical inputs where applicable), and IR/CSIRT liaison (alignment with incident handling).

###### Acceptance criteria
- A documented insider threat policy and implementation plan exist and are approved by the senior responsible official.
- Named cross-discipline team roles are in place, with documented authority to make decisions.
- The insider threat hub workflow supports the full case journey, with privacy and legal approval checks and audit records that cannot be changed.
- Technical and non-technical signals are combined in the hub using defined information sources and approved routes for sharing data.
- Evidence exists of at least one end-to-end test case (table-top exercise or live) showing intake, triage, privacy/legal approval checks, decision-making, and closure with audit records.
- Insider threat awareness training is delivered to employees, and self-assessment results are produced on a defined schedule.

###### Actions Required for Compliance

- [ ] Appoint a senior accountable person and publish their authority for accepting cases, escalating concerns, and closing cases
- [ ] Set up named roles for a cross-discipline insider threat incident response team, with clear responsibilities (Security/security operations centre liaison, Privacy, Legal, Human Resources (HR)/workplace liaison, and Incident Response/Computer Security Incident Response Team (CSIRT) liaison), and document their terms of reference.
- [ ] Create and get approval for the insider threat policy and its implementation plan. This should cover what starts a case, how cases are managed from start to finish, how evidence is handled, how long records are kept, and the privacy and legal approval checkpoints.
- [ ] Set up an insider threat “case management” workflow inside the existing security operations centre (SOC) and incident response (IR) tools. This should support the full process from receiving a report, to sorting and prioritising it, to assessing it, to making a decision, and then either referring it on or closing it—while keeping complete audit records.
- [ ] Define and put in place approved ways to share and link data from technical sources (identity and access management, monitoring, and security information and event management) and from non-technical sources (human resources and workplace inputs), with privacy and legal checks before any data is used.
- [ ] Run an end-to-end test (a discussion-based tabletop exercise or a controlled live test) to show that cases are handled in a way that can be audited, and that the correct escalation, referral, and closure decisions are made.
- [ ] Provide insider threat awareness training and carry out regular self-checks, keeping records of the results and the improvement actions taken

### PM-13 — Security and Privacy Workforce (Control)

This requirement is about making sure the right people are trained and developed for the organisation’s security and privacy responsibilities—not just recruited and left to figure it out. Without a structured approach, staff may not have the knowledge to prevent incidents, handle sensitive information correctly, or recognise early warning signs. This can result in data being misused, breaches of legal and regulatory requirements, financial loss, and harm to individuals.

The organisation is expected to set up separate, documented development and improvement programmes for security and for privacy. These programmes should clearly state what each role must know and be able to do, and who is responsible for running them. The organisation should provide training matched to specific roles, set measurable standards to assess and improve the skills of both current staff and job applicants, and use these standards in real hiring and promotion decisions.

Finally, the organisation should define clear career paths so qualified staff can move into higher-responsibility roles, and keep records showing that the programmes, training, and improvement actions are in place.

##### Security & privacy workforce programme with role KSAs and qualification standards

- Category: Manual
- Priority: Critical

###### WHAT
Set up and keep running documented **security** and **privacy** workforce development and improvement programmes. These programmes must:
- define the **knowledge, skills, and abilities (KSAs)** needed to carry out security and privacy duties
- provide **role-based training** for different job types
- use **measurable qualification standards** to check and build competence for both current staff (**incumbents**) and new candidates (**applicants**)
- include **security/privacy career paths** (where applicable) to support progression into higher-responsibility roles

###### WHY (control requirement)
PM-13 requires the organisation to **establish a security and privacy workforce development and improvement programme**. The guidance also specifies that the programme(s) must include:
1. defining KSAs for security and privacy duties
2. creating role-based training programmes
3. setting standards and guidance for measuring and building individual qualifications for incumbents and applicants
4. optionally including security/privacy career paths to encourage progression and help fill roles with greater responsibility

###### HOW (specific approach)
1. **Create two programme documents** (one for security, one for privacy). Assign named programme leads (for example, Head of Cybersecurity Training and Development; Privacy/DP Officer Training and Development). Each programme must state: scope, governance, covered roles, training schedule, assessment approach, how evidence is kept, and how improvements will be made over time.
2. **Define job categories and KSAs** for security and privacy roles relevant to the organisation’s application environment (for example, security operations centre/monitoring roles, security engineering roles, incident response coordinators, privacy governance roles, privacy operations/assessment roles). For each role, define measurable KSAs linked to the organisation’s security and privacy responsibilities.
3. **Build role-based training pathways** for each role using a training matrix that sets out: initial onboarding training, how often refresh training is needed, practical exercises (for example, scenario/tabletop sessions for incident handling; privacy impact scenario reviews), and how training will be delivered (for example, e-learning, workshops, practical labs).
4. **Set measurable qualification standards** for incumbents and applicants. For each role, specify: how people will be assessed (for example, scenario-based tabletop scoring, practical lab results, structured interviews with competency scoring, peer review), the pass level, when re-assessment is required (for example, when the role changes, when major controls/processes change, or when regulations change), and what evidence is required to qualify.
5. **Put qualification measurement into day-to-day people processes** by building the standards into HR and workforce activities: onboarding checklists, internal moves/promotions, and checks for external hiring. Make sure qualification decisions are based on evidence against the standards, not only on course completion.
6. **Define security and privacy career paths** with progression steps that require proven competence (for example, Analyst → Senior Analyst → Lead; Privacy Analyst → Privacy Lead → Privacy Governance/Programme Lead). For each step, state what changes (scope, decision-making authority, additional KSAs, and additional assessments).
7. **Run continuous improvement**: review training effectiveness and assessment results regularly (for example, at least annually), update KSAs and training when incidents, audit findings, or regulatory/privacy requirements change, and record the actions taken to improve the programme.

###### WHO (responsible roles)
- **Programme Owner (Security Workforce Development):** cybersecurity leadership responsible for security training governance.
- **Programme Owner (Privacy Workforce Development):** the Privacy/DP Officer (or equivalent) responsible for privacy training governance.
- **HR/People Operations Partner:** ensures hiring, onboarding, and promotion processes include the qualification standards.
- **Security Training Subject Matter Experts / Privacy Subject Matter Experts:** maintain KSAs, training content, and assessment scoring guides.
- **Line Managers:** ensure staff complete required training and are assessed as qualified for their roles.

###### Acceptance criteria
- Two documented programmes exist (security and privacy) with named owners, scope, and a continuous improvement approach.
- KSAs are defined for security and privacy roles and are linked to the role-based training pathways.
- Measurable qualification standards exist for incumbents and applicants, including assessment methods, pass levels, and required evidence.
- Qualification decisions for at least one security role and one privacy role are clearly based on evidence (for example, assessment records and sign-offs exist for incumbents and/or applicants).
- Security and privacy career paths are documented, with progression steps tied to evidence of competence.
- Programme records (training assignments/completions, assessment outcomes, and improvement actions) are kept in a way that can be checked during an audit.

###### Actions Required for Compliance

- [ ] Appoint named owners for the security workforce programme and the privacy workforce programme, and publish the scope of their governance responsibilities.
- [ ] Define a security job-role structure and a privacy job-role structure, and document the knowledge, skills and abilities (KSAs) required for each role.
- [ ] Create role-based training pathways and a training matrix that maps each job role to its initial training, how often training is refreshed, and the practical exercises to complete.
- [ ] Set clear, measurable qualification requirements for current job holders and applicants. This should include how candidates will be assessed, the minimum pass score, what proof they must provide, and when they must be reassessed.
- [ ] Build qualification standards into onboarding, internal transfers and promotions, and hiring processes so decisions are based on evidence.
- [ ] Document security and privacy career paths, with progression levels based on demonstrated competence and any additional knowledge, skills and abilities (KSAs) and assessments.
- [ ] Create a complete, auditable record of training assignments, assessment results, approvals (sign-offs), and updates for continuous improvement.

### PM-14 — Testing, Training, and Monitoring (Control)

This requirement is about making sure the organisation has a clear, organisation-wide plan for checking, training, and ongoing oversight of both security and privacy, and that these plans are actually carried out and kept up to date. Without this, testing, staff training, and ongoing monitoring can become inconsistent or outdated, which can lead to important weaknesses or privacy risks being missed, and the organisation responding to risks at the wrong time or in the wrong way.  

To meet this expectation, the organisation must:  
- create and maintain separate plans for security and for privacy that cover testing, training for the relevant roles, and monitoring across all organisational systems;  
- ensure the plans continue to run as scheduled, with evidence showing that the activities were completed; and  
- regularly review the plans so they still match the organisation’s overall approach to managing risk and the organisation-wide priorities for how risks will be handled, updating them when needed.

##### Security & privacy testing/training/monitoring portfolio with evidence

- Category: Manual
- Priority: Critical

###### WHAT
Set up and keep an organisation-wide **Testing, Training, and Monitoring Portfolio** covering both **security** and **privacy** activities across the relevant systems in the organisation (including the enterprise business application and the infrastructure that supports it). The portfolio must state what will be tested, who will be trained, what will be monitored, how often this will happen, and what proof (evidence) will be produced.

###### WHY (control requirement)
PM-14 requires an organisation-wide process for **security and privacy testing, training, and monitoring** to ensure these activities are **coordinated**, **based on up-to-date assessments of threats and weaknesses**, and supported by **plans that are kept current and reviewed** to ensure they match the organisation’s **risk management strategy** and **risk response priorities**.

###### HOW (specific steps/approach)
1. **Create two plan types** (Security and Privacy) within one portfolio. Each plan must use a standard template that requires: systems in scope, frequency, responsible roles, the test/training/monitoring activities, the evidence to be produced, and how each item links to current threat and vulnerability assessments.
2. **Base plan content on risk** by linking each portfolio item to the organisation’s current risk register and the latest threat and vulnerability assessment results (for example, the top threats, known vulnerabilities, and exposure trends). This ensures testing and monitoring coverage changes when risk changes.
3. **Track delivery and keep audit-ready evidence**: add portfolio items into the organisation’s normal workflow or ticketing system so that every scheduled activity generates the required evidence (scan reports, test results, training completion records, and monitoring review logs). If anything deviates from the plan, the reason must be documented and approved.
4. **Run regular governance reviews** (for example, quarterly) to confirm the portfolio plans still align with: (a) the organisation’s risk management strategy and (b) enterprise risk response priorities (risk treatment actions). Record the outcomes and update plans as needed.
5. **Coordinate across IT and operational technology (OT)** by including OT-relevant scope at a system-of-systems level (where applicable). Ensure the portfolio does not leave important IT/OT integration points to ad-hoc testing or monitoring.
6. **For the enterprise business application**, ensure the portfolio clearly covers: security testing (for example, vulnerability scanning and application security testing), security training (role-based for developers, administrators, and operations), security monitoring (log review and alert coverage), privacy testing (for example, privacy impact checks where applicable), privacy training (role-based for people who handle data), and privacy monitoring (for example, checks for privacy-relevant events and controls).

###### WHO
- **Information Security Manager / Chief Information Security Officer (CISO) office**: owns the Security Testing/Training/Monitoring plan and the governance review.
- **Privacy Officer / Data Protection Lead**: owns the Privacy Testing/Training/Monitoring plan and the governance review.
- **Application Owner / Product Security Lead**: ensures the enterprise application portfolio items are accurate and that evidence is produced.
- **Security Operations Centre (SOC) / Monitoring Lead**: ensures monitoring activities and evidence requirements are met.
- **Risk Management Function**: provides the risk strategy, risk response priorities, and threat and vulnerability assessment inputs.

###### Acceptance criteria
- A current portfolio exists for both **security** and **privacy**, with named owners, defined scope, agreed frequencies, and evidence requirements for all systems in scope.
- Portfolio items are clearly linked to the latest threat and vulnerability assessment outputs and the current risk register.
- Evidence exists for activities completed in the most recent cycle (or for any documented and approved deviations, including the rationale).
- Governance review records show checks against the organisation’s risk management strategy and enterprise risk response priorities, and plans are updated when misalignment is found.
- Evidence shows coordination between IT and OT where relevant (no critical system is left without an assigned element covering testing, training, and monitoring).

###### Actions Required for Compliance

- [ ] Create and publish a standard template for security and privacy testing, training, and monitoring plans. The template must include the required information: scope, how often it is done, who is responsible, what proof or records are kept, and how activities link to identified risks.
- [ ] Create one organisation-wide portfolio that brings together all plan items for the in-scope systems, including the enterprise business application and the services that support it.
- [ ] For each portfolio item, link it to the current risk register and the latest threat and vulnerability assessment results, and record this mapping.
- [ ] Integrate portfolio delivery into the organisation’s workflow or ticketing system so that every scheduled activity automatically generates the required evidence, and any changes or deviations are only allowed with documented approval.
- [ ] Carry out a governance review at least every quarter to confirm that activities remain aligned with the organisation’s risk management strategy and its enterprise risk response priorities, and update plans when any misalignment is found.
- [ ] Assign clear accountable owners (Chief Information Security Officer (CISO) office and Privacy Officer). Ensure the security operations centre (SOC) and monitoring leads, and the application owners, confirm that monitoring coverage is in place and that training completion evidence has been provided.

### PM-15 — Security and Privacy Groups and Associations (Control)

This expectation is about maintaining steady, formal contact with the right external security and privacy communities, so your organisation can learn, stay up to date, and share lessons safely. Without this, staff may miss new risks, outdated practices may continue, and the organisation may respond too late to real problems or share sensitive information in a way that breaches laws or internal rules.

To meet this, the organisation must set up and maintain ongoing contact with selected security and privacy groups and associations. These should be chosen based on the organisation’s mission and business needs, and named individuals must be assigned to manage these relationships. The organisation must document how these contacts are selected, maintained, and used; keep records of the groups and memberships; and use the information received to support regular staff training and to update internal guidance.

Where appropriate, the organisation must also share relevant information about threats, weaknesses, and incidents with these groups in an authorised way that is consistent with all applicable legal and policy requirements.

##### Governed external security/privacy contact portfolio with evidence

- Category: Manual
- Priority: High

###### WHAT
Set up and make routine ongoing contact with selected security and privacy community groups and associations. Create a managed “contact portfolio” (register) that lists the selected groups, names responsible owners, sets how often engagement should happen, and records proof of engagement and how the organisation has used the information internally.

###### WHY (control requirement)
PM-15 requires the organisation to “establish and institutionalize contact with selected groups and associations within the security and privacy communities”. It also requires that groups are chosen based on the organisation’s mission and business functions, that contact continues over time, and that shared threat, weakness, and incident information—along with related context—is used in line with all applicable laws, directives, policies, regulations, standards, and guidance.

###### HOW (specific steps/approach)
1. Create an approved procedure (owned by Security Governance and Privacy Governance) that defines:
   - how groups/associations are selected based on the organisation’s mission and business functions;
   - the minimum engagement frequency for each type of group (for example: professional association, forum/special interest group, peer group);
   - roles and responsibilities (named owners for security and privacy contacts);
   - rules for what information can be shared or received (including data classification, anonymisation/redaction, and when legal/privacy review is required);
   - record-keeping requirements and what evidence must be kept.
2. Keep an auditable register for security community contacts and a separate register for privacy community contacts. For each entry, record:
   - the group/association name and type (special interest group, professional association, forum, news group, users’ group, peer group);

   - the named owner(s) and backup owner(s);
   - start date and review date;
   - engagement frequency and the expected outputs.
3. Make engagement routine by building the portfolio into the organisation’s regular security and privacy working cycle (for example, quarterly security operations and privacy steering). Require each named owner to complete the minimum engagement activities for the agreed frequency and to produce traceable outputs.
4. Use an “intake-to-control” process for external information received:
   - log the received insight (including the source and date);
   - check how relevant it is to internal systems and controls (link it to the affected control areas and operating procedures);
   - update internal guidance/standards and/or trigger training updates where needed;
   - record decisions and the reasons for them.
5. Apply compliance checks before sharing any external threat/weakness/incident information and related context:
   - confirm the data classification;
   - anonymise/redact personal data and confidential organisational details unless sharing is explicitly authorised;
   - obtain legal/privacy approval for any regulated or personal information.
6. Review the portfolio regularly (at least once a year, and after major organisational changes) to add or remove groups based on mission relevance, and to confirm there is ongoing evidence of engagement.

###### WHO (role responsible)

- Data Protection Officer (DPO) delegate / Privacy Governance Lead: owns the privacy community contact procedure and the privacy contact portfolio.
- Security Operations Centre (SOC)/Security Operations (SecOps) Lead (or Threat Intelligence function): supports intake of threat/weakness/incident information and mapping it to internal actions.
- Legal/Compliance and Privacy Counsel (as required): approves any sharing that involves personal data or regulated details.

###### Acceptance criteria
- A documented, approved PM-15 contact procedure exists and is version-controlled.
- Security and privacy contact registers exist, with named owners, purposes, engagement frequency, and evidence links for each selected group.
- Evidence of ongoing engagement is available for each selected group for the most recent defined frequency period (for example, quarterly), and internal use is recorded (for example, updated guidance/operating procedures/training triggers).
- Any external sharing of threat/weakness/incident information includes documented compliance checks (data classification check, anonymisation/redaction, and legal/privacy approval where required).
- Portfolio review records show that group selection is based on mission/business functions and that groups are reassessed periodically.

###### Actions Required for Compliance

- [ ] Draft and get approval for an External Security and Privacy Community Contact Procedure. This should cover how contacts are chosen, how often they are contacted, what information can be shared, and how contact records are kept.
- [ ] Create and keep auditable records of security and privacy contacts, including the named owner, the reason for contact, how often it is reviewed, and links to supporting evidence.
- [ ] For each selected group, assign a named owner and a backup, and confirm these responsibilities in the role documentation.

### PM-16 — Threat Awareness Program (Control)

This requirement is about keeping your organisation alert to real-world cyber threats by regularly informing staff and sharing threat warnings with other organisations. Without a structured approach, attackers can take advantage of the fact that threats change quickly and become more advanced. In that case, a security breach may succeed simply because people did not know what to look for or what to do next.

To meet this expectation, the organisation must run a written threat awareness programme with clear, day-to-day procedures. These procedures must be based on the organisation’s risk assessment results for relevant threats. The organisation must appoint named people to run the programme and to manage sharing with other organisations. It must also identify which external organisations it shares with, and put practical arrangements in place to exchange information through agreed one-to-one or group arrangements.

The organisation must share relevant information, such as threats that have been observed, actions that have worked elsewhere, and warning signs staff should watch for. Sensitive information must be protected appropriately through suitable agreements. The organisation must also keep the required records to show how the programme and information sharing are carried out.

##### Threat awareness programme with approved cross-org intelligence sharing

- Category: Manual
- Priority: High

###### WHAT
Set up and run a documented threat awareness programme that (1) continuously updates relevant staff on current threats, including **threat events (how attacks are carried out, the methods used, and the steps involved)**, **effective ways to reduce impact**, and **signs and warnings**, and (2) provides a **cross-organisation way to share threat information** through **approved two-party and/or multi-party arrangements**.

###### WHY (control requirement)
PM-16 requires the organisation to **implement a threat awareness programme** that includes a **cross-organisation way to share threat information**. The guidance highlights that attackers change quickly (including advanced persistent threats), and that sharing threat information (events, effective mitigations, and signs/warnings) can improve how well the organisation defends itself.

###### HOW (specific steps/approach)
1. **Create a threat awareness programme document set** (policy + procedures) that defines scope, governance, timing (for example, scheduled briefings plus updates triggered by new events), and the end-to-end process: **intake → triage → validation → internal distribution → external sharing → recordkeeping**.
2. **Assign named roles** to run the programme and to manage cross-organisation sharing (for example, Threat Intelligence Lead, Security Communications Owner, External Sharing Approver). Set clear approval points for what can be shared internally and externally.
3. **Define external sharing partners and arrangement type** (two-party and/or multi-party). Record approved contacts, the agreed message format, and how to handle sensitive information (including what must be removed or protected).
4. **Set up an intelligence-to-awareness process** using the organisation’s existing security tools (for example, security operations centre/security operations workflows integrated with security information and event management (SIEM) and observability tools where available):
   - Receive threat information from partner/vendor feeds and lessons learned from incidents.
   - Convert it into a consistent internal format that clearly records: **threat event context (tactics, techniques, and procedures)**, **mitigations that worked**, and **signs and warnings**.
   - Require analyst checks and confirmation before anything is shared.
5. **Deliver internal threat awareness** through controlled channels (for example, a security bulletin portal, targeted alerts for specific roles, and short training updates). Make sure every message includes the three required elements: **what is happening (threat events/tactics, techniques, and procedures)**, **what mitigations are effective**, and **what to watch for (signs and warnings)**.
6. **Keep audit-ready records**: programme policy/procedures, partner list and agreements/handling rules (or references to them), proof of the workflow (intake/validation/distribution logs), and examples of internal and external sharing communications.

###### WHO
- **Threat Intelligence Lead**: responsible for the intake/triage/validation process and the intelligence-to-awareness template.
- **Security Communications Owner** (for example, Security Awareness Manager): responsible for internal distribution content and timing.
- **External Sharing Approver** (for example, Chief Information Security Officer (CISO) delegate/Legal-security liaison): approves external sharing content, channels, and handling rules.
- **Security Operations Centre (SOC)/Security Operations (SecOps) Analysts**: check the threat information and produce practical signs/warnings and mitigations.

###### Acceptance criteria
- A current **threat awareness programme policy and procedures** exists and defines the workflow, timing, and scope.
- Named individuals/roles are recorded for running the programme and approving external sharing.
- At least one **two-party and/or multi-party** sharing arrangement is documented, including partner list, approved channels/contacts, and handling/redaction rules.
- Internal threat awareness communications are produced using a template that consistently includes **threat events (tactics, techniques, and procedures)**, **effective mitigations**, and **signs and warnings**.
- Evidence shows the workflow was used (for example, validated threat items leading to internal distribution and, where applicable, external sharing) and that **records are kept** for audit.

###### Actions Required for Compliance

- [ ] Draft and get approval for the threat awareness programme policy and procedures, including the workflow (intake → triage → validate → disseminate → share → record) and how often the documents are updated (update cadence).
- [ ] Nominate specific named people for programme operations and for approving external sharing, and document the approval steps (gates) for both internal and external communications.
- [ ] Identify and record the external threat intelligence sharing partners, and state whether the arrangements are one-to-one (bilateral) and/or involve multiple organisations (multilateral). Also record the approved contact points and the communication channels to use.

- [ ] Set up a controlled internal way to share information (for example, role-based security bulletins or alerts) so that every bulletin includes the three required elements.
- [ ] Set up recordkeeping for programme governance, proof of workflow activity, and examples of internal and external sharing communications for audit purposes.

#### PM-16.1 — Automated Means for Sharing Threat Intelligence (Enhancement)

This requirement is about making sure the organisation can share information about real threats with other organisations quickly, in a format they can use straight away. Without automated sharing, threat information may be sent late, in different formats, or without enough detail for others to take action. This increases the risk that attacks spread before anyone can spot and stop them.

To meet this expectation, the organisation must use automated tools and processes to send threat information to agreed external partners without manual re-typing. The information shared must include specific, practical details that can be used to detect threats. The organisation must also set out what kinds of threat information its monitoring is intended to look for, use widely recognised information formats, and ensure the shared information can be turned into updates for its own monitoring and detection.

The organisation must document how sharing works, keep records up to date, assign named responsibility for the threat-awareness programme and for sharing information across organisations, and include these activities in its information security and privacy planning, based on the results of relevant risk assessments.

##### Automated STIX/TAXII threat intel sharing with detection-as-code

- Category: Software
- Priority: Critical

###### WHAT
Set up an automated, standards-based way to share threat intelligence. This will exchange practical “threat clues” (observables/indicators) with agreed external partners using automated connections (not one-off manual messages), and it will automatically send approved intelligence into the organisation’s monitoring and detection tools.

###### WHY (control requirement)
PM-16(1) Enhancement requires the organisation to “use automated mechanisms to maximise the effectiveness of sharing threat intelligence information”. The guidance notes that effective sharing depends on knowing which threat clues sensors should look for, and on using well-established frameworks, services, and tools to share quickly and feed relevant detection rules into monitoring tools.

###### HOW (specific steps/approach)
1. **Standards-based threat intelligence (TI) exchange layer**: Set up a central Threat Intelligence (TI) hub that supports **Structured Threat Information Expression version 2.x (STIX 2.x)** and **Trusted Automated eXchange of Indicator Information version 2 (TAXII)** (or an equivalent standards-based application programming interface (API) method) to automatically publish and receive information with external partners.
2. **Allowed indicator agreement + automated checks**: Define an “allowed indicator contract” (which indicator types and fields are permitted, plus required details such as confidence, validity/expiry, and classification/handling markings). Use automated schema validation (policy-as-code) to check every item before it is published.
3. **Automated sharing workflow with approvals**: Configure the process so that only **approved** TI items are packaged into STIX objects and sent to **authorised** partners via TAXII. Remove manual re-typing by generating the outgoing message directly from the TI hub record.
4. **Detection updates as code**: Create mappings from indicator types to internal detection controls (for example, security information and event management (SIEM) watchlists/correlation rules, endpoint detection and response (EDR) indicator feeds, and DNS/web/proxy blocklists). When an approved item is shared, automatically create a traceable detection update/change record and deploy it through the organisation’s standard change process (test to production, with rollback).
5. **Traceability and partner acknowledgement**: Keep end-to-end evidence for each item, including the intelligence item ID, analyst approval time, publish time, partner destination, and partner receipt/acknowledgement where supported by the TAXII workflow or partner API. Keep the automation logs for audit purposes.
6. **Security for the automated exchange**: Use mutual authentication and least-privilege credentials for the TI hub and automation services (for example, scoped API tokens or workload identities). Limit partner publishing and receiving to pre-authorised endpoints.

###### WHO (role responsible)
- **Cyber Threat Intelligence Lead**: owns the indicator contract, approval workflow, and partner onboarding criteria.
- **Security Operations Centre (SOC) Engineering / Detection Engineering**: owns the detection-as-code mappings, rule/list deployment logic, and rollback criteria.
- **Security Architecture / Platform Team**: owns TI hub configuration, TAXII/STIX integration, and secure service-to-service authentication.
- **Information Security Governance**: ensures the evidence and retention approach meets organisational audit requirements.

###### Acceptance criteria
- The organisation can share threat intelligence with at least one external partner using an automated STIX/TAXII workflow (with no manual re-typing of threat clues/indicators).
- Only TI items that pass the allowed indicator contract (schema validation) are published.
- Approved intelligence automatically triggers updates to at least one monitoring or detection mechanism (for example, SIEM watchlists/rules or an EDR indicator feed), with a traceable change record.
- For every published item, end-to-end audit evidence is available: item ID, approval time, publish time, partner destination, and partner acknowledgement/receipt where available.
- Automated exchange uses authenticated, least-privilege service credentials and restricts partner endpoints to an authorised list.

###### Actions Required for Compliance

- [ ] Choose and set up a TI hub that supports STIX 2.x and TAXII so partners can exchange information automatically.
- [ ] Define what an approved indicator must contain (indicator types and fields, required supporting information, and expiry and handling markings), then implement checks to ensure incoming data matches this format.
- [ ] Set up an approval step so that only approved STIX objects are sent through the Trusted Automated eXchange of Indicator Information (TAXII), without anyone needing to re-enter the data manually.
- [ ] Set up “detection-as-code” rules that link each indicator type to the right controls in a security information and event management (SIEM) system, endpoint detection and response (EDR) tools, and network protections. Then build these rules into the change process so they move from testing to live use, with a rollback option if something goes wrong.
- [ ] Enable end-to-end logging and evidence capture for every intelligence item, including approvals, publishing, partner destination details, and receipt or acknowledgement (where available).
- [ ] Secure the exchange using mutual authentication and account permissions limited to the minimum needed, and restrict partner endpoints to an approved allowlist.

### PM-17 — Protecting Controlled Unclassified Information on External Systems (Control)

This requirement is about making sure sensitive information that is not classified is handled correctly, even when it is shared with partners, suppliers, or other outside services. Without clear rules, staff could accidentally send or store this information in the wrong place, under the wrong circumstances, or without the right safeguards. This could result in legal breaches, contractual problems, reputational damage, or harm to people and organisations.

To meet this expectation, the organisation must produce written policy and step-by-step procedures explaining how controlled unclassified information is protected when it is processed, stored, or sent using external systems. These rules must also follow all relevant laws, government instructions, organisational policies, regulations, and standards. The policy and procedures must also explain how these protections are enforced through contracts.

Finally, the organisation must review and update both the policy and the procedures at least once every year, and keep records showing that they are maintained.

##### External-systems CUI handling standard with annual review evidence

- Category: Manual
- Priority: Critical

###### WHAT
Create and maintain a written **Controlled Unclassified Information (CUI) policy** and **CUI procedures** that clearly explain how **Controlled Unclassified Information (CUI)** is protected when it is **handled, stored, or sent using external systems** (for example: cloud/software-as-a-service, partner environments, contractor-managed systems, external email/file sharing, and third-party application programming interfaces (APIs)). Include **allowed uses/conditions** and **contracting requirements** so external providers and contractors must follow the same CUI protection and sharing rules. Carry out an **annual review and update** of both the policy and the procedures, and keep proof that this has been done.

###### WHY (control requirement)
PM-17 requires that CUI on **external systems** is protected using **organisation-defined, enforceable policy and procedures**, including through **contracting processes**. It also requires an **annual review/update** of both the policy and the procedures (the organisation sets the frequency, which is **annually**).

###### HOW (specific steps/approach)
1. **Define scope and external-systems categories** in the policy/procedures (what counts as an external system for this organisation: cloud/software-as-a-service, managed service providers, partner networks, contractor-managed environments, external collaboration tools, and third-party API endpoints).
2. **Publish one authoritative “CUI Handling Standard for External Systems”** that the policy and procedures refer to. The standard must state:
   - the **allowed uses/conditions** for CUI on external systems,
   - the **required protection measures** (for example: encryption while data is travelling and when stored, where applicable; access restrictions; secure sharing rules; and logging expectations that match organisational standards),
   - the **sharing/disclosure rules** (what may or may not be shared, and under what conditions),
   - **responsibilities and named roles** (for example: data owner, system owner, security/compliance approver, procurement/contract manager).
3. **Use the procedures as a required step-by-step process** for any external-system use involving CUI:
   - label the data as CUI,
   - choose the external-system category,
   - request approval to use that external system for that type of CUI,
   - check that the external system meets the standard’s conditions,
   - make sure the contract/procurement documents include the required CUI protection clauses,
   - keep proof of approval (who approved, which version of the standard was used, which CUI types, which external system, and which conditions were applied).
4. **Apply contracting requirements through procurement checks**:
   - require use of a standard set/template of contract clauses for CUI on external systems,
   - stop approvals if contracting confirmation is missing (or if the clauses cannot be applied).
5. **Set the annual review and update process**:
   - review the CUI policy and CUI procedures at least **annually** (organisation-defined frequency),
   - review the referenced external-systems handling standard at least **annually** as part of the same governance cycle,
   - record whether changes are needed, and if so, update the documents and re-approve any affected external-system authorisations.
6. **Create audit-ready proof**:
   - keep review records (date, reviewers, results, reason for changes),
   - keep proof of external-system approvals (including the version of the standard used).

###### WHO (role responsible)
- **Information Security / CUI Governance Lead**: owns the CUI policy, procedures, and the annual review.
- **Data Owners / System Owners**: approve the allowed uses/conditions for specific CUI types on specific external systems.
- **Procurement / Contract Management**: ensures the required contracting clauses/templates are used.
- **Application/System Security Architect** (for this enterprise application): ensures the application’s external connections follow the approved process and that evidence is captured.

###### Acceptance criteria
- A written **CUI policy** exists that explicitly covers CUI on **external systems**, including allowed uses/conditions and contracting requirements.
- A written **CUI procedures** document exists that gives staff clear step-by-step instructions for protecting CUI on external systems, including an approval process and how to record evidence.
- Contracting processes include enforceable CUI protection clauses/templates for external systems, with procurement checks preventing approval if clause confirmation is not provided.
- The organisation can show it has completed an **annual review/update** of both the policy and procedures (and the referenced standard), including recorded outcomes and proof of maintenance.
- For a sample of external-system authorisations involving CUI, the organisation can show approval evidence: identity of the approver, standard version, CUI type, external-system category, and the conditions applied.

###### Actions Required for Compliance

- [ ] Draft and get approval for a written controlled unclassified information (CUI) policy that clearly covers controlled unclassified information (CUI) stored or handled on external systems. The policy must state what is allowed, under what conditions, and what contract requirements apply.
- [ ] Create written controlled unclassified information (CUI) procedures that turn the policy into clear, step-by-step instructions for staff to follow when using external systems for controlled unclassified information (CUI).
- [ ] Create a clear classification (taxonomy) for external systems, and link each category to the required controlled unclassified information (CUI) handling conditions in the referenced handling standard.
- [ ] Set up an approval process that requires: (1) confirming the information is classified as controlled unclassified information (CUI), (2) choosing the correct external-system category, (3) applying the standard version label, and (4) recording supporting evidence.
- [ ] Update procurement and contract templates, and the approval steps (“contracting gates”), so that clauses protecting controlled unclassified information (CUI) are required for authorisations of external systems.
- [ ] Assign named responsibilities (data owner, system owner, security and compliance approver, procurement and contract manager) and publish the RACI (responsible, accountable, consulted, informed) within the policy and procedures.
- [ ] Carry out and record an annual review and update of the controlled unclassified information (CUI) policy and the controlled unclassified information (CUI) procedures (and the referenced standard), and keep evidence showing the results.

### PM-18 — Privacy Program Plan (Control)

This requirement is about having a clear, organisation-wide privacy plan that everyone can follow. - what resources are dedicated to it

### PM-21 — Accounting of Disclosures (Control)

This requirement is about keeping a clear, up-to-date record of when and where the organisation shares people’s personal information with others. Without this, it would be difficult to explain what happened, who received the information, and why. That could result in unfair treatment, incorrect decisions, or people being unable to challenge mistakes or misuse.

To meet this expectation, the organisation must record every time personal information is shared, including:
- the date
- what information was shared
- the reason for sharing
- the name and address (or other contact details) of the person or organisation that received it

These records must be kept for as long as the personal information is kept, or for five years after the sharing took place, whichever is longer. The organisation must also be able to provide the record of the sharing to the individual concerned when they make a request.

- Priority: Critical

### PM-22 — Personally Identifiable Information Quality Management (Control)

This requirement is about making sure the personal information you hold about people is correct, up to date, and complete, and that people can challenge it if it is not. Without this, wrong or out-of-date details can lead to unfair decisions, missed benefits or services, or the accidental sharing of incorrect information that damages someone’s reputation or position.

The organisation must set out and keep current organisation-wide rules and step-by-step procedures for regularly checking personal information for accuracy, relevance, how recent it is, and completeness across the entire way it is used, stored, and shared. This includes correcting or deleting information that is wrong or no longer needed, and telling the affected individuals, and any other relevant organisations, when changes are made.

The organisation must also provide a clear and easy-to-use way for people to appeal if their request to correct or delete information is refused. This should include giving reasons for the refusal, recording the person’s objections, offering a review, and communicating the outcome back to the person.

##### Organisation-wide PII quality reviews, correction/deletion & appeals

- Category: Manual
- Priority: Critical

###### WHAT
Create and document organisation-wide policies and procedures for **personal information (PII) quality management** across the full PII information life cycle (creation/collection, use, processing, storage/maintenance, sharing, disclosure, and final disposal). The procedures must explain how the organisation checks **accuracy, relevance, timeliness, and completeness**, how it **corrects or deletes** incorrect or out-of-date PII (including when it has discretion), how it **notifies individuals** (and other relevant organisations) when changes are made, and how it handles **requests that are refused** for correction/deletion through a clear **appeals and review** process.

###### WHY (control requirement)
PM-22 requires the organisation to **create and document organisation-wide policies and procedures** for PII quality management. This includes ways to seek correction/deletion, clearly defined processes that are publicly available, responses to refusals (including reasons, recording objections, and review requests), and notification when PII is corrected or deleted.

###### HOW (specific approach)
Use a single, standardised set of documents called the **“PII Quality Management Procedure” pack** across all business units and system owners, supported by a central case and workflow capability.

1. **Define PII quality expectations and life-cycle coverage** in the policy/procedure pack: accuracy, relevance, timeliness, and completeness, mapped to each life-cycle stage (creation/collection, use, processing, storage/maintenance, sharing, disclosure, final disposal).
2. **Set review triggers and timing** for each type of PII (for example, customer contact details, employment status, identifiers) and for each type of change (for example, updates from the source system, regular refreshes, requests from users, and detected inconsistencies). The procedures must state what evidence shows that quality checks have been completed.
3. **Define rules for correcting versus deleting**: document the decision criteria for when to correct, when to delete, and when deletion is limited (for example, where retention or legal obligations apply). Include how the decision is recorded and who approves it.
4. **Provide practical ways to request correction/deletion**: publish a standard way to receive requests from individuals and authorised representatives (for example, a privacy portal form or email address) and set out the required identity and authorised-representative checks.
5. **Define how refusals and appeals are handled**: if a request is refused, the procedures must require (a) the reasons for the refusal, (b) a way to record the individual’s objections, and (c) a way to request a review of the original decision, including who carries out the review and the target timeframes.
6. **Define notification requirements**: the procedures must require notifying the individual (or the authorised representative) when PII is corrected or deleted. They must also define how to identify and notify other relevant parties in the data environment (for example, downstream systems and data processors) so that correction/deletion is applied consistently.
7. **Make evidence and audit trail requirements operational**: require that every quality review and every correction/deletion/appeal decision produces records that can be audited (review results, before and after values, reasons for the decision, approval details, proof of notification, and appeal outcomes).

###### WHO
- **Senior agency official for privacy / Privacy Office**: owns the policy and ensures practical mechanisms exist and are easy to access.
- **Data owners / system owners**: carry out quality checks and implement correction/deletion actions in their systems.
- **Privacy case management team (or delegated privacy operations)**: manages requests, verification outcomes, decisions, refusals, objections, and reviews.
- **Legal/Compliance (as needed)**: supports the rules on when deletion is allowed versus when correction is required, where retention or legal constraints apply.

###### Acceptance criteria
- A documented, organisation-wide **PII quality management policy** and **procedures** exist and clearly cover all PII life-cycle stages and quality expectations (accuracy, relevance, timeliness, completeness).
- The procedures define the criteria for correction/deletion decisions and require recording the reasons for decisions and who approved them.
- A publicly available, practical way exists for individuals and authorised representatives to request correction/deletion, including defined verification steps.
- Refusal handling includes: reasons for refusal, recording objections, and a defined review/appeals route with named roles.
- The procedures require notifying individuals when PII is corrected or deleted, and define how to notify other relevant parties to keep the data environment consistent.
- Evidence requirements are defined and demonstrably produce audit-ready records for reviews, changes, notifications, and appeal outcomes.

###### Actions Required for Compliance

- [ ] Draft and approve an organisation-wide policy for managing personal information (PII) quality (PM-22). The policy must cover accuracy, relevance, timeliness, and completeness across the entire PII life cycle.
- [ ] Publish a standard procedure pack for correcting or deleting personal data (PII), including checks to verify the person’s identity and their authorised representative, clear rules on when corrections are allowed versus when deletions are allowed, and the evidence that must be collected.

- [ ] Define the process for notifying people and systems when personal data (PII) is corrected or deleted, including how to identify which other relevant organisations in the data ecosystem must be informed.

### PM-23 — Data Governance Body (Control)

This requirement is about ensuring the organisation has a clear, responsible group that decides how data is handled, shared, and protected. Without this, important information could be shared in inconsistent ways, data quality could be unreliable, and sensitive personal details could be accidentally exposed—leading to legal breaches, reputational damage, or harm to individuals.

To meet this expectation, the organisation must set up a **Data Governance Body** made up of the **Chief Information Officer**, the **Senior Agency Information Security Officer**, the **Senior Agency Official for Privacy**, a **Data Governance Lead**, and **Data Steward Representatives**. This group must set and keep up to date the rules and standards for managing data, define how data is organised and what “good quality” means, review and approve any release of data outside the organisation, require that personal information is de-identified where appropriate, and ensure that any released applications and data are archived afterwards.

Finally, the group must carry out monitoring after release to confirm that the original assumptions still hold, and keep records of decisions, requests, and its operating documents.

##### Establish Data Governance Body charter and release approval workflow

- Category: Manual
- Priority: Critical

###### WHAT
Set up a Data Governance Body with the required roles, and formalise how it works (its charter, who decides what, how often it meets) and what it is responsible for. This includes: data governance policies and standards, data models and data quality rules, approving data releases, de-identification requirements, archiving released applications/data, and monitoring after release.

###### WHY (control requirement)
PM-23 requires the organisation to “Establish a Data Governance Body” with the specified roles (param **pm-23_odp.01**) and the specified responsibilities (param **pm-23_odp.02**). The guidance also expects clear, consistent policies and the ability to balance usefulness of data with security and privacy. This includes approving applications to release data outside the organisation, archiving released items, and monitoring after release to confirm that release assumptions were correct.

###### HOW (specific steps/approach)
1. **Create and approve a Data Governance Body charter** (terms of reference) that lists the members and clearly sets out its scope, meeting frequency, how decisions are made, how issues are escalated, and how decisions are recorded.
2. **Define governance policies/standards as controlled documents** (for example, a data governance policy, a data quality standard, and a de-identification standard). Publish them in a central repository with an owner and review dates.
3. **Set up data models and measurable data quality rules** by creating agreed “source of truth” definitions and quality thresholds (for example, completeness/accuracy/validity). These must link to the organisation’s data classification and intended use.
4. **Put in place a formal “data release approval” process** for any dataset/report/application programming interface (API) export or application package intended to be released outside the organisation. 5. **Require de-identification where it applies** by using a mandatory de-identification checklist in the process. This must include the de-identification method/version used and how any remaining disclosure risk was assessed.
6. **Archive release items and governance evidence** automatically or as part of the process. This includes: the approved release request, the approval decision record, de-identification evidence, the data model/data quality rule versions, and the released application/data package (or an unchangeable reference to it).
7. **Carry out monitoring after release** by setting monitoring triggers and acceptance criteria (for example, whether data quality drifts beyond agreed thresholds, whether assumptions remain valid, and periodic review of release outcomes). Record results and use exceptions to trigger re-approval/re-release or updates to policies.

###### WHO
- **Data Governance Body Chair / Data Governance Lead**: owns the charter, governance documents, and runs the release approval process.
- **Chief Information Officer (CIO)**: accountable for the direction of governance policy and for providing resources.
- **Senior Agency Information Security Officer**: accountable for aligning security requirements and approving security and privacy controls for releases.

###### Acceptance criteria
- A signed/approved charter exists that names all roles from **pm-23_odp.01** and clearly defines decision rights, meeting cadence, and record-keeping.
- Governance policies/standards exist and are version-controlled, including data models and data quality rules aligned to the organisation’s data classification.
- A documented and enforced release approval process exists that requires approval by the Data Governance Body (or named approvers within it) before any external data release.
- De-identification is mandatory in the process for applicable personal information (PII) and includes method/version evidence.
- For each external release, archived evidence exists for: the approval record, de-identification evidence, data model/data quality rule versions, and the released item (or an unchangeable reference).
- Post-release monitoring is defined, carried out on schedule for released items, and exceptions trigger defined actions (re-approval/re-release or policy updates).

###### Actions Required for Compliance

- [ ] Draft and get approval for a Data Governance Group charter for **pm-23_odp.01**. The charter must list all roles and set out its scope, meeting frequency, decision-making authority, and escalation routes.
- [ ] Publish version-controlled data governance policies and standards covering data modelling, data quality rules, and de-identification expectations, as required by pm-23_odp.02.

### PM-24 — Data Integrity Board (Control)

This requirement ensures that whenever the organisation wants to compare its records with another system to match people or information, a senior group of decision-makers checks the plan first and then reviews it again every year. Without this oversight, the organisation could share or use information in the wrong way, for the wrong purpose, or without the right protections. This could result in unfair decisions, privacy breaches, or misuse of sensitive records.

To meet this expectation, the organisation must set up a **Data Integrity Board** appointed by the organisation’s head. The Board must include senior officials, including the senior privacy lead, and where applicable, the Inspector General. The Board must review every proposal to start or join a matching programme before any participation begins, and it must carry out an annual review of every matching programme the organisation has taken part in.

The organisation must keep clear records showing:
- the Board’s membership,
- its responsibilities,
- the decisions it makes, and
- the results of each annual review.

- Priority: Critical

PM-24 requires the organisation to establish a Data Integrity Board (set up and designated by the head of the agency/organisation). The Board must include, at minimum, the **Inspector General (if any)** and the **senior agency official for privacy**. The Board must:

### PM-25 — Minimization of Personally Identifiable Information Used in Testing, Training, and Research (Control)

This expectation is about using the smallest amount of personal information possible when you test systems, train staff, or carry out internal research—and only using it when there is a genuine need. The real risk is that personal details such as names, addresses, or contact information could be accidentally revealed, used in the wrong way, or shared with people who should not have access. To meet this requirement, the organisation must create, document, and follow clear rules and step-by-step procedures for testing, training, and research. These must explain how personal information will be limited, how it will be handled, and who must approve its use when it is required. The organisation must review and update these rules and procedures at least once every year, and keep evidence showing they are being followed in practice.

###### WHY (control requirement)
PM-25 requires organisations to minimise the use of PII in testing, training, and research, and to use placeholder data where possible. It also requires consultation with senior privacy and legal advisers to confirm the use is consistent with the original reason the PII was collected, and to ensure the use is governed through documented policies and procedures.

###### HOW (specific steps/approach)
1. **Create and publish PM-25-aligned policies and procedures** for internal testing, training, and research that set out: (a) when PII may be used, (b) minimisation expectations (placeholders/masking/aggregation/reduced fields), (c) what must be included in an approval request (reason it matches the original purpose, dataset inventory, minimisation method, retention and scope), and (d) approval and expiry rules.
2. **Create a dataset inventory template** that teams must complete for every dataset request, including: dataset ID, source system, list of PII items, assessment of whether it can identify people directly or indirectly, proposed minimisation method (placeholders/masking/synthetic data/aggregation), intended environment (development/testing/training/research), and retention period.
3. **Set up an approval process enforced through automation** (for example, a ticket or workflow system plus data platform controls):
   - Block, by default, the exporting, copying, or loading of datasets containing PII into non-production testing, training, and research environments.
   - Require an **approved dataset reference** (approval record ID plus version) before any PII dataset is made available.
4. **Apply minimisation controls when the dataset is created**:
   - Redact/mask/tokenise fields at the field level, or generate synthetic or placeholder values, using the approved minimisation method.
   - Make sure the resulting dataset contains only the approved PII items (and no extra fields).
5. **Keep evidence that can be audited** for every approved use, including: who approved it and their role, the privacy/legal compatibility reason, the approval date and time, the dataset version, the minimisation method used, and the expiry date.
6. **Review and update the PM-25 policies and procedures at least once a year** (or at the frequencies defined by the organisation), keeping evidence of the review results and any updates.

###### WHO
- **Data Protection Officer / Privacy Lead (or delegated privacy counsel)**: approves the purpose-compatibility and minimisation rationale.
- **Information Security / Compliance**: owns the PM-25 policies and procedures and ensures the approval workflow is enforced.
- **Application/Data Owners and Test/Training/Research Leads**: submit dataset inventories and minimisation plans.
- **Platform/Data Engineering Team**: implements the dataset gate and minimisation transformations.

###### Acceptance criteria
- No dataset containing PII is exported, copied, or loaded into internal testing, training, or research environments without an approved dataset reference.
- For every approved request, the dataset inventory includes the list of PII items, the purpose, and the minimisation method; the resulting dataset reflects the approved minimisation (placeholders/masking/reduced fields).
- Every approval record includes the privacy/legal compatibility rationale and is kept as audit evidence.
- The PM-25 policies and procedures are reviewed and updated at least annually, with documented evidence of the review outcomes.

###### Actions Required for Compliance

- [ ] Draft and get approval for the PM-25 policy and procedures covering how personal data is minimised, and the approval requirements for internal testing, training, and research.
- [ ] Define a dataset inventory standard, including: a list of personal data elements, an assessment of how identifiable the data is, the method used to minimise personal data, a justification that the intended use is compatible with the purpose, and the scope and retention period.
- [ ] Set up a “deny by default” process that blocks the creation of personal data (PII) datasets in non-production test, training, and research environments unless there is an approved dataset reference.
- [ ] Set up data delivery pipelines to use the approved data minimisation method (masking, tokenisation, synthetic placeholders, or reducing fields) and ensure that any unapproved personal data fields are not included.
- [ ] For every approved request, record approval evidence that can be audited, including the approver’s identity and role, the reason for approval, the dataset version used, the data minimisation method, and the expiry date.
- [ ] Plan and complete the annual review and update of the PM-25 policy and procedures, and keep documented evidence showing the results.

### PM-26 — Complaint Management (Control)

This requirement ensures people can easily raise concerns or ask questions about the organisation’s privacy and security practices, and that those concerns are dealt with properly and quickly. If there is no clear public way to complain and no reliable way to follow up, complaints may be missed, ignored, or left unresolved. This can harm trust and allow privacy issues to continue without being addressed.

The organisation must provide at least one simple, publicly available way to submit a complaint, such as a phone line, email address, or web form. It must also publish all the information needed to use that route, including contact details for the senior privacy lead or another designated person who receives complaints.

Each complaint must be recorded and tracked, with the following response times:
- reviewed within 24 hours  
- acknowledged within 1 business day  
- addressed within 10 business days  
- given a substantive response within 15 business days  

The organisation must keep records to show these timeframes were met.

##### Public complaints portal with case tracking and SLA timers

- Category: Software
- Priority: Critical

###### WHAT must be done
Set up a process that lets people submit complaints, concerns, or questions about the organisation’s security and privacy practices. Make sure every submission is received, acknowledged, reviewed, dealt with, and answered within agreed time periods. The process must include publicly available ways to submit (and the contact details for the senior privacy official, or another designated person who will receive submissions), with end-to-end tracking and records.

###### WHY (control requirement)
PM-26 requires a working process to receive and respond to complaints, concerns, or questions from individuals about the organisation’s security and privacy practices. This includes clear ways for people to submit (for example, a telephone hotline, email, or web forms) and the information needed to submit successfully, including the contact details for the senior privacy official (or designated receiver). It also requires agreed time periods for review, acknowledgement, action, and response.

###### HOW (specific steps/approach)
1. **Provide public submission routes**: publish at least one easy-to-use way to submit (a web form on the public website; optionally also a public email address and/or a telephone hotline) with clear navigation such as “Privacy complaints” / “Report a security concern”.
2. **Publish filing instructions and designated contact details**: on the submission page, include the information people must provide to submit successfully, including **contact details for the senior privacy official/designated receiver** and guidance on what to include.
3. **Implement a case management workflow**: connect the public form (and any email intake) to an enterprise case or ticket system (for example, ServiceNow, Jira, or Zendesk) so each complaint creates a unique case/reference ID and is assigned to a triage owner.
4. **Meet defined time periods using workflow timers** (based on business days):
   - **Review within 24 hours** (record the initial triage/review time and assign an owner).
   - **Acknowledge receipt within 1 business day** (send an acknowledgement to the complainant with the reference ID).
   - **Deal with within 10 business days** (complete the required actions, or record an agreed plan for remediation/response with dates).
   - **Respond within 15 business days** (send a meaningful final response).
5. **Escalate and keep proof**: set up automatic escalation to the responsible leads as deadlines approach, and keep audit evidence (timestamps, assignment history, messages sent, and the final outcome) that shows the time periods were met.
6. **Handle submissions safely for privacy**: if submissions include personal information, apply the organisation’s data handling rules (classification tagging and restricting access to people who need to know) within the case management system.

###### WHO (role responsible)
- **Data Protection Officer / Privacy Lead (or designated senior privacy official)**: owns the complaint submission guidance, the designated receiver contact details, and approval/governance of the final response.
- **Security/Privacy Operations (case management team)**: runs triage, assignment, and workflow timers.
- **IT Service Management/Platform Owner**: sets up and maintains the case management integration and audit logging.

###### Acceptance criteria
- A public user can submit a complaint using the published method and receives a **unique reference ID**.
- The system records and can show: **review within 24 hours**, **acknowledgement within 1 business day**, **dealing with within 10 business days**, and **a substantive response within 15 business days** for test submissions.
- The public submission page includes **contact details for the senior privacy official/designated receiver** and clear instructions for submitting.
- Audit evidence exists for each case (timestamps, assignment, escalation events, and messages sent), and access to case content is restricted to need-to-know roles.

###### Actions Required for Compliance

- [ ] Provide a public way to submit complaints (a web form and/or an email address and/or a phone hotline) with clear directions on how to find it and how to use it.
- [ ] Show the contact details for the senior privacy official and the designated recipient on the submission page.
- [ ] Link the complaint submission process to the organisation’s case management system so each complaint gets a unique reference ID.
- [ ] Set up workflow time limits and service-level agreements (SLAs) based on business days for: review (24 hours), acknowledgement (1 business day), addressing (10 business days), and response (15 business days).
- [ ] Set up escalation rules for approaching deadlines, and make sure every action taken on each case is recorded with a timestamp and logged for audit purposes.
- [ ] Apply privacy-safe handling in the case system by using classification tags and “need to know” access controls for submissions that contain personal data.
- [ ] Run complete end-to-end test cases to confirm that acknowledgements, reviews, follow-up actions, and final responses happen within the agreed time periods and that supporting evidence is kept.

### PM-27 — Privacy Reporting (Control)

This requirement is about making sure the organisation shares the right privacy information with the right people, in the right way, and on time. It should explain what the organisation is doing to protect people’s privacy and how it is meeting its legal duties. If privacy reporting is unclear or late, important responsibilities can be missed, regulators may not be able to assess how well privacy is being managed, and the organisation could be criticised for not acting responsibly when systems or data uses change.

To meet this expectation, the organisation must appoint a named lead who is responsible for producing the annual privacy reports required for the Senior Agency Official for Privacy. This includes reports that must be sent to the Office of Management and Budget, reports that must be provided to Congress where the law requires it, and any other public privacy reports the organisation is legally required to publish.

The organisation must share these reports externally with the Office of Management and Budget, Congress, the Department of Defense (DoD) Chief Privacy Officer, and any other external parties required by law. It must also share them internally with the Senior Agency Official for Privacy, privacy programme managers, legal counsel, and other staff who oversee privacy compliance.

The organisation must review and update the reports at least once every year and after any significant change. It must also keep records showing when the reports were shared and when they were reviewed or updated.

##### Privacy reporting pack with auditable internal/external dissemination

- Category: Manual
- Priority: Critical

###### WHAT
Create and run a repeatable **Privacy Reporting Pack** process that produces all **privacy reports required by law** and sends them to the required **internal and external oversight bodies**. Keep audit-ready evidence showing **who received what, when, and which version**, and evidence that the reports are **reviewed and updated at least once a year and after any significant change**.

###### WHY (control requirement)
PM-27 requires privacy accountability and transparency through **internal and external reporting**. This includes producing required privacy reports (for example, the annual senior agency official for privacy reports to the Office of Management and Budget (OMB), and reports to Congress where required), consulting legal counsel where appropriate, and making sure the reports are **reviewed and updated at least once a year and after any significant change**.

###### HOW (specific approach)
1. **Set the reporting scope and recipients** using the organisation’s agreed parameters:
   - Reports include: **annual senior agency official for privacy reports to OMB**, **reports to Congress**, and **other privacy reports required by law**.
   - External oversight bodies include: **OMB, Congress, the Department of Defense (DoD) Chief Privacy Officer**, and other required external regulators or stakeholders.
   - Internal officials include: **Senior Agency Official for Privacy**, **privacy programme managers**, and **legal counsel**.
2. **Create a Privacy Reporting Pack repository** (controlled document storage) with:
   - Version control enabled and an unchangeable history (or equivalent)
   - Restricted permission to edit
   - Controlled distribution links or attachments
3. **Use a governance workflow with review checkpoints** (for example, IT service management (ITSM), case management, or a workflow engine) that requires:

### PM-28 — Risk Framing (Control)

This requirement is about making sure the organisation sets clear rules for how it judges risk, decides what to do about it, and checks whether those decisions are still working. Without this, different parts of the organisation may make inconsistent choices, overlook important assumptions or limits, and accept or reduce risk in ways that do not match the organisation’s real priorities, budget, or tolerance for risk—leaving it exposed to avoidable harm.

To meet this requirement, the organisation must identify and record the assumptions and constraints that affect risk assessment, risk-reduction decisions, and ongoing risk monitoring. It must also record its risk-management priorities, the trade-offs it is willing to make, and the overall level of risk it is prepared to accept. The organisation must share these risk “framing” results with the mission and business owners, information owners or stewards, system owners, authorised officials, the senior information security lead, the senior privacy lead, and the senior accountable risk manager. It must review and update the framing at least every 90 days.

##### Risk Framing Charter with 90-day review and stakeholder distribution

- Category: Manual
- Priority: Critical

###### WHAT
Set up an organisation-wide **Risk Framing Charter** that records (1) **assumptions** and (2) **constraints** that affect risk assessment, risk response, and risk monitoring. It must also set out the organisation’s **risk priorities**, **risk trade-offs**, and overall **risk tolerance**. Compile these into **risk framing results**, share them with the required stakeholders, and review and update the framing **at least every 90 days**.

###### WHY (control requirement)
PM-28 requires that risk framing clearly identifies and documents assumptions, constraints, priorities, trade-offs, and risk tolerance. The results must be used to shape the risk management approach and the later risk assessment, response, and monitoring activities. The results must be shared with organisational personnel, including mission/business owners, information owners/stewards, system owners, authorising officials, SAISO, SAOP, and the senior accountable official for risk management. Risk framing must be reviewed and updated **at least every 90 days**.

###### HOW (specific steps/approach)
1. **Create the Risk Framing Charter** (single source of truth) with clear sections for:
   - Assumptions for **risk assessment**, **risk response**, and **risk monitoring**.
   - Constraints for **risk assessment**, **risk response**, and **risk monitoring**.
   - Risk priorities, risk trade-offs, and overall organisational risk tolerance (shown as clear decision thresholds and examples relevant to enterprise applications).
2. **Link the charter to risk work**: require that every risk assessment/response/monitoring document states the **charter version** it used and points to the relevant assumption/constraint statements.
3. **Share with stakeholders and keep proof**:
   - Publish the charter and a short “risk framing results” summary.
   - Share with: mission and business owners, information owners/stewards, system owners, authorising officials, SAISO, SAOP, and the senior accountable official for risk management.
   - Keep evidence of sharing (for example, signed acknowledgement, ticket/workflow approvals, or email distribution logs) in the organisation’s GRC repository.
4. **Set a 90-day review schedule**:
   - Set an automated reminder to start a review every 90 days.
   - Record the outcome as either “reviewed—no changes” or “updated—approved changes”, including the reason for any updates.
5. **Make it work for enterprise applications**:
   - Ensure the charter’s assumptions/constraints explicitly cover enterprise application realities (for example, separating environments, relying on central identity, monitoring, and logging services, and the organisation’s confidentiality/integrity/availability priorities).

###### WHO (role responsible)
- **Senior accountable official for risk management**: owns the charter and approves risk tolerance and trade-offs.
- **SAISO / SAOP**: confirm security and privacy assumptions/constraints and ensure stakeholders are aligned.
- **Mission/business owners and information owners/stewards**: confirm priorities and acceptable trade-offs.

###### Acceptance criteria
- A Risk Framing Charter exists, is version-controlled, and includes documented **assumptions**, **constraints**, **risk priorities**, **risk trade-offs**, and **overall risk tolerance**.
- Risk documents (risk assessments/response/monitoring plans) reference the **charter version** and the relevant assumption/constraint statements.
- Risk framing results are shared with all required stakeholder groups, with auditable evidence of sharing and acknowledgement.
- The charter is reviewed and updated **at least every 90 days**, with recorded outcomes and approval evidence for any changes.

### PM-29 — Risk Management Program Leadership Roles (Control)

This requirement is about ensuring risk decisions are led at senior level, with clear ownership and a view across the whole organisation. Without this, important risks to the organisation’s information and personal data could be missed, misunderstood, or dealt with differently in different departments. That could cause avoidable disruption, financial loss, regulatory issues, or harm to customers and staff.  

To meet this expectation, the organisation must appoint a senior leader who is personally accountable for risk management. This leader must drive risk management across the organisation by linking how information security and privacy are handled to the organisation’s strategic plans, day-to-day priorities, and budget decisions, and must keep a record of their actions.  

In addition, the organisation must set up a Risk Executive function. This function must review and analyse risks across the entire organisation and ensure risk management is carried out in a consistent way everywhere. Its role, policies, and procedures must be documented and kept up to date.

###### WHY (control requirement)
PM-29 requires that the **senior accountable leader for risk management leads the Risk Executive** in **organisation-wide risk management activities**. ### PM-30 — Supply Chain Risk Management Strategy (Control)

This requirement is about making sure the organisation has a clear, organisation-wide plan for how it will judge and manage risks from suppliers and other third parties, throughout the entire life of what the organisation builds, buys, runs, and eventually retires. Without this, a weak supplier, an unreliable service, or poor handling of components could lead to fraud, service disruption, loss of sensitive information, or personal data being misused—even if the problem starts outside the organisation.

To meet this expectation, the organisation must produce a documented strategy that covers risks during development, acquisition, maintenance, and disposal of systems, components, and services, including both security and privacy risks. The strategy must set out:

- what level of risk is acceptable  
- what actions the organisation will take to reduce risk  
- how risk will be checked and kept under review  
- how the strategy will be communicated  
- who is responsible for carrying it out  

The strategy must be applied consistently across the organisation and reviewed at least every year. It must also be updated within 30 days of significant organisational or mission changes, or when other organisational changes require it.

##### Lifecycle supply chain risk strategy with appetite, gates, and review

- Category: Manual
- Priority: Critical

###### WHAT
Create and maintain an organisation-wide **Supply Chain Risk Management Strategy** that clearly sets out:
- the organisation’s **supply chain risk appetite/tolerance**,
- which **supply chain risk reduction approaches and controls** are acceptable,
- a **consistent process** to assess and track supply chain risk,
- how the strategy is **put into practice and communicated**, and
- **roles and responsibilities**.
The strategy must cover security and privacy risks across the **development, acquisition, maintenance, and disposal** of systems, system parts, and system services.

###### WHY (control requirement)
PM-30 requires a clear, organisation-wide supply chain risk management strategy. It must include risk appetite/tolerance, acceptable risk reduction approaches, consistent assessment and monitoring, how it will be implemented and communicated, and who is responsible. It must also explicitly consider **security and privacy risks** across the full lifecycle.

###### HOW (specific approach)
1. **Write the strategy document** (or update the existing enterprise risk management strategy) with explicit lifecycle coverage: **development, acquisition, maintenance, disposal** for **systems, parts, and services**. Include security and privacy considerations for each stage (for example: exposure to vulnerabilities, remote support, handling and telemetry of data, third-party processing, and end-of-life data sanitisation).
2. **Set risk appetite/tolerance** as clear thresholds and categories that procurement and engineering can use to make decisions (for example: “critical vulnerabilities must have compensating controls or be rejected”, “a privacy impact assessment is required when personal data is involved”, “remote access/support must use multi-factor authentication (MFA) and session controls”).

   - checks during supplier onboarding/selection,
   - regular re-checks,
   - monitoring triggers (for example: critical Common Vulnerabilities and Exposures (CVEs), supplier incidents, changes in the service scope),
   - escalation and handling of exceptions.
5. **Put the strategy into day-to-day use using lifecycle “gates”** within existing governance processes (for example: design approval, procurement approval, maintenance/renewal approval, retirement/disposal approval) so teams must submit the required evidence documents that match the strategy.
6. **Assign roles and responsibilities** (for example: risk executive/owner, procurement, security, privacy, application owners, engineering, disposal/asset management) and define how the strategy will be communicated (training/briefings, internal portal, procurement playbooks, and guidance within workflows).
7. **Set how often the strategy is reviewed and updated** to match the organisation’s agreed requirement: review and update **annually**, or **within 30 days of significant organisational or mission changes**. Keep evidence suitable for audit, including review and version history.

- **Chief Information Security Officer (CISO) / Security Governance Lead**: confirms security risk considerations and expected risk reduction actions.
- **Privacy Officer / Data Protection Lead**: confirms privacy risk considerations and required privacy assessments.
- **Procurement Lead**: ensures supplier checks and contractual controls match the strategy.
- **Application/Service Owners & Engineering Managers**: apply the lifecycle gates and provide the required evidence.

###### Acceptance criteria
- A documented, organisation-wide PM-30 strategy exists and explicitly covers **development, acquisition, maintenance, disposal** for **systems/parts/services**.
- The strategy includes a clear **risk appetite/tolerance** and **acceptable risk reduction approaches/controls**.
- The strategy defines a **consistent assessment and monitoring process** (including triggers and escalation).
- The strategy defines the **implementation/communication approach** and **roles/responsibilities**.
- Evidence shows the strategy is **reviewed at least annually** and updated **within 30 days** of significant organisational/mission changes, with version history kept.
- Security and privacy risks are explicitly addressed for each lifecycle stage.

###### Actions Required for Compliance

- [ ] Draft or update the organisation-wide supply chain risk management strategy to clearly cover the full lifecycle of systems, components, and services—covering development, purchase, ongoing maintenance, and disposal.
- [ ] Set clear limits for how much supply chain security and privacy risk the organisation is willing to accept (risk appetite and tolerance), and publish the decision categories used to approve or reject risks.
- [ ] Document acceptable supply chain risk reduction strategies and controls, and link each one to a risk category.
- [ ] Set up a consistent process to evaluate and monitor performance, including staff onboarding, regular re-checks, clear monitoring triggers, escalation steps, and how to handle exceptions.
- [ ] Set out clear roles and responsibilities, and explain how the strategy will be communicated and built into procurement and delivery governance.
- [ ] Set a yearly schedule to review and update the content. If there are significant organisational or mission changes, update it within 30 days. Keep records that show the reviews and versions so it is ready for audit.

#### PM-30.1 — Suppliers of Critical or Mission-essential Items (Enhancement)

This requirement is about knowing exactly which suppliers are most important to keeping your business running, and checking that they are safe and reliable. If you do not clearly identify and rank the companies that support your most critical technologies, products, or services, you may face unexpected delays, poor quality, failure to meet required rules, or sudden changes that disrupt operations or prevent you from delivering your mission.

To meet this expectation, the organisation must:
- write down what it considers critical or mission-essential
- identify the suppliers that provide those items
- keep an up-to-date list of suppliers and the specific critical items each supplier provides

It must link each supplier to the critical items they supply, prioritise suppliers based on how important they are, and record the reasons for each priority. It then needs to review and assess the risk posed by the higher-priority suppliers using its established supplier review and supply-chain risk approach. The organisation must keep dated records of the assessment results and any required follow-up actions, and name the people responsible for maintaining the supplier list, completing the assessments, and ensuring everything aligns with the organisation’s wider risk plans.

##### Critical supplier register with item links, scoring, and review triggers

- Category: Software
- Priority: Critical

###### WHAT
Create and maintain an auditable record that (1) identifies and describes the organisation’s most important (“critical” or “mission-essential”) technologies, products, and services, (2) lists the suppliers providing them, (3) ranks suppliers using documented criteria, and (4) records the results of supply chain risk checks and the follow-up actions.

###### WHY (control requirement)
PM-30(1) enhancement requires the organisation to **identify, prioritise, and assess suppliers** of **critical or mission-essential** technologies, products, and services, using supplier reviews (SR-6) and supply chain risk assessment processes (RA-3(1)), and to keep records that support traceability and follow-up.

###### HOW (specific steps/approach)
1. **Define what “critical” means (policy + categories):** Create an approved definition of “critical”/“mission-essential” based on business impact (for example: service unavailability, safety or regulatory impact, impact on data exposure, and concentration or limited alternatives). Keep an approved set of categories for critical item types relevant to the application (for example: identity services, privileged access/managed services, core network/security tools, cloud/software-as-a-service dependencies, backup/disaster recovery services, managed monitoring/logging, database/storage platforms, messaging/shared services).
2. **Create a Critical Items Register (single source of truth):** Set up a register (in a governance, risk, and compliance (GRC) system or procurement system) with fields for: critical item name, owning business/application team, affected processes, environment(s) (production/testing/disaster recovery), description of dependencies, criticality tier, and the evidence needed to assess suppliers.
3. **Maintain a supplier list and link it clearly:** Keep a supplier inventory (legal entity, trading name, unique identifier, contract scope, regions, status) and create a many-to-many link **Supplier ↔ Critical Item(s)**, including effective start/end dates and change control. Make sure every critical item has at least one linked supplier (or an explicit documented reason for internal provision with no external supplier).
4. **Prioritise suppliers using documented, repeatable criteria:** Use a scoring approach (stored as controlled templates/checklists) to rank suppliers linked to High/Critical items. The scoring should be based on documented factors such as: impact if unavailable, relevance to how easily it could be exploited/attack exposure, concentration risk, regulatory or compliance impact, and how easily it can be replaced. Record the reason for the score and the evidence for each supplier–critical item link.
5. **Trigger supplier reviews and supply chain risk assessments:** For suppliers linked to High/Critical items, automatically start supplier reviews and supply chain risk assessments aligned to RA-3(1) through a workflow. Use both timing (for example, quarterly or annually by tier) and event-based triggers (for example: material contract changes, ownership changes, major product/service changes, new subcontractors, or a significant security incident affecting the supplier).
6. **Record results and required follow-up actions:** Store the assessment results (for example: acceptable / needs improvement / high risk) and the required improvements (for example: contract clauses, technical compensating controls, monitoring requirements, audit rights, and exit/contingency plans) against the supplier–critical item link. Keep supporting evidence, version history, due dates, and the named people responsible.
7. **Run governance and be ready for audit:** Assign named roles for: (a) maintaining the critical items register, (b) maintaining the supplier inventory and links, (c) running supplier prioritisation, and (d) carrying out/approving assessments. Produce audit reports that show: critical items → linked suppliers → priority reasons → assessment outcomes → mitigation actions → current status/overdue items.

###### WHO
- **Information Security / Supply Chain Risk Lead:** Owns the definition of criticality, the scoring criteria, and alignment of the assessment workflow to SR-6 and RA-3(1).
- **Procurement/Supplier Management Lead:** Owns the quality of the supplier inventory, contract scope information, and supplier change notifications.
- **Application/Business Owners:** Provide ownership of critical items, dependency descriptions, and affected processes.
- **GRC/Compliance Operations (or a delegated risk analyst):** Maintains the registers, mappings, evidence, and assessment records.

###### Acceptance criteria
- A controlled critical items register exists and is completed for the application’s critical dependencies.
- Every critical item has an explicit supplier link (or a documented internal provision rationale) with effective dates.
- Suppliers are prioritised using a documented, repeatable scoring model; each supplier–critical item link has recorded reasons and evidence.
- Supplier reviews and supply chain risk assessments are triggered for suppliers linked to High/Critical items, producing dated, auditable outcomes with evidence and named follow-up actions.
- An audit report can be produced end-to-end: critical item → supplier(s) → priority → assessment outcome → mitigations → status/overdue.

###### Actions Required for Compliance

- [ ] Define and get approval for the organisation’s “critical” or “mission-essential” categories and the rules for what technologies, products, and services are included.
- [ ] Set up a “Critical Items Register” in the selected governance, risk and compliance (GRC) or procurement system, with restricted fields and named ownership.
- [ ] Create and keep an up-to-date supplier list, and record which suppliers supply which critical items using a many-to-many supplier-to-critical-item link, including the start and end dates for each link (effective-from and effective-to).
- [ ] Set up a documented supplier prioritisation scoring model and require a clear reason and supporting evidence for every supplier–item link.
- [ ] Set up automated triggers to regularly run supplier reviews and supply chain risk assessments for suppliers linked to High or Critical risk, both on a scheduled basis and when relevant changes occur.
- [ ] Store dated assessment results, supporting evidence attachments, and named follow-up mitigation actions for each supplier-item link.
- [ ] Assign specific named responsibilities for maintaining the register, prioritising items, carrying out and approving assessments, and keeping evidence up to date; enable audit reporting so that every step can be traced end to end.

### PM-31 — Continuous Monitoring Strategy (Control)

This requirement is about maintaining a steady, organisation-wide view of how well your systems are protected and how well privacy is being handled, so problems are spotted early and fixed quickly. Without this ongoing oversight, weaknesses such as out-of-date software, missing extra login checks, slow fixing of known security issues, poor visibility of suspicious activity, incomplete reviews of who has powerful access, or missing records of system events could go unnoticed until they are exploited, harming customers, staff, and the business.

The organisation must set up and run a continuous monitoring programme overseen by the Chief Information Security Officer. It must define and regularly track key measures, including: how up to date the software patches are (patch compliance), coverage of extra login checks, how quickly vulnerabilities are fixed within agreed timeframes, coverage of endpoint protection, completion of privileged access reviews, whether system event records are being collected successfully, and the rate of critical alerts. It must set targets for how effective patching, extra login checks, and access reviews are, assess separately how effective it is at fixing issues within time and at collecting records, then link and analyse the results. It must take timely action to address findings and report security status weekly to the Security Operations Centre manager and system security officers, and privacy status monthly to the Privacy Office lead and system privacy officers.

##### CISO-owned continuous monitoring strategy with metric targets & cadence

- Category: Manual
- Priority: Critical

###### WHAT
Create an organisation-wide continuous monitoring approach and run continuous monitoring programmes that (1) define security and privacy performance measures, (2) set monitoring and review intervals that are frequent enough for risk-based decisions, (3) check whether security controls work effectively against agreed organisation targets, (4) link results together to guide risk response actions, and (5) report security status weekly and privacy status monthly.

###### WHY (control requirement)
PM-31 requires the organisation to develop an organisation-wide continuous monitoring approach and run continuous monitoring programmes that include ongoing monitoring of security and privacy performance. The intervals must be sufficient to support risk-based decisions, and the results must be used to guide risk response actions and ongoing authorisation decisions.

###### HOW (specific approach)
1. **Define the monitoring approach (owned by the Chief Information Security Officer (CISO)) and governance**: Record the approach, including scope (organisation-wide systems and common controls), who reports on security and privacy, escalation routes, and how monitoring results feed into risk response and authorisation decisions.
2. **Put organisation-defined measures into practice**: Implement one agreed catalogue of measures with clear calculation rules and data sources for: patch compliance percentage, multi-factor authentication (MFA) coverage percentage, vulnerability service-level agreement (SLA) adherence, endpoint detection and response (EDR) coverage percentage, privileged access review completion percentage, log collection success percentage, and the rate of critical alerts.
3. **Set monitoring vs review intervals**: Configure monitoring to run at the organisation-defined monitoring intervals (for the measures used to judge control effectiveness). Run control effectiveness reviews at the organisation-defined review intervals (for vulnerability SLA adherence and log collection success percentage).
4. **Set control-effectiveness targets and how they will be judged**: For patch compliance percentage, MFA coverage percentage, and privileged access review completion percentage, define effectiveness targets and a repeatable way to decide whether targets are met (including thresholds, time windows, and what evidence is required). For vulnerability SLA adherence and log collection success percentage, define how effectiveness will be assessed (for example, the percentage within SLA / the percentage of sources successfully collecting logs).
5. **Link results, analyse, and follow a response process**: Combine trends from the measures with outputs from control effectiveness reviews (for example, configuration compliance, access review outcomes, and vulnerability management results). Send findings into a managed fix-and-check process with named owners, due dates, and re-checking using the same measure definitions.
6. **Reporting schedule and content**: Produce audit-ready reporting packs: **weekly security status** for the security operations centre (SOC) manager and system security officers, and **monthly privacy status** for the Privacy Office lead and system privacy officers. Include measure values, effectiveness decisions, key trends, main drivers, and progress on remediation.

###### WHO
- **Chief Information Security Officer (CISO)**: Owns and approves the organisation-wide continuous monitoring approach and the measure catalogue.
- **Security operations centre (SOC) manager & system security officers**: Receive weekly security status reports and lead day-to-day response.
- **Privacy Office lead & system privacy officers**: Receive monthly privacy status reports and lead privacy-related response.
- **System security officers / security engineering**: Build the measure data feeds, dashboards, and integrate them into the remediation workflow.

###### Acceptance criteria
- A documented, CISO-approved continuous monitoring approach exists and clearly states scope, roles, monitoring and review intervals, and how results drive risk response.
- The organisation-defined measures are implemented with auditable calculation rules and evidence sources.
- Monitoring runs at the defined monitoring intervals; control effectiveness reviews run at the defined review intervals.
- Effectiveness targets are defined and applied for patch compliance percentage, MFA coverage percentage, and privileged access review completion percentage; effectiveness is assessed for vulnerability SLA adherence and log collection success percentage.
- Weekly security status reporting is produced for the SOC manager and system security officers; monthly privacy status reporting is produced for the Privacy Office lead and system privacy officers. Each report includes measure values, effectiveness decisions, and remediation progress.
- Linked findings lead to tracked risk response actions, with documented closure and re-validation.

###### Actions Required for Compliance

- [ ] Draft and get approval from the Chief Information Security Officer (CISO) for an organisation-wide continuous monitoring approach, including what it covers, how it will be governed, and how monitoring results link to risk responses.
- [ ] Create a metric catalogue that sets out how each metric is calculated and what evidence is used, covering: patch compliance percentage, multi-factor authentication (MFA) coverage percentage, adherence to vulnerability service-level agreements (SLAs), endpoint detection and response (EDR) coverage percentage, completion of privileged access reviews, log data ingestion success percentage, and the rate of critical alerts.
- [ ] Set and record how often the organisation will monitor and review the control effectiveness measures it defines.
- [ ] Set measurable targets for patch compliance (percentage), multi-factor authentication (MFA) coverage (percentage), and completion of privileged access reviews (percentage), and publish the method used to assess performance.
- [ ] Set up a process that links performance measurements and assessment results to recorded risk response actions, and then checks again to confirm the risk has been addressed.
- [ ] Create reporting packs and share weekly security status with the security operations centre (SOC) manager and system security officers, and share monthly privacy status with the Privacy Office lead and system privacy officers.

### PM-32 — Purposing (Control)

This requirement is about making sure the organisation’s most important computer systems are used only for the specific jobs they were designed to do. Over time, systems can quietly start being used for other activities. This can put sensitive information and critical services into the wrong environment, increasing the risk of misuse, disruption, or compromise, and ultimately harming the mission or business function that relies on them.

To meet this expectation, the organisation must keep:
- a documented list of the essential services and functions
- a record of the mission-essential systems and the supporting components for each
- a clear statement of the intended purpose of each system or component

The organisation must then regularly check how each system or component is actually being used, compare it to its intended purpose, and:
- identify any use that is outside its intended purpose
- assess how that could increase risk
- record what it found
- take corrective action to bring use back in line

The organisation must update its records when changes occur and ensure this work is reflected in its information security and privacy planning and its risk management approach.

##### Purpose drift analysis for mission-essential application use

- Category: Software
- Priority: Critical

###### WHAT
Create and maintain an auditable record that links each mission-essential system/component to the specific mission-essential service/function it is meant to support. Then, on a regular basis, compare real-world usage against that intended purpose to find and fix any use that falls outside the intended scope.

###### WHY (control requirement)
PM-32 requires the organisation to **analyse mission-essential services/functions** to ensure **information resources are used for their intended purpose**. This includes spotting use outside the intended scope, checking whether this increases exposure to threats, and making sure the results are recorded and corrected.

###### HOW (specific steps/approach)
1. **Define the intended purpose for each system/component**: For the enterprise-managed application (and supporting components such as application programming interface (API) endpoints, administrative consoles, databases, messaging topics/queues, and integration services), document the mission-essential service/function(s) it supports. Include clear “allowed use” and “not for” statements (for example: administrative tools, non-mission workloads, test-only functions).
2. **Create an authoritative list of what is in scope**: Keep an inventory of mission-essential systems/components in the system of record (for example, the configuration management database (CMDB)/asset inventory) and link each item to its intended purpose record.
3. **Gather evidence of actual use from existing enterprise logs** (no new agents required where they are not already deployed):
   - Identity and access logs (single sign-on (SSO) sign-ins, group/role usage, service account activity)
   - API gateway/web application firewall (WAF)/proxy logs (which endpoints are called, and request patterns)
   - Application and administrative audit logs (administrative actions, job/scheduler runs)
   - Network and segmentation flow logs (where relevant, to confirm environment boundaries)
4. **Carry out periodic intended-versus-actual checks**: On a defined schedule (for example, monthly for high-risk mission-essential systems; quarterly for others), compare observed access/use patterns against the allowed-purpose mapping. Flag:
   - Use by identities/roles not linked to the intended service/function
   - Access to endpoints/functions that are not mapped to the mission-essential purpose
   - Use across environments (for example, production resources used for non-mission activities)
5. **Assess exposure risk and take corrective action**: For each flagged out-of-purpose use, record the evidence and complete a risk assessment (likelihood and impact of increased threat exposure). Then apply corrective actions such as removing or adjusting access, removing OAuth/app authorisations, tightening conditional access, updating segmentation rules, moving workloads to the correct environment, or decommissioning/isolating misused components.
6. **Record results and update records after changes**: Store the analysis outputs (intended purpose, observed purpose, evidence, risk rating, actions taken, and remaining risk) in the organisation’s risk and compliance repository. Update the intended purpose mapping and inventory when the system’s mission-essential scope changes, and ensure the outputs feed into security/privacy planning and risk management.

###### WHO (role responsible)
- **Application Owner / Business Service Owner**: confirms the intended purpose and the “allowed/not for” statements.
- **Security Engineering / Identity and Access Management (IAM) and Monitoring Lead**: sets up log collection, runs analysis queries/detections, and manages corrective-action workflows.
- **Governance, Risk and Compliance (GRC) / Risk Manager**: ensures findings are recorded, risk-assessed, and included in risk management and security/privacy planning.

###### Acceptance criteria
- For every mission-essential system/component in scope, there is an auditable intended purpose record linked to an entry in the authoritative inventory.
- A periodic analysis is completed and produces evidence-based results showing intended versus observed use.
- Any out-of-purpose use is identified, risk-assessed, recorded, and has documented corrective actions (or documented acceptance of the remaining risk).
- Analysis outputs are kept for audit purposes and are included in the organisation’s risk management and security/privacy planning processes.

###### Actions Required for Compliance

- [ ] Document the mission-critical purpose of each in-scope system or component, including clearly stated allowed uses and uses that are not permitted.
- [ ] Link each mission-critical system or component in the inventory (configuration management database (CMDB) or asset system) to its intended purpose record.
- [ ] Set up logging for the systems that are actually used—such as single sign-on (SSO) and identity and access management (IAM), the application programming interface (API) gateway, web application firewall (WAF) and proxy, application and admin audit logs, and relevant network traffic flow logs—to provide proof of real usage.
- [ ] Set up a routine that compares the intended use of access against what actually happens, and flags any access or use that falls outside the approved purpose—broken down by device or function and by user identity or role.
- [ ] Carry out the analysis on the agreed schedule and produce an audit-ready report showing the intended purpose, what was actually used, the supporting evidence, and any exceptions that were flagged.
- [ ] For each flagged use that appears to be outside its intended purpose, carry out a risk assessment and then complete the documented fixes (for example, remove access, change conditional access settings, remove granted permissions, or isolate the misused components).
- [ ] After any change in scope, record the results and update the intended purpose mapping and inventory. Make sure the findings are used in risk management and in security and privacy planning.
