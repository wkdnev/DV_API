### AT-1 — Policy and Procedures (Control)

This requirement is about making sure everyone in the organisation understands what they are expected to do to protect the organisation’s information and personal data. It should be supported by clear written guidance and regular updates. Without an agreed policy and practical procedures, people may not know their responsibilities. Training could vary between teams and suppliers, and important updates—such as changes in the law or lessons learned from incidents—may be missed. This increases the risk of data loss, privacy breaches, and failing to meet legal and regulatory requirements.

The organisation must create and document an organisation-wide awareness and training policy. This policy must explain its purpose, what it applies to, who is responsible for what, the commitment from senior leadership, how different parts of the organisation work together, and how the organisation will meet compliance requirements. The policy must also be consistent with relevant laws and internal organisational rules.

The Chief Information Security Officer, together with the Security Awareness Programme Manager and the Privacy Officer, must manage producing, documenting, and distributing the policy and the supporting procedures.

The policy must be reviewed at least every year, and also after security incidents, audit findings, or relevant changes to law or policy. The procedures must be reviewed at least every year, and after major organisational or system changes, as well as after incidents or audit findings.

The policy must be shared with all employees, contractors, and third parties who have access to the organisation’s systems and data. The procedures must be shared with all people with security or privacy responsibilities, including system owners, operational technology (OT) operators, and data stewards.

##### CISO-owned awareness & training policy and procedures with review triggers

- Category: Manual
- Priority: Critical

###### WHAT
Create and document an organisation-wide **security and privacy awareness and training policy** and the **supporting awareness and training procedures**. Share them with the defined groups and keep them up to date.

###### WHY (control requirement)
AT-1 requires that **awareness and training policy and procedures** exist and cover the AT controls the organisation has put in place. The guidance says it is not enough to simply restate the controls. The policy and procedures must be organisation-wide, based on risk, and coordinated between the security and privacy programmes. It also sets out how often they must be reviewed and updated, and what events trigger updates.

###### HOW (specific steps/approach)
1. **Set policy ownership and governance**: appoint the **Chief Information Security Officer (CISO)** as the official responsible for managing the awareness and training policy and procedures.
2. **Create the organisation-wide policy** (single source of truth): include purpose, scope, roles and responsibilities, management commitment, coordination between the security and privacy programmes, and how compliance will be achieved. Ensure it aligns with relevant laws, regulations, standards, and organisational rules.
3. **Create supporting procedures**: document how the policy will be put into practice across the organisation (for example, how training is assigned, delivered, monitored, and recorded for different role groups such as system owners, operational technology (OT) operators, and data stewards).
4. **Define who receives what**:
   - Share the **policy** with **all employees, contractors, and third-party users who have access to organisation systems and data**.
   - Share the **procedures** with **all people with security or privacy responsibilities** (including **system owners, OT operators, and data stewards**).
5. **Set review and update triggers**:
   - Review and update the **policy at least every year**, and also **after** security incidents or breaches, assessment or audit findings, or changes to relevant laws, executive orders, directives, regulations, policies, standards, or guidelines.
   - Review and update the **procedures at least every year**, and also **after** major organisational or system changes, and after security incidents or breaches, audit findings, or changes to relevant laws, directives, regulations, policies, standards, or guidelines.
6. **Keep audit-ready records**: keep controlled, versioned copies of the policy and procedures, approval records, records showing who received them (for example, acknowledgement logs or distribution confirmations), and review/update records linked to the triggers above.

###### WHO
- **Chief Information Security Officer (CISO)**: accountable for managing the policy and procedures.
- **Security Awareness Programme Manager**: responsible day-to-day for drafting, coordinating, and maintaining the procedures.
- **Privacy Officer**: coordinates to ensure privacy requirements are included and consistent with the privacy programme.

###### Acceptance criteria
- An organisation-wide **awareness and training policy** exists, has been approved through the defined governance process, and has been shared with **all employees, contractors, and third-party users who have access** to organisation systems and data.
- Supporting **awareness and training procedures** exist, have been approved, and have been shared with **all people with security or privacy responsibilities** (including system owners, OT operators, and data stewards).
- The policy is scheduled for review **at least annually** and updated **after the defined events**; the procedures are scheduled for review **at least annually** and updated **after the defined events and major organisational or system changes**.
- Evidence is available for: the current versions, approvals, sharing/acknowledgements, and documented review/update decisions linked to the defined triggers.

###### Actions Required for Compliance

- [ ] Appoint the Chief Information Security Officer (CISO) as the official responsible for managing the awareness and training policy and procedures.
- [ ] Create and get approval for an organisation-wide awareness and training policy. It should set out the purpose, who it applies to, the roles and responsibilities of each group, how it will be coordinated, and how compliance will be handled.
- [ ] Draft and approve awareness and training procedures that explain how the policy will be put into practice for the defined groups of job roles.
- [ ] Share and distribute the policy to all employees, contractors, and third parties who have access to the organisation’s systems and data.
- [ ] Share and distribute the procedures with all staff who have security or privacy responsibilities, including system owners, operational technology (OT) operators, and data stewards.
- [ ] Carry out documented review and update cycles: review the policy every year and after defined events; review the procedures every year, after major organisational or system changes, and after defined events.
- [ ] Keep version-controlled records showing who approved, who received or acknowledged the information, and what decisions were made to review and update it, so the organisation is ready for audits.

### AT-2 — Literacy Training and Awareness (Control)

This requirement is about making sure everyone who uses the organisation’s systems understands, in plain terms, how to protect information and people’s privacy, and what to do if something looks wrong. Without regular, practical reminders and training, staff may fall for common scams, share access by mistake, mishandle sensitive information, or miss early warning signs of a security incident—leading to data breaches, legal issues, financial loss, and harm to individuals.

The organisation must provide security and privacy training when people first start using the systems. It must then repeat security training every 180 days and privacy training every 180 days. It must also provide extra training after major system changes, new threats, audit findings, security incidents or breaches, or changes to relevant laws and policies. This includes specific events such as phishing, malware, sharing login details, repeated failed login attempts, suspected data leaving the organisation, privacy handling mistakes, signs of health or data-protection breaches, or access to unauthorised records.

The organisation should reinforce learning using posters, messages at login, emails, awareness events, and reminder materials. It must update the training content quarterly and after major triggers, and include lessons learned from real incidents.

##### Automate security & privacy literacy training with trigger-based refresh

- Category: Software
- Priority: Critical

###### WHAT
Set up an identity-linked security and privacy learning programme that provides: (1) initial security and privacy training for all authorised system users, (2) refresher security training every **180 days** and refresher privacy training every **180 days**, (3) extra security and/or privacy training when defined events happen, and (4) ongoing awareness reinforcement using defined methods (posters, messages on the logon screen, email updates, awareness events, reminder materials). Update the training and awareness content on a defined schedule (**quarterly**) and after defined triggers (major system changes, new threats, audit findings, security incidents/data breaches, and changes to relevant laws, directives, or policies).

###### WHY (control requirement)
NIST Special Publication (SP) 800-53 **AT-2 Literacy Training and Awareness** requires organisations to provide basic and advanced training (including ways to check people understand it), tailor the content to the organisation’s needs, system access, and working environment (for example, working from home), and include security and privacy actions, what to do if a suspected incident occurs, operational security, and how to handle personal data. It also requires refresher training at least as often as required, and regular updates to the content after events such as assessment/audit findings, security incidents/data breaches, and changes to laws or policies.

###### HOW (specific steps/approach)
###### 1) Define training groups and what they cover
- Create **security** and **privacy** training tracks (basic vs advanced) and match each track to **system access categories** (for example, standard users, privileged operators, administrators) and people who work from home or remotely.
- Make sure the training covers: why security and privacy matter; what users should do to keep systems secure and protect personal privacy; what to do if they suspect an incident; operational security; and how to handle **personally identifiable information**.

###### 2) Set up automatic enrolment and enforcement using the organisation’s central identity system
- Link the training platform/learning system (LMS) to the organisation’s **central identity provider** so training is automatically assigned when people join the organisation or gain access.
- Require completion before people can access relevant application roles (for example, extra checks at sign-in for high-sensitivity roles), so all authorised users receive the initial training.

###### 3) Set the schedule and re-training triggers
Use the organisation’s agreed settings:
- **Security training frequency:** every **180 days** (at-02_odp.01)
- **Privacy training frequency:** every **180 days** (at-02_odp.02)
- **Security re-training triggers:** phishing compromise, malware infection, sharing credentials, not following policy, repeated failed logon attempts (at-02_odp.03)
- **Privacy re-training triggers:** suspected data being taken out of the organisation, mistakes in handling personally identifiable information, signs of a HIPAA/General Data Protection Regulation (GDPR) breach, access to unauthorised records (at-02_odp.04)
- **Content update frequency:** quarterly (at-02_odp.06)
- **Content update triggers:** major system changes, new threats, audit findings, security incidents/data breaches, changes to laws/directives/policies (at-02_odp.07)

Put these triggers into operation by linking detection sources (for example, alerts from the security information and event management (SIEM) system/security operations centre (SOC), identity and access management (IAM) logs, data loss prevention (DLP)/personally identifiable information (PII) handling signals, and audit findings workflows) to the training platform. This ensures affected user groups are re-enrolled for the correct training track(s) and the correct content version.

###### 4) Deliver awareness messages through standard channels
Set up awareness reinforcement to use the organisation’s defined methods (at-02_odp.05):
- messages on the logon screen (identity provider sign-in banner)
- email updates from security/privacy staff
- posters and reminder materials (digital displays and/or printable packs)
- awareness events (scheduled sessions/webinars)

###### 5) Test understanding and keep proof
- Include checks after each training module and set a **minimum pass score** for the programme.
- Keep evidence for audit purposes: enrolment, completion, assessment scores, the content version, and the reason for the trigger.

###### WHO
- **Security Awareness Programme Manager** (owns the programme, curriculum, and assessment criteria)
- **Identity and Access Management (IAM)/Identity Engineering Lead** (sets up the identity provider integration and enforcement)
- **Security Operations Centre (SOC)/Security Operations (SecOps) Lead** (defines the triggers and runs the alert-to-training process)
- **Privacy Officer / Data Protection Lead** (owns the privacy curriculum and scenarios for handling personally identifiable information)
- **Application Owner** (ensures system access categories and people working from home/remote are mapped correctly)

###### Acceptance criteria
- All authorised system users receive **initial** security and privacy training before being granted access to the defined application roles.
- Evidence shows that security training is completed for each user group at least every **180 days**, and privacy training is completed at least every **180 days**.
- If any defined **security** or **privacy** event occurs, affected users are re-enrolled on the correct track(s) within the organisation’s operational service level agreement (SLA) (to be defined in the programme), and completion is evidenced.
- Training and awareness content is refreshed **quarterly** and after each defined content-update trigger; evidence shows the content version used for each training instance.
- Awareness methods are active and evidenced (identity provider logon messages, email updates, posters/reminder materials, and awareness events).
- Knowledge testing results (assessment scores) are kept and linked to the relevant users and training instances.

###### Actions Required for Compliance

- [ ] Set up security and privacy training courses (basic and advanced) for each system access category, including separate groups for teleworking and remote working.
- [ ] Connect the learning management system (LMS) training platform to the organisation’s identity provider (IdP) to automatically assign new users to required training and enforce access rules.
- [ ] Set up recurring security and privacy training schedules so that each user group completes the training at least once every 180 days.
- [ ] Set up automated workflows that, when the agreed security and privacy events happen, start the corresponding training actions. Use security information and event management (SIEM), identity and access management (IAM), data loss prevention (DLP), and audit signals to detect these events.
- [ ] Set up security awareness delivery channels—such as identity provider (IdP) logon banners, email alerts, printed posters and reminder materials, and awareness events—with version-controlled content.
- [ ] Add post-training assessments with a clear pass mark, and keep records of enrolment, completion, and scores. Link these records to the specific training content versions and the reasons the training was triggered.
- [ ] Set up a quarterly process to update training materials, and a separate process to publish new versions when incidents, audits, or legal changes occur.

#### AT-2.1 — Practical Exercises (Enhancement)

This expectation is about making staff training feel real by running practical literacy and security-awareness exercises that copy what attackers try to do. Without realistic, hands-on practice, people may only remember guidance in theory and then fail when under pressure—for example, by sharing sensitive information, trying to access something they should not, or being tricked by messages into opening harmful email attachments or clicking harmful links.

To meet this expectation, the organisation must provide practical exercises for the relevant people and ensure each exercise is based on a realistic scenario that looks like a real incident. At least one exercise must be run without warning and include a safe, training-only attempt to influence participants to collect information and to try to gain unauthorised access, without affecting real systems.

The organisation must also include separate scenarios that simulate the harmful impact of opening malicious attachments and clicking malicious links, delivered in a targeted style for at least one of them. The organisation must document how it will do this, keep training materials and records, and assign named responsibilities for delivering and running the exercises.

##### No-notice literacy exercises with safe phishing and link/attachment sims

- Category: Manual
- Priority: Critical

###### WHAT
Deliver practical reading and awareness training exercises that mimic real security events and incidents, including:
- At least one **no-notice** social engineering attempt that **gathers information** and **tries to get unauthorised access** using **training-only** methods.
- Separate scenarios that show the **harmful effects** of opening **malicious email attachments** and clicking **malicious web links**, with at least one delivered in a **targeted spear-phishing style**.

###### WHY (control requirement)
The United States National Institute of Standards and Technology (NIST) Special Publication 800-53 **AT-2(1) Practical Exercises (Enhancement)** requires practical exercises that simulate events and incidents. The guidance says practical exercises must include **no-notice social engineering** attempts to gather information and try to gain unauthorised access, and must also simulate the harmful effects of opening malicious attachments and clicking malicious web links (including spear-phishing delivery for at least one scenario).

###### HOW (specific approach)
1. **Define the exercise scope and audiences**: identify the relevant groups of people (for example, all staff, contractors, privileged users, helpdesk, finance) and place them into exercise groups (tiers).
2. **Create training-only simulation materials**:
   - A **training-only landing page** (or equivalent) that records what participants do and provides safe “what to do next” guidance.
   - A **training-only restricted resource** (for example, a sandboxed document area, a training ticket queue, or a controlled web page) that can only be requested or accessed during the exercise period.
   - A **safe attachment simulation** (for example, an email attachment that sends people to the training landing page rather than running harmful code).
3. **Run at least one no-notice social engineering exercise per cycle**:
   - Use a scenario that attempts **information gathering** (for example, an “urgent form” asking for non-production information) and attempts **unauthorised access** (for example, a link to the training-only restricted resource with an access request process).
   - Make sure the simulation is **training-only** and cannot affect live (production) systems.
4. **Run separate harmful-effects scenarios for attachments and links**:
   - **Attachment scenario**: send a realistic email where “opening” the simulated malicious attachment takes the participant to the training landing page that explains the correct response.
   - **Link scenario**: send a realistic email where clicking the simulated malicious link takes the participant to the training landing page.
   - For at least one scenario, deliver it in a **targeted spear-phishing style** (for example, wording and sender context relevant to a role or department), while still being **training-only**.
5. **Measure results and keep evidence**:
   - For each scenario, record: the audience, the time it was sent, whether participants clicked/opened, whether they reported it, and whether they completed the safe learning step.
   - Keep evidence in an audit-ready format (scenario runbook, campaign configuration, and results report).
6. **Assign named responsibilities**:
   - Appoint an exercise owner (programme lead) and a person responsible for information security to approve the scenarios, ensure the training-only safety controls are in place, and confirm sign-off on results.

###### WHO
- **Exercise Programme Lead (manual/process owner)**: defines the audiences, approves the exercise schedule, and ensures at least one no-notice exercise is run per cycle.
- **Information Security Lead (manual/process owner)**: approves the social engineering approach and the safety boundaries (training-only), ensures evidence is kept, and confirms that unauthorised access attempts are limited to non-production environments.
- **Security Awareness Facilitator / Training Operations (manual/process owner)**: runs the exercises, monitors delivery, and compiles the results.

###### Acceptance criteria
- At least one **no-notice** exercise is carried out per cycle and includes both **information gathering** and **unauthorised access attempts** using **training-only** methods.
- Separate exercises are in place to simulate the harmful effects of **malicious attachments** and **malicious links**.
- At least one attachment/link scenario is delivered in a **targeted spear-phishing style**.
- For each exercise, evidence is kept showing scenario details and measurable outcomes (for example, click/open/report rates) and named approvals.
- Training materials are clearly separated from live (production) systems, with no impact on real systems or real data.

###### Actions Required for Compliance

- [ ] Identify the relevant staff groups and set training levels for literacy training exercises
- [ ] Approve and record the limits for training-only scenarios to ensure they cannot affect live production systems
- [ ] Create at least one “no warning” social engineering exercise that tries to collect information and gain unauthorised access using resources intended only for training.
- [ ] Create separate “malicious attachment” and “malicious link” training simulations, each with safe learning outcomes.
- [ ] Provide at least one example of targeted spear-phishing in the training simulation, while ensuring it is for training only.
- [ ] Store scenario settings, delivery dates, and measurable participant results in an evidence repository that is ready for audit.
- [ ] Assign named responsibilities for approving the exercise, delivering it, and providing information security sign-off.

#### AT-2.2 — Insider Threat (Enhancement)

This requirement is about making sure everyone in the organisation understands what warning signs of harmful insider behaviour can look like and how to raise a concern early. Without this, a trusted employee or contractor could misuse their access or cause harm—for example, by showing long-term dissatisfaction, trying to access information they do not need for their role, having unexplained financial problems, bullying or harassing colleagues, threatening or carrying out violence in the workplace, or breaking serious workplace rules and policies.

To meet this expectation, the organisation must provide training that is appropriate for each role to all relevant staff. This training should help them recognise these warning signs and explain clearly how and where to report them using the organisation’s established reporting routes. Staff must follow the organisation’s internal procedures and should not try to investigate concerns themselves.

The organisation should clearly assign responsibility for keeping the training up to date, maintain the training materials and course content, and keep records showing that the required people completed the training.

##### Role-based insider threat literacy training with reporting channels

- Category: Manual
- Priority: High

###### WHAT
Provide role-appropriate insider threat awareness training to all relevant people (employees, contractors, and anyone who can access confidential information). The training should help them recognise possible insider threat warning signs and report concerns quickly using the organisation’s established reporting channels.

###### WHY (control requirement)
The National Institute of Standards and Technology (NIST) Special Publication (SP) 800-53 **AT-2(2)** enhancement requires the organisation to **deliver training on recognising and reporting possible insider threat warning signs**. The guidance includes example warning signs (for example: long-term job dissatisfaction, attempts to access information they do not need for their job, unexplained access to financial resources, bullying or harassment, workplace violence, and serious breaches of policies). It also requires training to include **how to communicate concerns** through established channels and in line with the organisation’s policies and procedures.

###### HOW (specific steps/approach)
1. **Set the training scope and role-based tracks** in an insider threat awareness procedure. At minimum, create tracks for **employees/contractors** and **managers/team leaders** (optionally add a specialist track for security and privacy champions). Include anyone who can access confidential organisational information.
2. **Create training modules on warning signs** using clear, plain language and examples aligned to the guidance: long-term job dissatisfaction; attempts to access information not needed for job performance; unexplained access to financial resources; bullying/harassment; workplace violence; and other serious breaches of policies, procedures, directives, regulations, rules, or practices.
3. **Use “recognise → report” scenarios for each role track**. Each scenario must require the learner to spot possible warning signs and choose the correct reporting route. This should reinforce that they should **communicate concerns** and **not investigate the matter themselves**.
4. **Standardise reporting instructions within the training** so they match the organisation’s established channels and escalation or backup routes (for example: line manager/human resources/security contact and any confidential hotline or portal). Clearly state the expected reporting behaviour and timing (promptly).
5. **Deliver the training through a way that can be audited** (for example: learning management system (LMS) assignments for standard roles; facilitated sessions or toolbox talks for shift/field or higher-risk groups). Record training completion with **individual evidence**.
6. **Manage and update the training content**: appoint an owner (for example: human resources, with input from Security and Compliance), keep materials under version control, set a review and update schedule, and reassign training when job roles or reporting channels change.

###### WHO
- **Human Resources (HR) / People Operations**: owns the training programme, role mapping, delivery, and completion evidence.
- **Security / Insider Threat Programme Lead (or Security Compliance)**: checks that the warning sign content and reporting routes are correct.
- **Line Managers**: complete the manager track and reinforce reporting expectations.
- **Learning Management System (LMS) / Information Technology (IT) Service Owner**: ensures assignments and evidence capture work properly and can be audited.

###### Acceptance criteria
- All identified relevant personnel have completed the **appropriate role track** using the current version of the training.
- The training content clearly covers the guidance’s example warning signs and includes instructions on **how to report through established channels**.
- The training uses scenarios to reinforce **prompt reporting** and the expectation to **not investigate themselves**.
- There is evidence of completion for each individual, including completion date/time and the curriculum version.
- The curriculum has an assigned owner, a documented review and update process, and version control for materials and reporting instructions.

###### Actions Required for Compliance

- [ ] Define the scope of insider-threat training, including employees, contractors, and any staff with privileged access. Then create separate training tracks for employees and contractors, and for managers and team leads.
- [ ] Create training materials to help people understand the indicators described in the guidance examples, including: job dissatisfaction, attempts to access information that is not required, unexplained access to financial information, bullying or harassment, threats or incidents of workplace violence, and serious breaches of workplace policy.
- [ ] Create scenario-based training modules that ask learners to spot warning signs and choose the correct place to report them. Clearly tell learners not to investigate the issue themselves.
- [ ] Publish and include in the training materials the organisation-approved reporting instructions and the agreed escalation and backup routes, in line with the existing policies and procedures.
- [ ] Deliver the training using a method that can be independently checked (for example, learning management system (LMS) assignments and/or facilitated sessions), and record proof of completion for each person, including the curriculum version identifier.
- [ ] Assign a named owner to keep the training materials up to date using version control, and set a regular schedule for review and refresh. Reassign the owner whenever reporting channels or role definitions change.

#### AT-2.3 — Social Engineering and Mining (Enhancement)

This requirement is about making sure everyone in the organisation can recognise and respond to attempts to trick people or quietly collect information for future attacks. Without regular, practical training, staff may be persuaded to share confidential information, click on harmful messages, or pass on internal information to someone pretending to be legitimate. This can lead to fraud, loss of data, or disruption.

To meet this expectation, the organisation must set out a clear training approach and a documented way to deliver it. This should include who must attend, who is responsible for running the training, and how it will be updated and tracked over time. The training must cover both recognising and reporting social engineering (tricks used to manipulate people) and social information-gathering (actions used to collect information about people or systems). It must use plain-language examples, and it must explain exactly how and where staff should report concerns using the organisation’s established reporting channels and procedures.

The organisation should keep evidence that the required people completed the training and that it included both recognising issues and reporting them.

##### Enterprise “Social Engineering & Social Mining” literacy programme

- Category: Manual
- Priority: High

###### WHAT must be done
Provide documented, scenario-based awareness training that enables all relevant staff to **recognise and report** both **social engineering** (for example: phishing, pretexting, impersonation, baiting/quid pro quo, thread-jacking, social media exploitation, tailgating) and **social mining** (gathering information about the organisation to help plan future attacks), using the organisation’s established reporting channels.

###### WHY (control requirement)
NIST SP 800-53 **AT-2(3)** (enhancement) requires the organisation to provide **awareness training** on recognising and reporting **potential and actual** instances of social engineering and social mining, including how to raise concerns through **organisational channels based on established policies and procedures**.

###### HOW (specific steps/approach)
1. **Create and approve a training programme** (a policy and procedure pack) that clearly covers both recognition and reporting for social engineering and social mining, and sets the training schedule (initial training plus refresh) and the triggers for re-training (for example: a major incident, or a new campaign pattern).
2. **Set out the reporting process** in the training materials: the exact “where/how to report” route(s) (for example: security mailbox, ticket category, reporting form, security operations centre intake process) and the **immediate actions** staff must take (for example: do not click or respond; preserve evidence; report using the named channel).
3. **Deliver scenario-based training** using role-based examples that match the organisation’s environment (for example: finance/procurement vendor impersonation, human resources identity/benefits scams, helpdesk credential reset pretexting, executive impersonation, site access/tailgating cues, and social mining patterns such as probing organisational structure, staff schedules, vendor relationships, and system details).
4. **Record completion and keep evidence** through the organisation’s learning management system (LMS) and human resources onboarding process, including new-starter catch-up and periodic refresh completion; retain audit-ready records showing training was completed and included both recognition and reporting.
5. **Check training effectiveness** with short scenario questions after each module (for example: “choose your next action” and “identify the social mining indicators”) and use the results to update the training content.

###### WHO (role responsible)
- **Cybersecurity Awareness Lead / Security Training Owner**: owns the training content, the reporting process content, and how effectiveness is measured.
- **Security Operations (SOC/CSIRT) or Security Incident Management Lead**: confirms the reporting intake process and what staff should expect during triage.
- **Human Resources/People Operations + LMS Administrator**: ensures onboarding/catch-up, assignment rules, and completion evidence.
- **Line Managers** (for high-risk groups): ensure attendance for role-based sessions where required.

###### Acceptance criteria
- Training materials explicitly cover **both** social engineering and social mining, and include **recognition and reporting** instructions.
- The training clearly states the **authorised reporting channels** and the **immediate actions** staff must take when they suspect an incident.
- Completion records exist for all required staff (including onboarding/catch-up) and are kept for audit.
- Scenario checks show that participants can correctly identify and respond to at least the defined minimum set of social engineering and social mining examples.
- The programme is refreshed according to the agreed schedule/triggers, and curriculum updates are documented.

###### Actions Required for Compliance

- [ ] Document and get approval for a literacy training programme that covers how to recognise and how to report social engineering and social mining.
- [ ] Publish the exact reporting channels within the organisation and the immediate actions staff should take if they suspect social engineering or social mining.
- [ ] Develop role-based, scenario-based training modules with examples covering phishing and impersonation, and signs of social mining
- [ ] Set up training for all relevant staff through the learning management system (LMS) and human resources (HR) onboarding process, and make sure new starters complete any missed training as a catch-up.
- [ ] Keep audit-ready proof that the training has been completed, and confirm that it covers both recognising the issue and reporting it.
- [ ] After each module, run scenario checks and use the results to update the curriculum at the agreed schedule and when the agreed triggers occur.

#### AT-2.4 — Suspicious Communications and Anomalous System Behavior (Enhancement)

This requirement ensures that everyone in the organisation can recognise the warning signs of suspicious messages and unusual computer behaviour, so they do not accidentally help something harmful. Without this, staff may open unexpected email attachments or click strange links, trust messages that pretend to be from a familiar sponsor or contractor but are actually from an unfamiliar sender, or dismiss odd message quality such as poor grammar and unusual wording. They may also miss early warning signs such as repeated failed login attempts or unexpected activity running on their device, which can allow malicious software to spread.

To meet this expectation, the organisation must create and keep up to date a written training policy, procedures, training plan (curriculum) and materials. These must clearly set out the specific warning signs staff should look for and what staff must do when they see them, including how to report suspicious emails or web communications using the organisation’s agreed reporting route. The organisation must assign responsibility for delivering the basic training and for reviewing or approving the training content, include the training in relevant planning documents, keep evidence that staff have received it, and ensure trained staff can correctly recognise the warning signs and carry out the correct reporting actions.

##### Scenario-based training for suspicious comms and anomalous behaviour

- Category: Manual
- Priority: High

###### WHAT
Deliver role-based training that teaches personnel to recognise **suspicious messages** and **unusual system activity**, and to respond using the organisation’s agreed reporting routes.

###### WHY (control requirement)
NIST SP 800-53 **AT-2(4)** (enhancement) requires the organisation to provide training on recognising **suspicious messages** and **unusual behaviour** in organisational systems, using the organisation-defined indicators in **{{ insert: param, at-02.04_odp }}**. The guidance expects training to cover indicators (for example: unexpected attachments or links, impersonation (spoofing), poor grammar, repeated failed logins, unexpected actions/processes) and how to respond to suspicious email and web communications.

###### HOW (specific steps/approach)
1. **Define training indicators (from at-02.04_odp)** in plain language and build them into training scenarios:
   - Suspicious message indicators: **unexpected email attachments/links**, **impersonation (spoofing) of a known sponsor by an unfamiliar sender**, **poor grammar**.
   - Unusual behaviour indicators: **repeated failed logins** and **unexpected actions/processes**.
2. **Create a scenario library** of short, repeatable modules (microlearning) for:
   - “Suspicious email/web communication” (what it looks like, what not to do, and immediate response steps).
   - “Unusual system behaviour” (what it looks like and how to escalate/report).
   Each scenario must include the correct signs to recognise and the correct actions to take.

### AT-3 — Role-based Training (Control)

This requirement ensures that the right people receive the right security and privacy training for their specific responsibilities before they are allowed to access systems or handle information, and that the training stays up to date. Without this, staff and contractors may misunderstand how to protect data and privacy, follow the wrong procedures, or miss important updates—raising the risk of data leaks, privacy breaches, and costly incidents.

- identify which roles need security and privacy training;

- changes to systems;
- assessment or audit findings;
- security incidents or breaches;
- major changes in laws or policies; or
- major changes to systems or architecture.

###### WHY (control requirement)
NIST SP 800-53 **AT-3(3) Practical Exercises** requires the organisation to provide practical exercises in security and privacy training that reinforce training objectives. The guidance specifically expects simulated common software weakness attacks and targeted spear/“whale” phishing for senior leaders, and privacy modules that include PII recognition/handling quizzes and PIA scenario practice.

###### HOW (specific approach)
1. **Create role-based exercise tracks** linked to training objectives:
   - **Developers track (security)**: a guided lab that simulates exploitation of common software weaknesses (for example, injection and broken access control). Participants must identify the problem and apply a fix (for example, a secure code change and/or test evidence).
   - **Executive track (security)**: non-destructive spear/“whale” phishing simulations aligned to executive working routines (for example, board pack or invoice approval themes). Capture measurable behaviours (for example, whether they reported it or not, whether they clicked links, and whether they followed the reporting process).
   - **Privacy track (privacy)**: scenario-based quizzes to test PII recognition and the correct actions to take, plus a PIA practical where participants complete the required PIA steps and produce the required outputs for a given scenario.
2. **Set up scoring and evidence capture** in the training platform (or integrated learning management system (LMS)):
   - Require completion of each module.
   - Record results (scores/pass or fail, common mistakes, and time taken where appropriate) and keep completion logs.
   - Ensure each exercise includes a **debrief** that reinforces the correct response in line with organisational procedures.
3. **Run exercises on a planned schedule** (for example, quarterly or biannually) and ensure scenario templates are standardised and version-controlled.
4. **Manage and improve using outcomes**:
   - Review overall results and the most common reasons for failure.
   - Update scenarios, hints, and debrief content based on observed gaps.
   - Keep audit-ready evidence of delivery, participant completion, and recorded results.

###### WHO (roles responsible)
- **Security Training Owner / Security Awareness Lead**: responsible for designing security exercises, phishing templates, and debrief content.
- **Privacy Officer / Data Protection Lead**: responsible for designing privacy quizzes and PIA scenarios and ensuring they are correct.
- **Application Security Lead / Secure Coding Team**: responsible for the developer software weakness lab content and what remediation is expected.
- **LMS/Training Platform Administrator**: configures tracking, scoring, and evidence exports.
- **Compliance/Audit Liaison**: checks that evidence meets audit expectations.

###### Acceptance criteria
- Security training includes **(a)** simulated common software weakness attacks for developers and **(b)** spear/“whale” phishing simulations targeted at senior leaders/executives, with measurable behaviour capture and a documented debrief.
- Privacy training includes **(a)** quizzes on identifying and handling PII in scenarios and **(b)** scenario-based PIA practical exercises where participants complete the required PIA steps/outputs.
- For each exercise, the organisation can produce audit-ready evidence showing: the module was delivered, the participant completed it, and the results were recorded (scores/pass or fail and/or captured behaviours), plus debrief completion.
- Exercise scenarios are repeatable and version-controlled, and outcomes are used to update training content.

###### Actions Required for Compliance

- [ ] Define which modules each group of people can access: developers, senior leaders and executives, and privacy stakeholders.
- [ ] Create and publish a secure coding practical training lab for developers, including simulated examples of common security weaknesses being exploited and evidence of how the issues were fixed.
- [ ] Set up non-disruptive “spear phishing” and “whaling” email tests for senior leaders and executives, with measurable records of their responses and mandatory reporting and follow-up debriefs.
- [ ] Create privacy quiz scenarios to help people recognise personal data (PII) and choose the correct actions for processing and handling it, with automated scoring.
- [ ] Create a practical privacy impact assessment (PIA) scenario where participants must complete the required PIA steps and produce the required outputs, using a scoring rubric to assess performance.
- [ ] Set up the learning management system (LMS) or training platform to track course completion, record results, and export evidence for audit-ready reporting.
- [ ] Run the first practice cycle, hold debrief sessions, and update the scenarios based on the most common failure modes.

#### AT-3.5 — Processing Personally Identifiable Information (Enhancement)

This requirement is about making sure everyone who handles personal information understands exactly how to do it lawfully and openly. Without this, staff could use people’s details for the wrong reason, share them when they should not, or fail to meet the organisation’s commitments about how that information will be used. This can lead to complaints, legal issues, and real harm to individuals.

The organisation must identify every role that has access to personal information, including system owners, data stewards, and privacy officers. It must provide them with initial training before they start handling personal information, and then refresher training every year.

The training must explain what counts as personal information, the risks and responsibilities of handling it, and the organisation’s authority and permitted reasons for using it. It must also cover the organisation’s transparency expectations and point staff to the relevant privacy and data-sharing documents, such as privacy notices and policies, official notices about records, privacy statements, assessments, sharing agreements, and contracts.

##### Identity-gated PII training with annual refresher enforcement

- Category: Software
- Priority: Critical

###### WHAT
Set up role-based training for everyone who has access to personally identifiable information (PII). This includes **initial training** and **annual refresher training**. Make sure training completion is required before anyone can access systems or functions that process PII.

###### WHY (control requirement)
The National Institute of Standards and Technology (NIST) Special Publication (SP) 800-53 **AT-3(5)** enhancement requires: “Provide {{ insert: param, at-03.05_odp.01 }} with initial and {{ insert: param, at-03.05_odp.02 }} training in the employment and operation of personally identifiable information processing and transparency controls.”
The training must cover:
- the types of information that may count as PII,
- the risks, key considerations, and responsibilities/obligations,
- the organisation’s **authorisation to process PII** and the **purposes** for which PII is processed,
- and transparency controls, referencing relevant privacy and data-sharing documents (for example: privacy policies/notices, system of records notices, privacy impact assessments (PIAs), Privacy Act statements, contracts, information sharing agreements, and memoranda of understanding).

###### HOW (specific steps/approach)
1. **Define the PII-access roles**: create an official list of the “people or roles” that have access to PII (including system owners, data stewards, privacy officers, and any other roles that are allowed to process PII). Align this list to the application’s data areas and the organisation’s approval model.
2. **Create role-based training for the learning system**:
   - A baseline module for all PII-access roles covering: what counts as PII, the risks and responsibilities, transparency controls, and how to find the relevant privacy/transparency documents.
   - Additional modules for system owners/data stewards covering: the system-specific authorisation and permitted purposes for processing, and how to interpret system documentation.
   - An additional module for privacy officers covering: governance, escalation routes, and checking that processing aligns with the privacy documents.
   Each module must clearly reference the organisation’s privacy and transparency document set (privacy policies/notices, system of records notices, PIAs/Privacy Act statements, contracts/information sharing agreements/memoranda of understanding, and any other relevant sharing/processing agreements).
3. **Link training completion to identity records**: configure the learning system to share completion status (initial and refresher) with the organisation’s enterprise identity provider (IdP) using System for Cross-domain Identity Management (SCIM)/application programming interface (API). Map completion to groups, attributes, and entitlements.
4. **Control access using conditional access / entitlement checks**: configure the IdP (or access governance tool) so that any entitlement that allows PII processing requires:
   - completion of **initial** PII training before PII access is granted, and
   - completion of **annual refresher** training within the defined time window ({{ at-03.05_odp.02 }} = Annual refresher training).
   If training is overdue or missing, automatically restrict or remove PII processing entitlements. If the organisation allows temporary access, use an exception process that can be audited.
5. **Produce audit-ready records**: ensure the system logs (a) which roles were trained, (b) completion dates and times, (c) which entitlements were granted or withheld, and (d) the training material versions referenced at the time of completion.

###### WHO (role responsible)
- **Privacy Officer / Data Protection Lead**: owns the training content requirements, maps the transparency documents, and approves the training materials.
- **Application/System Owner & Data Stewards**: confirm the system-specific authorisation and permitted purposes, and ensure training refers to the correct system documentation.
- **Identity and Access Management (IAM) Lead**: implements IdP conditional access/entitlement checks and the group/attribute mappings.
- **Learning Platform Owner**: sets up courses, manages versions, and reports completion.
- **Security/Compliance**: checks that evidence is produced and supports audit readiness.

###### Acceptance criteria
- Everyone identified as **{{ at-03.05_odp.01 }}** who can access PII must complete **initial** training before being granted PII processing entitlements.
- Everyone in scope must complete **annual refresher** training at least once per year (**{{ at-03.05_odp.02 }}**), and access must be automatically restricted when refresher training is overdue.
- Training content must include: (1) what counts as PII, (2) risks and responsibilities/obligations, (3) the organisation’s authority and permitted purposes for processing, and (4) transparency controls with references to the organisation’s privacy/transparency document set.
- Audit evidence must exist showing training completion dates and times, and the related access decisions (granted/withheld/restricted) for a representative sample of users and roles.

###### Actions Required for Compliance

- [ ] Create and get approval for an official list of the people and job roles that are allowed to access personal data (PII) for the application and each of its data areas.
- [ ] Create role-based training modules for personal information (PII) that clearly explain what PII is, the risks and responsibilities involved, the permitted uses and who is authorised to use it, and the transparency controls required—referencing the specific privacy documents that must be produced.
- [ ] Set up the learning platform to send initial and yearly refresher completion status to the organisation’s identity provider (enterprise identity provider) using system-to-system integration (SCIM/API). Include time stamps that can be audited and record the course version for each completion.
- [ ] Set up identity provider (IdP) conditional access and entitlement checks so that permission to process personal data (PII) requires completing initial training and completing an annual refresher training within the defined time window.
- [ ] Set up an auditable process for any temporary access that bypasses the training requirement (if allowed by governance).
- [ ] Check and record the evidence produced for audit purposes, including which trained roles were used, the completion dates, and the results of access controls being enforced.

### AT-4 — Training Records (Control)

This requirement ensures that everyone in the organisation is properly trained to protect information and personal data, and that there is a clear written record showing the training took place. Without this, people may not know what they should (or should not) do, the same mistakes could happen again, and the organisation may not be able to prove who was trained and when—making it more vulnerable to data loss, privacy breaches, and avoidable incidents.

The organisation must record and track all information security and privacy training, including basic awareness training for everyone and additional training tailored to specific job roles. It must also regularly check that training is completed and follow up when it is missing or overdue. Finally, it must keep each person’s training records for at least 3 years, in a format that allows the records to be retrieved and verified when needed.

##### LMS-based security/privacy training records retained for 3 years

- Category: Software
- Priority: Critical

###### WHAT must be done
Set up a central, auditable way to record security and privacy training (for example, a learning management system (LMS)). It must capture proof that each person has completed the required training (general security/privacy awareness and any role-based or specialist training), allow the organisation to track who is up to date and who is overdue, and keep each person’s training records for at least the organisation’s defined period (default: **3 years**).

###### WHY (control requirement)
Control AT-4 requires the organisation to keep **records of individual training** and retain them for the required time period. The guidance also says that specialist training records may be kept by supervisors at their discretion, but the organisation must still be able to show that training was completed and retrieve the records when needed.

###### HOW (specific steps/approach)
1. **Use the LMS as the main record (system of record (SoR))** for security/privacy training, and make sure every completion is linked to a named person.
2. **Set up training catalogues** for (a) security awareness and (b) privacy awareness, and include **role-based/specialist training** matched to job families/roles.
3. **Capture completion proof at the time of completion**, including the course/topic name, completion date, completion status, and the learner’s identity (and, where applicable, the completion confirmation or assessment result).
4. **Monitor completion and spot gaps** using scheduled reports that show completed versus overdue training by training type and by person, with the ability to open the underlying record.
5. **Automatically follow up overdue or missing training**, for example by sending reminders and escalating to line managers, and record the follow-up actions in a workflow or ticketing system linked to the person.
6. **Keep records for at least 3 years** by setting the LMS retention and archiving rules for training completion records, and checking that records near the end of the retention period can still be retrieved and exported for audit.
7. **Use supervisor-held evidence only as extra support**, with a documented rule that the LMS remains the baseline record. If supervisors add evidence, ensure it is indexed and can be retrieved against the person’s LMS training record.

###### WHO (role responsible)
- **Information Security & Privacy Training Owner** (responsible for the training catalogue, evidence requirements, and how often monitoring is done)
- **LMS Administrator / Human Resources (HR) Systems Owner** (sets up the LMS, links records to identities, configures retention, and produces reports)
- **Line Managers** (receive escalations and ensure their teams complete the required training)
- **Compliance/Audit Function** (checks periodically that records can be retrieved and are kept for the required period)

###### Acceptance criteria
- For all people in scope, the organisation can produce an **audit-ready list** showing completed security/privacy training and **what training was delivered, when, and to whom**.
- The organisation can show **ongoing monitoring of completion status** and identify overdue or missing training within the agreed reporting schedule.
- Training records for a sample of people are **retrievable and exportable**, including records older than 3 years (or the configured retention period), without missing required information.
- If specialist training evidence is held by supervisors, it is **traceable to the individual** and does not prevent the organisation from retrieving a complete set of training records.

###### Actions Required for Compliance

- [ ] Choose and set up a learning management system (LMS), or a human resources (HR) learning module, as the official system that records when security and privacy training has been completed.
- [ ] Define security awareness, privacy awareness, and role-based (and specialised) training catalogues, mapped to job roles.
- [ ] Set up the completion evidence fields—topic or course name, completion date, completion status, and learner identity—and require a completion attestation or assessment where this applies.
- [ ] Set up scheduled reports and dashboards to show which training has been completed and which is overdue, for each person and for each type of training.
- [ ] Set up automated follow-up actions for overdue or missing training, including escalation to line managers, and link each action to the relevant individual’s training record.
- [ ] Configure the learning management system (LMS) to keep individual training records for at least 3 years, and confirm you can retrieve them by running test exports.
- [ ] Record a rule for keeping evidence of supervisor-held specialised training, and make sure it is filed and linked to the individual’s learning management system (LMS) record so it can be checked during audits.

### AT-6 — Training Feedback (Control)

This requirement ensures that the people responsible for training are promptly and clearly informed about how well staff have learned the information they need.

After each round of security training ends, the organisation must share the results within 30 days with the Chief Information Security Officer, the person responsible for managing the training programme, and the owner of the relevant information system. This covers both the results from general security awareness training and the results from training tailored to specific roles.

The risk is that if poor performance—especially in critical roles—is not identified quickly, the organisation may miss an early warning sign and remain exposed to preventable mistakes or misuse.

To meet this expectation, the organisation must provide feedback detailed enough to assess outcomes and to improve and update the training programme, rather than only confirming that training happened.

##### Automated training feedback packs to CISO, programme manager, system owners

- Category: Software
- Priority: Critical

###### WHAT
Within **30 days** of each security training completion cycle, create and send an outcome-focused training feedback package (including both **awareness training** and **role-based training** results) to the **Chief Information Security Officer (CISO)**, the **training programme manager**, and the relevant **information system owner**.

###### WHY (control requirement)
National Institute of Standards and Technology (NIST) Special Publication (SP) 800-53 **AT-6 Training Feedback** requires the organisation to “Provide feedback on organisational training results” to the people specified by **at-06_odp.01 (frequency)** and **at-06_odp.02 (personnel)**. The guidance stresses that training results—especially failures in critical roles—must be escalated to senior leadership. This helps the organisation take the right actions and update training, supporting the evaluation and update activities referenced in **AT-2b** and **AT-3b**.

###### HOW (specific steps/approach)
Set up an automated process that moves information from the learning management system (LMS) into a workflow, so that it:
1. **Closes the training cycle** using a defined “cycle end” time (end of the completion window plus any agreed grace period), then starts the feedback creation.
2. **Pulls training results** from the LMS for that cycle, including:
   - **Awareness training results** (for example, completion status and assessment outcomes)
   - **Role-based training results** (for example, results by role and/or module)
   - **Critical-role failures** (identify failures for roles that are mapped as critical)
3. **Presents results in a standard format** that supports review and updates (results, not just attendance), including links back to the LMS reports/exports as evidence.
4. **Selects the right recipients**:
   - Always include the **CISO** and the **training programme manager**.
   - Identify the **information system owner** using an authoritative system-to-owner register (for example, the governance/IT service catalogue mapping).
5. **Produces an auditable “feedback pack”** (portal view and/or PDF) for each cycle, including:
   - Summary figures (completion, pass/fail or proficiency)
   - Breakdown by role
   - Exceptions and highlights for critical-role failures
   - Evidence references and dates/times
6. **Delivers within 30 days** using a ticket/work queue with a service-level agreement (SLA) timer (for example, escalate if not delivered by day 25). Record delivery dates/times and the final documents in a controlled, auditable repository.

###### WHO (role responsible)
- **Training programme manager**: sets the criteria for when a training cycle is closed, governs who receives the packs, and approves the content of each feedback pack.
- **CISO**: receives the feedback and ensures appropriate response actions are taken.
- **Information system owner**: receives the system-relevant role-based results and starts remediation where needed.
- **Platform/Integration engineer (or learning management system (LMS) integration owner)**: builds the automation to extract results from the LMS, format them, and deliver them through the workflow.

###### Acceptance criteria
- For every training completion cycle, a feedback pack is created and delivered to the **CISO**, **training programme manager**, and the correct **information system owner(s)**.
- Delivery happens **within 30 days** of the cycle completion.
- The pack includes **awareness training results** and **role-based training results**, with **outcome-focused** content (assessment/proficiency and failure indicators), including clear identification of **critical-role failures**.
- Evidence links and delivery dates/times are kept in an auditable repository.
- The workflow shows it meets the SLA (for example, escalation/escalation logs for packs not delivered by day 25).

###### Actions Required for Compliance

- [ ] Define and publish the training cycle close criteria that are used to trigger the generation of feedback.
- [ ] Connect the learning management system (LMS) to a workflow or orchestration tool to extract results from awareness training and role-based training.
- [ ] Create a standard feedback-pack template that includes outcome measures and links to supporting evidence.
- [ ] Set up an official system-to-owner record so feedback is sent to the correct information system owner(s).
- [ ] Set up a service level agreement (SLA)-based delivery process so that feedback is sent within 30 days, with escalation to the next level before day 25 if it is not on track.
- [ ] Store the generated feedback packs and the times they were delivered in an auditable records repository.
