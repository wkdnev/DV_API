### CM-1 — Policy and Procedures (Control)

This requirement is about making sure your organisation has clear, written rules for how system settings and changes are planned, approved, recorded, and managed—and that everyone who needs to follow those rules knows what to do. Without this, important changes may be made in different ways, old or incorrect settings may remain in place, and mistakes or unauthorised changes could go unnoticed. This increases the risk of service disruption, data loss, and failing to meet legal and regulatory obligations.

To meet this expectation, the organisation must create an organisation-wide configuration management policy and supporting procedures. These must explain why they exist, what they cover, who is responsible, how senior leadership is committed, how different parts of the organisation work together, and how compliance will be checked. The policy and procedures must also align with all relevant laws, directives, regulations, and internal standards.

The chief information security officer (CISO), or another formally delegated policy owner, must be responsible for producing, documenting, and distributing the policy to the CISO, the configuration management board, system owners, security and privacy programme leads, and IT operations leadership. The CISO (or delegated owner) must also distribute the procedures to the configuration management team, change managers, system administrators, operational technology (OT) engineering leads, and security and privacy programme leads.

The policy must be reviewed and updated at least once every year, and after any event that requires review. The procedures must be reviewed and updated at least once every quarter, and after audit findings, security incidents or breaches, major legal or regulatory changes, or material changes to the system architecture.

##### Organisation-level CM policy & procedures with CISO ownership

- Category: Manual
- Priority: Critical

###### WHAT must be done
Create and maintain an **organisation-level configuration management (CM) policy** and **supporting CM procedures** that explain how CM is carried out across the organisation (including IT and, where applicable, operational technology (OT)/industrial environments). Make sure the policy/procedures are **owned**, shared with the **defined roles**, and **kept up to date** through both **annual** reviews and **updates triggered by events**.

###### WHY (control requirement)
CM-1 requires a CM policy and procedures that cover the CM-related controls used in systems/organisations. The guidance says the policy/procedures must be based on the **risk management strategy**, support **security and privacy assurance**, and that the **security and privacy programmes work together** when developing them. It also states that simply repeating the controls is not enough; the procedures must explain **how** the policy/controls are put into practice.

###### HOW (specific steps/approach)
1. **Appoint an official policy owner**: appoint the **chief information security officer (CISO) (or delegated Configuration Management Policy Owner)** as responsible for the CM policy and procedures.
2. **Produce an organisation-level CM policy** (one main document, or a small set of coordinated policies) that includes: purpose/scope, risk-based rationale, roles/responsibilities, management commitment, coordination between security and privacy programmes, and compliance expectations.
3. **Create practical CM procedures** that explain the implementation steps (not just a repeat of the CM controls). Include procedures for: identifying and recording configurations, change request and approval workflow, collecting evidence for audits, handling exceptions, and how configuration baselines are created and maintained.
4. **Set up and use dissemination lists** and distribute:
   - Policy to: **CISO, Configuration Management Board, system owners, security and privacy programme leads, IT operations leadership**.
   - Procedures to: **configuration management team, change managers, system administrators, OT engineering leads, security and privacy programme leads**.
5. **Set review/update schedules and triggers**:
   - Policy: review/update **annually** and after any event that requires a policy review.
   - Procedures: review/update **quarterly** and after events such as audit findings, security incidents/breaches, major changes to laws/regulations (for example, ITAR, EAR, CMMC, NIS2, HIPAA, GDPR), and significant changes to the overall system design (architecture).
6. **Use a controlled document process**: version control, approval workflow, controlled access, and records that show who received and acknowledged the documents (for example, through the organisation document management system).

###### WHO (role responsible)
- **CISO / delegated Configuration Management Policy Owner**: responsible for the policy/procedure content, approvals, and controlled publication.
- **Configuration Management Board**: provides governance and coordination across IT/OT, security, privacy, and system owners.
- **Security and privacy programme leads**: jointly develop and confirm that security/privacy assurance requirements align.
- **Configuration management team / change managers / system administrators / OT engineering leads**: receive the procedures and provide practical implementation feedback.

###### Acceptance criteria
- An **organisation-level CM policy** exists, is approved by the designated official owner, and is shared with the defined policy recipient roles.
- CM procedures exist, are approved, and are shared with the defined procedure recipient roles.
- The policy is scheduled for **annual review/update** and includes defined triggers for earlier updates.
- The procedures are scheduled for **quarterly review/update** and include defined triggers (audit findings, incidents/breaches, major legal/regulatory changes, material architecture changes).
- There is evidence of controlled publication (for example, version history) and controlled sharing (for example, distribution logs and/or acknowledgements).
- The procedures include implementation guidance (step-by-step operational expectations), not only references to CM controls.

###### Actions Required for Compliance

- [ ] Appoint the Chief Information Security Officer (CISO), or the delegated Configuration Management Policy Owner, as the person accountable for configuration management (CM) policy and procedures.
- [ ] Draft and get approval for an organisation-wide configuration management (CM) policy that matches the organisation’s risk management strategy and its security and privacy assurance requirements.
- [ ] Create clear, practical configuration management (CM) procedures that explain how CM is carried out, including the change process (workflow) and how proof (evidence) is recorded.
- [ ] Share the corporate policy (CM) with the defined policy recipient roles and record who received it and confirmed acknowledgement.
- [ ] Share the change management (CM) procedures with the assigned procedure recipients and record that they were distributed and acknowledged.
- [ ] Set and publish review and update schedules: review the policy every year and after specified events; review the procedures every quarter and after specified events.
- [ ] Set up a controlled document process for policies and procedures, including version control, approval steps, and restricted access.

### CM-2 — Baseline Configuration (Control)

This requirement is about keeping an agreed, up-to-date “starting point” for how your system is set up. This includes how it connects, how it works, how its parts communicate, and the protections it uses to keep data safe. Without this, teams may unknowingly change settings or connections, which can create gaps in protection, cause outages, or make it difficult to explain what was in place after an incident.

To meet this expectation, the organisation must create and document the current baseline for the system, and keep it up to date using formal change control, so that updates are approved and recorded. The baseline must be reviewed and updated at least every quarter, and also whenever there are significant changes to the system’s design, its protection measures, its network connections, or its communication interfaces—after any major incident that affects the configuration, and when any system component is installed or upgraded.

##### Versioned baseline configuration with quarterly review and change triggers

- Category: Manual
- Priority: Critical

###### WHAT must be done
Create and keep up to date a formally reviewed, agreed **baseline configuration** for the application system and its configuration items. The baseline must cover **connectivity, day-to-day operations, and communications**, include the **security and privacy measures** that have been implemented, and document **system component details, network layout, and where each component sits within the organisation’s overall architecture**. The baseline must be kept current through **controlled updates**.

###### WHY (control requirement)
CM-2 requires baseline configurations for systems and components to be **documented, formally reviewed, and agreed**, and to include connectivity, operational and communications details, security and privacy measures, component details, network layout, and where each component sits within the enterprise architecture. It also requires keeping baselines current over time by creating a new baseline when systems change.

Organisation-defined parameters specify:
- **Review/update frequency:** quarterly (cm-02_odp.01)
- **Circumstances requiring review/update:** material changes to system architecture, security control implementations, network connectivity, or communications interfaces; and after any major incident that affects configuration (cm-02_odp.02)

###### HOW (specific steps/approach)
1. **Create a baseline template/schema** for this application that clearly includes: (a) connectivity and network layout (including entry/exit points, segmentation boundaries, routing/ports/protocols), (b) operational settings (service endpoints, how the system runs, and parameters that vary by environment), (c) communications interfaces (for example, application programming interface gateways, message brokers, protocols, and the security settings for encrypted traffic), (d) security and privacy measures (for example, encryption for data in transit and at rest, points where authentication and authorisation connect, logging and monitoring endpoints, and data handling controls), and (e) component inventory and logical placement (presentation/application/data/admin layers; mapping of production/test/disaster recovery environments).
2. **Create the initial baseline** by extracting information from authoritative sources: the configuration management database (CMDB) and asset inventory, enterprise network diagrams, identity and access management (IAM) integration documentation, security control standards, and the configuration currently deployed (for example, outputs from infrastructure as code, approved container or orchestration manifests, and exported gateway/firewall configurations).
3. **Store the baseline as a version-controlled configuration record** in a controlled repository (for example, Git-backed documentation plus configuration exports) with a unique baseline ID and an effective date. Link each section of the baseline to the authoritative source(s) and to the configuration items it governs.
4. **Use a controlled update process** linked to change management and incident management:
   - Any change request assessed as affecting **architecture, connectivity, security, privacy, or communications interfaces** must trigger a **baseline update proposal** and recorded approvals.
   - Any major incident with configuration impact must trigger a **baseline impact assessment**; if impacted, create a new baseline version.
5. **Carry out a formal quarterly review:** bring together the agreed reviewers (Application Owner, Architecture/Network, Security/Privacy, Operations) to confirm the baseline still matches the current enterprise architecture and security/privacy measures. Record the results and approvals.
6. **Attach audit-ready evidence** to each baseline version: (a) the review record (ticket/change advisory board minutes/approval log), (b) configuration evidence (exports/compliance reports) showing the deployed state matches the baseline, and (c) traceability from the baseline version to the change or incident that caused the update.

###### WHO (role responsible)
- **Application Owner / System Owner:** responsible for ensuring the baseline is complete and correct.
- **Enterprise Architecture / Network Architect:** responsible for the network layout, logical placement, and connectivity/communications sections.
- **Information Security (and Privacy lead where applicable):** responsible for the security and privacy measures sections and approvals.
- **Configuration Management / Change Manager:** responsible for enforcing configuration control, versioning, and linking to change/incident records.
- **Operations/Platform Engineering:** responsible for producing configuration exports/evidence and implementing baseline updates.

###### Acceptance criteria
- A baseline configuration record exists for the application system with a unique baseline ID, version history, and effective dates.
- The baseline clearly documents connectivity, operational settings, communications interfaces, security/privacy measures, component inventory, network layout, and logical placement.
- The baseline is under configuration control with recorded approvals and traceability to change/incident records.
- Evidence exists that the baseline has been reviewed quarterly (or, if newly created, the first review is scheduled and documented).
- For at least one recent material change or major configuration-impacting incident (or a simulated exercise if none occurred), the process produces a new baseline version with documented approvals and evidence.

###### Actions Required for Compliance

- [ ] Create a baseline configuration template that covers connectivity, day-to-day operating settings, communication interfaces, security and privacy settings, a list of components, the system layout, and how components are logically placed.
- [ ] Create the initial baseline version using authoritative sources, including the configuration management database (CMDB), network diagrams, identity and access management (IAM) and security standards, and exports of the currently deployed configuration.
- [ ] Publish the baseline in a version-controlled, access-restricted repository, using a unique baseline ID and an effective date.
- [ ] Set up a controlled process for managing configurations that automatically raises proposals to update the baseline whenever there are significant changes to the overall architecture, security, network, or communications, or when there are major incidents that could affect configuration settings.
- [ ] Hold and complete formal quarterly reviews, with recorded approval from the Application Owner, the Architecture/Network team, and the Security/Privacy team.
- [ ] For each baseline version, attach evidence that is ready for audit, including: the review record, configuration exports and compliance evidence, and proof that links it back to the change or incident that triggered it.

#### CM-2.2 — Automation Support for Accuracy and Currency (Enhancement)

This requirement is about keeping an accurate, up-to-date record of what your systems actually are, so the approved “starting point” remains correct over time. Without this, systems can gradually become out of date without anyone noticing—missing required software or updates, or running the wrong versions—which can cause service disruption and leave the organisation exposed to avoidable weaknesses.

To meet this expectation, the organisation must use automated tools to automatically record the operating system, installed applications, firmware (where relevant), and patch levels, and then automatically compare what is found against the approved baseline.

The organisation must also keep the baseline current, complete, accurate, and available. It must do this by running these automated checks on schedule, producing clear results that show whether each system matches the approved baseline, and investigating and fixing any mismatches or missing information so the system returns to a compliant state.

##### Automated baseline inventory and drift checks for system configs

- Category: Software
- Priority: Critical

###### WHAT
Set up automated processes that continuously keep the system’s **baseline configuration** **up to date, complete, correct, and accessible** by (1) automatically listing the system parts (operating system, installed applications, firmware where applicable, and patch/update levels) and (2) automatically checking that each system still meets the approved baseline.

###### WHY (control requirement)
CM-2(2) enhancement requires the organisation to **keep the baseline configuration up to date, complete, correct, and accessible** using the organisation-defined parameter **cm-02.02_odp (automated mechanisms)**. The guidance states that this can be met by implementing **CM-8(2)**, where the organisation combines **system component inventory** and **baseline configuration** activities.

###### HOW (specific steps/approach)
1. **Define the baseline as rules that machines can check**: describe the approved baseline configuration items (operating system build/version, required application names/versions, patch/update set/knowledge base (KB) levels, and firmware/BIOS targets where applicable) in a structured format supported by the configuration management tools (for example, policy-as-code or baseline templates).
2. **Turn on automated inventory collection** across all in-scope tiers/environments (production, test, development, disaster recovery) using the enterprise configuration management tools:
   - Operating system version/build
   - Installed applications and versions
   - Patch/update status (for example, KBs or package versions)
   - Firmware/BIOS versions where the tools/device interfaces support this
3. **Run automated baseline compliance checks on a timetable and when changes happen**:
   - Run checks regularly (for example, daily/weekly) and make sure checks complete **within an agreed time window after changes** (for example, after patch deployments or configuration releases).
   - Produce results that clearly show **compliant vs non-compliant** status and, where possible, **which specific items do not match** (missing patch, wrong application version, incorrect operating system build, firmware drift).
4. **Fix any drift to bring systems back to the baseline**:
   - For low-risk differences, use controlled automated fixes.
   - For higher-risk differences, start a change process (ticket/change request) to bring the system back to the baseline using approved change management.
5. **Keep baseline proof (evidence) and results available**:
   - Store compliance run outputs and inventory snapshots with timestamps.
   - Keep evidence according to the organisation’s retention requirements so auditors can retrieve it.
6. **Check that inventory information is accurate**:
   - Periodically compare a sample of inventory data against independent sources available in the environment (for example, operating system package manager views, application version endpoints, or vendor tools) to confirm the inventory feed is correct.

###### WHO
- **Configuration Management Lead / Platform Engineering**: responsible for defining the baseline, configuring the tools, setting schedules, and managing remediation workflows.
- **Security Engineering / Compliance**: checks that the baseline rules cover what is required (operating system/applications/firmware/patches), that evidence requirements are met, and that drift handling expectations are understood.
- **Change Manager / Service Owner**: approves higher-risk remediation through the change control process.

###### Acceptance criteria
- Automated inventory is collecting operating system version/build, installed application versions, patch/update status, and firmware/BIOS (where applicable) for all in-scope systems.
- Automated compliance checks run on a defined schedule and after relevant change events, producing results that can be audited.
- For each compliance run, systems are reported as compliant/non-compliant, with item-level mismatch details where supported.
- Non-compliant findings are either fixed within defined timeframes or sent into the approved change workflow.
- Compliance results and inventory evidence are kept and can be retrieved for audit purposes.

###### Actions Required for Compliance

- [ ] Define the approved baseline configuration items—such as the operating system build, required application versions, patch or knowledge base (KB) levels, and firmware targets where applicable—as rules that can be automatically checked by a system.
- [ ] Set up enterprise configuration management tools to automatically record, for every in-scope environment, the operating system, installed applications, firmware (where supported), and current patch levels.
- [ ] Set up automated checks that compare the current inventory against the approved baseline and report any differences for each item.
- [ ] Run schedule compliance checks and start extra checks after patch or configuration releases, within the agreed time window.
- [ ] Set up automated fix workflows for low-risk drift, and send higher-risk drift through the approved change control process.
- [ ] Store compliance run results and inventory snapshots with dates and evidence-retention rules, so they can be retrieved for audits.
- [ ] Carry out regular checks that inventory records are accurate by comparing a sample of items from the system against independent sources.

#### CM-2.3 — Retention of Previous Configurations (Enhancement)

This requirement is about keeping a safe, retrievable record of the last three approved “starting settings” for your systems. This lets you quickly put things back the way they were if a change causes problems. Without this, an error, a faulty update, or an unintended change could mean you cannot restore a previously approved configuration. That would increase downtime and disruption, and raise the risk of operating in an unsafe or non-compliant state.

To meet this expectation, the organisation must keep **exactly the last three approved baseline configuration versions**, including all the information needed to restore them. This includes: the hardware, software, and firmware information; the configuration files and records; and the supporting documentation.

The organisation must also have clear written rules, day-to-day procedures, and a plan that explain how these versions are created, approved, stored, and kept for rollback. Named people must be responsible for managing and overseeing this work, and there must be evidence that the stored versions can be retrieved and used during a controlled restore.

##### Retain last 3 approved baseline config versions for rollback

- Category: Manual
- Priority: Critical

###### WHAT
Keep **exactly the last 3 approved baseline configuration versions** of the system. This includes **all rollback information** (hardware, software, firmware, configuration files/records, and related documentation) so authorised staff can restore a previously approved baseline.

###### WHY (control requirement)
NIST SP 800-53 **CM-2(3)** requires: “Retain {{ param, cm-02.03_odp }} of previous versions of baseline configurations of the system to support rollback.” The organisation-defined parameter **cm-02.03_odp = 3** means you must keep **three** previous approved baseline versions (**no more and no fewer**) and make sure they can be retrieved and used for rollback.

###### HOW (specific approach)
Set up an **immutable, access-controlled Baseline Package** process. Each approved baseline is saved as a complete rollback package, with enforced retention of only the most recent three approved versions.

1. **Define a baseline package format** (template) that includes:
   - A **manifest** listing the exact hardware model/serial (where applicable), software versions, firmware versions, and configuration set identifiers.
   - The **configuration files/records** needed to restore the baseline (for example: application configuration, infrastructure configuration exports, network/service configuration records, and any relevant firmware/application version evidence).
   - A **rollback runbook** that points to the package identifier and the exact restore steps.
   - Links or embedded copies of **associated documentation** needed to understand and apply the baseline.
   - References to the **approval/change record** that authorised the baseline.
2. **Store baseline packages in a controlled repository** with:
   - Version control and **immutability** (or equivalent tamper-evident controls).
   - Role-based access control (RBAC) using enterprise identity, following least privilege.
   - Audit logging for package creation, approval/promotion, retrieval, and deletion/archiving.
3. **Enforce “last three approved” retention** using workflow automation:
   - Only packages that pass the authorised baseline approval workflow are eligible to be retained.
   - When a new baseline is approved, automatically label it **Baseline-Current** and move the previously retained packages to **Baseline-Prev-1/2/3**.
   - Automatically remove or lock packages older than the third previous version, so the repository retains **exactly three** previous approved versions (while keeping required audit evidence of the removal/archiving event).
4. **Make rollback clearly usable**:
   - Use a controlled rollback procedure that retrieves a specific retained package (by identifier) and applies it in a test/staging environment.
   - Keep evidence that retrieval and restore worked for at least the oldest retained package (for example, during scheduled configuration management validation).
5. **Assign responsibilities**:
   - The Configuration Manager (or equivalent) owns package creation and promotion.
   - The Change Manager (or IT service management (ITSM) process owner) ensures baseline approval gating.
   - The System Owner/Information Security approves the baseline content and confirms documentation is complete.

###### WHO
- **Configuration Manager / Configuration Management Lead**: defines the package template, ensures completeness, and manages promotion into the repository.
- **Change Manager (ITSM) / Release Manager**: ensures the baseline approval workflow gates promotion.
- **System Owner & Information Security**: approves the baseline content and associated documentation.
- **Privileged Operators / System Administrators**: carry out controlled rollback using the retained package and runbook.

###### Acceptance criteria
- The repository retains **exactly three** previous approved baseline versions at all times (per cm-02.03_odp = 3).
- Each retained baseline package includes complete rollback contents: hardware/software/firmware details, configuration files/records, and associated documentation.
- A documented rollback procedure can retrieve a specific retained package and restore it in a controlled way.
- Audit logs show who approved, who retrieved, and when packages were promoted or removed.
- Evidence exists that rollback retrieval and restore were successfully tested for the oldest retained package within the defined validation schedule.

###### Actions Required for Compliance

- [ ] Define and publish a standard “baseline” package template that includes hardware, software, and firmware details, the relevant configuration files and records, and the associated documentation.
- [ ] Set up an approval step in the change and configuration process so that only authorised baseline versions are eligible to be kept.
- [ ] Set up a secure, access-restricted repository to store unchangeable baseline packages, with audit records of all actions to create, promote, retrieve, or remove them.
- [ ] Set up retention enforcement so the system keeps exactly the last three approved baseline versions and removes or locks any older versions.
- [ ] Write down and practise a rollback process that restores a specific saved baseline package identifier and applies it in a controlled way.
- [ ] Run a scheduled rollback readiness test by finding the oldest saved package, restoring it, and recording proof that the test succeeded.

#### CM-2.6 — Development and Test Environments (Enhancement)

This requirement is about having a clear, controlled “starting point” for what your development and testing systems look like, and keeping them separate from the live, operational version. Without this separation, changes made while building or testing new features could accidentally carry over into the live environment, leading to unexpected disruption, errors, or security weaknesses that are difficult to trace and expensive to fix.

To meet this expectation, the organisation must keep documented baseline settings for both development and test environments. Each baseline must be kept distinct from the live baseline. The organisation must also describe in a configuration management plan how these baselines are created, updated, and controlled. The plan should include supporting design and architecture records, record the exact settings that make up each baseline, and ensure the right people oversee the process, including those responsible for configuration and information security.

Test setups should be made to match the live setup as far as practicable, so test results are realistic—even if the separation is achieved through management processes rather than separate physical machines.

##### Separate DEV/TEST baselines with controlled promotion to OP

- Category: Software
- Priority: Critical

###### WHAT
Maintain separate, managed “starting point” configurations for **development** and **test** environments that are **kept apart from** the **operational (production) starting point**, and make sure test configurations match operational settings **as far as practicable**.

###### WHY (control requirement)
CM-2(6) enhancement requires that development and test environments have a **starting point configuration** that is **managed separately** from the operational starting point. This helps prevent changes made during development or testing from accidentally affecting operational stability and security, while keeping test results relevant to proposed operational changes.

###### HOW (specific approach)
Set up a baseline “release track” model with automated, auditable configuration records:
1. **Create three configuration tracks**: DEV, TEST, and OP. Each track has its own baseline repository/record set and its own change process.
2. **Define what each baseline includes** for DEV, TEST, and OP (for example: application configuration, infrastructure-as-code modules, network and security settings, database structure and migration baseline, references for how secrets are handled, and logging/monitoring settings). Record the exact versions/identifiers that make up each baseline.
3. **Build TEST baselines from OP baselines** using controlled templates/substitutions (for example: non-production endpoints, test datasets, scaled capacity, test credentials), so TEST matches OP **as far as practicable**.
4. **Enforce separation using promotion controls**:
   - OP baseline records cannot be used in DEV/TEST unless there is an explicit, documented promotion process.
   - DEV/TEST changes cannot be used in OP unless there is a formal change/release approval step.
5. **Use deployments that are repeatable and driven by the pipeline**: DEV/TEST deployments must be created from the baseline records (not by manual changes that can drift over time). Each pipeline run produces an auditable record (inputs, record versions, differences, approvals, and the resulting configuration state).
6. **Continuously check for configuration drift** in DEV/TEST baselines using compliance checks based on the baseline definitions; require fixes through the baseline pipeline (not ad-hoc manual changes).
7. **Keep supporting documentation**: a configuration management plan explaining how DEV/TEST/OP baselines are created, updated, approved, and promoted; and system design/architecture/security documentation that reflects the separation between baselines.

###### WHO (roles responsible)
- **Configuration Manager / Release Manager**: owns the baseline track definitions, promotion processes, and evidence generation.
- **Application Owner / Engineering Lead**: ensures the application-level baseline contents are complete and correctly mapped across tracks.
- **Information Security / Security Architect**: checks that baseline definitions include required security settings and that separation/promotion rules meet security governance.
- **Infrastructure/Platform Team**: implements the infrastructure-as-code baseline records and the drift detection tooling.

###### Acceptance criteria (verifiable)
- There are **documented baseline definitions** for DEV and TEST that are **different** from OP (separate record sets and processes).
- The TEST baseline is created from the OP baseline using controlled substitutions, and the organisation can show which OP settings are mirrored and which are intentionally different.
- A promotion workflow exists and is enforced so that **DEV/TEST cannot directly update OP** without approval, and OP cannot be applied to DEV/TEST without explicit promotion.
- Deployment evidence shows DEV/TEST environments are deployed from baseline records, and each deployment includes an auditable record (record versions, differences, approvals).
- Drift detection results for DEV/TEST are produced and fixes are carried out through the baseline pipeline (not ad-hoc manual changes).

###### Actions Required for Compliance

- [ ] Set up separate configuration tracks for development (DEV), testing (TEST), and operations (OP), each with its own baseline repository and its own change process.
- [ ] Create clear baseline definitions for **development (DEV)** and **testing (TEST)** that list the exact configuration items and their versions included in each baseline.
- [ ] Create a TEST baseline by starting from the OP baseline, using controlled templates and substitutions to account for differences between non-production environments.
- [ ] Put promotion controls in place so that **development and testing (DEV/TEST) items cannot be moved into production (OP)** without formal approval, and **production (OP) items cannot be moved back into development and testing (DEV/TEST)** without explicit authorisation.
- [ ] Only deploy development and testing (DEV/TEST) environments using repeatable, automated releases from approved starting files, and create an auditable deployment record (manifest) for every deployment run.
- [ ] Set up configuration compliance and drift detection for the development and testing environments (DEV/TEST) against the agreed baseline definitions, and fix any drift by using the baseline pipeline.
- [ ] Create and keep up to date a configuration management plan that explains how baselines are created, updated, approved, and promoted, and make sure it matches the system design, architecture, and security documentation.

#### CM-2.7 — Configure Systems and Components for High-risk Areas (Enhancement)

This expectation is about making sure that laptops and mobile devices used for travel are prepared as safely as possible before someone goes to a higher-risk location, and then thoroughly checked and cleaned when they return. Without this, a device could be physically interfered with during travel, have harmful software added, or be changed in ways that allow sensitive information to leak or be accessed by the wrong people.

The organisation must first decide which travel destinations are considered significant risk. It must then allow only its managed travel laptops and mobile devices for staff travelling to those destinations. Before travel, the organisation must wipe the device storage clean, protect the device storage using encryption, install only a minimal set of approved applications, and set the device to the organisation’s more secure settings.

When staff return, the organisation must inspect the device for signs of tampering, run a malware (harmful software) check, wipe and reinstall a clean operating system, and then restore the device using approved, known-good system images before it is returned to normal use.

##### Travel device lifecycle: hardened pre-travel and trusted reset post-return

- Category: Software
- Priority: Critical

###### WHAT
Set up an end-to-end “travel device lifecycle” for **Department of Defense (DoD)-managed laptops and mobile devices** used in **high-risk travel areas**. Devices are prepared before departure in a **known-clean, sanitised, security-hardened configuration**. After return, they go through **physical tamper checks**, **malware scanning**, then a **full wipe and re-install**, and are **restored from trusted, version-controlled images** before being used again.

###### WHY (control requirement)
CM-2(7) enhancement expects organisations to **set up systems and components for high-risk areas outside the organisation**, including:
- identifying **locations of concern**,
- defining the **required settings** for issued components,
- making sure components are **set up correctly before travel**,
- applying controls **after travel is completed**, including:
  - **checking for physical tampering**, and
  - **wiping and reimaging disk drives** (then restoring using trusted sources).

###### HOW (specific steps/approach)
1. **Define and run “high-risk areas”** using a maintained list (for example: country/region/city, or itinerary tags). Start the travel process when travel is booked or approved for those locations.
2. **Pre-travel issuance gate (enforced by mobile device management (MDM))**: only allow devices from a **“Travel-Eligible”** pool to be assigned. Apply a dedicated **Travel Profile** and confirm it is correct before departure:
   - **sanitised storage** (a managed wipe/sanitise action is completed immediately before handover, or within a defined time window),
   - **full-device encryption** enabled for all device storage,
   - **only the minimum approved apps** (an approved list; remove anything not compliant),
   - a **hardened configuration baseline** (secure settings defined by the organisation).
3. **Collect proof before departure**: capture unchangeable evidence from the endpoint management system (device identifier, timestamps, encryption status, app inventory, configuration baseline, and wipe completion). Only mark the travel ticket/work order as **“Ready for Departure”** when all required checks show compliant results.
4. **Quarantine and physical tamper inspection after return**: when the device comes back, check it in and move it to a quarantine group. Carry out a **physical tamper inspection** using a standard checklist (for example: seals/ports/casing signs) and record the outcome. If tampering is suspected, keep the device quarantined and escalate under the incident/forensics process.
5. **Malware scan and trusted reset after return**: run an enterprise malware scan and record the results. Then perform **purge and reimage** (managed wipe/re-install) and **restore from trusted, version-controlled images** (known-good baselines) before the device can return to normal user groups.
6. **Release gate for re-use**: return the device to normal operation only after evidence confirms the required sequence and outcomes: **inspection → malware scan → purge/reimage → restore from trusted images**.

###### WHO (role responsible)
- **Endpoint / MDM Administrator**: sets up Travel Profile policies, performs wipe/sanitise, encryption, approved-app lists, hardened baselines, quarantine groups, and automates reimage/restore.
- **Security Operations / security operations centre (SOC) Analyst**: reviews tamper inspection results and malware scan results; manages escalation and quarantine decisions.
- **IT Service Desk / Travel Coordinator**: ensures devices are checked in using the travel process and that departure/return gates are followed.
- **System Owner / Configuration Manager**: maintains the trusted, version-controlled images and hardened baselines.

###### Acceptance criteria
- For every approved trip to a defined high-risk area, the device must have **pre-departure compliance evidence** showing:
  - sanitised storage,
  - full-device encryption enabled,
  - only the minimum approved apps,
  - the hardened baseline.
- After return, the device cannot be issued again until evidence confirms the required order:
  - **physical tamper inspection recorded**,
  - **malware scan completed**,
  - **purge/reimage completed**,
  - **restore from trusted, version-controlled images completed**.
- Devices with suspected physical tampering must remain in quarantine and must not be returned to normal use.
- Audit logs/evidence must be kept to show compliance for each device and trip (device identifiers and timestamps).

### CM-3 — Configuration Change Control (Control)

This requirement is about making sure any change to your systems is properly planned, checked, and formally agreed before it goes live. Without this, staff might make unplanned or risky updates that accidentally disrupt services, weaken protections, or expose personal information, including during urgent fixes.

The organisation must first set out which types of system changes are always treated as “controlled”. It must also keep clear written procedures for how changes are proposed, explained, tested, checked, approved or rejected, carried out, and then formally closed.

All controlled changes must be reviewed by the Change Advisory Board, which meets weekly. The board must explicitly consider both security and privacy impacts before making a decision. The organisation must record the board’s decision, implement only what was approved, and keep complete records for 365 days.

The organisation must also monitor and review what happened before and after each change, including emergency changes, changes that affect security or privacy, and changes made to fix known weaknesses.

##### CAB-gated configuration change workflow with 365-day evidence

- Category: Software
- Priority: Critical

###### WHAT
Set up an end-to-end process to control configuration changes so that they: (1) are categorised, (2) are sent to the Change Advisory Board (CAB) for a clear approval or rejection decision, (3) have security and privacy impact considered before the CAB decides, (4) can only be implemented if the CAB has approved them, and (5) include evidence of what was done before and after the change, kept for 365 days.

###### WHY (control requirement)
CM-3 requires a structured approach to proposing, justifying, implementing, testing, reviewing, and closing system changes. This includes changes to baselines and configuration items, changes to operational procedures and configuration settings, vulnerability fixes, and any emergency or unauthorised changes. It also requires a configuration control step (the CAB) to review and approve proposed changes, with evidence collected before and after changes. The organisation-defined requirements are: **keep records for 365 days**, **CAB is the configuration change control step**, **CAB meets weekly**, and CAB must consider emergency changes, changes that affect security or privacy controls, and changes needed for vulnerability remediation.

###### HOW (specific steps/approach)
1. **Define the types of configuration-controlled changes** in the change system (for example, IT service management): baseline changes, configuration item (CI) changes, operational procedure changes, configuration settings for system components, vulnerability remediation changes, and any emergency or unauthorised changes.
2. **Use workflow rules to ensure CAB involvement** (policy-based automation or an equivalent workflow):
   - Automatically flag any change that meets the organisation-defined conditions: **emergency**, **security or privacy control impact**, and **vulnerability remediation**.
   - For any emergency or unauthorised change, require it to be raised as a controlled change and sent to the CAB for a decision.
3. **Provide structured security and privacy information for CAB submissions**:
   - Security evidence (for example, why the vulnerability remediation is needed, which components are affected, the test and verification plan, and what security controls are expected to be affected).
   - Privacy evidence where relevant (for example, references to privacy impact assessment outputs, and any system of records notice references where the change affects privacy risk).
4. **Set CAB decision checkpoints**:
   - The CAB must meet **weekly** and record a clear outcome: **approved** or **disapproved**, including the reasons based on the security and privacy evidence.
   - Stop the workflow moving to implementation unless CAB approval evidence is recorded (CAB meeting date/decision ID and the recorded decision).
5. **Collect evidence before and after the change**:
   - Before: capture a baseline/configuration snapshot, confirm rollback or backup readiness, and document the test and verification plan.
   - After: record test execution evidence, confirm that security and privacy-relevant controls work as expected, and check logs and monitoring.
   - Ensure the change record includes both the before and after evidence.
6. **Keep records for 365 days** for all configuration-controlled change records, including CAB decision records and the before/after evidence.
7. **Run emergency handling in a controlled way**:
   - For emergency changes, allow faster submission, but still require a CAB decision (either at the next weekly CAB meeting or through an agreed emergency CAB process that matches the weekly meeting requirement), and make sure all evidence is completed before the change is closed.

###### WHO (role responsible)
- **Change Advisory Board (CAB)**: approves or rejects changes and records the decisions.
- **Change Manager / IT service management (ITSM) Process Owner**: maintains the workflow, change categories, and decision checkpoints.
- **Information Security Officer / Security Engineering**: provides security evidence and the criteria for verification.
- **Privacy Officer (or delegated privacy lead)**: provides privacy impact assessment evidence where required.
- **System/Application Owners**: provide the justification, affected components, and test and rollback plans.

###### Acceptance criteria
- Every configuration-controlled change that matches the defined conditions (emergency, security or privacy control impact, vulnerability remediation, and emergency or unauthorised changes) is sent to the CAB and receives a recorded **approved/disapproved** decision in the change system.
- No change can move into implementation without recorded CAB approval evidence.
- CAB decisions reference the required security and privacy evidence where applicable.
- Each change record includes documented evidence of what was done before and after the change, including test and verification outcomes.
- All configuration-controlled change records (including CAB decisions and evidence) are kept for **365 days** and can be retrieved for audit.

###### Actions Required for Compliance

- [ ] Create a controlled system for classifying changes, then link it to your IT service management (ITSM) change categories and configuration items (configuration items are the parts of your IT environment that are tracked and managed).
- [ ] Set up workflow rules to automatically label changes that require a Change Advisory Board (CAB) for: emergency changes, changes with security or privacy impact, vulnerability fixes, and changes that are not scheduled or are not authorised.
- [ ] Define the required structured information fields and supporting documents to include with a change advisory board (CAB) submission (including evidence of security impact and references to privacy impact assessments where applicable).
- [ ] Set up change advisory board (CAB) approval and rejection decision points so implementation cannot go ahead unless there is recorded evidence that the CAB approved the change.
- [ ] Add mandatory evidence collection before and after each change, including a baseline snapshot, confirmation that rollback is ready, test and verification results, and checks of monitoring and logs.
- [ ] Set the retention period for change records to 365 days for all configuration-controlled change documents, including Change Advisory Board (CAB) decisions.

#### CM-3.2 — Testing, Validation, and Documentation of Changes (Enhancement)

This expectation is about making sure every change to your systems is tested, checked, and properly recorded before it is allowed to go live. Without this, even a small update to hardware, software, firmware, or settings could cause services to fail, disrupt day-to-day operations, or create problems that are only found after customers or staff are affected.  

To meet this, the organisation must plan and carry out testing for each change, then confirm that the results match what was expected, and keep clear evidence that this was done before final approval. The change must also be fully documented, including what was changed, what it affects, and where the supporting records are stored.  

Testing must be arranged so it does not interfere with business needs. If the system must be taken offline, this should be done during planned outages. If testing on the live system is not possible, suitable extra safeguards must be used and documented.

- Category: Software
- Priority: Critical

###### WHAT

- **completed test evidence**
- a **documented validation plan**
- **completed validation evidence**
- **complete change documentation**

###### WHY (control requirement)
CM-3(2) requires the organisation to **test, validate, and document changes before finalising implementation**. Further guidance also requires that testing **does not disrupt mission or business operations** (for example, by using planned downtime or replication where possible). If operational testing is not possible, the organisation must use **alternative (compensating) controls**, and these must be documented.

###### 1) Require the right documents for every change
- Update the organisation’s change record (for example, an IT service management (ITSM) change ticket) to include mandatory fields and attachments:
  - **Test plan** (linked to the change, covering scope, environments, test cases, expected results, and pass/fail criteria)
  - **Test evidence** (for example, continuous integration/continuous delivery (CI/CD) test reports, JUnit/pytest outputs, synthetic monitoring results, configuration validation outputs)
  - **Validation plan** (what will be checked beyond test execution, for example business acceptance checks, security regression checks, data integrity checks, performance/service level agreement (SLA) checks)

  - the reason why
  - the **compensating controls** (for example enhanced monitoring/alerting during rollout, stricter rollback criteria, additional security regression checks, limited-scope canary testing with automated rollback triggers)
  - evidence showing that the compensating controls were applied

- Ensure the change record includes pointers/IDs to where the evidence is stored.

  2) test evidence is attached and can be traced back to the change
  3) a validation plan is present and validation evidence is attached
  4) change documentation includes affected components and references to design/configuration records
  5) the change record explains how testing avoids operational disruption (planned outage window or replication), **or** explains the compensating controls used when operational testing is not feasible
- Evidence locations must be traceable from the change record and retained for audit.

#### CM-3.3 — Automated Change Implementation (Enhancement)

This requirement is about ensuring that only approved system updates are applied in a controlled, repeatable way, and then automatically rolled out to all the systems you operate. - Category: Software
- Priority: Critical

###### WHAT

After deployment, the process must check compliance and fix any differences (drift).

###### WHY (control requirement)
NIST SP 800-53 **CM-3(3) Automated Change Implementation** requires: “Implement changes to the current system baseline and deploy the updated baseline across the installed base using {{ insert: param, cm-03.03.03_odp }}.” The organisation-defined parameter **cm-03.03.03_odp** specifies “**continuous integration and continuous delivery (CI/CD) pipelines with infrastructure-as-code, automated configuration management, and signed baseline deployment**.”

6. **Check compliance after deployment and fix drift**: Run automated configuration compliance checks after deployment. If drift is found, automatically correct it back to the signed baseline (within approved safety limits) and re-check compliance.
7. **Create audit-ready evidence**: Ensure the pipeline keeps records that can be traced to the approved change, including commit/pull request, change ID, approval evidence, pipeline run ID, signed baseline file ID/digest, and results for each target.

- **Security/Compliance Analyst**: Defines compliance checks, validates evidence, and reviews pipeline outputs.

- Post-deployment automated configuration checks confirm targets match the signed baseline; drift is either corrected automatically or escalated according to approved safety rules.

#### CM-3.5 — Automated Security Response (Enhancement)

This expectation is about stopping harm quickly if someone changes a system’s agreed settings without permission. ##### Automated baseline-change kill-switch with SOC and owner alerts

###### WHAT
Set up an automated security response that triggers when an approved security configuration baseline is changed in an unauthorised way. The response must (1) stop the affected system functions/processing and (2) immediately send alerts/notifications to the right people in the organisation (at minimum the security operations centre (SOC) and the system owner).

###### WHY (control requirement)
NIST Special Publication (SP) 800-53 Configuration Management (CM)-3(5) enhancement requires: “Implement the following security responses automatically if baseline configurations are changed in an unauthorized manner: {{ insert: param, cm-03.05_odp }}.” The guidance says automated security responses must include “halting selected system functions, halting system processing, and issuing alerts or notifications to organisational personnel” when an unauthorised configuration item change is detected.

###### HOW (specific steps/approach)
###### 1) Define machine-checkable baselines and unauthorised-change criteria
- Record the application’s approved configuration baseline as machine-checkable rules (for example, “policy-as-code” for configuration values, feature flags, security headers, transport layer security (TLS) settings, identity and access management (IAM)/authorisation settings, and critical runtime settings).
- Define what counts as an “unauthorised manner” using an organisation-approved authorisation signal that automation can verify (for example, a signed change request ID, a maintenance-window token, or a change-control approval marker).

###### 2) Detect baseline deviations and create a linked event
- Continuously, or on a defined schedule, compare the live configuration against the approved baseline (using agent-based checks where allowed, or agentless checks for managed services).
- If a difference is found, create a “baseline-change event” that includes: the baseline item identifier, the before/after values (or hashes), the time the change was detected, the host/service identifiers, and whether the change authorisation was valid.

###### 3) Automatically run the agreed response workflow
- If the change is unauthorised, automatically run a containment workflow that:
  - **Stops selected system functions/processing** in a safe, limited way that fits the application design. Examples for enterprise applications: pause/disable the specific workflow(s) or scheduled job(s) that depend on the changed configuration; revoke/disable affected application programming interface (API) routes or feature flags; isolate the affected service instance(s) from incoming traffic; or block the specific processing step while keeping unrelated services running.
  - **Sends alerts/notifications immediately** to both:
    - the SOC (via security information and event management (SIEM) / security orchestration, automation and response (SOAR) ticketing or the alert queue), and
    - the system owner (via the information technology service management (ITSM) / notification channel).
- Add a correlation identifier (event ID / workflow run ID) and attach supporting evidence (the baseline item, before/after, and what action was taken).

###### 4) Ensure auditability and traceability
- Keep records for: the detection result, the decision that the change was unauthorised, the exact automated actions taken, and whether the halt/containment and alerting succeeded or failed.
- Keep logs for as long as required by the organisation’s audit retention rules, and make them searchable using the correlation ID.

###### 5) Test using controlled scenarios
- Run tests where a baseline item is changed without the authorisation signal, and confirm the workflow stops the affected processing and sends alerts to both the SOC and the system owner.
- Run tests where a baseline item is changed during an authorised window, and confirm the automated response does not trigger.

###### WHO
- **Application Security Engineer / Platform Engineer**: implement the baseline rules, detection, and the automated response workflow.
- **SOC Lead / Security Operations (SecOps) Engineer**: set up alert routing, ticket creation, and triage procedures.
- **System Owner (Application Owner)**: define which safe halt/containment actions are acceptable for the affected functions and confirm the alert content.
- **Change Management / Configuration Management Lead**: define and maintain the authorisation signal used to tell authorised from unauthorised changes.

###### Acceptance criteria
1. For an unauthorised baseline configuration change, the system automatically stops the affected functions/processing within the organisation’s agreed response time objective (for example, minutes), without needing human approval.
2. For the same event, two alerts are automatically sent: one to the SOC and one to the system owner. Both must include the correlation ID and evidence of the baseline deviation.
3. For an authorised change (authorisation signal present), the automated halt/alert workflow does not trigger.
4. Audit evidence exists for each triggered event showing: the baseline item, before/after (or hashes), the unauthorised decision, the automated actions executed, and the result of alert delivery.
5. The halt/containment action is limited to the impacted processing path(s) and does not unnecessarily disrupt unrelated application functions.

###### Actions Required for Compliance

- [ ] Convert the application’s approved configuration baseline into machine-checkable policy rules, using item identifiers.
- [ ] Define and put in place an authorisation signal (for example, a signed change request ID or a maintenance-window token) that automation can check to confirm it is allowed to proceed.
- [ ] Set up continuous or scheduled checks to detect when a baseline changes, and record a linked alert that includes evidence of what was before and what changed to after.
- [ ] Set up an automated response process that, if an unauthorised deviation is detected, stops the affected processing route(s) safely (for example, pause the job, disable the feature flag, or quarantine the instance).
- [ ] Set up the workflow so that it works with the security operations centre (SOC) alerts and the information technology service management (ITSM) and notifications sent to the system owner, including a correlation identifier and supporting evidence attachments.
- [ ] Record and keep audit logs to support detection, investigate unauthorised access decisions, confirm the results of any automated stop actions, and verify whether alerts were successfully delivered.
- [ ] Run tests for both unauthorised and authorised changes to confirm that the “halt” and “dual alerts” are triggered only when changes are unauthorised.

#### CM-3.6 — Cryptography Management (Enhancement)

This requirement is about making sure the organisation’s “scrambling and proving” tools—used to protect information and verify identities—are managed like important company assets, not handled informally. In particular, it covers the encryption used for data stored and sent, digital signatures, and the certificates used for secure connections, code approval, and authentication.

If these protections are not tightly managed, they could be changed by mistake or by the wrong people, expire without anyone noticing, or be replaced in a way that breaks trust—leaving data exposed or allowing impersonation.

To meet this expectation, the organisation must keep a formal, documented record of all these cryptographic items, including what they are, where they are used, and who maintains them. It must also control any changes through an approved change process. The organisation must maintain clear plans, documented system designs and settings, procedures for keeping certificates up to date, and traceable records showing what was changed, when it was changed, and that the correct approvals were obtained.

##### Manage cryptography mechanisms as configuration items with traceable change

- Category: Manual
- Priority: Critical

###### WHAT
Set up a capability to manage cryptography settings through configuration management. This means every cryptographic mechanism used for **encryption**, **digital signatures**, and **certificate-based authentication and signing** (for example: **Transport Layer Security (TLS) certificates**, **code signing certificates**, **authentication certificates**, and the encryption/signing settings that use them) must be treated as configuration items (CIs). They must be **defined, recorded (inventoried), and managed** under configuration management.

###### WHY (control requirement)
The National Institute of Standards and Technology (NIST) Special Publication (SP) 800-53 **Configuration Management (CM)-3(6)** enhancement requires that the cryptographic mechanisms used to meet the referenced controls are under configuration management. The guidance also clarifies that, regardless of which cryptographic mechanisms are used, the organisation must manage them through defined processes and procedures (for example, handling certificate expiry).

###### HOW (specific approach)
1. **Define what “cryptographic mechanisms” includes** in the organisation’s configuration management policy and procedures. Explicitly include: encryption for data **stored** and **sent over networks**, digital signatures, **TLS certificates**, **code signing certificates**, and **authentication certificates** (aligned to the organisation-defined parameter `cm-03.06_odp`).
2. **Create and maintain a cryptography inventory/register**. Represent each cryptographic mechanism as a configuration item (CI), at minimum including: mechanism type (encryption/signature/certificate-based authentication), certificate identifier (serial number/thumbprint), purpose (server authentication/client authentication/signing), owning team, and the configuration artefact(s) it is linked to (for example: load balancer TLS policy, application keystore/truststore settings, service mesh mutual Transport Layer Security (mTLS) policy, and CI/continuous integration and continuous delivery (CI/CD) signing configuration).
3. **Link cryptography CI updates to the formal change process**. Any change to a cryptography CI (renewal, replacement, revocation/retirement, change of algorithm/profile, keystore/truststore change, signing policy change) must be raised as a configuration change request, approved by the appropriate change authority (including security input where required by your governance), implemented through the approved release/change workflow, and linked to verification evidence.
4. **Version and store evidence together**. Keep versioned configuration artefacts and the cryptography CI record together (for example: version-controlled TLS configuration templates, keystore configuration definitions, and certificate deployment instructions). This must support audit traceability from the CI record → change record → the configuration that was deployed.
5. **Manage certificate lifecycle through configuration management**. Ensure the inventory CI includes validity periods, and that renewal/replacement/retirement actions are planned, carried out, and recorded using the same configuration change workflow. This must include explicit handling for expiry and revocation/retirement.

###### WHO (roles responsible)
- **Configuration Manager / Configuration Management (CM) Lead**: owns the cryptography CI model, the inventory/register structure, and the links to configuration management processes.
- **Change Manager / Change Control Board (or equivalent)**: ensures cryptography CI changes follow approved change procedures.
- **Information Security / Security Engineering**: confirms the scope of cryptography, approves risk decisions for cryptography-related changes, and checks lifecycle/expiry handling.
- **Application/Infrastructure Owners**: provide accurate mapping of cryptographic mechanisms to systems and implement the approved changes.

###### Acceptance criteria
- A cryptography inventory exists and is complete for the application’s production (and other in-scope) environments, covering encryption in transit/at rest, digital signatures, and certificate-based authentication/signing mechanisms defined in `cm-03.06_odp`.
- Every cryptographic mechanism in use is represented as a CI and is linked to the exact configuration artefact(s) and system/service endpoints where it is deployed.
- For the most recent cryptography CI change (for example, certificate renewal/replacement), there is a complete end-to-end audit trail: CI record updated → change request/approval recorded → deployment/release completed → verification evidence captured.
- Certificate lifecycle events (renewal/replacement/retirement/revocation) are carried out through the configuration change workflow and recorded with dates/validity and outcomes.

###### Actions Required for Compliance

- [ ] Update the configuration management policy or procedure to explicitly include the cryptographic mechanisms listed in **cm-03.06_odp** (encryption, digital signatures, and Transport Layer Security (TLS) / code signing / authentication certificates).
- [ ] Create a register of all cryptography used and set the minimum required information for each item: mechanism type, certificate identifier, purpose, owner, validity dates, and the configuration items it is linked to.
- [ ] Treat each cryptographic mechanism used in production as a “configuration item” (CI) in the organisation’s configuration register (for example, the configuration management database (CMDB) or governance, risk and compliance (GRC) system), and link it to the relevant system endpoints and configuration records.
- [ ] Update the change management process so that any changes to cryptography continuous integration (CI) require explicit approval, and each change must be linked to the proof of verification and the services that will be affected.
- [ ] Set up certificate lifecycle procedures for renewal, replacement, retirement, and revocation. Each change must trigger a configuration change request, and the configuration item record must be updated with the certificate’s validity period and the outcome.
- [ ] Store cryptography configuration files using version control, and make sure every continuous integration (CI) update can be traced back to the matching change or release record.

#### CM-3.7 — Review System Changes (Enhancement)

This requirement is about making sure any changes to your systems are checked, so that unauthorised interference is spotted quickly. Without regular checks, someone could quietly change software or settings, or changes could happen by mistake. The business might not notice until harm is done, such as disrupted services, altered records, or sensitive information being exposed.

The organisation is expected to review system changes every day, and also immediately after any configuration change. It should also review whenever automated checks report something unexpected or when an integrity check fails (meaning the system may not match what it should be).

These reviews must be carried out using the organisation’s documented change procedures and plans. They must be based on the evidence available, such as change records, event logs, and system documentation, and involve the right people, including those responsible for change control and security. The organisation must keep dated records showing the review was completed and the result, including whether any unauthorised changes were found.

##### Evidence-based review workflow for daily and event-triggered changes

- Category: Software
- Priority: Critical

###### WHAT
Set up an automated, evidence-based process to ensure system changes are reviewed **every day**, **after any configuration change**, and **when continuous monitoring finds unexpected changes or failed integrity checks**. Each review must be carried out using the documented change and configuration procedures, and must produce a dated, audit-ready record of the result (including whether any unauthorised changes were found).

###### WHY (control requirement)
National Institute of Standards and Technology (NIST) Special Publication (SP) 800-53 **CM-3(7)** requires the organisation to **review system changes** at the defined **frequency** and under defined **circumstances** to determine whether **unauthorised changes** have occurred. The guidance also expects reviews to be driven by signals from the **configuration change process** and **continuous monitoring**.

###### HOW (specific steps/approach)
1. **Create a “review pack” template** that is automatically filled with: the trigger type (daily / configuration change / unexpected change / failed integrity check), affected components or scope, linked change record(s), linked monitoring and integrity evidence, and references to the relevant configuration documentation.
2. **Create review tasks** on a schedule for all in-scope systems and components (daily), and **immediately** when either:
   - a configuration change is recorded in the change management system, or
   - continuous monitoring raises an unexpected-change alert or an integrity-check failure.
3. **Ensure evidence is complete** (using policy checks within the workflow) so a reviewer cannot close the task without attaching or confirming the evidence used (for example: change ticket reference, extracts from audit logs, integrity-check results, and the expected configuration references).
4. **Send tasks to authorised reviewers** (configuration management and change control, and security roles) using the organisation’s identity and access controls. Require named reviewers to record the outcome (authorised/expected versus unauthorised/unexpected) and any remediation actions.
5. **Record and keep dated results** in an unchangeable audit record, linking each review result back to the trigger (the change or alert identifier) for traceability.

###### WHO
- **Configuration Management Lead / Change Control Manager**: owns the review pack template, scope rules, and reviewer assignment.
- **Security Monitoring and Detection Engineer**: ensures continuous monitoring alerts and integrity-check evidence are correctly mapped into review packs.
- **Authorised Reviewers (Change Control and Security)**: carry out the review and record the outcomes.
- **Platform / Operational Technology (OT) / Information Technology (IT) Operations Lead** (where applicable): ensures the system and component inventory mapping is accurate for scoping.

###### Acceptance criteria
- Daily reviews are created and completed for all in-scope systems and components at the configured frequency (**daily**).
- Event-triggered reviews happen **after any configuration change** and **when continuous monitoring detects unexpected changes or failed integrity checks**, with no missed triggers for tested scenarios.
- Every completed review includes: (a) a dated record, (b) evidence links (change record and monitoring/integrity evidence), (c) references to configuration documentation and expected state, and (d) a clear statement of whether unauthorised changes were found.
- Audit evidence demonstrates traceability from each review outcome to the triggering change, alert, or integrity-check event.

###### Actions Required for Compliance

- [ ] Define the scope rules for the in-scope system or component, and make sure they match the organisation’s official inventory.
- [ ] Set up a review process (“review packet”) that links each change record to the relevant continuous monitoring alerts and integrity-check results.
- [ ] Set up automated creation of tasks for daily reviews, and create tasks immediately when configuration changes occur or when monitoring triggers an alert.
- [ ] Require workflow checks before a review can be closed, including attaching supporting evidence and referencing the relevant configuration documentation.
- [ ] Assign and enforce named authorised reviewer roles for each review task using enterprise identity controls.
- [ ] Set up tamper-proof audit logs to record review completion details (who completed it, when, the outcome, and links to supporting evidence) and keep these records for audit purposes.

#### CM-3.8 — Prevent or Restrict Configuration Changes (Enhancement)

This requirement is about preventing important system settings from being changed at the wrong time or without the right permission. If configuration changes happen while security fixes are being applied, or if changes are made to systems that handle highly sensitive information, or if the organisation’s approved “secure standard” settings are changed without authorisation, attackers or mistakes could take advantage of the gap. This could weaken privacy and safety protections.

To meet this expectation, the organisation must clearly define when changes are restricted, including:
- during vulnerability remediation windows (the period when security weaknesses are being fixed)
- for systems that handle Secret data
- for any changes to security-relevant baseline settings, unless there is an approved change ticket

The organisation must also enforce these restrictions in practice. It should use documented procedures and a plan, keep records showing what was allowed or blocked and why, and maintain enough documentation and system inventories to identify what can be changed and which systems are involved.

##### Automated change gate for restricted config change circumstances

- Category: Software
- Priority: Critical

###### WHAT
Put in place an automated “change gate” that **stops or limits configuration changes** whenever any of the organisation’s defined restricted situations apply: **during vulnerability remediation windows**, for **systems that handle Secret data**, and for **changes to security-relevant configuration baselines without an approved change ticket**.

###### WHY (control requirement)
NIST SP 800-53 **CM-3(8)** requires the organisation to **prevent or limit changes** to system configuration **in the defined situations**. The guidance explains that configuration changes can harm security and privacy functions, and that these restrictions can be enforced using **automated controls**.

###### HOW (specific steps/approach)
1. **Turn the restricted situations into policy rules**
   - Create machine-readable rules for the three situations from the organisation parameter **cm-03.08_odp (circumstances)**:
     - “during vulnerability remediation windows”
     - “for systems handling Secret data”
     - “for changes to security-relevant configuration baselines without approved change tickets”
2. **Label and link configuration items to the rules**
   - Keep an up-to-date, authoritative mapping between:
     - systems/services and whether they handle **Secret data** (from the data classification register)
     - configuration items/baselines and whether they are **security-relevant configuration baselines** (from the secure configuration baseline catalogue)
3. **Add the gate to the configuration change process**
   - For infrastructure-as-code/config-as-code: enforce at **plan time** (stop or delay) and again at **apply time** (final check).
   - For manual/admin console changes: require a **ticket number** when the request is made, and check it using the IT service management (ITSM)/change system application programming interface (API) before allowing the change.
4. **Automatically check vulnerability windows and ticket approval**
   - Get remediation/change-freeze windows from the vulnerability management/patch programme schedule.
   - For baseline changes: confirm the linked ticket is **approved** and that it covers the specific baseline/configuration items being changed.
5. **Apply the decision and keep proof for audit**
   - When a restricted situation applies, the gate must **block or limit** the change (for example, delay it or route it to an emergency exception process) based on the organisation’s defined exception rules.
   - Record audit proof for every attempt: system/configuration item, which rule triggered, whether it is within a remediation window, whether the Secret-data label applies, the ticket reference, the identity of the approver, the decision (allowed/blocked/delayed/exception), and the time(s).
6. **Detect and alert on attempts to bypass or on unauthorised changes**
   - Add monitoring to detect configuration changes that were not made through the gated process (for example, drift detection/configuration monitoring) and alert security and compliance teams for investigation.

###### WHO (role responsible)
- **Platform/DevOps Engineering Lead**: implement the change gate in continuous integration/continuous delivery (CI/CD) and admin tools.
- **Security Governance/Compliance Lead**: approve the policy rules and the mapping for “Secret data” and “security-relevant baselines”.
- **ITSM/Change Management Owner**: ensure change tickets provide an API for approval status and scope.
- **Vulnerability Management Lead**: provide remediation window schedules in a format that can be read automatically.

###### Acceptance criteria
- Configuration changes are **blocked or limited** when any of the three cm-03.08_odp situations apply.
- For security-relevant baseline changes, the gate **only allows** the change when there is an **approved** change ticket that is scoped to the specific baseline/configuration items.
- For systems handling Secret data, the gate applies the restriction consistently across all environments.
- For vulnerability remediation windows, the gate prevents changes according to the defined window schedule.
- Every allowed/blocked/delayed/exception decision produces **audit proof** that includes the required decision details (rule triggered, system/configuration item, ticket reference/approval, time(s), approver identity).
- Alerts are raised for configuration changes detected outside the gated execution process.

###### Actions Required for Compliance

- [ ] Define policy-as-code rules for the three restricted circumstances listed in **cm-03.08_odp**
- [ ] Create and keep up to date official mapping records for systems that handle secret data and for security-critical configuration baselines.
- [ ] Add the change gate to the continuous integration and continuous delivery (CI/CD) plan and apply steps, and also to the admin change request workflow.
- [ ] Set up automated checks to confirm that vulnerability fixes are completed within the agreed remediation timeframes, and that information technology service management (ITSM) change requests have been approved.
- [ ] Apply allow, block, delay, or exception outcomes for every decision, and create an audit record for each one.
- [ ] Set up drift and bypass detection to alert you when configuration changes happen outside the approved (gated) process.

### CM-4 — Impact Analyses (Control)

This expectation is about making sure every planned change to the organisation’s systems is checked in advance for possible harm before anything is switched on or released. Without this, a routine update could accidentally weaken protections, expose information, or create new privacy risks for individuals, including through knock-on effects on existing safeguards or on partners who help deliver services.

To meet this expectation, the organisation must carry out a security and privacy impact analysis for each system change before it is implemented. This must be done by people with clear responsibility for security and privacy and the right skills to judge the effects. The analysts must follow documented procedures, review the relevant security and privacy plans and rules, examine how the change will be built and operated, consider impacts on supply chain partners, and carry out risk assessments in plain language to decide whether extra safeguards are needed.

The results must be recorded as part of the formal change process, with evidence that the people who approve changes considered them before giving approval.

##### Security & privacy impact analysis gates in IT change workflow

- Category: Software
- Priority: Critical

###### WHAT

###### WHY (control requirement)
CM-4 requires the organisation to **review proposed system changes to identify possible security and privacy impacts before the change is implemented**. Guidance also requires that these impact analyses are carried out by people with security and privacy responsibilities and the right skills. They must follow documented procedures, review relevant security and privacy plans and system design/operational documentation, consider impacts involving supply chain partners with input from stakeholders, and include risk assessments to decide whether extra controls are needed.

###### HOW (specific steps/approach)
1. **Write down the procedures and required proof** for CM-4 impact analyses (security and privacy), including the minimum required sections (for example: affected components, affected controls, reviewed design/operational documentation, risk assessment result, and whether additional controls are required).
2. **Update the ITSM change workflow** to add mandatory stages: *Impact analysis required → Security review → Privacy review → Change Advisory Board (CAB) approval → Implement*. Prevent scheduling and implementation until both analyses are present and marked complete.
3. **Apply automated checks (“policy-as-code” or equivalent workflow validation)** to confirm that the change record includes:
   - a security impact analysis document with a completion date/time **before** approval/implementation,
   - a privacy impact analysis document with a completion date/time **before** approval/implementation,
   - a clear decision on whether additional controls are needed (including the reason when the decision is “no additional controls”),

When triggered, require stakeholder sign-off (or a documented record of stakeholder consultation outcome).
5. **Capture evidence** automatically so the change record links to the impact analysis documents and risk assessment outputs, so it is ready for audit.
6. **Limit who can complete and approve** the change so only the named security/privacy roles (or their formally delegated equivalents) can mark the analyses as complete and approve the change.

- The impact analysis documents include evidence that relevant security/privacy plans, policies, and procedures, and relevant system design/operational documentation, were reviewed.
- Each change record includes a documented risk assessment result and an explicit decision on whether additional controls are required.
- For changes affecting supply chain/partner components, the workflow records stakeholder consultation/sign-off or a documented rationale.
- Audit checks confirm that change records can be traced to the impact analysis documents, and that the recorded timestamps show completion before implementation.

###### Actions Required for Compliance

- [ ] Define and publish documented change-management (CM-4) impact analysis procedures for both security and privacy. Include the required evidence information fields and the minimum content that must be provided.
- [ ] Update the IT service management (ITSM) change process to include mandatory CM-4 steps—impact analysis required, security review, privacy review, change advisory board (CAB) approval, then implementation—with hard stops that prevent progress if any step is not completed.
- [ ] Set up “policy-as-code” and workflow checks to confirm that security and privacy impact assessment documents are present and have been approved, including the approval date and time.
- [ ] Set up evidence capture so that each change record automatically links to the impact analysis documents, the reviewed design and operational records, and the relevant outputs from the tools used.

#### CM-4.1 — Separate Test Environments (Enhancement)

This expectation is about making sure every change to your systems is tested in a separate test area first, before it affects live services or real customer information. Without this, a faulty update could damage something important, expose sensitive information, or be used on purpose in a harmful way—leading to service disruption, financial loss, regulatory issues, and harm to people’s privacy.

To meet this requirement, the organisation must keep the test environment clearly separated from the live environment, so testing cannot affect live operations or accidentally share live information.

Before any change is released, it must be assessed in the test setting for security and privacy risks caused by weaknesses, flaws, incompatibilities, or deliberate harmful behaviour. The results must be recorded and linked to the specific change.

The organisation must also keep clear documentation showing how the test and live areas are separated, who is responsible for these checks, and evidence that testing and assessments were completed before deployment.

##### Isolated test deployment with security/privacy gates before production

- Category: Software
- Priority: Critical

###### WHAT
Set up a delivery process where every proposed change is deployed into a **separately isolated test environment** and is checked for **security and privacy impacts** before it can be moved into the operational (production) environment.

###### WHY (control requirement)
CM-4(1) enhancement requires that changes are **checked in a separate test environment** before being used in the operational environment. This is specifically to identify **security and privacy impacts** caused by **flaws, weaknesses, incompatibilities, or deliberate wrongdoing**. The guidance also requires that the test environment is **physically or logically separate**, so test work cannot affect operational work, and so **operational information is not accidentally shared** with the test environment.

###### HOW (specific steps/approach)
1. **Set up and document separation** between test and operational environments (for example: separate cloud accounts/subscriptions, separate virtual private clouds or virtual networks, network segmentation, and separate identity and access management identities/service accounts). Keep an auditable record explaining how separation works and how cross-environment access and data transfer is prevented.
2. **Enforce separation in the deployment process**: configure continuous integration and continuous delivery (CI/CD) so build and deployment jobs can only be sent to **test environment identifiers**. Make sure production credentials are not available to test pipelines (for example: separate runners/agents, separate service connections, and rules that block targeting production resources).
3. **Prevent data leakage**: ensure test uses cleaned-up datasets or synthetic data. Put controls in place to stop production data being copied or exported into test by default (for example: data access policies, separate storage locations/buckets, and automated checks that reject deployments that reference production data sources).
4. **Carry out security and privacy checks before release in the test environment**, and produce evidence for each change:
   - Security checks covering flaws/weaknesses/incompatibilities (for example: static security testing, checks for dependencies and software bill of materials, scanning for infrastructure-as-code misconfiguration, and dynamic testing in test).
   - Privacy checks covering privacy impacts (for example: identifying personal data in new schemas/configurations, reviewing how data moves for new interfaces, and validating retention and export rules).
   - Include a structured review for **deliberate wrongdoing** scenarios (for example: bypassing authentication, privilege escalation, injection or data theft attempts) using a checklist and documented outcomes from reviewers.
5. **Use a release gate**: you cannot move a change into the operational environment unless the change record includes (a) evidence of deployment to the test environment, (b) security analysis outputs, (c) privacy analysis outputs, and (d) named approvals from security and privacy stakeholders (or delegated roles) recorded for that specific change.

###### WHO
- **Release/Change Manager**: ensures the release gate is followed and approvals are recorded.
- **Application Security Engineer**: owns the security checking tools, thresholds, and evidence quality.
- **Privacy Officer / Data Protection Lead**: owns the privacy checking template, personal data/data-flow review, and sign-off.
- **Platform/Cloud Engineer**: implements environment separation, CI/CD targeting restrictions, and data leakage prevention.

###### Acceptance criteria
- For each production release, there is an auditable record showing the change was deployed and tested in the **separate test environment**.
- Evidence shows test activities could not affect operational activities (for example: separate accounts/networks/identities and CI/CD targeting restrictions).
- Evidence shows operational information was not accidentally shared with test (for example: sanitised datasets and automated checks that reject references to production data).
- Each change has completed **security and privacy impact analysis** in the test environment, with documented results linked to the change.
- Moving to the operational environment is blocked (technically and procedurally) unless the required evidence and approvals are in place.

###### Actions Required for Compliance

- [ ] Define and document how test and live (operational) environments are kept separate, including the rules for network access, user identity, data storage, and software deployment boundaries.
- [ ] Set up continuous integration and continuous delivery (CI/CD) so deployment jobs can only be used for the test environment, and they cannot use production login details or production system identifiers.
- [ ] Set up automated checks to stop real production data being used in test deployments. Use cleaned or synthetic (fake) datasets by default.
- [ ] Add security checks to the test deployment process and produce proof documents for each change, as relevant (static application security testing, dependency checks, infrastructure as code, and dynamic testing).
- [ ] Add privacy checks to the testing process (find personal data, review how data moves, and confirm rules for how long data is kept and how it can be exported), and produce proof records for each change.
- [ ] Create and carry out a checklist to review potential harmful or incompatible effects of changes, with named security and privacy reviewers. Record the results against each change.
- [ ] Set up a release check (release gate) that prevents a change from being moved into operational use unless the change record includes: proof of test deployment, security analysis, privacy analysis, and the required approvals.

#### CM-4.2 — Verification of Controls (Enhancement)

This requirement is about making sure that when you change something in a live system, you double-check that the protections for safety and the rules for handling people’s data still work properly. Without this, a change could accidentally weaken safeguards, break privacy protections, or produce the wrong results—leaving the organisation exposed to misuse, complaints, regulatory action, or harm to individuals.

The organisation is expected to:
- identify which security and privacy protections could be affected by each change, and
- verify after the change is installed in the operational system that the affected protections are set up correctly, are working as intended, and are delivering the expected security and privacy outcomes.

These checks must be based on the documented analysis for the change, follow the defined verification procedures, involve responsible staff and authorised assessors, and keep records showing what was checked and the results—linked to the specific change made.

##### Post-change verification playbooks with evidence capture

- Category: Software
- Priority: Critical

###### WHAT
Create reusable, evidence-based verification checklists (“playbooks”) that are run in the live operational environment after every approved system change. Each playbook confirms that the security and privacy controls affected by the change are: (1) set up correctly, (2) working as intended, and (3) delivering the expected security and privacy results.

###### WHY (control requirement)
CM-4(2) requires that after system changes are installed in the live operational system, the organisation checks that any affected controls are set up correctly, working as intended, and achieving the expected security and privacy results. The guidance explains that “set up” includes installing changed code in the live system that may affect security or privacy controls.

###### HOW (specific steps/approach)
1. **Create a mapping from control to change impact**: Keep an authoritative link between types of changes or components (for example, authentication logic, authorisation rules, logging and telemetry pipeline, data retention jobs, encryption settings, API gateway policies) and the specific security and privacy controls they may affect.
2. **Turn security/privacy impact analysis into playbooks**: For each affected control, create a playbook that includes: starting conditions, the live checks to perform, the expected results, and the evidence needed (for example, configuration snapshots, test run identifiers, security information and event management (SIEM) query outputs, synthetic transaction results, and proof for privacy requirements such as the outcomes of retention or deletion jobs).
3. **Automatically choose the right playbooks from the change record**: When a change is approved, the change process selects the relevant playbooks based on the security and privacy controls identified as impacted in the change’s security/privacy impact analysis.
4. **Run playbooks in production after deployment**: Execute the playbooks against the correct live operational components using the system component inventory and the relevant environment identifiers (live production only, not just test). Use automation where it is safe (for example, checks at application programming interface (API) level, configuration drift checks, synthetic transactions, and checks on logs and metrics).
5. **Check results and handle exceptions**: Security and privacy assessors (or other authorised validators) review the results to confirm all three CM-4(2) requirements (set up correctly, working as intended, and delivering the expected outcomes). If a step cannot be carried out due to operational limits, require alternative compensating checks and documented approval.
6. **Record and keep audit evidence linked to the change**: Save the playbook results, evidence items, and assessor sign-off in the change control record, and keep supporting evidence so the work can be audited.

###### WHO (role responsible)
- **Change Manager / Release Manager**: ensures playbook selection and execution are mandatory for relevant changes.
- **Application Security/Privacy Engineer**: creates and maintains the playbooks and expected results.
- **Security/Privacy Assessor (authorised)**: reviews and validates results, and approves exceptions.
- **Platform/DevOps Engineer**: builds automation to run playbooks after deployment and collect evidence.

###### Acceptance criteria
- For every live production change that installs code affecting security or privacy controls, the organisation runs the selected playbooks and records the results linked to that specific change.
- The evidence shows that affected controls are set up correctly, working as intended, and achieving the expected security and privacy outcomes (as stated in the playbook expected results).
- Playbook execution covers the correct live operational components (as listed in the system component inventory) and is not limited to non-production environments.
- Where playbooks cannot be fully executed, compensating checks are performed and the assessor approval is documented.

###### Actions Required for Compliance

- [ ] Define and keep up to date a link between each type of change (and its components) and the security and privacy controls that it affects.
- [ ] Create verification checklists (playbooks) for each affected control, including the operational steps to follow, the expected outcomes, and the required proof documents (evidence artefacts).
- [ ] Include playbook selection in the change process, using the change’s security and privacy impact assessment results.
- [ ] Automatically run the selected playbooks in the production environment immediately after deployment.
- [ ] Set up evidence capture (test run identifiers, configuration snapshots, security information and event management (SIEM) or log query results, and privacy enforcement proof) and link it to the change record.
- [ ] Require authorised security and privacy assessors to check the results, confirm they are valid, and approve any alternative checks if full verification cannot be completed.

### CM-5 — Access Restrictions for Change (Control)

This requirement is about ensuring that only the right, properly trained people can physically enter the areas where changes are made, and that they use the correct system accounts and tools to carry out those changes. Without clear rules and permission checks, someone could interfere with equipment, install unauthorised software or updates, or follow the wrong steps—leading to service disruption, privacy breaches, or fraud.

To meet this expectation, the organisation must set out in writing who is allowed to access change-related areas and who is allowed to use change-related systems and procedures. It must obtain the required approvals before access is granted, enforce these restrictions in day-to-day work, and keep records of the approvals, the change activity, and who accessed what. The organisation must also ensure this approach is included in its formal policies, plans, and system documentation so it is applied consistently.

##### JIT change-privilege access with enforced change windows

- Category: Software
- Priority: Critical

###### WHAT
Set up an identity- and work-process-based approach to limit **logical access** to change-related capabilities (for example: admin consoles, deployment and continuous integration/continuous delivery (CI/CD) tools, firmware and software update portals, package and media libraries, infrastructure-as-code runners, and related operational procedures). This ensures that only **suitably qualified and authorised people** can make changes, **only during approved change periods**, using time-limited permission increases that are automatically removed.

###### WHY (control requirement)
CM-5 requires the organisation to **define, document, approve, and enforce physical and logical access restrictions** linked to changes to the system. The guidance specifically highlights logical access restrictions, including **software libraries, workflow automation, media libraries, abstraction layers, and change periods**, and stresses that access must be allowed only to **suitably qualified and authorised people**.

###### HOW (specific steps/approach)
1. **List change capabilities**: identify all logical “routes” that allow changes to the application and its supporting components (admin roles, deployment pipelines, release tools, package and media repositories, firmware and software update tools, remote management consoles, and any scripts or workflows that change operational procedures).
2. **Define change-specific permissions**: create detailed roles/entitlements based on least privilege (for example: Change Implementer, Release Manager, Firmware/Update Operator, Change Approver) and link each permission to the exact tools, endpoints, and actions it allows.
3. **Require approval-linked, time-limited permission increases (just-in-time (JIT))**: connect the change management system to the identity provider so that elevated access is granted only when the change ticket is in an **approved** state and within the **approved change period**; automatically remove the permission when the period ends or when the ticket is closed.
4. **Apply conditional access for change permissions**: use policy controls for change entitlements such as multi-factor authentication (MFA) that is resistant to phishing, requirements for managed devices, approved admin workstations or jump hosts, and network or location restrictions.
5. **Record and keep evidence**: ensure the system stores audit evidence linking (a) the user identity, (b) the granted permission/role, (c) the change ticket ID, (d) the tool/action used, and (e) the timestamps for when permission was granted and removed, and when the change was carried out.
6. **Enforce and monitor**: prevent or block privileged actions when the user is not currently authorised for the approved change period; alert on attempts to use change permissions outside approved periods or by roles that are not approved.

###### WHO (role responsible)
- **Application Owner / Change Manager**: owns the change process, sets the change periods, and ensures approvals are recorded.
- **IAM (identity and access management) / Identity Engineer**: implements just-in-time (JIT) and entitlement integration, conditional access, and permission lifecycle automation.
- **Platform/DevOps Lead**: maps change capabilities to entitlements and ensures pipelines and tools follow the enforced access model.
- **Security Monitoring Lead**: configures security information and event management (SIEM) alerts and checks that audit evidence is complete.

###### Acceptance criteria
- Privileged access to each identified change capability is granted **only** through the approved change process and is **time-limited** to the approved change period.
- Users cannot carry out change actions using admin/change tools unless their identity has an active, approval-linked entitlement.
- Audit records show, for each privileged change action: user identity, change ticket ID, permission/role, tool/action, and the timestamps for permission grant and removal.
- Automated permission removal happens at the end of the change period and/or when the ticket is closed, with no ongoing change permissions left for people who are not approvers.
- Monitoring generates alerts for privileged change attempts outside approved change periods and for use by unauthorised roles.

###### Actions Required for Compliance

- [ ] Take an inventory of all ways the system can be changed logically, including admin consoles, deployment pipelines, update tools, software and media libraries, and any change-related scripts or workflows.
- [ ] Set up change-specific roles and permissions using the least amount of access possible, and link each permission to the exact tools, endpoints, and actions it allows.
- [ ] Link the change management process to the identity provider so that “just-in-time” access is granted only for a limited time, and only after the change request is approved and during the approved change window.
- [ ] Set up conditional access for change permissions, including phishing-resistant multi-factor authentication (MFA), limits on which managed devices and jump hosts can be used, and restrictions on allowed networks and locations.
- [ ] Automatically remove change permissions when the change window ends and when the ticket is closed. Any attempt to make changes outside the approved window will be blocked by default.
- [ ] Set up audit logging to record the user’s identity, the change ticket ID, the privilege that was granted, the tool and action used, and the times when the privilege was granted or revoked. Send these records to security information and event management (SIEM).
- [ ] Set up security information and event management (SIEM) alerts to detect privileged changes made outside approved time windows and attempts by unauthorised roles.

#### CM-5.1 — Automated Access Enforcement and Audit Records (Enhancement)

This requirement is about ensuring that only the right people can make approved changes to systems, and that every time a system allows or blocks a change, it automatically records what happened. Without this, unauthorised staff or compromised accounts could make risky changes, such as altering settings or deployments, and the organisation might not be able to prove who made changes and when—making it harder to investigate mistakes or suspected wrongdoing.

To meet this expectation, the organisation must configure its on-premises identity and privileged access tools so they automatically enforce the access rules whenever configuration changes are applied through its change-management process, without relying on manual approvals. It must also automatically create audit records for each access decision, clearly showing whether the change was allowed or blocked, who attempted it, what change was targeted, and the time—so the organisation can review these records later if anything suspicious is found.

##### IAM/PAM-gated config changes with allow/block audit events

- Category: Software
- Priority: Critical

###### WHAT
Set up automated checks that control whether configuration changes are allowed. This is done by linking the organisation’s configuration/change process with the organisation’s on-prem identity and access management (IAM) and privileged access management (PAM) decision-making. For every decision—whether a change is allowed or blocked—the system must automatically create an audit record showing who requested it, what change was targeted, the result, and the time.

###### WHY (control requirement)
The CM-5(1) enhancement guidance requires organisations to **log system access related to applying configuration changes**. This ensures configuration change control is in place and supports **investigations after the fact** if unauthorised changes are found. It also expects **automated enforcement** during configuration change activity, rather than relying on manual checks.

###### HOW (specific steps/approach)
1. **Define change items and what they can affect**: map configuration management outputs (for example, continuous integration/continuous delivery (CI/CD) deployment jobs, infrastructure-as-code modules, configuration templates, change tickets/releases, and target environments) to clear “change items” and “target scopes” (system/service/cluster/zone).
2. **Add an approval gate to the change process**: in the configuration management pipeline (or change orchestration service), add a required step before changes are applied. This step asks the on-prem IAM/PAM system for an approval decision for the requesting user/service identity, for the specific change item and target scope.
3. **Apply the decision automatically**: if IAM/PAM says “not authorised”, stop the change before any configuration is applied. If “authorised”, continue using the short-lived access granted by IAM/PAM (where supported) so the change runner cannot skip the approval step.
4. **Create structured audit records for every decision**: for each approval gate result, automatically generate an audit event that at minimum includes: outcome (allowed/blocked), requester identity (user/service account), change request/job/template ID, target(s), time stamp, and the IAM/PAM decision reference (correlation ID).
5. **Centralise and protect audit records**: send audit events to the organisation’s security information and event management (SIEM) / logging platform using an append-only or tamper-evident method. Limit administrative access and keep records for the period required by enterprise logging rules.
6. **Ensure audit evidence is complete and easy to search**: make sure audit events can be searched by request ID, user identity, target, environment (production/test/disaster recovery), and time window to support after-the-fact investigations.

###### WHO (role responsible)
- **Platform/DevOps Engineering Lead**: implements the pipeline/change-orchestration approval gate and ensures it cannot be bypassed.
- **IAM/PAM Administrator**: sets up entitlements/policies and provides the decision interface/integration.
- **Security Operations (SOC) / SIEM Engineer**: checks the audit event format, ensures events are sent to SIEM, manages retention, and sets up alerting.

###### Acceptance criteria
- For configuration changes carried out through the approved change workflow, the pipeline performs an IAM/PAM authorisation check **before** applying configuration.
- For every allow/block decision, an audit record is created automatically and includes: requester identity, change request/job/template ID, target(s), outcome, time stamp, and correlation/decision reference.
- Blocked changes must not apply any configuration.
- Audit records are sent to SIEM and can be searched by requester identity, target, and time window.
- **Demonstrated evidence**: a test run showing both an allowed change and a blocked change, with the corresponding SIEM audit events.

###### Actions Required for Compliance

- [ ] Map configuration management change records to enforceable change items and the specific systems or areas affected (target scopes).
- [ ] Implement a change-workflow step that requires authorisation before any change is applied, and cannot be skipped. This step must check the on-premises identity and privileged access management (IAM/PAM) system.
- [ ] Set up identity and access management (IAM) and privileged access management (PAM) permissions and rules for each change item and its target scope, including cases involving service accounts and privileged operators.
- [ ] Create audit records at the access gate that show whether each request was allowed or blocked, including the requester’s identity, the change reference, the affected target(s), the result, the time, and a matching reference number (correlation ID).
- [ ] Set up audit event forwarding to the security information and event management (SIEM) or log platform using a tamper-evident, append-only method for receiving data.
- [ ] Use test cases to confirm that blocked decisions stop configuration changes from being applied, and that the system creates the matching security information and event management (SIEM) audit records.

#### CM-5.4 — Dual Authorization (Enhancement)

This requirement is designed to ensure that important changes are never made based on one person’s decision alone. For any security-relevant change that affects day-to-day operations—including operational procedures and sensitive settings—two suitably qualified and accountable people must review and approve the change before it is carried out. Without this, a mistake, an unauthorised change, or a malicious change could go unnoticed, potentially weakening access protections, making attacks easier, disrupting monitoring and alerts, or causing production procedures to be incorrect.

To meet this expectation, the organisation must:
- Clearly define what is included in scope.
- Put in place a process that requires two-person approval for changes to privileged authentication and authorisation settings, firewall rules, and security monitoring and alerting components.
- Also require two-person approval for changes to operational procedures, runbooks, and production and security-relevant configuration documentation.

The organisation must also:
- Limit who is allowed to approve changes and who is allowed to implement them.
- Keep records showing that both approvals were completed before implementation.
- Ensure the two approvers have the right knowledge and responsibility.
- Where this approach is used, rotate duties to reduce the risk of collusion.

##### Dual authorisation workflow for security and production changes

- Category: Software
- Priority: Critical

###### WHAT
Apply **dual authorisation** (two-person control) before making changes to the organisation-defined **system components and system-level information** that are in scope. This includes, specifically:

- **Privileged configuration** of:
  - authentication and authorisation services
  - firewall rules
  - security monitoring and alerting components
- **Operational procedures and runbooks**, and
- **Security-relevant configuration documentation**

###### WHY (control requirement)
The NIST SP 800-53 **CM-5(4)** enhancement requires that changes within the specified scope **cannot be made unless two suitable people approve and carry out the change**.

- The two people must be **suitable** to judge whether the approved change has been implemented correctly.
- They must also be **accountable** for the change.
- The guidance also notes that dual authorisation may be called a **two-person control**, and that organisations should **rotate responsibilities** to reduce the risk of collusion.

###### HOW (specific steps/approach)
Put dual authorisation in place as a **strong enforcement step** inside the change process and tools used for:
- production releases, and
- updates to operational procedures.

1. **Define and map the scope in the change system**
   - Create clear change categories/tags that match the organisation-defined parameter **cm-5.4_prm_1** (“All system components and system-level information that are security-relevant or affect production operations, including operational procedures and privileged configurations”).
   - Ensure categories include at least:
     - privileged authentication and authorisation configuration
     - firewall rule or policy changes
     - security monitoring and alerting rule changes
     - operational procedures and runbooks
     - security-relevant production configuration documentation

2. **Require two separate approvals before implementation**
   - Configure the workflow so that, for any change in the dual-authorisation scope, the change cannot move to **Ready to Implement/Implemented** until **two different suitable approvers** have approved.
   - Ensure the **same person cannot provide both approvals** on the same change record.

3. **Separate approval from implementation (no bypass)**
   - Use role-based permissions so that:
     - only members of a dedicated **Dual Approver** group can carry out the approval steps
     - only members of a separate **Change Implementer** role can carry out the implementation
   - Block any situation where one person can both approve and implement for dual-authorisation-scoped changes (or require a compensating control that is auditable if your tools cannot fully separate roles).

4. **Make approvals accountable and keep clear evidence**
   - Require each approver to complete structured confirmations linked to the change record, for example:
     - confirmation the change correctly implements the approved change request
     - confirmation the operational impact has been assessed for production procedures and runbooks
     - confirmation rollback or contingency is checked where relevant
   - Ensure the system records:
     - **who approved** (identity)
     - **when they approved** (timestamp)
     - the **approval outcome**

5. **Rotate dual authorisation responsibilities to reduce collusion risk**
   - Apply a rotation rule for the Dual Approver group (for example, rotation by change type or on a schedule) so the same pair is not used repeatedly.
   - Record rotation assignments to support auditability.

6. **Only implement after both approvals**
   - Connect the workflow to the deployment and operations execution method (for example, the release pipeline, runbook execution, or the configuration management “apply” step) so execution is blocked unless the change record shows **two approvals**.

###### WHO
- **Change Manager / Change Management Lead**: defines scope mappings and sets up workflow rules.
- **Dual Approvers (suitable and accountable individuals)**: provide the two independent approvals and confirmations.
- **Change Implementers (separate role)**: carry out the approved change after the workflow authorises it.
- **Identity and Access Management (IAM) / Platform Security**: ensures role separation and prevents bypass.

###### Acceptance criteria
- For any change tagged within **cm-5.4_prm_1** scope, the workflow prevents implementation unless **two separate suitable approvers** have approved.
- The same person cannot approve twice on the same change record.
- Approval and implementation permissions are separated so that no single user can both approve and implement dual-authorisation-scoped changes.
- Audit evidence exists for each change showing **two approvals with different identities and timestamps**, and implementation happens only after both approvals.
- Dual approver responsibilities are rotated according to the agreed rotation approach, and rotation evidence is retained.

###### Actions Required for Compliance

- [ ] Define two-authorisation change categories and map them to **cm-5.4_prm_1** (privileged access, firewall, monitoring and alerting, operational procedures and runbooks, and security-relevant documentation).
- [ ] Set up the change process so it cannot move to “Ready to Implement” or “Implemented” until two separate approvals have been recorded.
- [ ] Set up and enforce separate user roles or groups for **dual approvers** and **change implementers**, so that no single user can both approve and carry out changes that require dual authorisation.
- [ ] Set up workflow checks to stop the same person from giving both approvals for the same change record.
- [ ] Add structured approval confirmations that require approvers to confirm the approved change has been implemented correctly and that production and operational impacts have been considered.
- [ ] Link the actions that carry out changes (deployment, runbook execution, and configuration updates) to the change workflow, so the system will not allow execution unless two approvals have been provided.
- [ ] Set up and record rotation of duties that require two approvers, to reduce the risk of collusion.

#### CM-5.5 — Privilege Limitation for Production and Operation (Enhancement)

This requirement ensures that only the right people can make changes to the systems that support day-to-day operations, and that these permissions are reviewed regularly. If too many people can change parts of the systems or the operational instructions that guide how work is carried out, mistakes or unauthorised changes could disrupt services, harm business processes, or create hidden problems that are difficult to detect later.

To meet this expectation, the organisation must clearly limit who is allowed to change system components and system-related information in production or operational environments, including operational procedures, and must document what information counts as system-related. The organisation must also review and re-check these change permissions every 90 days to confirm they are still appropriate, and keep records showing who was authorised to make changes and evidence that the 90-day reviews and re-evaluations were completed.

##### 90-day recertification for production change and procedure editors

- Category: Software
- Priority: Critical

###### WHAT must be done
Put limits on who can change **production/operational system parts** and **information used in operations (including operational procedures)** by:
1) limiting who is allowed to make those changes in production/operational environments, and
2) carrying out a **review and re-check** of those change permissions **at least every 90 days**, with evidence that can be audited.

###### WHY (control requirement)
CM-5(5) requires limiting permissions for changes to operational systems and operational information (including operational procedures), and making sure those permissions are **reviewed and re-checked at a set frequency** (set by the organisation; here: **every 90 days**).

###### HOW (specific steps/approach)
###### 1) Define what counts as “operational information” for production/operations
- Create an organisation-approved way to classify what is covered by production/operational changes, explicitly including **operational procedures/runbooks** and any related items (for example: runbook pages, workflow definitions, deployment scripts, and configuration templates used for operations).
- Link each type of item to the relevant entry in the system/component inventory (configuration management database (CMDB)/asset system) so the scope of re-checks can be traced.

###### 2) Create limited authorisation roles for production changes
- Set up separate, “least privilege” roles, such as:
  - **Production Component Change – [system/component class]**
  - **Operational Procedure Editor – [domain]**
- Give these roles only to users who have an approved business reason and change-management approval.
- Separate responsibilities (for example, request/approve versus implement) so that procedure editors are not automatically allowed to deploy components.

###### 3) Apply the restriction in the application and administration tools
- Configure the application’s administration screens, continuous integration/continuous delivery (CI/CD) controls, and any operational procedure repositories so that only the limited roles can:
  - change production configuration/components, and
  - edit and approve operational procedures.
- Block all other users (including standard administrators) from writing to the production change areas.

###### 4) Set up automated 90-day review and 90-day re-check workflows
- Use the organisation’s identity provider (IdP)/privileged access management (PAM)/identity governance and administration (IGA) platform to run two separate workflow steps on a **90-day cycle**:
  - **Review**: confirm the user still needs the permission to change the relevant production component/procedure type.
  - **Re-check**: confirm the permission coverage (which systems/components/procedure areas) is still correct and has not changed over time.
- Record the outcome of each workflow (approved/removed/changed) and keep it for audit.

###### 5) Keep audit-ready evidence
- Keep:
  - snapshots of role/permission membership and any changes,
  - records showing completion of the review and re-check (including dates and outcomes),
  - links from permissions to the relevant production component/procedure inventory entries.
- Send logs to the security information and event management (SIEM) system for secure, tamper-evident retention and audit matching.

###### WHO (role responsible)
- **Application Owner / Service Owner**: approves what counts as production change areas and which procedure items are included.
- **Identity and access management (IAM)/identity governance and administration (IGA) Owner (or PAM Administrator)**: sets up the limited roles and the 90-day review/re-check workflows.
- **Configuration Management Lead**: maintains the mapping between permissions and the system/component inventory.
- **Change Management Lead**: ensures approvals follow operational change governance.

###### Acceptance criteria
- Production/operational change areas (system components and operational procedures) can be changed only by users assigned to the limited production change roles.
- For every user given production change permissions, there is evidence that both the **review** and **re-check** were completed **at least every 90 days**.
- Review/re-check outcomes (approved/removed/changed) are recorded and can be linked to named reviewers.
- Permission coverage is traceable to the production component/procedure inventory entries (no unused or “orphaned” permissions).

###### Actions Required for Compliance

- [ ] Define and document which items count as system-related information for production and operations, explicitly including operational procedures (runbooks).
- [ ] Create limited authorisation roles for making changes to production components and for editing operational procedures, with separation of duties.
- [ ] Set up the application and administration tools, and the procedure repositories, so that only the approved production change roles can write to them.
- [ ] Connect the roles to the organisation’s identity and access management (IAM), identity governance and administration (IGA), and privileged access management (PAM) platform, and link each entitlement to the relevant entries in the production component and procedure inventory.
- [ ] Set up automated workflows to carry out both 90-day checks of user access privileges and 90-day re-assessments, and to record the results and the dates.
- [ ] Export and keep audit evidence (changes to entitlement membership, results of reviews or re-checks, and scope mappings) for use with security information and event management (SIEM) and audit correlation.

#### CM-5.6 — Limit Library Privileges (Enhancement)

This requirement is about ensuring that only the right people can change the software stored in shared software libraries, including powerful, privileged programmes that can affect how systems behave. Without this, unauthorised staff or attackers could quietly replace software with altered versions, which could lead to fraud, service disruption, or hidden backdoors that are hard to detect later.

To meet this expectation, the organisation must clearly set out in its software change rules who is allowed to make changes to these libraries, and then enforce that only named information security–responsible personnel and system or network administrators can do it. The organisation must keep written procedures and a documented plan showing how these restrictions are applied. This should be supported by system design and configuration information, a list of the software libraries and components involved, and records of each approved change, including the audit trail of who made the change and when.

##### RBAC-gated library change with auditable approvals

- Category: Software
- Priority: Critical

###### WHAT
Put in place technical and process controls so that only explicitly authorised roles can update software that is stored in defined software libraries (including privileged programmes).

###### WHY (control requirement)
CM-5(6) enhancement requires: **"Limit privileges to change software resident within software libraries"**. Guidance explains that **software libraries include privileged programmes**, so permission to create, update, or replace these items must be tightly controlled and recorded for audit purposes.

###### HOW (specific approach)
1. **Define what counts as “software libraries”** for this application/system (for example: package registries, container image registries, approved “golden” images, shared binaries directories, privileged programme stores). Keep an up-to-date, official inventory that links each library location/repository to an owning team and environment (production/test/disaster recovery).
2. **Use identity-based role-based access control (RBAC)** linked to the organisation’s identity provider so that only two named role categories can make changes to libraries:
   - **Library-change performer** (limited to the smallest necessary group of administrators/build operators)
   - **Library-change approver** (information security–responsible personnel and/or system/network administrators, as defined by the organisation)
   All other roles (including general administrators) must be blocked from write/replace access to the library locations.
3. **Apply the restriction at the point where access is enforced**:
   - Set repository/storage permissions so that only the performer role can perform library write/replace actions.
   - Use **just-in-time (JIT) privileged access** for the performer role (through a privileged access management workflow) so elevated access is only granted for a limited time and requires strong authentication.
4. **Require approval before changes can proceed**:
   - Get an approval record from the approver role before the change process can run.
   - Make sure the approval identity and time are tied to the specific item being changed (for example: repository path, package name/version, image digest).
5. **Make changes auditable and keep evidence**:
   - Configure logging so that every library change records: the person who acted, the approver identity (where relevant), the date and time, the library item identifier (name/version/digest), and the change request identifier.
   - Send logs to security information and event management (SIEM) and keep them according to the organisation’s logging and retention requirements.

###### WHO (responsible roles)

### CM-6 — Configuration Settings (Control)

This requirement is about ensuring every part of your systems is configured in the safest, most restricted way possible, while still allowing it to do its job. ### CM-7 — Least Functionality (Control)

This expectation is about keeping systems as simple as possible. Only the features you truly need should be enabled, and anything risky or unnecessary should be turned off or removed. If this is not done, attackers may take advantage of leftover “default” features, older or insecure ways of connecting, and hidden services to break in, steal data, or take control of systems—especially through file sharing, anonymous access, guest logins, weak remote access methods, or unauthorised tools.

To meet this requirement, the organisation must configure systems so that only authenticated users can access them, only the required application services are available, approved management access is permitted, and necessary security monitoring is in place. It must prohibit or restrict file sharing, directory browsing, anonymous access, dynamic DNS updates, guest accounts, and unused web administration. It must block specific network connection points and older connection methods, including Telnet, File Transfer Protocol (FTP), Trivial File Transfer Protocol (TFTP), Server Message Block version 1 (SMBv1), Remote Shell (RSH), and rlogin. Web access must use secure encrypted connections only.

The organisation must remove or disable default sample applications, unused drivers, browser plug-ins, remote desktop agents, and unauthorised third-party software. It must also disable anonymous web services and unnecessary discovery features. Remote administration must be limited to approved source locations and must require a second factor, such as a code or token.

   - Require multi-factor authentication (MFA) for administrative actions.

#### CM-7.2 — Prevent Program Execution (Enhancement)

This requirement is about preventing unauthorised software from running on your systems, including software that tries to start itself without you asking. Without these safeguards, a harmful or unapproved programme could be launched, licences could be breached, and vendor copyright terms could be ignored. This could result in financial loss, legal risk, and disruption to business operations.

To meet this expectation, the organisation must set clear, documented rules for which software is allowed to run. Only software approved by the Department of Defence (DoD) should be able to run. Features that allow software to start automatically must be blocked. Any attempt to run a programme must be supported by an approved information technology (IT) change record, with the minimum level of authorisation needed for the task.

The organisation must also allow only the specific approved programmes listed in its documentation, keep an up-to-date inventory of software components, and limit how many copies can run at the same time to match licence and end-user terms. Finally, it must record both the approvals for changes and the results of each allowed or blocked attempt, so the activity can be reviewed during audits.

##### Application allowlisting with change-approval execution gating

- Category: Software
- Priority: Critical

###### WHAT
Set up a “deny by default” rule for programme execution so that only software that has been explicitly approved can run. This blocks automatic “run by itself” behaviours and allows execution only when (1) an IT change approval for that specific system is currently active and (2) the user or session has the required least-privilege authorisation.

###### WHY (control requirement)
The National Institute of Standards and Technology (NIST) Special Publication 800-53 Configuration Management (CM)-7(2) enhancement requires: “Prevent program execution in accordance with {{ insert: param, cm-07.02_odp.01 }}.” The organisation-defined parameter (cm-07.02_odp.02) sets restrictions including: **only Department of Defense (DoD)-approved software**, **block auto-execute**, **execution requires IT change approval and least-privilege role authorisation**, and **limit concurrent instances per licence terms**.

Include licence/end-user licence agreement (EULA) constraints, including **maximum concurrent instances per programme where enforceable**.
2. **Apply “deny by default” execution control** using endpoint/application control (or an equivalent workload control for the hosting model). Configure the rules to:
   - Deny execution of any programme binary that does not match the approved list.
   - Deny software from unsigned or unknown publishers.
   - Allow execution only for approved programme identities (publisher plus hash/signature plus version).
3. **Block auto-execute** by strengthening removable media and installer execution routes:
   - Disable or restrict execution from removable media and prevent autorun-like behaviour.
   - Stop installers or scripts from automatically launching from user-writable locations.
   - Require an interactive, authorised session for any execution that matches an approved programme.
4. **Add execution checks linked to IT change approval and least privilege**:
   - Connect the execution decision to user identity and authorisation (role-based access control (RBAC)) so only a designated least-privilege role (for example, “execution enablement approver” or “authorised change operator”) can enable execution.
   - Require an active, system-specific change record (change window/entitlement) before allowing approved programmes to run.
   - Implement the check as an automated validation against the organisation’s change management system (for example, using an entitlement token or an application programming interface (API)-supported “policy as code”), so the decision is auditable and limited to the approved time period.
5. **Enforce limits on simultaneous programme instances** where the platform supports it (for example, application control/workload policy, job scheduler controls, or a controlled wrapper/launcher). 6. **Produce audit records for both allowed and blocked execution attempts**:

   - Send logs to the security information and event management (SIEM) system for retention and correlation with change-management activity.

###### WHO (role responsible)
- **Security Engineering / Endpoint Security Team**: implement application control allowlisting, block auto-execute, and manage logging.
- **IT Change Management Lead**: ensure change records include system identifiers, programme entitlements, time windows, and the approver identity.
- **Identity and Access Management (IAM) / Platform Engineering**: implement least-privilege role mappings and integrate with identity services.
- **Application Owner**: maintain the approved-software list entries for the business application and its approved components.

###### Acceptance criteria
- Execution is **denied by default** for any programme not listed in the approved catalogue.
- **Auto-execute** from removable media and installer locations is blocked (no unauthorised automatic launching).
- For approved programmes, execution succeeds only when:
  - the user/session is in the designated least-privilege role, and
  - a valid, active system-specific IT change approval/entitlement exists.
- Concurrent instance limits are enforced for catalogue entries where enforceable (either natively or via a controlled launcher). Excess attempts are denied and logged.
- Audit logs show allow/deny decisions including programme identity, user/session identity, host/environment, and the change record reference (or an explicit “no active change entitlement”).

###### Actions Required for Compliance

- [ ] Define an approved-software catalogue that includes the software’s programme identity (publisher, signature, and hash), the available versions, where the software is allowed to run, and the maximum number of simultaneous installations allowed under the licence/end-user licence agreement (EULA).
- [ ] Set up endpoint and workload application control to block programme execution by default, and allow only programmes that match the approved catalogue.
- [ ] Secure removable storage and the installer’s execution process to prevent automatic execution and silent launching from locations that users can write to.
- [ ] Before allowing execution, check that the user has the required least-privilege role membership and that they have an active, system-specific information technology change entitlement.
- [ ] Use built-in platform controls, or a managed launcher, to limit how many instances can run at the same time and block any additional instances beyond the limit.
- [ ] Turn on and send audit logs for every allow or deny decision for programme execution, including the programme identity, the user or session identity, the host or environment, and the change reference.

#### CM-7.3 — Registration Compliance (Enhancement)

This requirement is about keeping an accurate, up-to-date master record of what each system can do and how it connects, before it is put into use and every time anything changes. If this record is not kept current, you may lose track of services that are running, connections that are open, or features that have been added or changed. It must then register, in its central configuration record, all system functions, network ports, communication methods (protocols), and services before deployment. After deployment, whenever any of these items are added, removed, or changed, the organisation must update the same central record so it always matches the current setup. The organisation must also keep dated evidence showing the record was completed before go-live and updated when changes were made.

#### CM-7.4 — Unauthorized Software — Deny-by-exception (Enhancement)

This expectation is about making sure only approved software can run, and that everything else is blocked by default. It addresses the risk that unapproved or altered software—for example, installers or programmes that have not been checked, or that have not been properly verified—could be used to steal data, disrupt day-to-day operations, or introduce malicious software (malware) without anyone noticing.

To meet this expectation, the organisation must:
- keep an up-to-date list of software it has approved
- use a “block unless approved” approach, so that any software not on the approved list cannot run on its systems, including unsigned or unapproved installers and programme files
- identify any unauthorised software that is already present
- maintain a clear list of what is blocked
- review and update the blocked list at least every 90 days

The organisation must also be able to provide evidence that these reviews and updates have been carried out, and ensure the right people are responsible for spotting unauthorised software and for putting the blocking controls in place.

In this approach, software is treated as unauthorised if it is not on the approved allowlist (including unsigned or not-approved installers and files), and the system blocks it from running. The guidance also requires identifying unauthorised software and reviewing and updating the decisions about it at a defined frequency.

###### HOW (specific steps/approach)

2. **Set up endpoint/application execution control** (for example, endpoint application control and allowlisting) in **deny-by-default** mode, with clear rules that explicitly allow approved software (allowlist-based, deny-by-exception behaviour).
3. **Block unsigned or not-approved software** by requiring valid signatures from approved publishers and/or matching approved installer/package identifiers or hashes for higher-risk software. Configure the control to stop execution when signature checks fail or when the software does not match allowlist rules.
4. **Keep identifying unauthorised software** by collecting endpoint/application inventory and execution activity (for example, installed applications, installer runs, and blocked execution events). Maintain a derived “unauthorised software” list based on allowlist mismatches and signature failures.

6. **Keep audit evidence**: store policy versions, approvals for allowlist changes, and execution-control logs showing blocked attempts (including reason codes such as “unsigned”, “unapproved”, or “no allowlist match”), and evidence that the review and update happened at least every 90 days.

- Evidence shows the allowlist/unauthorised decisions are **reviewed and updated at least every 90 days**, including policy version history and approvals.

###### Actions Required for Compliance

#### CM-7.5 — Authorized Software — Allow-by-exception (Enhancement)

This expectation is about ensuring that only approved software is allowed to run on your systems, and that everything else is blocked by default. Without this, unauthorised or altered software could be installed or run, which could lead to data theft, service disruption, or attackers gaining a foothold by using malicious code disguised as legitimate software.

#### CM-7.6 — Confined Environments with Limited Privileges (Enhancement)

This expectation is about keeping risky software—such as software that has been downloaded or obtained from third parties—away from the rest of your systems while it is running. If this software contains malicious code or behaves in an unexpected way, it could spread harm, steal information, or disrupt services across the wider organisation. Running it in a separate, tightly controlled environment helps ensure any damage stays contained.

To meet this expectation, the organisation must first identify and keep a documented list of the specific user-installed software from untrusted sources that must be treated this way. It must then configure its systems so that, whenever someone tries to run any item from that list, it runs only inside a confined physical or virtual machine environment and with limited permissions, rather than full administrative access.

The approach must be described in configuration and design documentation. It must be supported by defined system settings and an up-to-date inventory of the components involved. Compliance must be checked using secure configuration checklists, and there must be retained records showing the software was run in the restricted way.

Named roles must be assigned for identifying the software in scope, overseeing the process, and implementing and maintaining the confinement and permission limits.

- Category: Software
- Priority: Critical

3. **Apply confinement during execution** by using **one approved execution route** (for example, a managed “sandbox launcher” process) that creates the confined virtual machine and runs the software there. - **Information Security (Compliance)**: check the audit evidence and approve the sandbox profiles.

###### Acceptance criteria
- For every software item listed in the `cm-07.06_odp` catalogue, execution is carried out using the sandbox process and **takes place inside a confined physical/virtual machine**.
- The execution account inside the sandbox is **not administrative** (or is otherwise clearly limited) and any ways to gain higher privileges are blocked or controlled.
- Sandbox network access is **restricted** as defined in the profile (default deny with explicit allowlisting where needed).
- For each execution, the organisation can provide **audit evidence** showing: software identifier/version, sandbox profile/virtual machine image ID, execution time, and recorded execution telemetry.
- The confinement mechanism is **enforced during execution** (not just documented as a recommendation).

- [ ] Set up a managed “sandbox” execution service (launcher/portal) that creates a locked-down virtual machine and runs the approved software inside it.

#### CM-7.7 — Code Execution in Protected Environments (Enhancement)

This requirement is about ensuring that any compiled computer code—including commercial software, firmware, and open-source components—can only run in a tightly controlled environment. This must be either a locked-down physical computer or a restricted virtual machine, and it must happen only after a senior accountable decision has been recorded.

To meet this expectation, the organisation must stop code from running outside the approved confined environments, apply the same rule to all code sources, and require explicit approval from the Authorising Official or the Information System Owner before running code that has limited or no warranty and/or when source code is not available.  

The organisation should also keep clear records of: what executable code is present, how systems are configured to confine it, who approved it, and audit evidence showing where and when such code ran.

2. **Create an authoritative list of approved executables**: keep a central, auditable record of approved executable items (including vendor firmware images and packaged open-source binaries). 3. **Apply the approved-list rule**: configure endpoint/server application control (approved-list) so that only inventory-approved, signed executable items (or approved hashes) can run. Block any executable that is not on the approved list, and ensure this enforcement applies only within the restricted execution environments.
4. **Require AO/ISO approval for restricted-source cases**: if an executable item is marked as **limited/no-warranty** and/or **no source code available**, require an explicit approval record by the **AO/ISO** before it is added to the approved list and published policy. Store the approval together with the executable identity (hash/signature), the environment mapping, the approver identity, the approval date/time, and the reason for approval.
5. **Stop and record any “out-of-bounds” execution**: ensure the system logs include (a) the attempted executable identity, (b) the host/virtual machine identity, (c) whether the attempt was allowed or blocked, and (d) whether the item is approved and approved by the AO/ISO. Send these logs to **security information and event management (SIEM)** for audit-ready retention.
6. **Use the organisation’s release/change process**: build the approved-list and policy publication into the organisation’s release management process, so policy updates (including newly approved executable items) cannot be deployed without the required AO/ISO approval record for restricted-source cases.

###### WHO
- **Information System Owner (ISO)** or **Authorising Official (AO)**: provides explicit approval for restricted-source items (cm-07.07_odp).
- **Application/Infrastructure Security Engineering**: designs the restricted execution boundaries and implements the approved-list enforcement.
- **Release/Configuration Management**: ensures policy publication is only allowed when the required approval records exist.
- **SIEM/Monitoring Operations**: ensures audit logs are collected, kept, and can be searched.

###### Acceptance criteria
- Any attempt to run an unapproved binary is blocked in the production execution environments and produces an audit log entry.
- For items that are limited/no-warranty and/or have no source code, execution is possible only after an explicit AO/ISO approval record exists, and that record is linked to the executable identity and the permitted environment(s).
- Audit evidence exists showing: approved executable identities (hash/signature), permitted execution environments, approver identity (AO/ISO), approval date/time, and the outcome of execution attempts (allowed/blocked) including host/virtual machine context.
- The approved-list enforcement is clearly limited to the restricted physical/virtual environments, so binaries cannot run outside them.

###### Actions Required for Compliance

- [ ] Define and document the approved “confined execution environments” (the permitted physical or virtual boundaries) for running production workloads.
- [ ] Create an auditable, executable list of approved items, using cryptographic identifiers (hashes and signing identities) linked to the environments they are allowed to run in.
- [ ] Set up application control (allow-list) enforcement on the computers that run the application to block any executable file that is not in the approved list (inventory).

- [ ] Add an allow-list or policy deployment step to the release pipeline, with approval-record checks and a link to the change-control process.
- [ ] Send and enable logs of execution attempts (allowed or blocked, the user or service identity, the host or virtual machine identity, and the approval status) to the security information and event management (SIEM) system, and keep them for the required retention period.

#### CM-7.8 — Binary or Machine Executable Code (Enhancement)

This requirement is about not using compiled software or device firmware that you cannot properly support. If something goes wrong, you may not be able to check what it does, fix it, or get help.

If an item comes from a supplier that provides little or no warranty, or if it is supplied without the underlying source code, it is harder to review for hidden problems. It is also harder to repair or improve, and there may be no clear party you can ask to make changes for you. With open-source software, there may also be no warranty or support, and the code could include unwanted features.

To meet this expectation, the organisation must:
- keep a record of all such executable items and where they came from;
- confirm whether each supplier provides source code and what level of warranty applies; and
- carry out a security and practical impact assessment when source code or warranty is limited.

Any exception must be justified by a strong operational need, and it must be approved and recorded by the system’s authorised official. This must be done through an internal approval process with named responsibilities and enforcement mechanisms.

##### SBOM-led executable code governance with exception approvals

- Category: Software
- Priority: Critical

###### WHAT must be done
Put in place a governance process to make sure the organisation only uses binary or machine-ready software code (including firmware and commercial or open-source components) from suppliers or sources that either provide adequate warranty and/or make the source code available. Keep a complete, auditable record of every piece of executable code used, including where it came from, the warranty and support terms, and whether source code is available. If executable code is taken from sources with limited or no warranty or without source code, carry out a documented security and practical impact assessment and get formal approval to use it as an exception.

###### WHY (control requirement)
CM-7(8) enhancement guidance says you must assess executable code from sources with limited or no warranty and/or without accompanying source code, because this code can be hard to review, fix, or improve and may not have a clear owner responsible for making repairs. For open-source, the assessment must also cover the lack of warranty or support and the risk of hidden access (back doors) or malware. Any exception must be formally approved and recorded.

###### HOW (specific steps/approach)
1. **Set policy thresholds and required evidence** for:
   - what counts as **“limited or no warranty”** (for example: no security support service level agreement, no indemnity, or warranty period shorter than an organisation-defined threshold), and
   - what counts as **“without source code”** (for example: no offer to provide source, no escrow, no evidence of a build that can be reproduced, or no contractual right to obtain source code).
2. **Create and maintain an auditable inventory** of all binary or machine-executable code used in application environments (development, testing, production, and disaster recovery), including:
   - third-party commercial binaries, container images, libraries, and firmware packages,
   - open-source components (including components they depend on).
   Use **software bill of materials (SBOMs)** (for example, SPDX or CycloneDX). Where an SBOM is not practical (for example, for certain firmware), use an equivalent component inventory.
3. **Add supplier and evidence details** to each SBOM or component record for:
   - warranty and support terms (from the contract, vendor security policy, or support agreement), and
   - source-code availability (from the vendor statement, licence terms, escrow agreement, or reproducible-build evidence).
4. **Apply a default “deny” rule** in the release and deployment process, so that components that meet the prohibited criteria (limited or no warranty and/or no source-code availability) **cannot be moved into production** unless an **approved exception ID** is attached to the release.
5. **Set up an exception process** for any prohibited component:
   - require a **clear operational or mission need** reason,
   - require a **security impact assessment** that explicitly covers how difficult it will be to review, fix, or extend the code, and **who is responsible for repairs**,
   - for open-source, require assessment of the **lack of warranty/support** and the risk of **back doors or malware**,
   - require approval by an **authorising official** (named role) and store the approval decision and assessment documents in an auditable repository.
6. **Produce audit-ready evidence automatically**:
   - SBOMs for each release,
   - deployment pipeline logs showing pass, deny, and exception links,
   - exception records (assessment plus approval) kept according to the organisation’s retention rules.

###### WHO (role responsible)
- **Application Owner / Service Owner**: responsible for ensuring only approved releases are deployed and that exceptions are requested with operational justification.
- **Security Engineering / Application Security (AppSec)**: carries out or checks the security impact assessment for prohibited components.
- **Procurement / Vendor Management**: provides evidence of warranty/support and source-code availability during supplier onboarding.
- **Authorising Official (named)**: approves exceptions after reviewing the assessment evidence.
- **Release Manager / DevOps Lead**: implements pipeline controls and ensures SBOM or equivalent manifest generation is mandatory.

###### Acceptance criteria
- For every production release, an SBOM (or equivalent manifest) exists and can be traced to what was actually deployed.
- Each SBOM entry includes supplier origin, warranty/support evidence, and source-code availability evidence.
- Moving a release into production is blocked for components meeting the prohibited criteria unless an approved exception ID is present.
- For any exception, there is a complete security and practical impact assessment (including repair responsibility) and a recorded approval by the authorising official.
- Audit logs show the controls were enforced (gate decisions) and that releases are linked to the relevant exception records.

###### Actions Required for Compliance

- [ ] Set clear thresholds for when software is classified as having “limited or no warranty” and when it is “without source code”, and publish these thresholds as enforceable policy.
- [ ] Choose a software bill of materials (SBOM) format—either SPDX or CycloneDX—and make sure an SBOM (or manifest) is automatically produced for every build output and every procurement item.
- [ ] Set up a software bill of materials (SBOM) enrichment process to record, for every component: where the supplier is based, evidence of warranty and support, and evidence that the source code is available.
- [ ] Set up continuous integration and continuous delivery (CI/CD) release pipeline rules that automatically block the promotion of prohibited components unless an approved exception reference number is attached.
- [ ] Set up an exception process that is only allowed when there is a strong operational reason. Each request must include a security and practical impact assessment, including who is responsible for owning and repairing the issue. The exception must then be approved by an authorised official.
- [ ] Store software bill of materials (SBOMs), gate logs, and exception records in an auditable repository, with retention periods aligned to organisational requirements.

#### CM-7.9 — Prohibiting The Use of Unauthorized Hardware (Enhancement)

This requirement is about ensuring that only approved equipment can be connected to, and used on, your systems. This prevents unknown or unapproved hardware from quietly introducing risk. Without it, someone could connect a rogue device, counterfeit or faulty equipment, or unauthorised industrial control equipment. That could then be used to disrupt services, steal information, or create a hidden route into your organisation.

To meet this requirement, the organisation must keep a current inventory of all approved devices. This inventory must include serial numbers and cover all endpoints, servers, network devices, operational technology (OT) controllers, and approved peripherals. For each item, it must record the vendor and model. The organisation must also block any hardware that is not on the approved list from being used or connected, including for endpoints, servers, network devices, OT controllers, and peripherals.

Finally, the approved hardware list must be reviewed and updated at least every quarter to keep it accurate as equipment is added, replaced, or retired.

##### Authorised hardware allowlist with serial/vendor enforcement

- Category: Software
- Priority: Critical

###### WHY (control requirement)
CM-7(9) enhancement requires stopping unauthorised hardware from being used by making sure only approved hardware can be installed or connected to organisational systems. This must be supported by a current inventory and controlled permission to add new hardware.

###### HOW (specific steps/approach)
1. **Create and maintain an authoritative approved hardware inventory** that, at minimum, records the vendor, model, and a unique identifier (serial number or equivalent) for each approved item. 2. **Bring together inventory sources** (procurement/asset management/configuration management database (CMDB), endpoint management, network device management, and OT asset registers where applicable) to fill in vendor/model/serial details and to identify “drift” (items found in the environment that are not on the approved list).

4. **Put the “no unauthorised hardware” rule into day-to-day operation** using a controlled exception process: any request to add hardware not currently on the approved list must go through change management, be checked (vendor/model/serial), and only then be approved for the relevant scope.
5. **Review and update quarterly:** run a quarterly process that (a) refreshes the approved list from authoritative sources, (b) removes items that are retired or returned, (c) updates the inputs used for enforcement, and (d) produces evidence that the review and updates were completed.

- Any device not on the approved list is blocked or quarantined from the relevant network segments/zones (IT and OT where enforcement points exist).
- New hardware cannot be added into production management and enforcement scope without a verified inventory record.
- Quarterly review evidence exists showing approved list updates and enforcement rule refreshes.
- Drift detection generates useful, actionable alerts for devices seen on networks/zones that do not match the approved list.

###### Actions Required for Compliance

### CM-8 — System Component Inventory (Control)

This requirement is about keeping a complete, up-to-date written record of every part that makes up each system, so you always know what you have and where it is used. Without this, organisations can lose track of hardware and software, accidentally use outdated or incorrectly licensed items, and find it hard to assign responsibility when something goes wrong—because the same component may be recorded twice or linked to the wrong system.

The organisation must create and maintain an inventory for each system that matches what is actually in use. It must include all hardware, software, and firmware, and record each component only once using a unique identifier and a clear link to the correct system.

For every component, the inventory must record:
- system name  
- component unique identifier  
- software owners  
- software version  
- hardware specifications  
- licence information  
- model, serial number, manufacturer, and supplier  
- component type  
- physical location  
- receipt date  
- cost  
- where relevant, the networked machine names and Internet Protocol (IP) addresses for both Internet Protocol version 4 (IPv4) and Internet Protocol version 6 (IPv6) for each protocol used  

It must also include a system association that prevents duplicate recording.

The inventory must be reviewed at least every quarter and updated within 10 business days of any component change, new system being brought into use, or system being decommissioned. Records must be kept showing when each review and update was completed.

##### Authoritative CMDB inventory with system-scoped unique IDs

- Category: Software
- Priority: Critical

###### WHAT
Set up and maintain an official, documented list of all system components in the application’s IT environment (hardware, software, and firmware). The list must be accurate, avoid duplicates, and cover only the correct system(s). Each component must have a **unique identifier** and be linked to the correct **system name** (system association) so the same component is not counted more than once.

###### WHY (control requirement)
Control CM-8 requires organisations to keep an inventory of system components so they can be held accountable. The guidance says effective accountability needs details such as **system name, component unique identifier, software owners, software version numbers, hardware inventory specifications, software licence information, and for networked components, machine names and network addresses across all implemented protocols (for example, IPv4 and IPv6)**. It also requires preventing **duplicate accounting** by using a **unique identifier** and a **system association**.

###### HOW (specific steps/approach)
1. **Define the CMDB data structure and required fields** for this application’s scope, aligned to CM-8 guidance: system name, component unique identifier, software owners, software version, hardware details (date received, cost, model, serial number, manufacturer, supplier, component type, physical location), licence information, and for networked components the machine names and IP addresses for each protocol (IPv4 and IPv6 where used).
2. **Set a unique identifier approach** and use it to remove duplicates:
   - Hardware/firmware: use a consistent identifier based on manufacturer + model + serial number (or an equivalent asset identity).
   - Software: use package identity (for example, vendor + product + version) and, where available, a signed file fingerprint (hash) to tell different builds apart.
   - Ensure there is **one authoritative record per unique identifier** in the CMDB.
3. **Apply system association rules** so each component instance is linked to exactly one system name for accountability. If software is installed or managed at the system level across multiple systems, record it as separate instances for each system association (even if the same software appears multiple times in a central inventory) to avoid duplicate accounting.
4. **Automate population and reconciliation**:
   - Use existing enterprise discovery/inventory sources (for example, endpoint/server inventory, cloud workload inventory application programming interfaces, and network discovery/IP address management integration) to collect component details.
   - Match discovered results into the CMDB using the unique identifier approach and system association rules.
   - Make sure networked components capture **machine names and IP addresses for each protocol** (IPv4 and IPv6) so duplicates do not appear across different address ranges.
5. **Add validation and audit-ready evidence**:
   - Add automated checks that stop CMDB updates that would create duplicate unique identifiers or leave required accountability fields blank.
   - Record review/update dates and keep evidence of the quarterly review and any changes triggered by updates.
6. **Run the review/update process on a set timetable**:
   - Complete a **quarterly** review of the application’s system component inventory.
   - Update the inventory within **10 business days** of any component change, new system onboarding, or decommissioning, with evidence recorded in the CMDB workflow.

###### WHO (role responsible)
- **CMDB/Configuration Manager**: owns the CMDB structure, unique identifier rules, and reconciliation processes.
- **Application Owner / Service Owner**: provides/approves software owner mappings and confirms system association accuracy.
- **Infrastructure/Platform Engineering**: ensures discovery integrations and network/IP address management data feeds are available for IPv4 and IPv6.
- **IT Asset Management (ITAM)**: provides hardware receipt, cost, model/serial/manufacturer/supplier, and physical location details.

###### Acceptance criteria
- The CMDB holds an inventory for the application’s defined system scope with **all required CM-8 accountability fields** completed for each component.
- Each component uses a **unique identifier** and is linked to the correct **system name**; duplicate accounting is prevented (no duplicate unique identifiers for the same component in the authoritative record).
- Networked components include **machine names and IP addresses for each implemented protocol** (IPv4 and IPv6 where applicable).
- A **quarterly review** is completed and evidenced.
- Any component change/onboarding/decommissioning results in CMDB updates within **10 business days**, evidenced by workflow logs.

###### Actions Required for Compliance

- [ ] Define the configuration management database (CMDB) structure and the required CM-8 fields for the application’s system scope.
- [ ] Put in place and require a consistent, unique identification method for each hardware, firmware, and software component.
- [ ] Set up system association rules so that every component instance is linked to exactly one system name, to prevent duplicate accounting.

#### CM-8.1 — Updates During Installation and Removal (Enhancement)

This requirement is about keeping an accurate, up-to-date list of the organisation’s computer equipment and software. This list must be updated every time anything changes. #### CM-8.2 — Automated Maintenance (Enhancement)

This requirement is about keeping a reliable, always-available record of the computers and other key technology your organisation uses, and making sure that record is up to date, complete, accurate, and easy to access. Without this, you risk not knowing what you have, missing hidden or unused virtual machines, using incorrect information during changes, or being unable to restore your technology records after an outage—leading to delays, mistakes, and avoidable disruption.

To meet this expectation, the organisation must use automated processes to update the inventory every day by scanning on-premises systems and applying updates based on changes. It must also reconcile the inventory every week by comparing what is found in the virtualisation platform, the network, and endpoint devices (including identifying dormant virtual machines) and correcting any gaps.

The organisation must also automatically verify that key details match trusted records using tamper-evident reports, and keep the inventory available by using redundant storage, automated backups, monitored replication, and alerts at all times if any part fails.

##### Automated CMDB inventory reconciliation with daily discovery and weekly audits

- Category: Software
- Priority: Critical

###### WHAT
Set up automated processes to keep the system component inventory up to date, complete, correct, and accessible. This will be done through **automated inventory checks using configuration database (CMDB) discovery, configuration baselines, and scheduled reviews for all on-premises assets**.

###### WHY (control requirement)
NIST SP 800-53 **CM-8(2) Automated Maintenance** requires the organisation to “maintain the currency, completeness, accuracy, and availability of the inventory of system components using {{ insert: param, cm-8.2_prm_1 }}.” This requirement expects automated processes that keep the inventory current, reconcile it for completeness (including inactive virtual machines), confirm it is accurate, and ensure the inventory remains available.

###### HOW (specific steps/approach)
1. **Define the automated check coverage and inventory structure**: Set up the configuration database (CMDB) categories (configuration items, or CIs) and the key details needed for all on-premises assets (for example: hypervisor hosts, virtual machines, endpoints, and network devices). Also define what counts as “all on-premises assets”, including inactive or paused virtual machines.
2. **Daily updates (automated mechanisms)**: Run **daily CMDB discovery scans** that update the CMDB with any changes (deltas). Use the appropriate collection methods for the environment (for example: hypervisor application programming interfaces (APIs), endpoint management inventory, and network information such as DHCP (Dynamic Host Configuration Protocol), DNS (Domain Name System), and ARP (Address Resolution Protocol) where feasible). 4. **Weekly completeness check, including inactive virtual machines**: Do a **weekly reconciliation** that compares the CMDB against the most reliable inventories from the hypervisor, network, and endpoints. Include **inactive virtual machine detection** (for example: virtual machines that are powered off/not running and/or not seen by network or endpoint monitoring for a defined period). Produce a reconciliation report listing differences and inactive virtual machine candidates, with the required outcome (add/update/exception).
5. **Automated accuracy checks**: Automatically verify key component details using **signed reports from agents** and mapping rules that identify the correct source of truth. Only update the CMDB when signature checks and mapping rules both succeed. If they do not, send the item to an exception process.
6. **Ensure the inventory stays available**: Keep the CMDB available using **redundant CMDB services**, **automated backups**, and **monitored replication**. Provide **24x7 alerting** for CMDB service health, replication delays, and backup failures. Make sure the reconciliation process stops safely (for example: queue changes for later processing) rather than silently discarding updates.

- **CMDB / Configuration Management Lead**: owns the CI structure, the scope of the reconciliation, and the rules for handling discrepancies/inactive virtual machines.
- **Infrastructure / Platform Engineering**: builds the discovery collection methods, integrates hypervisor and network data, and implements baseline-triggered CMDB update workflows.
- **Security Engineering**: defines how signed attribute validation works and what the exception handling requirements are.
- **IT Operations / Site Reliability Engineering (SRE)**: ensures CMDB redundancy, backups, replication monitoring, and **24x7 alerting**.

###### Acceptance criteria (verifiable)
- Daily discovery runs complete successfully for the defined on-premises asset scope, producing time-stamped logs and CMDB delta evidence.

#### CM-8.3 — Automated Unauthorized Component Detection (Enhancement)

This requirement is about automatically checking every day whether anything unapproved has been added to your systems. This includes physical parts, installed programmes, and device firmware. The checks compare what is currently there against approved lists and confirm that nothing has been changed.

Without this, unauthorised or altered components could be used quietly to steal data, disrupt services, or give attackers a foothold that is difficult to notice and even harder to stop.

To meet this expectation, the organisation must run daily automated checks for hardware, software, and firmware, using trusted approved lists and integrity checks. It must also be able to clearly identify exactly what is unauthorised and explain why.

If unauthorised items are found, the organisation must immediately disable their access to the network and isolate or quarantine the affected component so it cannot operate normally. The organisation must also notify the Chief Information Security Officer (CISO).

The organisation must document how this is done, keep an up-to-date inventory and records of changes, and retain evidence of alerts, monitoring activity, maintenance actions, and audits. It must also define clear responsibilities for running the checks and handling the response.

##### Daily unauthorised component detection with signed allowlists

- Category: Software
- Priority: Critical

###### WHAT
Set up automated checks at least once every day to detect unauthorised **hardware, software, and firmware**. This is done by comparing what is found on devices against **signed approved lists (allowlists)** and confirming **integrity** using hash values, signatures, and/or attestations.

###### WHY (control requirement)
CM-8(3) enhancement requires automated ways to detect unauthorised system components. This must be done in addition to existing monitoring for unauthorised remote connections and mobile devices. The guidance allows checks to be continuous or periodic (here: **daily**). It also asks organisations to consider whether the approach depends on agent or supplicant support. It notes that isolation or quarantine (sandboxing) can be used to limit the impact of unauthorised components.

###### HOW (specific steps/approach)
1. **Create and sign the approved lists (allowlists)** for:
   - **Hardware identifiers** (for example, device model, serial number, trusted platform module (TPM), and asset tags), using a signed manifest.
   - **Software packages** (name, version, and build), using signed software bill of materials (SBOM) or manifests, plus package signature validation.
   - **Firmware versions**, using a signed firmware version allowlist.
2. **Run daily automated inventory and integrity checks** across managed endpoints/servers and managed devices:
   - **Hardware**: use an agent-based endpoint inventory to collect identifiers and compare them with the signed hardware allowlist (including hash or identifier integrity checks where supported).
   - **Software**: collect software package inventory continuously or on a daily schedule; validate package signatures and compare results against policy-based allowlisting derived from the signed SBOM/manifest.
   - **Firmware**: use device management (for example, mobile device management (MDM)) queries to retrieve reported firmware versions and compare them with the signed firmware allowlist.
3. **Create detection alerts with clear reason codes** (for example, “hash mismatch”, “signature invalid”, “not in allowlist”, “firmware version not approved”). Attach the supporting evidence, including observed identifiers, the allowlist version, and the integrity/attestation results.
4. **Automatically contain (isolate/quarantine) each detection**:
   - Immediately block or deny network access for the affected component/device (for example, move it to a quarantine network segment such as a quarantine virtual local area network (VLAN) and apply deny-by-default firewall rules and zero trust network access (ZTNA) policy).
   - If network isolation is not possible, isolate at the closest practical boundary (for example, a dedicated subnet or domain) consistent with the guidance.
5. **Notify the chief information security officer (CISO)** for unauthorised detections and create an auditable incident/change record that links:
   - the detection alert, evidence, allowlist version, and containment actions taken.
6. **Keep audit-ready evidence**:
   - store daily scan results, allowlist signing and verification logs, containment action logs, and notification records; keep them according to the organisation’s retention policy.

###### WHO
- **Security Engineering / security operations centre (SOC) Engineering**: build the detection process, evidence creation, and containment playbooks.
- **Asset/Endpoint Management Team**: maintain coverage for the agent and mobile device management (MDM) inventory, and ensure firmware query capability.
- **CISO (or delegated security governance)**: receive notifications and approve the next steps for handling the issue.
- **Configuration Management / Change Control Owner**: manage signing and controlled updates to the allowlists.

###### Acceptance criteria
- Daily automated checks run successfully for hardware, software, and firmware across the defined managed scope.
- For each unauthorised finding, the system records: the observed component details, allowlist match status, integrity/attestation result, and the specific reason code.
- Unauthorised components trigger automated containment that blocks network access and/or quarantines the affected device/component within minutes of detection.
- The CISO is notified for each unauthorised detection, with auditable evidence attached.
- Allowlists are signed, and the detection process verifies signatures before using allowlists.

###### Actions Required for Compliance

- [ ] Create signed allowlists of approved hardware identifiers, software software bill of materials (SBOM) and package versions, and firmware versions, with controlled change management.
- [ ] Set up and activate an agent on endpoints within the agreed managed area to collect an inventory of both hardware and software.
- [ ] Set up daily scheduled inventory checks and integrity validation: verify hardware using hash or identifier checks, and verify software using package signature checks.
- [ ] Enable device management (MDM) firmware attestation checks, and compare the firmware versions reported by devices against the signed approved firmware list (allowlist).
- [ ] Set up detection rules that record auditable events with clear reason codes for: a hash mismatch, an invalid digital signature, and a failure to match the approved allowlist.
- [ ] Automate response plans to isolate the affected device or component and block its network access immediately when detected
- [ ] Set up a notification process to alert the Chief Information Security Officer (CISO) every time an unauthorised threat is detected, and keep the supporting records (scan results, the current version of the allowlist, and any containment actions taken).

#### CM-8.4 — Accountability Information (Enhancement)

This requirement is about making sure every important piece of technology has a clearly identified person who is responsible and can be held accountable for looking after it. Without this, issues can be missed—for example, when systems need updating, when a problem is traced to a specific part, or when equipment must be recalled, replaced, or moved—because there is no clear person to contact or hold responsible.

To meet this expectation, the organisation must keep an up-to-date record of its system components. For each component, it must record the accountable administrator using the organisation’s defined fields: the person’s name, their job title (position), and their role. The organisation should also have the right internal policies and working procedures to ensure these details are recorded consistently and updated whenever responsibilities change, so the inventory always shows who to contact for each component.

- Priority: High

- **Application/Infrastructure Service Owner**: nominates accountable administrators for each component.
- **Identity and Access Management (IAM)/HR Integration Owner**: implements and maintains HR/IAM-driven updates.
- **Information Security / Compliance**: reviews the approved role list and monitors the completeness checks.

###### Acceptance criteria

- There are **no** inventory records with missing/blank accountable-administrator fields or invalid role values (for example, “TBC/Unknown”).

- Completeness checks run on schedule and produce clear, actionable remediation outputs for any gaps found.

- [ ] Set up a controlled list (taxonomy) of allowed values for the “role” field, and enforce checks when records are saved to prevent blank, placeholder, or unknown values.
- [ ] Make sure every accountable administrator record is linked to the official component identifier used in the inventory.

#### CM-8.6 — Assessed Configurations and Approved Deviations (Enhancement)

This requirement is about making sure your organisation’s master list of key technology parts is accurate and current. It should show both (1) which settings have been formally checked and (2) any officially approved exceptions that are currently being used. Without this, teams may believe systems are set up safely when they are not, or they may overlook changes that were allowed—raising the risk that weaknesses are introduced or left unaddressed.

To meet this expectation, the organisation must keep an up-to-date inventory of system components that clearly identifies:
- which specific components were assessed
- the exact configuration settings that were checked (with references to the supporting assessment information)
- any authorised deviations from the required settings, linked to the affected deployed component and the approval evidence

The inventory must match what is actually deployed today, not outdated information or exceptions that have been withdrawn.

##### CM inventory entries for assessed configs and in-effect deviations

- Category: Software
- Priority: Critical

###### WHAT
Update the system component inventory so it clearly includes:
1) **Assessed component configurations** (the exact configuration settings that were checked, the specific deployed components that were checked, and references to the proof used), and
2) **Approved deviations** that are currently in effect (what is different from the agreed configuration settings, which deployed components are affected, and the proof of approval).

###### WHY (control requirement)
The National Institute of Standards and Technology (NIST) **CM-8(6)** requires that the system component inventory **includes assessed component configurations and any approved deviations to the current deployed configurations**. This ensures the inventory shows the **current deployed state**, not outdated baselines or forgotten exceptions.

###### HOW (specific approach)
Set up an inventory data model and working process that treats “assessed configurations” and “approved deviations” as key, auditable records linked to deployed components.

1. **Define inventory record types**
   - Create or update inventory objects for:
     - **AssessedConfiguration**: (deployed component ID, configuration item/setting name, baseline/required rule identifier, assessed result or rule outcome, date assessed, reference to assessment evidence).
     - **ApprovedDeviation**: (deployed component ID, affected configuration item(s), deviation description, permitted alternative value/rule, approval reference, approval date, status = *in-effect*).

2. **Link records to deployed components using stable identifiers**
   - Make sure each record points to the same **deployed component identifier** used elsewhere in the inventory (for example: service instance ID, host/cluster ID, application component ID, environment tag such as production/testing).
   - Standardise identifiers so assessment outputs map consistently to inventory components.

3. **Capture assessment results with evidence references**
   - For each assessed configuration item, record:
     - the **baseline/standard identifier** that was assessed,
     - the **assessment outcome** (pass/fail/exception) and/or the assessed value,
     - a **traceable evidence reference** (for example: scanner report ID, assessment run ID, report link, timestamp).

4. **Record deviations only when formally approved, and mark them in-effect**
   - When a deviation is approved, create an **ApprovedDeviation** record that:
     - clearly states what differs from the required configuration,
     - lists the affected configuration item(s),
     - includes the **approval reference** (change/ticket ID, approver, date, and any linked approval document reference),
     - sets **status = in-effect**.

5. **Check the inventory against the current deployed configuration**
   - Set up a regular reconciliation job/process that re-checks the current configuration against:
     - the baseline required settings, and
     - the set of deviations marked *in-effect*.
   - If a deviation no longer matches the current deployed configuration, automatically flag it for review and set status to *not in-effect* (or require explicit re-approval).

6. **Add checks to ensure audit-ready completeness**
   - Add validation rules to prevent incomplete “orphan” records, such as:
     - assessed configuration entries missing evidence references,
     - approved deviation entries missing approval references,
     - deviations that reference configuration items not included in the baseline mapping.

###### WHO
- **Configuration Manager / Configuration Management Database (CMDB) Owner**: defines the inventory structure, record lifecycle, and reconciliation approach.
- **Security Engineering / Application Security (AppSec)**: maintains the mappings from baselines to configuration items and the evidence requirements for assessments.
- **Platform/Infrastructure Engineering** (or Operational Technology (OT)/automation team where relevant): ensures deployed component identifiers and configuration data sources integrate with the inventory.
- **Change Management / Change Advisory Board (CAB)** (or equivalent approvers): provides approval references for deviations.

###### Acceptance criteria
- For each deployed component in scope, the inventory includes **AssessedConfiguration** entries that identify:
  - the specific configuration settings assessed,
  - the specific deployed component assessed,
  - the baseline/required configuration identifier,
  - and a traceable evidence reference.
- For each configuration difference that is currently permitted, the inventory includes an **ApprovedDeviation** entry that:
  - is linked to the affected deployed component,
  - clearly describes the deviation from the required configuration,
  - includes an approval reference,
  - and is marked **status = in-effect**.
- Reconciliation shows that the inventory’s *in-effect* deviations and assessed configuration records match the **current deployed state** (no outdated deviations remain without re-approval).

###### Actions Required for Compliance

- [ ] Define the inventory data fields (schema objects) for **AssessedConfiguration** and **ApprovedDeviation**. Each must include the required information: **component ID**, **configuration item**, **baseline identifier**, **evidence and approval references**, and whether it is **currently in effect**.
- [ ] Create a consistent, repeatable way to match the results from assessments (scanner or checker reports) to the identifiers of the components that have been deployed and recorded in the inventory.
- [ ] Automate the upload, or use a controlled process to enter, assessment results into **AssessedConfiguration** records, with traceable references to the supporting evidence.
- [ ] Set up a process to record only deviations that have been formally approved. Store each one as an **ApprovedDeviation** record, linked to the affected deployed components and the approval evidence (approval artefacts).
- [ ] Run a reconciliation check that compares the current configuration against the agreed baseline settings and identifies any differences that are currently in effect. Highlight any outdated differences so they can be reviewed.
- [ ] Add audit checks to prevent records being accepted if they do not include evidence references (for assessed configurations) or approval references (for deviations).

This expectation is about keeping one single, central record of the organisation’s key building blocks—hardware, software, and firmware—so staff can quickly see what exists, where it is, and who is responsible for it. #### CM-8.8 — Automated Location Tracking (Enhancement)

This requirement is about automatically keeping track of where important company equipment and systems are located. It uses automated location-finding tools to record the site, building, and room where each item is connected, and then links that information to the organisation’s equipment records.  

This matters because if something goes wrong—such as a device being compromised or accessed without permission—the organisation needs to quickly identify the exact location of the affected component and the person responsible for it. This helps the organisation act fast and reduce damage.  

To meet this expectation, the organisation must use automated asset-tracking agents that update its central equipment records with location details. It must determine location based on where the device connects on the network, and keep the location information up to date.  

The organisation must also be able to use the location information to identify the responsible individuals and support timely mitigation. It must coordinate with senior leadership on any privacy implications and document the decisions made.

This enhancement specifically expects components to be assigned to systems and for those assignments to be formally acknowledged.

###### HOW (specific steps/approach)
1. **Define the authoritative inventory (system of record):** Create and maintain a configuration inventory (for example, a configuration management database (CMDB) or equivalent) that, at minimum, includes:
   - `component_id`
   - `component_type`
   - `assigned_system` (a controlled list of named systems)
2. **Make assignment mandatory and validate it:** Apply validation rules so `assigned_system` cannot be blank or unknown, and so components cannot be present in the inventory without a valid system assignment.
3. **Use a controlled workflow for assignments and moves:** For any new component onboarding, or any move of a component between systems, use a workflow step that records the target `assigned_system` against the exact `component_id`.
4. **Require two sign-offs for each assignment:** In the workflow, collect explicit acknowledgements from:
   - the **CM Officer** (as defined by the organisation’s roles/personnel), and
   - the **system owner** for the target system.
   Store the sign-off evidence in the same auditable record as the assignment (for example, the change or ticket record linked to the inventory entry).
5. **Record evidence with traceability:** Link the inventory entry and the workflow record so auditors can trace:
   component → assigned system → date/time → CM Officer sign-off → system owner sign-off.
6. **Run reconciliation checks:** Regularly check the inventory for any components with missing or invalid `assigned_system` values, and for any open remediation work items that also require the same two sign-off process.

###### WHO (role responsible)
- **CM Officer:** owns the configuration management process, checks that sign-offs are captured correctly, and approves assignments in the workflow.
- **System Owner:** confirms that the component-to-system assignment is correct for the target system.
- **Configuration Management / IT service management (ITSM) Process Owner** (or equivalent): implements and maintains the workflow, validation rules, and reconciliation reporting.

###### Acceptance criteria
- Every component in the authoritative inventory has a non-empty, valid `assigned_system` value.
- For each component assignment (including onboarding and moves), the auditable record includes explicit sign-offs from both the CM Officer and the relevant system owner.
- Evidence is traceable end-to-end: the `component_id` and `assigned_system` in the inventory match the workflow/change record containing both sign-offs.
- Reconciliation reports show zero components with missing or invalid assignments after remediation is completed.

###### Actions Required for Compliance

- [ ] Create or update the official configuration inventory, ensuring it includes the required fields for component ID and the assigned system (from the approved controlled system list).
- [ ] Set up workflow enforcement so that component onboarding and component moves cannot be completed unless a valid assigned system value is provided.
- [ ] Set up the assignment process so that, for every component assignment, the Chief Maintenance (CM) Officer and the owner of the target system must give explicit approval or acknowledgement.
- [ ] Link workflow and change records to the relevant inventory entries so that proof of acknowledgement is stored and can be traced for each component-to-system assignment.
- [ ] Add automated checks to prevent blank or unknown assignments, and to highlight invalid or duplicate assignment situations.
- [ ] Run regular checks to find any components that are unassigned or invalid, and raise remediation tickets that must follow the same two-step approval process.

### CM-9 — Configuration Management Plan (Control)

This expectation is about making sure your organisation has a clear, written plan for how it manages and controls changes to the parts that make up a system. This helps ensure that nothing important is changed by mistake or without proper checks. Without this, the wrong version of software or settings could be used, key documents could become out of date, and changes could be made informally—leaving you unable to explain what changed, when it changed, and who approved it.

To meet this expectation, the organisation must create and document a system-specific configuration management plan. The plan must clearly set out:
- who is responsible for what
- how configuration items are identified across the system’s life cycle
- which items are included (for example, hardware, software, and key documents)
- how changes to those items are recorded and controlled

The plan must be reviewed and approved by the Authorising Official and the Configuration Management Lead. It must also be kept secure so only authorised people can view it or make changes, and the organisation must follow the plan in practice.

##### System CM plan with AO/CM Lead approval and workflow-controlled baselines

- Category: Manual
- Priority: Critical

###### WHAT
Create, document, and run a system-specific configuration management (CM) plan. This plan must set out who does what, what counts as a configuration item (hardware, software, firmware, and key documents), and the end-to-end process for moving changes through change management. It must also cover how configuration settings/baselines are updated, how component records are kept up to date, and how development, testing, and live (operational) environments are controlled.

###### WHY (control requirement)
CM-9 requires the organisation to **develop, document, and implement** a CM plan for the system. This includes processes and procedures that support activities across the system development life cycle, and explains how changes are progressed through change management. It must also describe how baselines and inventories are updated, and how configuration items are controlled. The plan must follow the organisation’s CM policies and be tailored to the system.

###### HOW (specific approach)
1. **Create the system CM Plan** using the organisation’s CM plan template, tailoring it to this application’s design and environments (development, test, production, and disaster recovery). Clearly list the **types of configuration items** included (for example: application components, infrastructure components, operating system images, container images, third-party dependencies, and **key documents** such as runbooks and architecture diagrams).
2. **Define roles and responsibilities** in the plan, including the **Authorising Official (AO)** and the **Configuration Management Lead** as the named reviewers/approvers (as defined by the organisation). Where relevant, include implementers, the configuration librarian, the change manager, and security and privacy impact analysts.
3. **Use a workflow-based change process** that requires every change to be linked to configuration item identifiers and a target baseline/version. Use the organisation’s main ticketing and change system as the single source of record for change requests and approvals.
4. **Control baselines and inventories** by updating a configuration item register and environment baseline records as part of the workflow (for example: link change tickets to continuous integration/continuous delivery (CI/CD) release outputs and record the resulting version/baseline reference). Ensure the plan explains how baselines are created, reviewed, approved, and replaced.
5. **Control access to the CM Plan itself** by storing it in a controlled document repository with the minimum necessary permissions and audit logging. Limit editing rights to the Configuration Management Lead and document controllers; give read access to other stakeholders as needed.
6. **Apply approval checkpoints** in the workflow so that the AO and/or the Configuration Management Lead must approve changes before they are implemented. Also ensure the plan states how security and privacy impact analysis evidence is captured when required by organisational policy.
7. **Maintain audit-ready traceability**: for every approved change, keep evidence of the approvals, the configuration items affected, the baseline/version updated, and the verification/validation results relevant to the change.

###### WHO
- **Configuration Management Lead**: owns the CM Plan content, maintains the configuration item register and baselines, and controls edits to the plan.
- **Authorising Official (AO)**: reviews and approves the system CM Plan and authorises changes and baseline updates as required by organisational policy.
- **Change Manager / Release Manager**: ensures the workflow is followed and that changes cannot bypass CM approval checkpoints.
- **Security/Privacy Impact Analyst (where required)**: provides the impact analysis evidence referenced by the workflow.

###### Acceptance criteria
- A **system-specific CM Plan** exists, is version-controlled, and is stored in a controlled repository.
- The CM Plan clearly defines **roles** including the AO and Configuration Management Lead, and sets out processes for identifying configuration items and controlling changes across development, test, and operational environments.
- The CM Plan defines the configuration items in scope (hardware/software/firmware and key documents) and explains how inventories and baselines are maintained and updated.
- The change workflow requires mapping to configuration item IDs and requires AO/CM Lead approval before implementation.
- Audit evidence exists showing that approved changes update baselines and inventories, and that the CM Plan is protected from unauthorised changes.

###### Actions Required for Compliance

- [ ] Create the application’s system-specific configuration management (CM) plan using the organisation’s template, and tailor it to the application’s environments and the configuration item types included in scope.
- [ ] Define and document the roles for configuration management (CM) and who is responsible for approvals. Clearly name the Authorising Official (AO) and the Configuration Management Lead.
- [ ] Publish the configuration management plan (CM Plan) in a controlled repository where only authorised users can edit it (least-privilege access), and where all changes are recorded in an audit log.
- [ ] Set up a process where every change request must include the configuration item ID(s) and the baseline or version that needs to be updated.
- [ ] Set up approval gates so the Authorising Officer (AO) and/or the Configuration Management Lead must approve any proposed changes before they are implemented.
- [ ] Create and keep a record of configuration items and their baseline settings, and update these records as part of the approved change process.
- [ ] Check that changes are traceable for audit purposes by reviewing a sample of recent updates to confirm that approvals were recorded, the affected configuration items were identified, and any baseline or inventory updates were logged.

#### CM-9.1 — Assignment of Responsibility (Enhancement)

This requirement is about ensuring that the rules for managing and tracking system changes are written by people who are not the same people who build or integrate the system. If this separation is not in place, the people making the changes could also influence the rules that control those changes. That would weaken quality checks and oversight, increase the risk of errors, and make unauthorised or poorly controlled changes more likely, while also leading to inconsistent decisions.  

To meet this expectation, the organisation must clearly assign responsibility for developing its documented approach for controlling and tracking system changes to staff who are not directly involved in system development or system integration. The organisation should name the responsible roles or individuals in its key documents and supporting records. It must also be able to demonstrate that these people are independent from the teams that build or integrate the system, including in practice by showing they own the related process-development activities.

##### Independent CM Process Owner for CM process development

- Category: Manual
- Priority: High

###### WHAT
Assign responsibility for developing the organisation’s configuration management (CM) process to organisational personnel who are **not directly involved** in system development or system integration. Record the assigned responsibilities (roles/named individuals) in the CM governance documents.

###### WHY (control requirement)
CM-9(1) enhancement requires that responsibility for developing the CM process is given to people who are independent of system development and system integration. This separation of duties supports quality control and effective oversight.

###### HOW (specific steps/approach)
1. **Create or update a CM governance pack** that includes the CM process development procedure and a responsibility matrix (for example, a RACI) covering at least: CM process design, CM tool/workflow configuration, baseline/branching rules, and CM workflow/approval logic.
2. **Appoint a named “CM Process Owner/Lead”** (role and/or individuals) from a non-development/non-integration function (for example, Governance/Quality/Compliance/Information Security). Ensure this person(s) is accountable for writing and maintaining the CM process.
3. **Define “direct involvement” boundaries** in the CM process development procedure (for example, the CM Process Owner must not be the same person(s) who make application code changes, own integration engineering, or write/review the target system build/release pipeline for the in-scope application).
4. **Set separation of duties in the RACI** so that developers/integrators are only **Consulted/Informed** for CM process development, while the CM Process Owner is **Responsible/Accountable** for the CM process content and updates.
5. **Keep evidence that can be audited**: version history of CM process documents/workflow definitions, approval records showing CM Process Owner sign-off, and periodic review records confirming independence continues.

###### WHO
- **CM Process Owner/Lead** (appointed individual/role) responsible for developing and maintaining the CM process.
- **Governance/Compliance/Information Security** function (to appoint and oversee independence).
- **Application development and integration leads** (to be consulted/informed only for CM process development).

###### Acceptance criteria
- CM governance documentation clearly names the personnel/role responsible for **developing** the CM process.
- A documented definition of “direct involvement” exists and is reflected in the RACI/responsibility matrix.
- Evidence shows CM process changes are written and approved by the independent CM Process Owner (for example, signed approvals and document/workflow version history).
- Periodic review evidence shows the CM Process Owner remains independent from development/integration responsibilities for the in-scope application.

###### Actions Required for Compliance

- [ ] Appoint a named change-management process owner or lead from a team that is not involved in development or integration.
- [ ] Document the procedure for developing the change management (CM) process, including a clear definition of “direct involvement”.
- [ ] Create a RACI (Responsible, Accountable, Consulted, Informed) and responsibilities matrix showing that the configuration management (CM) process development is **Responsible/Accountable** for the CM Process Owner, and that developers and integrators are **Consulted/Informed**.

- [ ] Set up an approval process for configuration management (CM) process changes that require sign-off from the configuration management (CM) process owner, and keep the approval records as evidence.
- [ ] Carry out and record an initial and regular independence review to confirm that the change management process owner is not directly involved in system development or system integration.

### CM-10 — Software Usage Restrictions (Control)

This expectation is about making sure the organisation uses software correctly, following the terms of its licences and copyright law, and that file-sharing tools are not used to share protected material without permission. Without this, people could copy or share software beyond what the organisation has paid for, or use peer-to-peer sharing to distribute films, music, books, or software in ways that break copyright and contract terms. This creates legal and financial risk.

To meet this expectation, the organisation must:
- write and keep a clear list of which software can be used and the conditions for using it
- make sure staff can access these rules
- ensure all software and its documentation are used in line with licence contracts and copyright law
- identify software covered by quantity licences, track how many copies or uses are in place, and keep evidence of this tracking
- set documented rules for peer-to-peer file sharing, limit it to authorised use only, and keep records showing that these restrictions are applied

##### Automated licence entitlement tracking with install enforcement

- Category: Software
- Priority: Critical

###### WHAT

If software use goes beyond the licence terms, the system should trigger enforcement actions.

#### CM-10.1 — Open-source Software (Enhancement)

This requirement ensures that only open-source software your organisation has already approved is used, and that it is checked and made safe before it is put into live use. Without this, teams could accidentally install software that contains known weaknesses, uses terms that do not match how you plan to use it, or includes hidden parts you cannot account for—leaving your systems exposed and creating legal or operational problems.

To meet this expectation, the organisation must:
- allow open-source packages only if they are listed in its own approved repository; and
- before any approved version is deployed, carry out a security review for the specific purpose, prepare a clear list of what the software includes, confirm the licence terms are compatible with your intended use, and identify and fix any known vulnerabilities for that exact version.

If any vulnerabilities have not been properly addressed to the organisation’s standard, the software must not be deployed.

- Priority: Critical

3. licence compliance confirmation, and
4. evidence that known vulnerabilities have been fixed to the required standard.

###### WHY (control requirement)
NIST SP 800-53 configuration management (CM)-10(1) enhancement requires the organisation to “establish the following restrictions on the use of open-source software: {{ insert: param, cm-10.01_odp }}.” The organisation-defined parameter states: “Use only approved open-source software from the organisation repository; require security review, SBOM, licence compliance, and vulnerability remediation before deployment.”

- Drift detection identifies any deployed differences and triggers remediation actions.

### CM-11 — User-installed Software (Control)

This expectation is about preventing people from installing software that the organisation has not approved, while still allowing necessary updates. Without these rules, a user could accidentally (or deliberately) install unknown or suspicious software, or use an unauthorised method to install it. That software could then be used to steal information, disrupt work, or introduce hidden malware. At the same time, if legitimate security updates were blocked, systems would be left exposed.

To meet this expectation, the organisation must publish clear written rules covering which software users may install and which they may not. This includes that new applications can only be installed from the organisation-approved app store, unknown or suspicious software is blocked, unauthorised installers are not allowed, and security patches and updates for existing software are permitted.

The organisation must enforce these rules using automated checks that only allow approved applications, plus a process where system owners confirm that installed software matches the rules. Compliance must be monitored every week, and the organisation must keep evidence of the checks and any follow-up actions.

CM-11 requires organisations to define what actions are allowed and what actions are not allowed for software installed by users. ###### HOW (specific steps/approach)

3. **Apply automated approval at device level (endpoints)** using endpoint application control/allowlisting, so only catalogue-approved application packages/signatures can be installed and/or run. - Security patches and updates for already-approved software are delivered through the approved process and are not blocked.

- For each exception/drift item, there is a record from a system owner confirming the approval decision and the remediation outcome.

#### CM-11.2 — Software Installation with Privileged Status (Enhancement)

This requirement is about ensuring that only people who have been explicitly authorised can install software on a system, and that everyone else is prevented from doing so. The real-world risk it addresses is that an ordinary user could accidentally or deliberately install unauthorised software, which could then be used to steal information, disrupt services, or introduce malicious software (malware) that the organisation cannot easily control.

To meet this expectation, the organisation must clearly state which roles or individuals are allowed to install software with elevated permissions (for example, people acting as system administrators). It must also ensure the system only allows software installation when those elevated permissions are present, using practical safeguards that block installation attempts by anyone without them.

The organisation must keep written procedures for handling software installed by users, reflect this rule in its overall planning documents, and document how the system enforces it. Finally, it must record software installation activity, raise alerts when unauthorised installation is attempted, and continuously monitor to ensure these events are detected and escalated.

##### JIT privileged installation gated by identity and audited sessions

- Category: Software
- Priority: Critical

###### WHAT
Put in place technical controls so end users can install software **only when they have explicit privileged status**, and make sure every installation attempt (both allowed and blocked) is **logged and triggers alerts**.

###### WHY (control requirement)
NIST SP 800-53 **CM-11(2)** enhancement requires: **“Allow user installation of software only with explicit privileged status.”** Guidance notes that privileged status can be granted through a role such as **system administrator**. This approach allows installation only during a privileged session that can be audited, and blocks it at other times.

###### HOW (specific steps/approach)
1. **Define who can be privileged installers**: Create an official list of the roles or individuals allowed to install software with privileged status (for example, the “System Administrators” group) in the organisation’s identity provider (IdP) and/or privileged access management (PAM).
2. **Use just-in-time (JIT) / just-enough administration (JEA)**: Remove permanent local administrator rights and use **just-in-time elevation** (or just-enough administration) so privileged status is granted only when an installation is requested, for a short time window, and only to approved users.
3. **Control installation execution**: Set up endpoints so software installation requires an elevated permission context that is only available during the privileged session (for example, Windows user account control (UAC) policy / administrator token requirement; macOS authorisation; or equivalent operating system/endpoint hardening). Ensure non-privileged users cannot bypass this by running installers without elevation.
4. **Require strong, explicit approval for elevation**: Require phishing-resistant multi-factor authentication (MFA) for elevation, and where possible, an approval workflow for higher-risk installs (for example, new publishers or unsigned files), so privileged status is truly explicit.
5. **Centralise audit records**: Ensure logs record **who** (user identity), **what** (installer/application), **when** (timestamp), **where** (host), and **outcome** (success/denied) for both privileged and blocked attempts; send these to security information and event management (SIEM) for correlation.
6. **Alert on unauthorised installation attempts**: Create detection rules for events showing installation attempts without privileged status (for example, privilege request denied, installer launch blocked) and send them to the security operations centre (SOC) and ticketing system with escalation.
7. **Document enforcement and procedures**: Keep written procedures explaining how privileged status is granted and checked for installers, how exceptions are handled, and how user-installed software is managed (including review/approval expectations).

###### WHO (role responsible)
- **Security Architect / Identity and Access Management (IAM) Lead**: Define privileged installer roles and implement just-in-time (JIT) / just-enough administration (JEA) and conditional access.
- **Endpoint Engineering Lead**: Configure endpoint installation controls and hardening.
- **SOC / SIEM Engineer**: Set up logging, correlation, and alerts for both blocked and allowed installation attempts.
- **IT Operations / Change Manager**: Maintain the written procedures and ensure operational runbooks match the controls.

###### Acceptance criteria
- A non-privileged user cannot install software successfully; installation attempts are **blocked** and recorded.
- An authorised privileged user can install software **only** when an explicit privileged session is active (JIT/JEA), and the session is auditable.
- Audit logs show user identity, host, installer/application, timestamp, and outcome for both allowed and denied attempts.
- Alerts are generated for unauthorised installation attempts and sent into the agreed incident/ticket workflow.
- Written procedures are in place and up to date, describing how privileged status is granted/verified and how exceptions are handled.

###### Actions Required for Compliance

- [ ] Define and approve the privileged installer role(s) (for example, the system administrator group) in the identity provider (IdP) and privileged access management (PAM), and record the named individuals who are authorised to use them.
- [ ] Set up just-in-time (JIT) and just-enough-access (JEA) controls for installation permissions, using short-lived privileged sessions and phishing-resistant multi-factor authentication (MFA).
- [ ] Set up endpoint and operating system installation controls so that installing requires an elevated token or session that non-privileged users cannot obtain.
- [ ] Enable and centralise audit logging for installation attempts, including the user identity, device (host), installer or application, time of the attempt (timestamp), and whether it was successful or denied.
- [ ] Set up security information and event management (SIEM) alerts for unauthorised software installation attempts and for requests to gain higher privileges that are denied, including rules to escalate incidents to the security operations centre (SOC).
- [ ] Publish and keep written procedures for checking whether users have privileged access, dealing with exceptions, and managing software that users install themselves.

#### CM-11.3 — Automated Enforcement and Monitoring (Enhancement)

This requirement is about preventing unauthorised software from being installed on your computers and keeping a continuous watch for anything unexpected. Without it, harmful software could be installed by mistake or by someone with bad intent, and it might run quietly until it causes real damage, such as stealing information or disrupting day-to-day operations.  

To meet this expectation, the organisation must use automated tools to enforce its software installation rules on managed devices. This means only allowing approved software that has been verified as genuine, automatically blocking anything else, and automatically correcting cases where a device’s software setup has changed away from the approved standard.  

At the same time, it must continuously check which software is installed, raise immediate alerts when new or unexpected software appears, and produce clear compliance reports for the Security Operations Centre, using central dashboards so any non-compliance can be quickly identified and acted on.

##### Signed allowlisting with automated install blocking, drift remediation

- Category: Software
- Priority: Critical

###### WHAT
Put in place automated measures that (1) enforce compliance with software installation rules by stopping unauthorised software from being installed, (2) continuously check what software is installed to spot any new or unexpected software, (3) automatically fix any configuration changes that drift away from the approved standard, and (4) report compliance status and enforcement results to the Security Operations Centre (SOC) through central dashboards.

###### WHY (control requirement)
CM-11(3) enhancement requires the organisation to **enforce and monitor compliance with software installation rules using automated measures** (parameter: **cm-11.3_prm_1**) so that unauthorised software installation can be detected and dealt with faster. This can be a sign of hostile activity.

###### HOW (specific steps/approach)
1. **Set the approved software installation standard** as a list of permitted software (an allowlist) including software name, version, publisher, signing requirements, and allowed installer sources. Store this as version-controlled policy documents (policy-as-code where possible) and manage changes through formal change control.
2. **Set endpoint management to enforce signed-software allowlisting**: only allow software to be installed and run if it matches the approved allowlist and its digital signature is valid (including the certificate chain and how certificate revocation is handled). Block/stop installation attempts that do not match.
3. **Enable automated blocking and keep enforcement records**: stop common installation methods used in the environment (for example, MSI/EXE installers and package manager installs) and record the outcome of each attempt (allowed/blocked, reason, device identity, and software details).
4. **Run continuous software checks and detect changes**: continuously or event-driven collect information about installed software and relevant file details (such as hash/signature where supported). Identify and flag newly seen software and any signature or allowlist mismatches.
5. **Automatically fix configuration drift**: when drift is detected (for example, approved software is removed, unauthorised software is present, or settings no longer match the approved standard), automatically remove or isolate the unauthorised software and restore the approved components/settings. Use a defined service level agreement (SLA) for remediation.
6. **Send enforcement and software-check results to the SOC**: send real-time alerts and compliance events into the security monitoring workflow using the security information and event management (SIEM) system, with enough detail (device, software name/version, signature status, allowlist match status, enforcement action taken, and remediation status). Provide central compliance dashboards showing which devices are compliant or not compliant and how quickly issues are fixed.

###### WHO
- **Endpoint management and security engineering lead**: designs and implements the allowlisting, enforcement, and remediation processes.
- **SOC/SIEM engineer**: integrates alerts and dashboards into the SOC monitoring and triage process.
- **Information security governance owner**: approves the approved software installation standard and the remediation SLAs.

###### Acceptance criteria
- Unauthorised software installation attempts are **automatically blocked** and produce **audit-ready enforcement records**.
- The system performs **continuous software checks** and identifies newly installed or unexpected software within the agreed monitoring period.
- Any detected drift is **automatically fixed** back to the approved standard within the defined SLA, with remediation outcomes recorded.
- The SOC receives **real-time alerts** and can see compliance status via **central dashboards** showing device-level compliance and remediation progress.
- Evidence is available for: allowlist versioning, enforcement logs, software inventory and change-detection results, remediation actions, and SOC alert/dashboard outputs.

###### Actions Required for Compliance

- [ ] Create a version-controlled, signed-software approval list baseline (approved software packages, versions, publishers, signing requirements, and permitted download sources).
- [ ] Set up endpoint management to check digital signatures and only allow software packages on an approved list (allowlist), blocking all other installation attempts.
- [ ] Turn on enforcement event logging for every allow or deny decision, including details about the device (endpoint) and the software package involved.
- [ ] Enable continuous software inventory and change detection to spot newly installed or unexpected software, and to identify mismatches between software signatures and the approved allowlist.
- [ ] Define and start automated drift remediation actions (remove, restore, or repair) and agree a remediation service level agreement (SLA).

### CM-12 — Information Location (Control)

This requirement is about knowing exactly where your most sensitive information is kept and who is allowed to access it—like having a clear map of every room where confidential files are handled and stored. Without this, sensitive information could be processed or stored in the wrong place, or be accessible to the wrong people, increasing the risk of unauthorised viewing, loss, or accidental exposure—especially when systems change.

The organisation must:
- define which types of confidential and secret information are covered;
- create and keep up to date a documented record of where that information is processed and where it is stored, including the specific systems and parts involved and the specific authorised users who can access those parts;
- record any changes over time to where the information is processed or stored;
- ensure the documentation matches the organisation’s system records and access lists, with updates supported by change records.

##### Authoritative register mapping Confidential/Secret data to locations

- Category: Software
- Priority: Critical

###### WHAT
Create and maintain an auditable, authoritative register that shows **where Confidential and Secret information is processed and stored** across the application’s system components (including environments such as production, test, and disaster recovery). It must also record **which authorised users and roles** can access those components. The register must capture **how these locations change over time**, linked to controlled change records.

###### WHY (control requirement)
CM-12 requires understanding and documenting **information location**—specifically **where each information type is held within system components**, **how the information is processed**, and the **security category** of the information so it can be protected appropriately and managed through policy. The information and component locations must be reflected in architecture and design decisions (see guidance references to SA-4/SA-8/SA-17).

###### HOW (specific steps/approach)
1. **Define what counts as “Confidential” and “Secret”** for this application/system (data labels, datasets, and included items such as databases, object storage, file shares, caches, backups, exports, and logs that contain sensitive content). Use the organisation-defined parameter: *“Confidential and Secret information processed and stored by the information system”*.
2. **Design the register format** with, at minimum, fields for:
   - Data type/dataset (classification label)
   - Processing location(s): application/service component name, environment, and processing endpoint/component
   - Storage location(s): database/schema, bucket/share, retention tier, environment
   - Authorised access mapping: authorised user groups/roles (use groups/roles rather than listing individual users)
   - Evidence pointers: links to inventory sources (component inventory, role access information, storage inventory)
   - Change linkage: change ticket or release identifier(s) that moved or modified processing or storage locations
   - Effective dates and last-reviewed date
3. **Populate processing and storage locations** using technology-specific discovery and mapping sources, then reconcile:
   - Application/service architecture and integration diagrams (data flow mapping)
   - Database inventory and schema inventory; use query and log information to confirm actual processing
   - Cloud/object storage inventory (buckets/shares, retention tiers)
   - ETL/ELT orchestration lineage (where data is transformed and where outputs are stored)
   - Backup and disaster recovery inventories to identify where sensitive data is stored outside the main systems
4. **Reconcile against the system component inventory** so every register entry points to a real, managed component, and flag any differences for correction.
5. **Link authorised users** by mapping each processing/storage component to identity and access management (IAM) roles and groups (from the organisation’s identity provider). Use evidence by sampling group membership at review time rather than maintaining a fragile list of individual users.
6. **Keep the register up to date** by linking it to change management:
   - Require that any change affecting data flows, environment promotions, migrations, storage moves, or backup/retention settings updates the relevant register entries and attaches the change record.
   - Do periodic reconciliation (for example, monthly or quarterly, aligned to release timing) and record review dates.
7. **Store the register in a controlled repository** (version-controlled, access-controlled, with an owner and a review process) so audit evidence is consistent and can be retrieved.

###### WHO
- **Application Owner / Data Owner**: defines what counts as Confidential/Secret and approves the register content.
- **Security Architect / Compliance Lead**: ensures the register meets CM-12 expectations and that reviews happen on time.
- **Platform/Infrastructure Engineering**: provides component inventory links and environment mapping.
- **IAM/Identity Team**: provides the authoritative role and group mappings.
- **Change Manager / Release Manager**: ensures change records trigger register updates.

###### Acceptance criteria
- The register includes entries for all defined Confidential/Secret data types/datasets processed and stored by the application, including **both processing and storage locations** and the **environment**.
- Each location entry references the **specific system component(s)** and includes the **authorised access mapping** (roles/groups).
- For each location change (for example, migration, environment promotion, storage relocation), the register shows an **effective date** and is linked to the **controlled change record**.
- The register is reconciled against the system component inventory, with no unresolved mismatches except through an approved risk or exception process.
- The register is stored in a controlled, version-controlled repository with documented ownership and review dates, and evidence can be produced for an audit sample.

###### Actions Required for Compliance

- [ ] Define what counts as **Confidential** and **Secret** information for this application, including the **data labels**, **data sets**, and any **included supporting materials (artefacts)**.
- [ ] Create a register (data record) schema that captures where data is processed, where it is stored, which environment it belongs to, and which users or systems are authorised to access it.
- [ ] Set up the processing and storage locations using the system’s architecture and data-flow map, the storage inventory, the database inventory, and the extract, transform, load (ETL) lineage.
- [ ] Compare the register with the system component inventory and fix any differences, or report them as exceptions.
- [ ] Map each data-processing and data-storage component to the authorised identity and access management (IAM) roles or groups, and record evidence locations (pointers).
- [ ] Integrate updates to the register into the change management process, so that location changes are linked to the approved change and release records.
- [ ] Keep the register in a secure, version-controlled storage location, and carry out regular checks and reconciliation, recording the dates of each review.

#### CM-12.1 — Automated Tools to Support Information Location (Enhancement)

### CM-13 — Data Action Mapping (Control)

This requirement is about creating a clear, documented “who does what with personal information” map for each system. This helps the organisation understand exactly how people’s details are handled. Without it, personal data could be collected, updated, shared, stored, or deleted in ways that are not properly understood or controlled, increasing the risk of privacy breaches, inappropriate sharing, or keeping information for longer than intended.

The organisation must develop, document, and keep up to date a data action map that covers the full journey of personal information—from collection and creation, through use, sharing, retention, and disposal. For every activity that handles personal information, the map must record:
- which specific types of personal details are involved
- how those details are processed
- which parts of the system carry out the work
- who owns or runs those parts

The map must be coordinated with both privacy and security planning, stored in the organisation’s system and privacy documentation, and updated through the organisation’s normal change process, with changes tracked.

- the **PII elements** being handled,
- how the PII is handled (including whether it is visible to people and any later processing),

- the **owners/operators** responsible for those components.

Control CM-13 requires the organisation to **develop and document a map of system data actions**. 4. **Keep the map up to date using configuration management**: version the mapping document, link each data action entry to the relevant system/component identifiers, and require updates through the organisation’s change control process whenever data actions or PII processing paths change (for example, a new API endpoint, a new data store, a new retention schedule, or a new disclosure integration).
5. **Create audit-ready evidence** by storing the mapping document in the organisation’s controlled repository and keeping records of changes showing when and why updates were made.

### CM-14 — Signed Components (Control)

This requirement is about ensuring that only trusted software and device updates can be installed. Before any update—such as a patch, service pack, device driver, or BIOS/UEFI firmware update—is applied to your systems, the organisation must confirm that it has a digital signature and that the signature was created using a certificate the organisation recognises and has approved. Otherwise, attackers could disguise harmful or altered code as a legitimate update, which could allow them to take control of systems, steal data, or disrupt operations.

To meet this expectation, the organisation must:
- keep a clear list of approved signing certificates
- define which software and firmware items must be signed
- ensure the installation process blocks anything that is not signed, or that has a signature that does not pass verification

The organisation must also:
- document who is responsible for checking certificates
- include this approach in its configuration management and system security planning
- keep records of authorised changes and maintain an inventory of components
- use automated installation or checking tools where available to enforce the rule for both IT systems software and operational technology (OT) device firmware

##### Signed-only enforcement for IT/OT updates using approved certs

- Category: Software
- Priority: Critical

###### WHAT
Put in place a control that only allows installation of the organisation-defined **software and firmware components** (updates, patches, service packs, device drivers, and BIOS/UEFI updates) if the component has a **digital signature** that is **present, valid, and checked against a set of certificates approved by the organisation**.

###### WHY (control requirement)
CM-14 requires stopping installation of the organisation-defined software/firmware components **unless they have been verified as digitally signed using a certificate that the organisation recognises and approves**. This applies to software/firmware types including updates, patches, service packs, device drivers, and BIOS/UEFI updates.

###### HOW (specific steps/approach)
###### 1) Create and manage an approved signing-certificate list
- Keep a controlled list of **approved signing certificates** (for example, certificate thumbprints/issuers and any required restrictions such as extended key usage (EKU) relevant to code signing).
- Set rules for how certificates are handled over time, including intake, approval, expiry monitoring, and removal (revocation/blacklisting), with emergency additions or removals only under change control.

###### 2) Define what components are covered and how they map to the rules
- For **software components** (parameter: cm-14_odp.01), specify which update/patch/driver items are included (by component type and/or specific items).
- For **firmware components** (parameter: cm-14_odp.02), specify which device firmware/BIOS/UEFI update packages are included.
- Keep an auditable record linking each covered component to the expected signature-checking rule.

###### 3) Only allow signed components at the point of installation
- **IT software deployment**: configure the application/endpoint/server deployment process to block installation if signature checking fails or if the signing certificate is not on the approved list.
- **Operational technology (OT) firmware/BIOS/UEFI**: configure the firmware update process/tooling (for example, the vendor update method, management platform, or flashing workflow) to verify the signature and certificate chain against the approved list before the update can run.
- Make sure the checks cover both: (a) the signature is present and valid, and (b) the signing certificate is in the organisation-approved set (not just “any valid signature”).

###### 4) Keep proof of checks for audit purposes
- For every attempted installation of a covered component, record: component identifier/version, verification result, which certificate was matched (or why it did not match), and the time/date.
- Send these logs to the organisation’s monitoring system and security information and event management (SIEM) so CM-14 evidence can be retrieved.

###### 5) Manage exceptions without weakening the control
- If an exception is needed (for example, an emergency vendor release), require change control approval and a time-limited exception that still records the certificate used and the reason. The default remains “signed-only”.

###### WHO (role responsible)
- **Configuration Manager / Release Manager**: owns the covered component list and ensures the enforcement is applied in release and deployment processes.
- **Security Engineering / identity and access management (IAM) and public key infrastructure (PKI) team (or equivalent)**: owns the approved signing-certificate list and its lifecycle.
- **Platform/OT Engineering**: implements the firmware/BIOS update enforcement in the OT update process/tooling.
- **Change Control Board**: approves any time-limited exceptions.

###### Acceptance criteria
1. For all covered **software and firmware components** (parameter: cm-14_prm_1), installation is blocked if the component is unsigned or if the signing certificate is not in the approved certificate list.
2. For all successful installations, keep verification evidence (component ID/version, certificate matched, verification outcome, timestamp) and ensure it can be retrieved for audit.
3. The approved certificate list is maintained under change control, with documented lifecycle steps (approval, expiry/revocation handling).
4. The OT firmware/BIOS/UEFI update process enforces the same signed-only rule for the organisation-defined firmware components.
5. Any exceptions (if any) are time-limited, approved, and still generate audit logs showing the certificate used and the justification.

###### Actions Required for Compliance

- [ ] Set up an organisation-approved register of signing certificates, with controlled approval and ongoing management throughout their life cycle (including how certificates are revoked or expire).
- [ ] Identify and document which in-scope software and firmware components must be verified using digital signatures for **cm-14_odp.01** and **cm-14_odp.02** (by component type and/or by specific items).
- [ ] Set up IT deployment tools so that software installation is blocked if the file’s digital signature cannot be verified or if the signing certificate is not listed in the approved certificate register.
- [ ] Set up operational technology (OT) firmware, basic input/output system (BIOS), and unified extensible firmware interface (UEFI) update tools and processes to check digital signatures and only allow updates when the signing certificate matches an approved list, before installing or flashing the software.
- [ ] Set up audit logs for every in-scope installation attempt, recording the component identity, the result of the checks, and the details of whether the certificate matched.
- [ ] Set up a time-limited exception process under change control that records which certificate was used and why, while keeping “signed-only” as the default.
